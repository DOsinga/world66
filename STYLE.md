# World66 Style Guide

## Voice & Tone

World66 writes like a well-traveled friend telling you about a place over drinks — knowledgeable but never academic, enthusiastic but never breathless. The tone is **warm, direct, and honest**.

- **Second person when giving advice** ("You will find...", "If you are interested in nature..."), **third person when describing places** ("Botswana is a very dry country").
- **Conversational but not sloppy.** Contractions are fine. Slang is not.
- **Opinionated.** We have favorites and we say so. "We invite you to disagree." Don't hedge everything — if a place is great, say it's great. If it's dangerous, say that too.
- **Honest about difficulties.** We don't sell destinations. If a country is poor, the infrastructure is bad, or the streets are dangerous after dark, we say so plainly. No sugar-coating, no scaremongering either.
- **Encouraging of adventure.** We lean toward the traveler who wants to get off the beaten track. We cover Chad and the Central African Republic with the same enthusiasm as Paris and Rome.

## What We Write About

World66 covers the whole world, with equal interest in famous destinations and obscure ones. Every place deserves a page.

### The standard sections

- **Country/region overview** — the heart of the guide. What makes this place distinctive, what are the highlights, why should someone go.
- **Destinations** — list of cities and places within a country or region, with links.
- **Getting There** — how to arrive (flights, overland, boats). Practical.
- **Getting Around** — transport within the country/region.
- **Sights** — what to see. Monuments, nature, architecture, oddities.
- **Eating Out** — restaurants, street food, local cuisine. Specific names, dishes, prices when known.
- **Bars & Cafes** — where to drink and hang out.
- **Nightlife & Entertainment** — clubs, live music, theater.
- **Beaches** — where they are, what they're like, what you can do.
- **Shopping** — markets, shops, what to buy.
- **Museums** — individual museums with descriptions.
- **Festivals** — events worth timing a visit around.
- **Day Trips** — excursions from a base city.
- **Things to Do** — activities: hiking, diving, wildlife, sports.
- **Top 5 Must Do's** — the absolute highlights, numbered, with a paragraph each.
- **Budget Travel Ideas** — how to do it cheap.
- **Family Travel Ideas** — what works with kids.
- **7-Day Itinerary** — a week-long trip plan.
- **Tours & Excursions** — organized trips available.
- **Practical Information** — visas, money, safety, health, climate, when to go.
- **People** — demographics and cultural context.
- **Books** — reading recommendations (travel writing, history, fiction set there).
- **When to Go** — climate and seasons.

Not every place needs every section. A small town might only have an overview and a couple of sections. That's fine.

### What we care about

- **The texture of a place.** Not just what's there, but what it feels like. "The Corsicans speak their own language and have a great sense of freedom." "Life is more laid back; reggae is the favourite kind of music."
- **History as context, not lecture.** We mention history to explain why a place looks and feels the way it does. We don't reproduce encyclopedia entries.
- **Practical specifics.** Actual restaurant names, actual dishes, actual prices, actual addresses. Vague advice ("try the local food") is useless — name the restaurant and the dish.
- **Nature and landscape.** We describe terrain, climate, wildlife. "Forests of palms, grasslands, islands, flood plains and lagoons."
- **The people.** Who lives here, what languages they speak, what the culture is like.
- **The less obvious.** We love the fact that you can ski in Morocco, that the Kalahari blooms in March, that you can stand on a stone sundial at the Library of Alexandria and your shadow tells the time.

### What we don't care about

- **Hotel reviews and booking links.** We're a guide, not a booking engine.
- **Star ratings and scores.** We describe, we don't rate.
- **Corporate tourism copy.** No "world-class amenities" or "unforgettable luxury experiences."
- **Exhaustive lists.** Better to describe five places well than list fifty with no detail.

## How to Write an Overview

The country or city overview is the most important page. It should make someone want to go there — or at least understand the place. Structure:

1. **Open with what makes this place distinctive.** One or two sentences that capture the essence. "France is the number one tourist destination in the world. It has everything that you could ever want to see on your holidays." Or: "Albania is the poorest country of Europe and one of the least visited by travelers."

2. **Paint the picture.** Walk the reader through the geography, the regions, the highlights. Move naturally from north to south, coast to interior, or whatever makes geographic sense. Mention specific places and link to them.

3. **Include something surprising or personal.** A contrast, an aside, an opinion. "Chobe National Park has a wonderful concentration of animals but has become very crowded." This is what makes us different from a Wikipedia article.

4. **End with destinations.** A linked list of the places covered within this country/region.

## How to Write a "Top 5 Must Do's"

These are our most opinionated pages. Each entry should:

1. Have a bold, clear title.
2. Describe the experience in one substantial paragraph — what you'll see, what makes it special, a practical tip or two.
3. Appeal to the senses and imagination. "Stand, speak in the softest of whispers, and hear your voice amplified to a shout."
4. Mix different types of activity: history, nature, food, adventure, relaxation.

## How to Write About Restaurants, Bars, Sights

Be specific. Give the name, say what's good about it, mention a signature dish or a standout feature. A few vivid sentences beat a long generic description. Include address, hours, and prices when you have them.

## Formatting

- **Markdown throughout.** Headings, bold, italic, links.
- **Breadcrumb path** in italics below the title: *Africa > Botswana > Okavango delta*
- **Internal links** to other World66 pages wherever a destination is mentioned: `[Maun](/africa/botswana/maun)`.
- **Bold for emphasis** on key terms, place names in lists, and section labels.
- **Short paragraphs.** Travel writing should be easy to scan. Two to four sentences per paragraph is ideal.

## Language

- Write in English.
- British and American spelling are both fine — don't mix them within a single page.
- Keep sentences relatively short and clear. Complex grammar obscures rather than impresses.
- Avoid jargon, marketing speak, and cliché. No "hidden gems," no "mecca for foodies," no "a feast for the senses."
- It's okay to be funny or wry, but don't force it.

## The World66 Perspective

We believe every place on earth is interesting. We believe in the independent traveler who wants to understand where they are, not just consume it. We believe a good travel guide is honest, specific, and written by people who actually care about the places they describe.

We'd rather have a short, genuine, specific article than a long generic one. If you've been there and you know something useful or vivid, write it down. If you haven't, don't fake it.
