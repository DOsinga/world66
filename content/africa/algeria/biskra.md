Biskra sits right at the line where the Tell Atlas gives way to the Sahara, and you can feel the transition. To the north, green mountains; to the south, palm groves and then open desert. This is Algeria's "gateway to the Sahara," a phrase that for once actually means something -- the climate, the light, and the landscape all change dramatically as you arrive.

The city has been drawing outsiders for a long time. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, European writers and painters came here seeking warmth and exoticism -- Andre Gide wrote parts of "The Immoralist" here, and Etienne Dinet settled permanently, painting scenes of local life that now hang in major European museums. The palm groves that surround the city are still its most distinctive feature, stretching for kilometers and producing some of Algeria's best dates. The Ziban oases, as this region is known, contain hundreds of thousands of date palms.

Modern Biskra is a working Algerian city of around 300,000 people, not a tourist destination in any conventional sense. But it has genuine character. The old town and the Sidi Zarzour mosque are worth a visit, and the hammam culture here is still very much alive. The real draw, though, is using Biskra as a base for exploring the edge of the desert -- the Gorges d'El Kantara to the north, where the road cuts through a dramatic limestone canyon, and the M'Chounech canyon further south. The weekly market is lively and worth timing a visit around.

The best time to visit is between October and April. Summers in Biskra are genuinely fierce, with temperatures regularly exceeding 45 degrees. The city is well connected by road and rail from the north, with direct trains from [Constantine](/africa/algeria/constantine) and buses from most major cities.
