Ghardaïa is the main city of the M'zab Valley, a UNESCO World Heritage site in the northern Sahara about 600 km south of Algiers. The valley contains five fortified cities (ksour) built by the Ibadi Mozabites over a thousand years ago, and they are among the most extraordinary urban landscapes in Africa — tiered clusters of pastel-coloured houses rising to a minaret at the summit of each hill, all designed with a strict religious and social logic that Le Corbusier studied and admired.

The old town of Ghardaïa itself, centred on its marketplace and mosque, is the largest and most accessible of the five. Beni Isguen, the holiest of the ksour, is a walled city that remains conservative and closed to outsiders without a guide. El Atteuf, the oldest settlement, has Saharan architecture at its most austere and beautiful.

Visiting requires some planning. The Mozabite communities are private and conservative — modest dress is essential, photography restrictions are strict, and a local guide is mandatory for entering the old towns. The valley is best reached by domestic flight from Algiers or by shared taxi from the north.
