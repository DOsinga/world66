Sétif sits on a high plateau in the Tell Atlas mountains at around 1,100 metres, which gives it a surprisingly mild climate for Algeria and distinguishes it sharply from the scorching lowlands to the south. It is the largest city in eastern Algeria and one of the country's main commercial and agricultural centres — a working city rather than a tourist one, but with enough history and character to reward a day or two.

The city has been inhabited since Roman times, when it was known as Sitifis and served as the capital of the Roman province of Mauretania Sitifensis. A significant archaeological museum in the city centre holds finds from this period. Sétif entered modern history violently: on 8 May 1945, as Algerians joined celebrations marking the end of World War II, protests turned deadly when French police fired on demonstrators. The massacre and its brutal aftermath — in which French forces killed thousands of Algerians — is considered one of the catalysts for the independence war that followed a decade later.

At the heart of the city is a large public park with an amusement area, an artificial lake, fountains, and a small zoo. It is genuinely popular with local families and gives the city centre an unexpectedly pleasant, liveable feel. The ruins of a Byzantine fortress sit just to the west of the park, and the archaeological museum lies to the east. The surrounding countryside — rolling farmland and Atlas foothills — is easy to explore from here.
