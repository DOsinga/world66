A collection of Neolithic rock engravings scattered across several sites near [Taghit](/africa/algeria/taghit), the most accessible at Barrebi and along the Zousfana valley. The carvings date back thousands of years to a time when the Sahara was fertile grassland, and they depict the animals that once lived here: elephants, ostriches, cattle, antelopes, and human figures with bows and spears.

The engravings are cut directly into flat rock surfaces and are remarkably clear despite their age. Some panels show hunting scenes; others depict cattle herds that suggest a pastoral society. The contrast between these images of a green, animal-rich landscape and the barren desert that surrounds them today is striking — a powerful reminder of how dramatically the climate of North Africa has changed.

The sites are a short drive from Taghit and can be visited independently, though a local guide will help you find the less obvious panels and provide context. The rock surfaces are exposed and unprotected, so visit in the morning or late afternoon to avoid the worst of the heat. There is no entrance fee or formal site management.
