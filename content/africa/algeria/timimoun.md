Timimoun is an oasis town in central Algeria, deep in the Sahara, built on the edge of a great salt flat called the Grand Erg Occidental. It is one of the most visually striking towns in the Algerian south: the buildings are painted in a deep, rust-red colour — a style called Soudano-Saharien architecture — set against the pale dunes and a vast palm grove below. The old ksar (fortified quarter) is a maze of arched alleyways and crumbling mud-brick towers.

The area around Timimoun is dotted with smaller oasis villages and sebkha (salt lake) viewpoints that reward a few days of exploration. The town comes alive during the regional festival celebrating the date harvest in autumn. Timimoun is not easy to reach — it lies far from the main Saharan routes — but that distance keeps it relatively free of the tourist infrastructure that shapes visits to better-known oases like [Ghardaïa](/africa/algeria/ghardaia) or [Tamanrasset](/africa/algeria/tamanrasset). If you make the effort to get here, you are unlikely to regret it.
