Tipasa is where Roman ruins meet the Mediterranean, and the combination is hard to beat. This small coastal town about 70 kilometers west of [Algiers](/africa/algeria/algiers) holds one of Algeria's most atmospheric archaeological sites -- a UNESCO World Heritage Site where crumbling temples and basilicas stand among wildflowers with the sea crashing below.

The ruins span Punic, Roman, and early Christian periods. The most striking features are the Roman amphitheater overlooking the water, the remnants of two Christian basilicas, and the Punic necropolis on the eastern hill. Unlike many archaeological sites that feel like open-air museums, Tipasa's ruins are tangled up with nature -- stone walls disappear under fig trees, mosaics peek through grass, and the scent of eucalyptus hangs in the air. Albert Camus loved this place and wrote about it in his essay "Nuptials at Tipasa," describing the sensual pleasure of sun and stone and sea. You can still feel exactly what he meant.

The on-site museum houses mosaics, sarcophagi, and smaller finds from the excavations. Beyond the ruins, Tipasa is a pleasant enough seaside town with a few restaurants serving fresh fish along the waterfront. It makes an easy day trip from Algiers -- shared taxis and buses run regularly -- though the site deserves a full, unhurried morning. Come early to have the ruins to yourself before the afternoon visitors arrive.
