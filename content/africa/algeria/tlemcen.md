Tlemcen sits in the hills of northwestern Algeria, close to the Moroccan border. Historically it had closer cultural ties to [Fez](/africa/morocco/fez) than to other Algerian cities, and that Moorish influence is still visible in its architecture and traditions. The city was the capital of a medieval Berber kingdom and a major centre of Islamic learning.

The Mosque and Tomb of Sidi Bou Mediene, on the outskirts of the city, is the most important sight -- a beautifully decorated complex dating from the 14th century, built around the tomb of the Sufi saint who is the city's patron. The Great Mosque in the old town dates from 1136 and is one of the finest Almoravid buildings in the Maghreb.

The Ville Nouvelle, the French colonial quarter, is among the most handsome in North Africa -- wide boulevards, arcaded buildings, and a pleasant central square. Tlemcen's altitude (about 800 metres) keeps it noticeably cooler than [Oran](/africa/algeria/oran) on the coast, making it a pleasant place to spend a few days.
