The Tsodilo Hills rise dramatically from the flat Kalahari sand in northwestern Botswana, a cluster of four quartzite hills that have been sacred to the San people for thousands of years. UNESCO designated the site a World Heritage Site in 2001, recognizing its extraordinary concentration of rock art — over 4,500 paintings spread across the hills, making it one of the highest densities of rock art anywhere on earth. The San call it the "mountain of the gods."

The paintings range from simple animal outlines to complex scenes of hunting and ritual. Eland, giraffe, rhino, and hippo appear alongside geometric patterns and handprints, some dating back 24,000 years. Guided walks with a local San guide are essential — without one, many panels are easily missed. The landscape is striking in its own right: the hills are a geological anomaly visible from many kilometres across flat sand.

Getting here requires a 4WD and a willingness to navigate rough tracks. There is a small campsite at the base run by the Department of Wildlife and National Parks. Come prepared with water, food, and sun protection. The remoteness is part of the experience.
