**Of Water and the Spirit** by Malidoma Patrice Some is the essential book about Burkina Faso for Western readers. Some, a Dagara man from the southwest, was kidnapped as a child by Jesuit missionaries and spent 15 years in their schools before returning to his village for initiation. The book describes that initiation — encounters with the spirit world, shape-shifting, communion with nature — in vivid, matter-of-fact prose. Whether you read it as literal truth or powerful metaphor, it will change how you think about what constitutes reality. Some went on to earn PhDs in the West while maintaining his role as a traditional healer. It is unforgettable.

**Freedom in Fulani Social Life** by Paul Riesman is an introspective ethnography of a Fulani community in the Sahel. Riesman lived with the Jelgobe Fulani in northern Burkina Faso and wrote honestly about what he observed and felt, including his own limitations as an outsider. It is one of the most readable works of anthropology about the region — more personal journal than academic text.

**African Spaces** by Jean-Paul Bourdier and Trinh T. Minh-ha is a gorgeous study of vernacular architecture in Burkina Faso, with a focus on the decorated buildings of the Kassena, Nankani, and other groups. The photographs alone are worth the price, but the text is thoughtful too — exploring how architecture, art, and social life intertwine.

**Burkina Faso: Unsteady Statehood in West Africa** by Ernest Harsch provides historical and political context from the colonial period through the Sankara revolution and beyond. If you want to understand why Burkina Faso is the way it is — the coups, the poverty, the resilience — this is a good starting point.

**Thomas Sankara Speaks** collects the speeches and interviews of the revolutionary leader who renamed the country and governed from 1983 to 1987. Sankara is still a towering figure in Burkinabe identity — his face is on murals across [Ouagadougou](/africa/burkinafaso/ouagadougou) — and reading his words helps explain the country's political culture and the idealism that persists despite everything that followed.
