---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Burkinabe cuisine is hearty, unpretentious, and built around grains and sauces. It does not have the international profile of Senegalese or Ethiopian cooking, but it has real character, and eating well here means eating the way locals do — at street stalls, in family courtyards, and at maquis (open-air bars that double as restaurants).

The staple you will encounter everywhere is **to** (also spelled tô), a thick, smooth porridge made from millet, sorghum, or maize flour. It arrives as a firm mound alongside a bowl of sauce — typically a rich, dark stew made from baobab leaves, okra, or groundnut paste, often with dried fish or meat. You tear off a piece of to, dip it in the sauce, and eat with your right hand. It is comfort food in the truest sense, and the quality of the sauce is what separates a good meal from a great one.

**Riz gras** — literally "fat rice" — is the other everyday dish. Rice cooked with tomatoes, onions, and meat in a single pot, absorbing all the flavor. It is the Burkinabe version of jollof rice, served at celebrations and roadside restaurants alike. Every cook has their own version. **Riz sauce** is the simpler cousin: plain rice with a ladle of sauce on top, available everywhere for a few hundred CFA francs.

The dish Burkina Faso is most famous for is **poulet bicyclette** — free-range chicken grilled over charcoal. The name comes from the old practice of transporting live chickens to market strapped to bicycle handlebars. The birds are lean, flavorful, and nothing like factory-farmed chicken. You will find them at maquis across the country, served with fried onions, tomato sauce, and attieke (fermented cassava couscous) or fries. A whole chicken at a maquis in [Ouagadougou](/africa/burkinafaso/ouagadougou) costs around 3,000-5,000 CFA francs.

For drinks, **dolo** is the traditional millet beer — cloudy, slightly sour, mildly alcoholic, and brewed by women called dolotieres. Drinking dolo is a social ritual, especially in the south and west. You sit on low benches in a courtyard, share calabash gourds, and the dolotiere keeps refilling. It is the cheapest drink in the country and an experience in itself. **Zoom-koom** is the non-alcoholic alternative: a sweet, cold drink made from millet flour, ginger, lemon, and sugar, sold in plastic bags on street corners. It is refreshing and filling — almost a meal in a cup. Bissap (hibiscus tea) and ginger juice are also everywhere.

In [Bobo-Dioulasso](/africa/burkinafaso/bobodioulasso) the food scene leans a little more diverse, with Ivorian and Malian influences. [Banfora](/africa/burkinafaso/banfora) is sugarcane country, and the fresh-pressed juice is worth seeking out. Wherever you are, the maquis is the place to eat — find one that is busy, order poulet bicyclette and a cold Brakina or So.B.Bra beer, and you will eat well.
