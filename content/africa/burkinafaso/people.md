title: "People"
type: section
---

Burkina Faso is home to over 60 ethnic groups, and this diversity is something you will notice as you travel. The largest group is the Mossi, who make up roughly half the population and dominate the central plateau around [Ouagadougou](/africa/burkinafaso/ouagadougou). The Mossi have a strong hierarchical tradition centered on the Mogho Naba, a king whose court still holds a ceremonial role. Every Friday morning in Ouagadougou, a public re-enactment of a centuries-old royal ceremony draws crowds and is worth seeing.

In the west, the Bobo, Dioula, and Senoufo peoples give [Bobo-Dioulasso](/africa/burkinafaso/bobodioulasso) and [Banfora](/africa/burkinafaso/banfora) a distinctly different character — the pace is slower, the music different, the architecture rounder and more organic. The Lobi and Dagara in the southwest, around [Gaoua](/africa/burkinafaso/gaoua), are known for their fortified mud compounds and fierce historical independence. The Kassena people of [Tiebele](/africa/burkinafaso/tibl) are famous for their painted houses. In the north, Fulani (Peul) herders move with their cattle through the Sahel, and Tuareg communities live near the Malian border.

The country's name, chosen in 1984 when Thomas Sankara renamed Upper Volta, translates roughly as "land of upright people" — drawing from Moore ("Burkina," meaning honest or upright) and Dioula ("Faso," meaning homeland). It is a name Burkinabe are proud of, and the reputation is earned. Visitors consistently describe the people as among the most welcoming in West Africa. Greetings matter enormously — take your time with them. In Moore, "Ne y beogo" (good morning) and "Ne y windiga" (good afternoon) will earn you smiles.

About 60% of the population is Muslim, 25% Christian (mostly Catholic), and the rest follow traditional religions — though in practice, these categories overlap. You will see mosques and churches side by side, and many families include members of different faiths. Religious tolerance is a genuine feature of daily life here, and traditional mask ceremonies, particularly in the Bobo and Bwa communities, remain vibrant.

Burkina Faso is young and poor — median age is about 17, and it ranks among the least developed countries in the world. But it punches well above its weight culturally. The country has a disproportionately strong film and arts scene, anchored by the FESPACO festival, and a musical tradition that ranges from the balafon orchestras of the west to the contemporary Afropop coming out of Ouagadougou.
