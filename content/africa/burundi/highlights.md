---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**Lake Tanganyika** is the reason most travelers come to Burundi, and it does not disappoint. The world's second-deepest lake stretches along the country's western border, and the beaches around [Bujumbura](/africa/burundi/bujumbura) are genuinely beautiful -- warm, clear water backed by mountains. Saga Beach and Karera Beach are popular spots for swimming and sundowners. The lake is also home to hundreds of endemic fish species, including the prized mukeke, which you will find on every restaurant menu in town.

**The Royal Drummers of Burundi** are the country's most famous cultural export. The tradition of ritual drumming, known as karyenda, was central to the Burundian monarchy and is now recognized by UNESCO as intangible cultural heritage. Performances feature a semi-circle of massive drums played with extraordinary energy and precision. You can sometimes catch performances in [Gitega](/africa/burundi/gitega) or at cultural events in Bujumbura -- if you get the chance, do not miss it.

**Kibira National Park** covers 400 square kilometers of montane rainforest along the Congo-Nile Divide in the northwest. It is Burundi's largest protected area and one of the last significant stretches of mountain forest in the region. The park shelters chimpanzees, colobus monkeys, and around 200 bird species. Access is limited and infrastructure is basic -- you will need a local guide -- but the forest itself is magnificent. Note that the UK advises against travel to the provinces bordering this park due to security concerns; check current conditions.

**The Source of the Nile at Rutovu** is a small spring in the south of the country that Burundians identify as the southernmost source of the Nile River. A modest stone pyramid marks the spot. The claim is disputed -- several countries vie for the honor -- but the site is a place of real local pride, set in lovely hill country. Getting there requires some effort, but the drive through Burundi's green interior is part of the reward.

**The National Museum of Gitega** in [Gitega](/africa/burundi/gitega), the political capital, houses the country's most important collection of historical and ethnographic artifacts. Highlights include royal regalia, traditional crafts, and exhibits on the drumming tradition. The museum provides essential context for understanding Burundian culture and history. Gitega itself is a quieter, more traditional city than Bujumbura and worth a day or two.

**Rusizi National Park** sits at the delta where the Rusizi River meets Lake Tanganyika, just 15 kilometers north of Bujumbura. It is a compact but rewarding reserve, home to hippos, crocodiles, and sitatunga antelopes, with excellent birdwatching. A boat trip through the wetlands is the best way to see it. The park is one of the most accessible wildlife experiences in Burundi and can be visited as a half-day trip from the city.
