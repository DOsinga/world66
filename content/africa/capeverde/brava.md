Brava is the smallest inhabited island in Cape Verde and one of the hardest to reach -- there is no airport, and the only way in is by ferry from [Fogo](/africa/capeverde/fogo), a crossing that can be rough and is sometimes cancelled due to weather. That isolation is part of the appeal. Brava is lush, green, and covered in flowers, earning it the nickname "the island of flowers."

The main settlement is Vila Nova Sintra, a quiet town perched at 500 meters in the island's interior, with a pleasant climate and old colonial houses. Faja d'Agua, a tiny village wedged between cliffs on the coast, has a small black-sand beach and a handful of guesthouses. The hiking between the two is spectacular.

Brava has a deep connection to the Cape Verdean diaspora in New England. Many families here have relatives in Massachusetts and Rhode Island, and American influence shows up in unexpected ways. The island produces some of the best coffee in Cape Verde, grown on small hillside plots.

This is not an island for nightlife or beaches. It is an island for walking, for quiet, and for feeling like you have found somewhere the rest of the world has not noticed yet.
