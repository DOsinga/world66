title: "People"

---

Cape Verdeans are among the most welcoming people you will meet in West Africa. The population of around 600,000 is predominantly Creole -- a mix of West African and Portuguese ancestry that reflects the islands' history as a waypoint in the Atlantic slave trade. The diaspora is enormous: more Cape Verdeans live abroad than on the islands, with large communities in Portugal, the US (especially New England), the Netherlands, and France. This global spread gives the islands a cosmopolitan streak that belies their small size.

The culture is a genuine blend. Catholicism runs deep, but so do African traditions in music, dance, and community life. Family ties are strong, and social life revolves around food, music, and conversation. You will notice that people take time with each other -- greetings are not rushed, meals are shared, and strangers are drawn into conversation easily.

Music is inseparable from Cape Verdean identity. The *morna*, a slow, melancholic song form made famous by Cesaria Evora, expresses *sodade* -- a longing for distant loved ones, for the sea, for something just out of reach. The *coladeira* is faster and more playful. *Funana*, from Santiago, is raw, accordion-driven dance music with African roots. In [Mindelo](/africa/capeverde/mindelo), you can hear all of these on any given weekend night.

Cape Verdean Creole, Kriolu, is the real language of daily life. Portuguese is official and used in schools and government, but Kriolu is what people speak at home, in the market, and in the bars. Each island has its own variant -- a speaker from Santiago and a speaker from Sao Vicente can tell each other apart immediately. Learning even a few phrases (*tudu dretu?* -- everything alright?) will earn you smiles.

The concept of *morabeza* -- a spirit of hospitality and easygoing warmth -- is something Cape Verdeans are genuinely proud of. It is not a marketing slogan. You will feel it in the way people offer directions, share food, and invite you to sit down.
