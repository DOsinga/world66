Berberati is the second largest city in the Central African Republic, set in the southwestern corner of the country where the savanna gives way to dense equatorial rainforest. It is the capital of the Mambere-Kadei prefecture and has historically been the center of the CAR's diamond trade -- the surrounding region produces alluvial diamonds, and the industry, both formal and artisanal, shapes much of the local economy. The city has a rougher, more frontier feel than Bangui, but it also has a certain energy that comes from being a trading hub in a remote part of Central Africa.

Getting to Berberati is an adventure in itself. The road from Bangui is about 600 kilometers and can take anywhere from twelve hours to several days depending on the season and the state of the road. During the rainy season, sections become virtually impassable. If you make it, you will find a city with basic infrastructure -- a few guesthouses, simple restaurants, a market that sells everything from bush meat to Chinese electronics. The Catholic mission is often the most reliable source of information and assistance for visitors.

The real attraction of the Berberati area is the surrounding forest. The Dzanga-Sangha Reserve, one of Central Africa's finest protected areas, is accessible from here (though most visitors enter via Bayanga to the south). The forests around Berberati are home to forest elephants, gorillas, and the Bayaka (Ba'Aka) people, one of the region's indigenous groups known for their extraordinary polyphonic music. Travel in this part of the CAR requires serious planning, flexibility, and acceptance of discomfort, but for those willing to make the effort, it offers encounters with one of the most biodiverse and least-visited landscapes on earth.
