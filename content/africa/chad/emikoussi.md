Emi Koussi is the highest point in the Sahara, a massive shield volcano rising to 3,445 meters in the Tibesti Mountains of northern [Chad](/africa/chad). The caldera at the summit is 15 km wide and contains fumaroles, mineral deposits, and an eerie moonscape of volcanic rock. It is one of the most spectacular and least-visited peaks on the continent.

Getting here is a serious undertaking. The Tibesti region has no paved roads, no fuel stations, and no infrastructure of any kind. Access is by 4x4 expedition from [N'Djamena](/africa/chad/ndjamena), a journey of several days across open desert. The Toubou nomads who inhabit the region are the only permanent residents.

Security is a genuine concern. The Tibesti has seen intermittent armed conflict, and most governments advise against all travel to the province. If you are determined to go, work with an experienced Chadian tour operator who knows the current security situation and has relationships with local communities. Do not attempt this independently.

Those who do reach Emi Koussi describe it as one of the most extraordinary landscapes they have ever seen: volcanic towers, hot springs, and a silence broken only by the wind.
