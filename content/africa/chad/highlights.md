title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**Ennedi Plateau** — The Ennedi Massif in northeastern Chad is a UNESCO-inscribed sandstone wonderland. Towering rock arches, narrow canyons, and mushroom-shaped pillars rise from the desert floor. The Aloba Arch, at 120 meters high and 77 meters wide, is the largest natural arch in Africa and the second largest on the planet. Deep in the Archei Gorge, one of the last colonies of Saharan crocodiles survives in a permanent pool where Toubou nomads bring their camels to drink at nightfall. The rock art scattered across the plateau, depicting cattle, giraffes, and human figures, dates back thousands of years to a time when the Sahara was green. Getting here requires a multi-day 4x4 expedition, typically organized through operators based in [N'Djamena](/africa/chad/ndjamena), and November to February is the best window.

**Zakouma National Park** — Chad's flagship wildlife reserve, 800 km southeast of N'Djamena, covers 3,000 square kilometers of Sudanese savanna. After devastating poaching nearly eliminated its elephants in the 2000s, a partnership with African Parks has brought the herds back. Zakouma is now home to around half the world's remaining Kordofan giraffes, roughly 950 animals, along with lions, leopards, buffalo, and enormous flocks of crowned cranes. The park is best visited between November and March, when animals concentrate around shrinking water sources. Several tented camps operate inside the park during the dry season.

**Ounianga Lakes** — Chad's first UNESCO World Heritage Site (2012), the Lakes of Ounianga are a chain of eighteen interconnected lakes in the heart of the Sahara. The western group at Ounianga Kebir holds water six times saltier than the ocean, stained deep red and orange by algae. The eastern lakes at Ounianga Serir are freshwater, kept from evaporating by floating mats of reeds, and support small fish populations. The colors are surreal, deep blues and greens set against endless sand. [Ounianga](/africa/chad/ounianga) town is also the entry point for travelers arriving overland from Libya.

**Tibesti Mountains** — The Tibesti range in the far northwest contains the highest peaks in the Sahara, including [Emi Koussi](/africa/chad/emikoussi), a massive shield volcano rising to 3,445 meters. The volcanic landscape includes hot springs, calderas, and rock formations of unearthly beauty. The Toubou people, tough desert nomads, are the traditional inhabitants. This is genuinely remote and difficult territory. Security is a serious concern, the region sees periodic armed conflict, and most governments advise against all travel here. Those who do go report it as one of the most extraordinary mountain landscapes on earth.

**Lake Chad** — The lake that gave the country its name was once one of Africa's largest bodies of water. It has lost roughly 90% of its surface area since the 1960s due to climate change and irrigation, but it remains a vital ecosystem for millions of people across four countries. The Chadian shore is home to fishing communities who build traditional reed boats, the same design that inspired Thor Heyerdahl's transatlantic Ra expeditions. You can visit from [N'Djamena](/africa/chad/ndjamena) as a long day trip or camp at Bol on the lakeshore. A 4x4 is essential for the deep sand. Do not swim or wade in the lake, as bilharzia parasites are present.
