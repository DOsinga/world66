Ounianga is barely a town, just a scattering of buildings in the northeastern Sahara, but it sits at the edge of one of [Chad's](/africa/chad) most extraordinary natural wonders. The **Lakes of Ounianga**, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2012, are a chain of eighteen interconnected desert lakes with no business existing where they do.

The western group, **Ounianga Kebir**, holds water six times saltier than the ocean, stained vivid red and orange by algae. The eastern lakes at **Ounianga Serir**, about 40 km away, are freshwater, their surfaces covered by floating mats of reeds that reduce evaporation. These lakes support fish and birdlife in the middle of the world's largest desert. The colors, deep blues, emerald greens, and burnt orange set against pale sand, are surreal.

Ounianga is also the traditional entry point for travelers arriving overland from Libya via Kufra, one of the most remote border crossings on earth. The town has minimal facilities; bring everything you need. Getting here requires a multi-day 4x4 expedition, typically as part of a longer circuit that includes the Ennedi Plateau. November to February is the only practical season.
