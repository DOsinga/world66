---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Comorian cuisine is an aromatic collision of African, Arab, Indian, and French influences, built on coconut, rice, and the spices that have grown on these islands for centuries. The cooking is not flashy or well-known internationally, but it is deeply flavored and genuinely distinctive.

**Langouste a la vanille** -- spiny lobster in a vanilla-infused sauce -- is the closest thing the Comoros has to a national dish, and it perfectly captures the islands' identity: the sea meets the spice plantation on a single plate. The vanilla is local, the lobster is fresh, and when it is done well it is one of the best things you will eat in the Indian Ocean. You will find it in Moroni and at the handful of guesthouses on Moheli that serve meals.

**Mataba** is the everyday comfort food: cassava leaves slow-cooked in coconut milk with fish or meat until everything breaks down into a rich, thick stew. It is served over rice and eaten across all three islands. Every family has their version. Mataba is humble food, but the coconut and long cooking give it a depth that surprises.

**Pilaou** is Comorian pilaf -- rice cooked with meat (usually goat or chicken), cinnamon, cardamom, saffron, and cloves. It is the dish for celebrations and Friday gatherings, aromatic and rich. The spicing reflects the Arab influence that runs deep in Comorian culture.

Fish dominates the diet. Tuna, kingfish, and red snapper are grilled over coconut husks at roadside stalls and served with rice, green banana, and a fiery chili paste. Octopus is common too, usually braised in coconut sauce. Breadfruit is roasted or fried as a starchy side. Fresh tropical fruit -- mangoes, jackfruit, papaya, soursop -- is abundant and excellent.

Vanilla and ylang-ylang show up not just in perfume but in the kitchen. Vanilla flavors sauces, drinks, and desserts. The French influence lingers in the bakeries of Moroni, where you can find decent baguettes and pastries. Coffee is grown locally and served strong and sweet.

There are very few formal restaurants outside Moroni. On the smaller islands, you eat at guesthouses or at someone's home. This is one of the pleasures of the Comoros -- the cooking is personal, and meals are often the best way to connect with the people around you.
