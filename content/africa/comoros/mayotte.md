Mayotte is geographically part of the Comoros archipelago but politically part of France -- it voted to remain French when the other islands chose independence in 1974, and became a full French department in 2011. This creates a surreal contrast: European-standard roads and supermarkets sit alongside traditional villages and one of the most spectacular lagoons on earth.

The island consists of two parts, Grande-Terre and Petite-Terre, connected by a ferry. The capital, Mamoudzou, is on Grande-Terre. The administrative center, Dzaoudzi, sits on Petite-Terre.

Mayotte's lagoon is one of the largest in the world, covering more than 1,000 square kilometers and protected by a barrier reef over 160 kilometers long. The diving and snorkeling are outstanding -- the lagoon shelters sea turtles, dolphins, manta rays, and from July to October, humpback whales. This is one of the few places in the world where you can reliably swim with whale calves in shallow water.

Because Mayotte is French territory, the visa requirements, currency (euro), and infrastructure are completely different from the rest of the Comoros. Flights connect Mayotte with Reunion, Paris, and Moroni on [Ngazidja](/africa/comoros/ngazidja). It is significantly more expensive than the other Comoros islands but also much easier to navigate.
