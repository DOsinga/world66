Tadjoura is the oldest town in Djibouti, sitting on the north shore of the Gulf of Tadjoura about 100 kilometers from the capital by road (or a scenic boat crossing from L'Escale in [Djibouti City](/africa/djibouti/djibouticity)). It was a trading port and seat of an Afar sultanate long before the French arrived, and its whitewashed buildings and old mosques still give it a quiet, historic character that the capital lacks.

The diving around Tadjoura is excellent. Sites like Ras Ali, Ras Ouan, Trevally Valley, and Sables Blancs offer drop-offs to twenty meters, large schools of trevally and barracuda, giant reef rays, and sea turtles. Between November and February, whale sharks feed in the gulf waters nearby. A day of diving followed by a picnic on one of the nearby white-sand beaches is about as good as it gets in Djibouti.

Behind the town, the Goda Mountains rise to over 1,700 meters — the only real highlands in the country, and the only place with significant vegetation. The Day Forest National Park protects a rare patch of juniper woodland and offers hiking with views over the gulf. The contrast with the parched lowlands is striking.

Tadjoura is also a useful base for trips to [Lake Assal](/africa/djibouti/lakeassal), which lies to the southwest. The town has basic accommodation and a few restaurants. It is quieter and cheaper than the capital, though facilities are limited.
