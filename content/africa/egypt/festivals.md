---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

**Ramadan** is the most significant event in the Egyptian calendar. For the entire holy month, Muslims fast from dawn to dusk, and the rhythm of daily life transforms. Streets are quiet during the day but come alive after sunset with iftar feasts, lanterns (fanous), and a festive atmosphere. Special Ramadan tents serve food and tea late into the night. Even if you are not fasting, the communal energy at iftar time is something to experience. **Eid al-Fitr**, the celebration marking the end of Ramadan, is three days of feasting, family visits, and gifts.

**Eid al-Adha** (the Feast of Sacrifice) is the other major Islamic holiday, commemorating Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his son. Families purchase and slaughter a sheep, distributing the meat to family, friends, and the poor. It is a big family occasion and public holiday.

**Sham el-Nessim** is a uniquely Egyptian celebration with roots stretching back to pharaonic times. Held on the Monday after Coptic Easter (usually in April), it marks the arrival of spring. Millions of Egyptians head outdoors to picnic in parks, along the Nile, and in gardens, eating traditional foods: salted fish (fesikh), colored eggs, spring onions, and lettuce. It is secular rather than religious and celebrated by Muslims and Christians alike.

**Coptic Christmas** (January 7) and **Coptic Easter** are important for Egypt's Christian minority. The midnight mass at the Hanging Church in Old Cairo and at the great Coptic cathedrals is a significant event. The week before Coptic Easter, many Copts visit monasteries in the desert -- Wadi Natrun is especially popular.

**The Abu Simbel Sun Festival** happens twice a year, on February 22 and October 22, when the rising sun aligns precisely with the temple entrance and illuminates the statues of Ramesses II and the gods deep inside the inner sanctuary. This astronomical alignment was planned by the temple's builders over 3,000 years ago. Crowds gather before dawn to witness it.

**Moulid el-Nabi** (the Prophet Muhammad's birthday) is celebrated with street processions, sweets, and sugar dolls in Egyptian towns and cities. Local moulids (saint's day festivals) happen throughout the year across Egypt, often with Sufi music, dhikr ceremonies, and carnival-like atmospheres. The Moulid of Sayeda Zeinab and Moulid of al-Hussein in Cairo are the largest.
