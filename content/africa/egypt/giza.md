Giza is the western half of greater Cairo — a teeming city in its own right of over eight million people, sprawling up to the desert edge where the **Great Pyramids** stand on their plateau. The pyramids are what everyone comes for, and they deserve it: these are the last surviving Wonder of the ancient world, the **Pyramid of Khufu** (Cheops) alone containing more than two million limestone blocks and older than almost anything else still standing on earth. The Sphinx crouches just below, weather-eaten and half the height of its pyramid, staring east toward the Nile.

The site is both overwhelming and frustrating. The monuments are genuinely awe-inspiring, but the edge of the city now laps right up to them and the area is a circus of touts, camel handlers, and persistent "guides". Arrive early — ideally at opening, around 8 am — buy your ticket, hire a guide or firmly decline all offers, and walk the complex in the cool of the morning. The new **Grand Egyptian Museum**, opened nearby, collects the best of the pharaonic artefacts previously scattered across Cairo, and makes a natural half-day companion to the pyramids.

Beyond Giza itself, the older pyramid fields at **Saqqara** (the stepped pyramid of Djoser, oldest of them all) and **Dahshur** (the Red and Bent pyramids) make for a quieter and in some ways more rewarding day than the main site. They are 30 and 40 km south of Cairo respectively and best combined into one trip.
