Heliopolis carries two histories at once. The ancient one — Iunu, City of the Sun, seat of the cult of Ra — is mostly gone. Where priests once tended to towering temples, Cairo's northeastern suburbs now sprawl. The one surviving monument is the Obelisk of Senusret I, a 68-tonne spike of pink granite that has stood in what is now the working-class neighbourhood of Matariyya since around 1950 BCE. It is the oldest standing obelisk in Egypt.

The modern Heliopolis — officially Masr al-Gedida, "New Cairo" — is something else entirely. In 1905, a Belgian industrialist named Édouard Empain struck a deal with the Egyptian government: he would build a new city in the desert northeast of Cairo, connected by electric tram to the old centre. The result is one of the most distinctive planned suburbs anywhere in the Arab world: tree-lined boulevards, Moorish arcaded buildings, Art Nouveau villas, and at its heart the Baron Empain Palace — a Hindu-temple-inspired fantasy of a mansion that Empain had built for himself and that still stops people dead in their tracks.

Through much of the twentieth century, Heliopolis was one of Cairo's most desirable addresses — prosperous, cosmopolitan, and relatively quiet. Today it is fully absorbed into the city, connected by metro and hemmed in by traffic, but it retains its distinct character. The wide streets, the belle-époque architecture, the large gardens attached to older villas — all of it sets Heliopolis apart from the crowded districts closer to the Nile.

The neighbourhood is also the location of the Presidential Palace, which means you will encounter checkpoints, but for visitors the draw is simply wandering. The Baron Palace is now open to the public after decades of neglect and a lengthy restoration. The Basilica of Notre Dame de Heliopolis is worth a look. And the tram — the last surviving tram line in Cairo — still runs, though barely.
