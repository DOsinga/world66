Maadi is a leafy, low-density district on the east bank of the Nile, about 12 km upstream from downtown Cairo. It is the least crowded neighbourhood in Greater Cairo, with wide boulevards, large villas, and mature gardens — a legacy of its 1905 town plan by Captain Alexander Adams, who imposed strict rules on house sizes, setbacks, and even shutter colours.

The area has been home to a large expatriate community for decades, and services reflect it: western restaurants, international schools, churches, and a synagogue sit alongside the local markets. The Maadi Sporting Club has served the community since 1921. Several embassies are located here, including the Japanese, Peruvian, Mexican, and Argentinian missions.

Maadi is connected to central Cairo by Metro Line 1, with three stops in the district — the same route as the original 1904 Cairo–Helwan railway that gave rise to the town. The Corniche, Cairo's Nile-side promenade, runs along the riverfront and is the main road into the city centre. There is no bridge at Maadi; the nearest crossing is at Mounib to the north.
