Maadi is an affluent residential district of Cairo, about 12 km south of the city centre on the east bank of the Nile. It was planned in 1905 by retired Canadian officer Captain Alexander Adams, whose vision produced the wide boulevards, large villas, and generous garden setbacks that still distinguish the area from the rest of Cairo. Strict early building regulations specified house sizes, garden proportions, and even shutter colours.

The area took its name from an ancient Predynastic town on the same site — now recognised as a significant archaeological site, though building activity has destroyed much of what remained. Its modern history began in 1904 when the Cairo-Helwan railway opened, triggering land development by the Mosseri family.

During World War II, Maadi served as New Zealand's main overseas base: around 76,000 members of the 2nd New Zealand Expeditionary Force trained and were stationed at a camp at the base of the desert slopes of Wadi Degla between 1940 and 1946.

Today Maadi is the least densely populated neighbourhood in Greater Cairo and home to many expatriates and embassies. Services cater strongly to the international community — there are Western restaurants, several churches, a synagogue, and foreign-language publications. The Maadi Sporting Club has served the community since 1921. Three Cairo Metro Line 1 stations connect the district to central Cairo.
