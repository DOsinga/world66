The Sinai Peninsula is a triangular wedge of desert and mountains wedged between Africa and Asia, connecting the two continents while belonging fully to neither. To the west lies the Gulf of Suez; to the east, the Gulf of Aqaba. The Mediterranean closes it off in the north. It is a place of granite mountains, deep sandy wadis, turquoise water, and some of the world's best coral reefs — all packed into a relatively small area that rewards travellers willing to look beyond the beach resorts.

Most visitors arrive via [Sharm el-Sheikh](/africa/egypt/sharmelsheikh) in the south, where the diving and snorkelling at Ras Mohammed National Park are genuinely world-class. The northern beaches of the Mediterranean coast are quieter, more Egyptian, and largely overlooked by foreign tourists. In between, the interior is a landscape of harsh pink granite — ancient, dry, and dramatic.

The spiritual heart of Sinai is the southern mountain range around [St Katherine](/africa/egypt/sinaidesert/stkatherinesmonastery). At 2,285 metres, Mount Sinai is the peak where Moses is said to have received the Ten Commandments. Thousands of pilgrims and hikers climb it each year, most starting at 2am to catch the sunrise from the summit. At its foot, St Katherine's Monastery has been inhabited continuously since the 6th century and holds a library of ancient manuscripts surpassed only by the Vatican. The surrounding high desert — managed as a national park — is excellent trekking country, with Bedouin guides leading multi-day camel routes between remote mountain villages.

[Dahab](/africa/egypt/dahab), on the Gulf of Aqaba coast, strikes a different tone: relaxed, bohemian, and built around diving and windsurfing rather than package tourism. The Blue Hole — a famous and technically demanding dive site — lies just north of town. Between Dahab and Sharm, the Coloured Canyon near Nuweiba offers one of the Sinai's most photogenic hikes, its narrow sandstone walls stained in bands of red, gold, and purple by iron oxide deposits.

The Bedouin people of the Sinai have lived in this landscape for centuries, and much of the trekking and desert tourism depends on their knowledge and hospitality. The region requires a separate permit from the rest of Egypt for some areas near the Israeli and Gazan borders, though the main tourist destinations — Sharm, Dahab, and the St Katherine area — are freely accessible.
