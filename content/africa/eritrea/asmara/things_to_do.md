---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

Asmara's main draw is its extraordinary collection of early twentieth-century architecture, scattered across a compact and walkable city centre. The Italian colonial buildings range from Rationalist to Art Deco to Futurist, and the whole ensemble earned UNESCO World Heritage status in 2017. Beyond the architecture, the city has good museums, lively markets, and some of the best cafe culture in East Africa.

Liberation Avenue stretches from the Government Administrative Center to September First Stadium and is lined with landmarks: Asmara Theater (1918), the Catholic Cathedral (1922, Lombard style), the Supreme Court, and the Central Bank. In the late afternoon, the avenue fills with residents on their evening passeggiata, stopping at cafes for macchiatos and fruit juice.

The former Ghibi palace, at the western end of Liberation Avenue, was built by the first Italian civil administrator in 1897 and now serves as the National Museum, with sections on art, culture, and the independence struggle, plus a pleasant garden. A few blocks northeast of the Cathedral, the main mosque Khulafa el Rashidin (1937, built with Dekemhare travertine and Carrara marble) and the Coptic Inda Mariam church (1917-1920) stand close together.

The extensive central market, north of the Cathedral, is grouped into sections for grain, vegetables, spices, used furniture, ceramics, baskets, and textiles. Gold and silver jewellers can be found in the streets between the main mosque and the Government Administration Center. At the edge of town, Asmara University and the Mai Jahjah fountain are worth a visit, and on the road to Dekemhare a British cemetery marks a quieter corner of the city's layered history.
