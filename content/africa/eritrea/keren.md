Keren is Eritrea's second city, sitting in a bowl of rocky hills about 90 km northwest of [Asmara](/africa/eritrea/asmara) at an elevation of 1,390 metres. It is quieter and less visited than the capital, but many travelers who make the trip find it more immediately likeable — a market town that feels genuinely lived in.

The city is surrounded by dramatic granite outcrops and is home to a large and ethnically mixed population: Tigre, Bilen, Tigrinya, and Saho peoples all live here, giving the place a different character to Asmara's more uniformly Tigrinya-speaking highland feel. You can hear several languages on a single walk through the market.

The weekly camel market is one of the most vivid scenes in the country. Held on Mondays, it draws herders from a wide area and gives the city an energy it doesn't have the rest of the week. The old covered market in the center of town is good for the rest of it — copper goods, baskets, incense, spices.

Keren was also the scene of one of the hardest-fought battles of the East African campaign in World War II. The Italians held the town for two months before British and Commonwealth forces finally broke through in March 1941. The battle is still remembered here; Commonwealth War Graves cemeteries sit quietly on the edge of town.
