Dire Dawa is Ethiopia's second city, a railway town built in the early 1900s when the French-built Djibouti–Addis Ababa line reached this spot in the Rift Valley lowlands. The Kezira district, the old European quarter, has wide boulevards, art deco buildings, and a relaxed, almost Mediterranean feel that is completely different from the highland cities — helped by the hot, dry climate.

The Megala, the old market area on the other side of the seasonal river, is a sprawling, vibrant Somali-influenced quarter where chat (khat) trading dominates the economy. The contrast between the two halves of the city is striking. The old railway station, once the heart of the city, has grand ambitions visible in its architecture even if the trains no longer run as they once did.

Dire Dawa is a gateway to Harar, the walled Islamic city about 50 km to the south, and to the Somali region beyond. The city has good domestic flight connections to Addis Ababa.
