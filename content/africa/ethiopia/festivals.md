---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Ethiopia follows its own calendar — thirteen months, roughly seven years behind the Gregorian calendar — and its festival cycle is unlike anywhere else. The major celebrations are Orthodox Christian, rooted in traditions over a thousand years old, and they are among the most extraordinary spectacles in Africa.

**Timkat** (January 19-20) — Ethiopian Epiphany and the biggest festival of the year. The Tabot (a replica of the Ark of the Covenant) is carried in procession from each church, wrapped in cloth, accompanied by priests in full ceremonial dress, chanting, drumming, and dancing. The Tabot is blessed at a body of water and the crowds are sprinkled or immersed. [Gondar](/africa/ethiopia/gondar) is the most famous place to experience Timkat, where the ceremony takes place at Fasilides' Bath, and [Lalibela](/africa/ethiopia/lalibela) is equally powerful. The celebration goes on for two days with music, feasting, and genuine joy.

**Meskal** (September 27) — The Finding of the True Cross, celebrated with enormous bonfires called demera in every town square across the country. The largest celebration is in Meskal Square in [Addis Ababa](/africa/ethiopia/addisababa), where hundreds of thousands gather. Priests in colorful robes circle the bonfire, singing and dancing. When the fire collapses, the direction it falls is said to predict the coming year. Meskal marks the end of the rainy season and the start of the most beautiful time of year.

**Genna** (January 7) — Ethiopian Christmas, a quieter, more religious affair than Timkat. Church services begin before dawn and last for hours. The traditional game of genna — a kind of field hockey — is played in some areas. It is a family holiday, and if you are in Ethiopia for it, you may be invited to share a meal.

**Leddet/Fasika** (Ethiopian Easter, variable date) — The most solemn religious period. After 55 days of strict fasting (no animal products at all), Easter is celebrated with all-night church services and a feast at dawn. The fasting period means excellent vegan food is available everywhere, and the joy of the Easter feast is palpable.

**Irreecha** (late September or early October) — The Oromo thanksgiving festival, held at Hora Harsadi lake near Bishoftu (Debre Zeit). Millions of Oromo people gather in white and colorful dress to give thanks at the water's edge. It is a massive, joyful event and a powerful expression of Oromo cultural identity.
