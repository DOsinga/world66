Gondar was founded by Emperor Fasilidas around 1635 and served as Ethiopia's royal capital for over two centuries. It is famous for a remarkable complex of medieval castles — a walled enclosure called the Royal Enclosure (Fasil Ghebbi) — that looks more like something from medieval Europe than from equatorial Africa. UNESCO has recognised the site as a World Heritage area.

The church of Debre Berhan Selassie, a short walk from the enclosure, is considered one of the finest examples of Gondarene ecclesiastical art. Its interior walls and ceiling are covered in paintings, including the celebrated ceiling with its rows of cherub faces — hundreds of winged heads looking down in every direction.

Gondar is probably the most immediately impressive of Ethiopia's historical towns, though it may be less profoundly moving than Lalibela or Axum. The city is large by Ethiopian standards, and it has a reputation as one of the most welcoming stops on the northern circuit. The town centre has some Italian colonial-era architecture from the occupation of 1936–41. It is well placed as a base for visits to the Simien Mountains.
