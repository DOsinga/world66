---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**Lalibela's Rock-Hewn Churches** — Eleven churches carved downward into solid volcanic rock in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, connected by tunnels and trenches. They are not ruins — priests in white robes still conduct services by candlelight, and pilgrims travel for days on foot to reach them. Bet Giyorgis, carved in the shape of a cross and standing in a deep pit, is the most photographed, but the entire complex is staggering. [Lalibela](/africa/ethiopia/lalibela) is the single must-see destination in Ethiopia.

**Simien Mountains** — A dramatic landscape of jagged peaks, deep gorges, and sheer escarpments rising above 4,500 meters. This is one of Africa's best trekking destinations, and the only place in the world to see the gelada baboon, a grass-eating primate that gathers in troops of hundreds on the cliff edges. The Simien Mountains National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Multi-day treks with local guides and scouts are the standard way to explore.

**The Danakil Depression** — The hottest inhabited place on earth, below sea level in the Afar region. Sulfur springs at Dallol create an alien landscape of neon yellows, greens, and oranges. Erta Ale volcano has a permanent lava lake that glows red at night. Afar salt miners still carve blocks by hand and load them onto camel caravans. Tours run from [Mekele](/africa/ethiopia/mekele) and take three to four days. It is physically demanding and genuinely extreme, but unforgettable.

**Omo Valley Communities** — The lower Omo Valley in southern Ethiopia is home to some of the most culturally distinct communities in Africa. The Mursi are known for their lip plates, the Hamer for their bull-jumping ceremonies, the Karo for their elaborate body paint. Visiting requires sensitivity — these are real communities, not exhibits — but done respectfully, it is a profound experience. Access is from Jinka or Turmi, reachable by road from [Addis Ababa](/africa/ethiopia/addisababa) or by flight to Jinka.

**Gondar's Royal Enclosure** — A walled compound of seventeenth-century castles and palaces built by Emperor Fasilides and his successors, often called the Camelot of Africa. The architecture blends Ethiopian, Portuguese, Indian, and Moorish influences. Nearby, the Debre Berhan Selassie church has one of Ethiopia's most famous painted ceilings — rows of winged angel faces staring down from every inch. [Gondar](/africa/ethiopia/gondar) is a pleasant base with good food and a mild highland climate.

**Axum and Its Obelisks** — The ancient capital of the Aksumite Empire, which rivaled Rome and Persia in its day. Towering granite stelae — some over 20 meters tall — mark the royal tombs. The largest fell centuries ago and lies in pieces. The Church of Our Lady Mary of Zion claims to hold the Ark of the Covenant, guarded by a single monk who is the only person allowed to see it. [Axum](/africa/ethiopia/axum) is a small, quiet town, but the weight of history is immense.

**Harar's Walled City** — Ethiopia's Islamic holy city, with 82 mosques packed inside ancient walls and narrow alleys that twist and branch in every direction. The old town has a distinctly Middle Eastern feel. Every evening at dusk, hyena men feed wild spotted hyenas by hand at the city gates — a tradition that has been going on for generations. [Harar](/africa/ethiopia/harar) is also known for its distinctive coffee and the house where Arthur Rimbaud once lived.

**Blue Nile Falls (Tis Abay)** — The Nile begins its journey here, pouring over a 45-meter cliff in a wide curtain of water. The falls are most impressive during and just after the rainy season (July to October). A short walk and a boat ride from [Bahar Dar](/africa/ethiopia/bahardar), they are easy to visit as a half-day trip. The flow has been reduced by a hydroelectric dam upstream, but after good rains, the falls are still dramatic.
