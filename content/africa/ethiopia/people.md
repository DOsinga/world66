Ethiopia is one of the most ethnically diverse countries in Africa, with over eighty distinct groups and as many languages. The two largest are the Oromo, who live mainly in the south and west, and the Amhara, who dominate the central highlands and have historically held political power. The Tigray people of the north have their own language and a strong cultural identity rooted in the ancient Aksumite civilization. The Somali and Afar peoples inhabit the eastern lowlands, while dozens of smaller groups live in the southern Omo Valley and the western borderlands.

Religion shapes daily life. The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church is one of the oldest Christian denominations in the world, with roots going back to the fourth century. Its traditions — the fasting calendar, the elaborate liturgy in Ge'ez, the distinctive church architecture — are visible everywhere in the highlands. Islam is the other major faith, dominant in the east and in cities like [Harar](/africa/ethiopia/harar) and Dire Dawa. The two religions coexist with a generally tolerant, live-and-let-live attitude, and it is common to see a mosque and a church on the same street.

Ethiopians are proud of their history and independence. The country was never colonized (the Italian occupation of 1936-1941 is considered an occupation, not colonization), and this sense of national identity runs deep. People will tell you about it, and they are right to.

Hospitality is central to Ethiopian culture. You will be invited for coffee, offered food, and asked about yourself with genuine curiosity. The coffee ceremony — the roasting, grinding, and brewing of beans over charcoal — is a social ritual that happens multiple times a day in homes and businesses across the country.

Greetings matter. People shake hands, often touching the opposite forearm with their free hand as a sign of respect. Elders are addressed with particular courtesy. If you are invited to eat, the host may feed you the first bites by hand — this is called gursha and is a gesture of warmth and friendship.

Around tourist sites, you will encounter children asking for money, pens, or sweets. This is common across Ethiopia. A polite but firm "no" is the best response. In cities, be aware of common scams — people offering to be your guide, then demanding payment, or pointing you to overpriced shops.
