African food is not a single cuisine but a continent's worth of cooking, shaped by wildly different climates, agricultural traditions, trade histories, and colonial influences. Anyone who tells you they don't like African food has probably only encountered a sliver of it. The common threads are a reliance on starchy staples, bold spicing, and a cooking culture built around communal eating.

**North Africa** has the most internationally recognized cuisine on the continent. [Morocco](/africa/morocco)'s tagines — slow-cooked stews of lamb, chicken, or vegetables with preserved lemon, olives, and a complex palette of spices — are the country's calling card, but the real revelation is harira soup, bastilla (pigeon pie wrapped in pastry and dusted with cinnamon sugar), and the street food of Fez's medina. Couscous is eaten across the Maghreb on Fridays. [Tunisia](/africa/tunisia) is spicier than Morocco, with harissa paste working its way into almost everything. [Egypt](/africa/egypt)'s street food is outstanding: ful medames (slow-cooked fava beans with garlic and lemon), koshari (lentils, rice, pasta, and tomato sauce piled together and topped with fried onions), and ta'ameya (Egyptian-style falafel made with fava beans rather than chickpeas) can all be had for pennies from roadside stalls.

**West Africa** builds its cooking on a handful of powerful ingredients: palm oil, peanuts, chili, and fermented products that give depth to sauces. Rice is central in the coastal countries; millet and sorghum further inland. In [Senegal](/africa/senegal), thieboudienne — rice cooked in a rich tomato and fish broth — is the national dish and one of the great one-pot meals anywhere. Yassa chicken (marinated in lemon and caramelized onion) is another staple worth seeking out. [Ghana](/africa/ghana) has its chop bars serving fufu (pounded cassava and plantain) with palm nut soup or groundnut soup. [Nigeria](/africa/nigeria) has jollof rice — a fiercely contested West African dish, with Senegal, Ghana, and Nigeria each claiming the superior version — plus suya (spiced grilled beef skewers sold from roadside braziers) and egusi soup thickened with ground melon seeds. West African cooking rewards adventurous eating; the fermented and smoked ingredients can be challenging at first and deeply satisfying once you're used to them.

**East Africa** is more restrained in its spicing, with the notable exception of the Swahili coast. The staple of Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda is ugali — a stiff maize porridge eaten with the right hand and used to scoop up stews and greens. Sukuma wiki (braised kale with onion and tomato) is the most common accompaniment. [Ethiopia](/africa/ethiopia) is the great exception to everything: its cuisine is one of the most distinctive in Africa, built around injera (a spongy, slightly sour flatbread made from teff grain) that serves as both plate and utensil, piled with berbere-spiced meat stews (tibs, doro wat), lentil dishes, and vegetables. Ethiopian food is the easiest East African cuisine to find outside the continent, and it tastes better in Addis Ababa. Along the Swahili coast — Zanzibar, Mombasa, Dar es Salaam — Indian Ocean trade routes left a legacy of coconut milk curries, pilau rice spiced with cardamom and cloves, and charcoal-grilled seafood that is among the best in Africa.

**Southern Africa** has a braai culture as central to social life as the barbecue is in Australia or the American South. The braai — open-fire grilling — is the occasion around which South African social life organizes itself, with boerewors (spiced beef-and-pork sausage), lamb chops, and corn on the cob as the constants. Pap (a softer version of ugali, made from white maize meal) is the staple starch. [South Africa](/africa/southafrica) also has a Cape Malay culinary tradition — a legacy of enslaved people brought from Indonesia and Malaysia — that produced dishes like bobotie (spiced minced meat topped with egg custard) and a pastry and spice vocabulary that shows up across Cape Town's cooking. Biltong (air-dried cured meat) is everywhere and excellent. [Mozambique](/africa/mozambique) has peri-peri chicken and grilled prawns from the Indian Ocean, a legacy of Portuguese cooking combined with local ingredients. In [Madagascar](/africa/madagascar), romazava — a broth of zebu beef and mixed greens — is the national dish, simple and restorative.

A note on where to eat: across most of Africa, the best food is not in hotel restaurants or places aimed at tourists. It's in local markets, chop bars, and roadside stalls where you eat what the cook is making that day, at prices that will seem absurd by any Western standard. Lean into it. Stomach problems are possible anywhere — use judgment about where food looks fresh and hot — but the rewards of eating where locals eat far outweigh the risks of playing it safe.
