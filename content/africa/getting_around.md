---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Travel in Africa ranges from effortless to genuinely difficult, depending on where you are. East and Southern Africa have well-worn overland routes and decent infrastructure. Central Africa is another story entirely. The key is to be flexible — delays happen, plans change, and that is part of the experience.

**Flying** is often the most practical way to cover long distances. [Kenya](/africa/kenya), [Ethiopia](/africa/ethiopia), [South Africa](/africa/southafrica), and [Morocco](/africa/morocco) are the main regional hubs. Ethiopian Airlines has the widest network on the continent and connects most African capitals. Within countries, domestic flights are often cheap and save days of overland travel — this is especially true in large countries like [Tanzania](/africa/tanzania), [Nigeria](/africa/nigeria), and the DRC.

**Buses** are the backbone of overland travel across most of Africa. Quality varies enormously. In East Africa, companies like FlixBus and Modern Coast run comfortable coaches between major cities. In West Africa, bush taxis and shared minibuses are more common — they leave when full, not on schedule. In Southern Africa, intercity buses are reliable and affordable, and services like Intercape connect [South Africa](/africa/southafrica), [Namibia](/africa/namibia), [Botswana](/africa/botswana), [Zimbabwe](/africa/zimbabwe), and [Mozambique](/africa/mozambique).

**Trains** are limited but worth seeking out where they exist. South Africa has the Shosholoza Meyl network connecting major cities cheaply, plus the luxury Blue Train and Rovos Rail. The TAZARA line from Dar es Salaam to Kapiri Mposhi in Zambia is a classic African rail journey. [Morocco](/africa/morocco) has the continent's only high-speed rail line, connecting Tangier to Casablanca.

**Self-driving** works well in Southern Africa and parts of East Africa, where roads are paved and car rental is straightforward. A 4x4 is essential for game parks and rural roads, especially in the rainy season. In West and Central Africa, self-driving is harder — roads deteriorate quickly, fuel stations are sparse, and border crossings can be time-consuming. Wherever you drive, an international driving permit is worth having.

**Crossing borders** overland is generally straightforward in East and Southern Africa. The East African Community means easy movement between [Kenya](/africa/kenya), [Tanzania](/africa/tanzania), Uganda, and [Rwanda](/africa/rwanda). In West Africa, ECOWAS facilitates movement for its member states. Central Africa remains the toughest region for overland travel — poor roads, limited public transport, and more bureaucratic border crossings. Crossing the Sahara overland is possible but requires serious preparation and ideally a local guide.
