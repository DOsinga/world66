---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's"
type: section
---

**1. Walk through Cape Coast Castle**

Stand in the dungeons where hundreds of enslaved people were held in darkness before being forced through the "Door of No Return" onto slave ships. The guided tour takes about 90 minutes and is one of the most powerful historical experiences in Africa. Visit Elmina Castle too, just 15 km west -- it is the oldest European building in sub-Saharan Africa and tells a parallel story. These sites matter, and they will change how you think about history.

**2. Walk with elephants at Mole National Park**

Mole is unlike most African wildlife parks because the safaris are done on foot, led by armed rangers. You will walk through savannah grass, past waterholes, and come face to face with elephants at remarkably close range. Baboons, antelope, warthogs, and buffalo are common sights. Go during the dry season (December to March) when the animals gather around the water and the grass is low. The park is remote -- the journey from Tamale takes several hours on rough roads -- but that is part of the adventure.

**3. Cross the canopy walkway at Kakum National Park**

Seven rope bridges strung 30 metres above the forest floor, swaying gently as you cross. Below you is dense tropical rainforest; around you is the sound of birds and insects. The walkway at Kakum is one of only a handful in Africa and takes about 45 minutes to complete. Combine it with a visit to Cape Coast Castle for one of Ghana's best day trips.

**4. Eat your way through Accra**

Ghanaian food is spectacular and Accra is where you get the full range. Start with waakye from a street vendor for breakfast, eat banku and tilapia at a beachside spot for lunch, and sit down for jollof rice and fried chicken at a chop bar for dinner. Try kelewele (spiced fried plantain) from a night market, wash it down with sobolo (hibiscus juice), and argue with Ghanaians about whether their jollof is better than Nigeria's. It is.

**5. Watch the Ashanti king receive his chiefs at Akwasidae**

Every six weeks, the Asantehene (Ashanti king) holds court at the Manhyia Palace in [Kumasi](/africa/ghana/kumasi). Sub-chiefs arrive in palanquins under huge umbrellas, drums pound, horns blare, and gold regalia gleams in the sun. It is one of the most vivid displays of living traditional culture anywhere in Africa, and visitors are welcome. Check the schedule locally -- the dates follow the Akan calendar, not the Western one.
