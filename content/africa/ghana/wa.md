Wa is the capital of [Ghana](/africa/ghana)'s Upper West Region, a quiet town in the dry northern savanna that few travelers ever reach. The pace here is unhurried, the people are welcoming, and the streets have a dusty, sun-baked charm that feels a world away from the bustle of Accra or Kumasi. What draws the eye is the mud-brick architecture — Wa Naa's Palace and several traditional mosques built in the Sudanese style, with their thick earthen walls and tapered buttresses. It is a place where Islam and traditional culture sit comfortably side by side, and the muezzin's call drifts over compounds where elders share stories in the shade.

If you have made it this far north, you are the kind of traveler who will appreciate Wa for what it is: an honest, unpolished window into a part of Ghana that tourism has barely touched. The town itself rewards a day or two of wandering, but the real draw nearby is the Wechiau Community Hippo Sanctuary on the Black Volta River, about an hour west, where you can paddle a dugout canoe past pods of hippos in one of West Africa's best community-run conservation projects. Wa makes a natural base for that trip and for exploring the surrounding savanna villages, where you will find some of the most striking traditional architecture in the country.
