Guinea is one of West Africa's most rewarding and least visited countries. It has staggering natural beauty -- the sandstone plateaus and waterfalls of the Fouta Djallon highlands, the dense rainforests of the south, and a long Atlantic coastline -- but almost no tourist infrastructure. You come here for adventure, not comfort.

The country is often called Guinea-Conakry to distinguish it from its neighbors Guinea-Bissau and Equatorial Guinea. It gained independence from France in 1958 under Ahmed Sekou Toure, whose authoritarian rule lasted until 1984 and left the country economically isolated. The decades since have brought coups, political instability, and the devastating 2014 Ebola outbreak. Guinea remains one of the poorest countries in the world despite sitting on some of the planet's largest bauxite and iron ore reserves.

None of this should put you off entirely. Guineans are famously welcoming, the music scene (particularly the djembe and balafon traditions) is extraordinary, and the landscapes are genuinely spectacular. The Fouta Djallon alone -- a highland region of deep canyons, cascading waterfalls, and Fulani villages -- would be a major trekking destination if it were easier to reach. [Dalaba](/africa/guineeconakry/dalaba) and [Labe](/africa/guineeconakry/labe) are the main bases for exploring it.

[Conakry](/africa/guineeconakry/conakry), the capital, is chaotic, congested, and spread along a narrow peninsula. It is not the highlight of a trip to Guinea, but it is where you will arrive and where you will sort out onward transport. The [Iles de Los](/africa/guineeconakry/ilesdelos) just offshore offer a quick escape to quiet beaches.

Further afield, [Kankan](/africa/guineeconakry/kankan) is the spiritual center of the Malinke people, and [Nzerekore](/africa/guineeconakry/nzerekore) in the forested south gives access to Mount Nimba, a UNESCO-listed nature reserve on the borders with Liberia and Ivory Coast.

Travel here is slow and often rough. Roads outside the main corridors are unpaved and can become impassable in the rainy season. But if you have patience, a sense of humor, and some basic French, Guinea will reward you with experiences that few other travelers share.
