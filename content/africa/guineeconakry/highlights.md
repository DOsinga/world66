---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**The Fouta Djallon highlands** are the main reason most travelers come to Guinea. This vast sandstone plateau in central Guinea is cut by deep canyons and dotted with waterfalls -- Ditinn Falls near [Dalaba](/africa/guineeconakry/dalaba) drops 80 meters, and the falls at Kambadaga and Kinkon are equally impressive. The hiking is superb, passing through Fulani villages, across open grassland, and along river gorges. [Dalaba](/africa/guineeconakry/dalaba) and [Labe](/africa/guineeconakry/labe) are the best bases.

**The Iles de Los** are a small archipelago just off [Conakry](/africa/guineeconakry/conakry), reachable by pirogue in under an hour. Ile de Kassa is lively on weekends with Guinean day-trippers, while Ile de Roume is quieter and more relaxed. The beaches are not pristine Caribbean sand, but they are a welcome break from the capital.

**Mount Nimba** straddles the border with Liberia and Ivory Coast and rises to 1,752 meters. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, home to viviparous toads found nowhere else on earth and one of the last populations of West African chimpanzees. Access is from [Nzerekore](/africa/guineeconakry/nzerekore) in the southeast.

**Guinean music** is a highlight you do not need to travel far to find. Guinea has produced some of West Africa's greatest musicians, and the djembe drum tradition originates here. In [Conakry](/africa/guineeconakry/conakry), live music is everywhere -- from formal concerts to impromptu sessions in neighborhoods. The balafon (wooden xylophone) ensembles of the Malinke heartland around [Kankan](/africa/guineeconakry/kankan) are mesmerizing.

**The Kakimbon Caves** near Conakry are sacred to the Baga people and hold deep spiritual significance. The caves themselves are atmospheric, set among lush vegetation, and the stories attached to them give a window into Guinea's pre-Islamic traditions.

**Kankan's markets and mosque** offer a taste of the Malinke world. [Kankan](/africa/guineeconakry/kankan) was once the capital of the Mali Empire, and while little of that grandeur remains, the Grande Mosquee and the bustling covered market are worth the long journey east.
