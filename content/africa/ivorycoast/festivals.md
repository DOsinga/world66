## Fête du Dipri

The most distinctive festival in the country, held each April in the village of Gomon near Agboville (about 100 km north of Abidjan). The Dipri commemorates an ancestral sacrifice made to save the Abidji people from famine. The ceremony begins at midnight: villagers go into trance states, believed to be possession by beneficent spirits called sékés, and perform acts that would be impossible in a normal state. It continues through the following afternoon. The festival also serves a social function — feuds and grievances are supposed to be resolved in the days before it begins. It's raw, genuinely strange, and not staged for tourists. If you have the chance to go, take it.

## Abissa

Celebrated by the N'Zima people of [Grand Bassam](/africa/ivorycoast/grandbassam) in late October or early November, Abissa is a week-long festival of purification and celebration that marks the new year in the N'Zima calendar. People confess misdeeds publicly, settle debts, and then dance for days. The dancing and drumming are spectacular, and the atmosphere is festive rather than solemn by the end of the week.

## Poro Initiation

Not a public festival exactly, but a profound cultural institution among the Sénoufo people of the north. Young men undergo a multi-year initiation cycle in the Poro sacred grove, learning the history, values, and spiritual knowledge of the Sénoufo. Outsiders will not witness the secret ceremonies, but the masked dances that accompany Poro events are sometimes performed publicly, particularly around [Korhogo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korhogo). Ask locally.

## Music

Côte d'Ivoire has a lively popular music scene year-round. Abidjan is the regional capital of coupé-décalé, the frenetic dance music genre that spread across West Africa in the 2000s. Magic System, from the Abobo neighbourhood of Abidjan, brought Ivorian sound to international audiences. Live music venues and maquis with outdoor stages are common in the city.
