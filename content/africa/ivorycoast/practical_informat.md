## Currency

The currency is the West African CFA Franc (XOF). The franc is pegged to the euro at a fixed rate (655.957 XOF = 1 EUR), which makes budgeting straightforward for European travellers. Notes come in 10,000, 5,000, 2,000, and 1,000 denominations; coins in 500 and lower.

ATMs are readily available in Abidjan, but unreliable outside the capital. Carry sufficient cash if heading to Man, the northwest, or any rural destination. Visa and Mastercard work at major hotels and some restaurants in Abidjan. Elsewhere, cash is king.

## Language

French is the official language and the medium of education, business, and government. Most Ivorians also speak at least one local language. Dioula is widely understood as a trade language across much of the country, especially in the north and west. In Abidjan, you will hear a mix of French, Dioula, and Nouchi — a lively Abidjan street patois. Some basic French will take you a long way.

## Telecommunications

Mobile coverage is good in Abidjan and most larger towns. MTN and Orange are the main networks. SIM cards are cheap and easy to buy; bring your unlocked phone. Internet is generally fast in Abidjan; slower but functional in secondary cities. Rural coverage is patchy.

## Electricity

220V, 50Hz. French-style two-pin round plugs (Type C/E). Power cuts are not uncommon, especially outside Abidjan.

## Safety

Côte d'Ivoire is significantly calmer than during the political crises of the 2000s and 2010s. [Abidjan](/africa/ivorycoast/abidjan) is a functioning major city, not a war zone. That said, petty theft is common in crowded areas and markets. Keep bags close, avoid displaying expensive electronics, and take taxis after dark rather than walking. The northwestern border zones near Burkina Faso and Mali have seen occasional security incidents related to regional instability — check current government advisories before heading there.

## Time Zone

GMT (no daylight saving).
