Isak Dinesen's 1937 memoir of her years running a coffee farm in the Ngong Hills outside Nairobi remains the most famous book ever written about Kenya. It covers the period from 1914 to 1931 and is less about farming than about landscape, light, and the people she lived among — her Kikuyu workers, her lover Denys Finch Hatton, and the Somali and Maasai communities nearby.

The prose is slow and deliberate, full of precise observation. Dinesen writes about shooting lions, treating sick workers, and watching storms roll across the highlands with the same measured calm. It is a colonial book, unmistakably, but also a deeply personal one.

The Karen Blixen Museum in the Nairobi suburb of Karen occupies the farmhouse where she lived. The Ngong Hills she describes are still there, still beautiful, and still worth the drive from the city. If you read the book before visiting, the landscape clicks into place in a way it otherwise would not.
