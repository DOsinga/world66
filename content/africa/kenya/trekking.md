---
title: "Trekking"
type: section
---

Kenya has three significant mountains for trekking, each with a different character.

**Mt Kenya** (5,199m) is Africa's second highest peak and an extraordinary mountain to climb. The standard trekking routes — the Sirimon, Naro Moru, and Chogoria tracks — take 4–6 days and don't require technical climbing skills to reach the lower summits (Lenana Point at 4,985m). The upper peaks (Batian and Nelion) are serious technical rock climbs for experienced alpinists only. The mountain sits on the equator but the upper zones are genuinely cold, with moorlands, giant lobelia, and glacier views. A guide is strongly recommended; the park is managed and you need a permit. The Naro Moru route is the most frequented; Chogoria is the most scenic approach.

**Mt Elgon** (4,321m) straddles the Kenya–Uganda border in the west. It's a massive, ancient volcano with a huge caldera you can walk into. The Kenya side is accessed from Kitale. Less trekked than Mt Kenya, with long approach walks through thick forest. The mountain caves here — particularly Kitum Cave, where elephants come at night to mine salt from the walls — are one of Africa's stranger sights.

**The Aberdare Range** in central Kenya is a highland plateau rather than a peak, running between Nyeri and Naivasha. The Aberdare National Park covers the moorlands and bamboo forests, with several trekking routes possible. More commonly visited for game driving, but the walking is excellent.

For all mountains, go with a licensed guide and register at the park gates. Weather above 3,000m can change rapidly; bring warm layers and waterproof gear even in the dry season.
