Liberia's population of around five million people is made up of roughly 16 indigenous ethnic groups, among them the Kpelle (the largest group), Bassa, Grebo, Kru, Gio, Mano, and Mandingo. A small but historically dominant group are the **Americo-Liberians** — descendants of freed American slaves and free Black people who settled here from 1822 onward. Their cultural and political influence far outweighed their numbers for most of the country's history, a tension that contributed to the conflicts of the 1980s and 1990s.

The two civil wars (1989–1997 and 1999–2003) left deep marks. An estimated 250,000 people died, and over a million were displaced. The country has been rebuilding since, and in 2006 Ellen Johnson Sirleaf became Africa's first elected female head of state. That political chapter is part of the context you'll encounter in conversations — Liberians are candid and often reflective about what their country has been through.

Away from the capital, people are generally open and welcoming. In smaller towns and villages, greetings matter. If you pass someone on a path or enter a gathering, stopping to greet is expected and failing to do so can cause offense. A simple "how you?" goes a long way.

## Language

English is the official language and is widely spoken, but Liberian English has its own rhythm and vocabulary that can take time to tune into. "Old road" sounds like "o ro"; silent letters and compressed vowels are common. It's not difficult once your ear adjusts.

Women in rural areas, in particular, may speak only their local language. Around 30 indigenous languages are spoken across the country, most unwritten. In practical terms this means that in remote villages, communication may require patience, gestures, and any local contacts you can find.

## Bartering

Markets are the place for this. Vendors sell carvings, fabric, and crafts, and prices are negotiable. A reasonable approach is to ask the price, offer roughly half, and work toward a middle ground. Don't push too hard on items where the margin is already thin. For food stalls and small produce, prices are often fixed; bulk purchases are where you have room to negotiate.
