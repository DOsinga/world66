---
title: "Getting There"
type: section
---

Almost all independent travelers arrive by air or overland from Tunisia or Egypt. The security situation since 2011 means that planning well ahead is essential — you cannot simply show up.

**By air.** Mitiga International Airport in Tripoli and Benina International Airport near Benghazi handle most international flights. Turkish Airlines, Tunisair, and a handful of Arab carriers fly regular routes. Flights are frequently delayed or cancelled without warning, so build buffer time into your itinerary. The US prohibits its commercial airlines from operating in Libyan airspace.

**By road from Tunisia.** The main crossing is at Ras Jdir, west of Tripoli on the coastal highway. A second crossing at Dehiba–Wazin leads into the Nafusa Mountains. Both crossings are generally functional but can close without notice during security incidents.

**By road from Egypt.** The Musaid–Sallum crossing is the standard entry point in the east, connecting to Tobruk and Benghazi. Road conditions on the Libyan side are reasonable but deteriorate in stretches.

**Visas.** Libya introduced an eVisa system in March 2024. Most nationalities can apply online for a single-entry tourist visa (valid 90 days, maximum 30-day stay) for around USD 63. Citizens of Algeria, Egypt, Tunisia, and Turkey enter visa-free. Nationals of Israel, Iran, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sudan, Syria, and Yemen are not admitted. Allow six to eight weeks for processing and logistics — all tourist visits must be pre-arranged through a licensed tour operator, who will handle permits and arrange the mandatory police escort.

**Important note.** The UK, US, Australia, Canada, and most EU governments advise against all travel to Libya. If you go, register with your embassy and ensure your tour operator has current security contacts on the ground.
