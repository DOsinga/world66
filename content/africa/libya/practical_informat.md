title: "Practical Information"
type: section
---

**Safety.** Libya has been in a state of conflict and political division since the 2011 revolution that ended Gaddafi's 42-year rule. As of 2025, two rival administrations govern the country: the internationally recognised Government of National Unity (GNU) in Tripoli and the eastern Government of National Stability (GNS) backed by the House of Representatives in Tobruk and military commander Khalifa Haftar. Armed militias operate throughout the country. Sporadic inter-militia clashes occur with little warning, including in Tripoli (most recently in 2023 and 2025). The UK, US, Australia, Canada, and most European governments advise against all travel. If you choose to go, travel only through a reputable licensed operator with current on-the-ground contacts.

**Tour operators.** Independent travel is not practical. Your operator must arrange permits, police escorts, and logistics before you arrive. Allow at least six to eight weeks to organise a trip.

**Currency.** The Libyan Dinar (LYD). Banking infrastructure is limited and ATMs unreliable outside Tripoli and Benghazi. Bring sufficient cash in euros or US dollars; your operator will advise on the current exchange situation. You are required to carry at least 1,000 LYD upon arrival or may be denied entry.

**Language.** Arabic is the official language. English and Italian are understood in parts of Tripoli and by those working in tourism. Learning a few words of Arabic will be warmly appreciated.

**Religion.** Libya is overwhelmingly Sunni Muslim. Dress conservatively, especially away from Tripoli. During Ramadan, eating and drinking in public during daylight hours is inappropriate.

**Health.** Medical facilities are poor outside Tripoli. Bring all medication you need, and carry comprehensive travel insurance with medical evacuation cover. Unexploded ordnance and landmines from multiple rounds of fighting are a hazard in areas that saw combat, including parts of Misrata, Benghazi, and the Western Desert.

**Communications.** Libyana and Madar are the main mobile networks. Data can be slow and patchy outside cities. Power cuts are common throughout the country.

**Photography.** Ask before photographing people, and avoid photographing military installations, checkpoints, government buildings, and infrastructure. Sensitivity around this is high.
