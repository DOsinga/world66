Sabratha was founded by the Phoenicians as a trading post, grew into a significant Roman port city, and today holds one of the most striking ancient theatres in the world. The three-tiered stage wall — columns rising against a backdrop of sea and sky — is the defining image of Libyan archaeology.

The theatre, built in the late 2nd century AD, seats around 5,000 and was restored by Italian archaeologists in the 1920s and 30s. The restoration is more extensive than at Leptis Magna, which some purists find intrusive, but the effect is undeniably dramatic. The surrounding ruins include temples, baths, a forum, and a well-preserved Capitoline temple.

Sabratha is about 65 km west of Tripoli on the coastal road — an easy half-day or day trip from the capital. The site was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1982 along with Leptis Magna and Cyrene.
