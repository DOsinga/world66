---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Libya's sights are overwhelmingly archaeological — the country holds an extraordinary concentration of Roman, Greek, and prehistoric sites that received very little tourist traffic even before 2011. Combined with the Saharan interior, it adds up to a destination unlike anything else on the continent.

The standout sites near Tripoli are [Leptis Magna](/africa/libya/leptismagna) and [Sabratha](/africa/libya/sabratha), both on the coast and both manageable as day trips from the capital. Leptis Magna is the larger and more impressive of the two — a complete Roman city buried in sand for centuries and largely unexcavated. Sabratha's theatre is perhaps the most photographed monument in Libya.

Further inland, [Ghadames](/africa/libya/ghadhames) is a UNESCO-listed desert oasis town with a unique vernacular architecture. The traditional old city is built of mud brick, with rooftop pathways connecting the houses above street level. It's 600 km southwest of Tripoli but the drive through the Nafusa Mountains makes it worthwhile.

The Nafusa Mountains themselves contain Berber villages and underground granaries, including the remarkable Qasr al-Haj — a circular fortified granary of 114 rooms, each belonging to one family, stacked four storeys high around a central courtyard. It's about 150 km south of Tripoli, in use for centuries and still standing.

In the deep south, the Akakus (Acacus) Mountains hold thousands of prehistoric rock paintings and engravings — some 12,000 years old, depicting hippos, giraffes, and cattle in what is now pure desert. A UNESCO World Heritage Site accessible from Ghat. Nearby [Germa](/africa/libya/germa) was the capital of the ancient Garamantian civilization, which built a sophisticated irrigation system under the Sahara.

In the east, the Greek and Roman city of Cyrene (near Shahhat) was one of the great cities of the ancient Mediterranean. The Temple of Zeus was larger than the Parthenon. Access to the east has been severely limited by the security situation since 2011.
