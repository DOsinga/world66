Fianarantsoa is the largest town in the highlands south of Tana and the capital of the province of the same name. Like all Madagascar's highland towns it is very hilly, so prepare for some steep walks.

Your most likely introduction will be the dusty, bustling taxi-brousse station along Rue MDRM, where it is easy to find a ride to your next destination or directions to a nearby hotel. **Hotel Arinofy**, close to the station but up a steep hill, is extremely clean, has a restaurant on the ground floor and magnificent views over the town. The chef may offer to guide you around on his time off — he is knowledgeable and does not demand a fortune.

The town has more to offer than you might expect. There is an **Antaimoro paper factory** near Hotel Arinofy where you can watch a family go through every stage of papermaking, from mushing up the sisal to adding decorative flower petals and drying the sheets. The finished products make fine gifts. A larger factory exists in Ambalavao, but this one is more intimate.

A walk to the site of the **old Rova**, the highest point in town, is well worth the sweat. The Rova itself is gone — the French destroyed it in the 1940s — but magnificent views over Fianarantsoa and the surrounding countryside await. To get there, find your way to the upper town and follow Rue P. Ramasitera.

Fianarantsoa has a wonderful **market** in the upper town, off Avenue Fahaleovantena. Not as crushingly crowded as Tana's markets, but full of bustle and selling almost anything. The **Hotel Sofia**, on the way into town from the north, is worth a look for its interesting architecture and boutique shops even if you are not staying there.

Fianarantsoa is also the starting point for Madagascar's most spectacular rail journey. The **train to Manakara** on the east coast winds through magnificent mountain scenery, stopping at every village along the way. **Ranomafana National Park**, with its dense forest, hot springs, and lemurs, is also easily accessible from here.
