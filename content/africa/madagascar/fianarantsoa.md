Fianarantsoa is the largest town in the southern Malagasy highlands and the cultural capital of the Betsileo people. The name means "place of good learning" and the town has a history of missionary education that still shapes it. The place is extremely hilly, so be prepared for steep walks between the upper and lower quarters.

The old upper town (Haute Ville) is the most atmospheric part. The site of the old Rova (royal palace) at the highest point offers panoramic views over the terrace-farmed landscape, even though the French destroyed the palace itself in the 1940s. The nearby market, off Avenue Fahaleovantena, is large and worth exploring. Near the Arinofy Hotel, a small Antaimoro paper factory lets you watch the whole paper-making process — pulping sisal, adding flower petals and stems as decoration, drying — and the finished products make excellent souvenirs.

The most compelling reason to pass through Fianarantsoa, however, is the train to Manakara. This narrow-gauge line descends from the highlands to the east coast through some of Madagascar's most dramatic mountain scenery, stopping at every village along the way. It is one of the great slow train journeys of Africa.

Ranomafana National Park, about 65 km northwest, is easily accessible and is one of the best places in Madagascar to see lemurs, including several rare species found only there.

Most travellers arrive at the dusty taxi-brousse station on Rue MDRM, which is also the best place to find onward transport.
