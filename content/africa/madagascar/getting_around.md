Getting around Madagascar takes time, patience, and planning. The country is large, only about 15 percent of its roads are paved, and many routes become impassable during the rainy season (November to March). There is no railway network that connects major cities. Budget accordingly — both in money and in days.

## Taxi-Brousse

The **taxi-brousse** (bush taxi) is the backbone of Malagasy public transport. These are typically minivans or converted trucks that depart when full, connect almost every town, and are the way most Malagasy travel. They are cheap, slow, and genuinely interesting — you will share the vehicle with chickens, sacks of rice, and people going about their daily lives.

Long-distance taxi-brousse rides typically cost a few dollars for several hours. Buy tickets at the *gare routière* (bus station) in person — there is no online booking. Departures are usually early morning. Budget travelers doing the Tana–Tulear route via RN7 often spend two to three days in transit, stopping in Antsirabe, Fianarantsoa, or Ranomafana along the way.

## Domestic Flights

Air Madagascar and smaller operators serve about 50 airstrips around the country. Flying is the only practical way to reach distant destinations quickly — Nosy Be from Tana takes under 2 hours by air versus two or more days overland. Fares are high by regional standards (typically €150–300 per sector) and schedules can be unreliable, but for longer trips the time saving is real.

Book domestic flights as far in advance as possible, especially for Nosy Be during high season (July–September).

## Renting a 4x4 with Driver

For travelers who want to go off the main routes, hiring a 4x4 with a driver is the most flexible option. Drivers typically know the roads, can navigate washed-out tracks, and double as informal guides. Expect to pay around €80–120 per day including fuel. Arrange through a reputable tour operator or your accommodation in Antananarivo.

Self-drive rental is technically available but strongly discouraged — road conditions are dangerous, insurance claims are complicated, and navigating without local knowledge is genuinely risky.

## In Cities

In Antananarivo, ordinary taxis are metered but it is common to agree a price before getting in. Tuk-tuks (*tricycles motorisés*) operate in some towns. In [Antsirabe](/africa/madagascar/antsirabe), the local *pousse-pousse* (cycle rickshaw) is the signature way to get around — and a genuine experience in itself.
