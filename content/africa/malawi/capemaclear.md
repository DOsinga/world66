Cape Maclear is Malawi's most famous backpacker beach, strung along a five-kilometre arc of sand on the southwestern shore of Lake Malawi. The lake here is at its most dramatic: crystal-clear, warm, and ringed by granite hills that tumble straight into the water. The National Park designation — Lake Malawi National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site — has kept development low-key, and the village that lines the beach remains a scatter of guesthouses, bar-restaurants, and fishing boats rather than a resort strip.

The beach has a slow, easy rhythm that lends itself to staying longer than planned. You can snorkel straight off the sand among hundreds of species of cichlid fish, rent a kayak or a wooden dhow, or hire a local guide for a short hike up to the rocky viewpoints above the bay. Diving courses are available and the visibility, on a calm day, is remarkable. The fishing village at the northern end of the beach is worth a walk: Chembe is one of the oldest communities on the lake shore and the contrast between tourist Cape Maclear and working village life is only a few minutes apart.

Accommodation runs from basic reed-and-thatch bandas to more comfortable en-suite guesthouses. The popular spots fill up fast in peak season (June to September), so arriving before midday gives you the best choice. Fish braais — lake fish grilled over charcoal — are the thing to eat here, usually arranged through your guesthouse with a few hours' notice. Prices for everything, including boat trips, are negotiable and worth asking about before you commit.
