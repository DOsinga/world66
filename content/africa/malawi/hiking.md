---
title: "Hiking"
type: section
---

Malawi has some of the best hiking in southern Africa, and it's rarely crowded.

**Mount Mulanje** is the star. The massif rises to 3,002 m — the highest point in central Africa — and its granite face appears dramatically above the tea estates of the south. A network of Forest Department huts makes multi-day traverses possible without carrying camping equipment; huts have firewood and a caretaker. The standard approach is from Likhubula Forest Station, where you pay accommodation fees (roughly USD 5 per night) and hire a guide and porter. The trails are not technically difficult but routes are steep and the weather can close in quickly — come with proper footwear and rain gear. Allow 3–5 days for a full traverse.

**Zomba Plateau** is more accessible from Blantyre and requires less planning. The plateau sits at around 1,800 m, is covered in pine and cedar forest, and has waymarked trails, a trout stream, and views over the Shire Valley. Good for a day walk or an overnight.

**Nyika Plateau** in the north offers something different: not forest and rocky peaks but a vast, open highland at 2,200–2,600 m. Long-distance hiking through rolling grassland, with eland, roan antelope, zebra, and leopard sharing the plateau. The Chelinda Camp area is the base for most walks, and guided walking safaris are available. Nyika is also open to horse riding, which gives access to areas too distant for day walks on foot.

Hiking in Malawi is generally safe in terms of security. Wildlife encounters on the trails at Mulanje and Zomba are limited; Nyika requires more caution given the game. Always hire a guide for Mulanje — not because routes are impossible to follow, but because guides know the weather patterns and the huts.
