Malawi is a small, landlocked country in southeastern Africa — thin and elongated, running north to south along the western edge of the Great Rift Valley. The country is dominated by Lake Malawi, one of the world's great freshwater lakes, which occupies almost a fifth of the national territory. The lake gives Malawi its defining character: a long blue horizon, fishing villages on the shore, and some of the best freshwater diving anywhere.

Despite being one of the world's poorest countries by GDP, Malawi has a reputation for friendliness that isn't just tourist brochure language. People here are genuinely welcoming, English is widely spoken, and the country is considered one of the safest in sub-Saharan Africa for independent travel. It earned the nickname "the warm heart of Africa" and, for once, the cliché holds.

The country divides neatly into three regions: the north, with its highlands, the mission town of Livingstonia, and Nyika Plateau; the central region, dominated by the capital Lilongwe and the lake's middle shores; and the south, home to Blantyre, Zomba, Liwonde National Park, and the Mulanje massif. Most travellers stick to the lake and one or two parks, but there is enough to fill three weeks easily.

Infrastructure is basic by regional standards but functional. Roads between main centres are generally paved and passable; rural roads can be rough, especially after rain. Electricity cuts and water shortages are common even in cities. Come prepared for things not working as expected, and you'll be fine.
