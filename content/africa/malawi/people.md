---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Malawi has a population of around 22 million (2024 estimate), making it one of the more densely populated countries in sub-Saharan Africa relative to its size. Population growth remains high. Most people live in rural areas, though Lilongwe and Blantyre are growing rapidly.

The largest ethnic group is the **Chewa**, who predominate in the central and southern regions. Chichewa is their language and the most widely spoken vernacular in the country — you'll hear it everywhere. Other significant groups include the Tumbuka in the north (who speak Chitumbuka), the Yao around the southern lake shore, the Lomwe in the south, and the Tonga, Ngoni, Sena, and Ngonde. With more than a dozen distinct ethnic groups, Malawi is diverse, but inter-ethnic tensions are comparatively low by regional standards.

**English** is the official language of government, education, and business. Most Malawians in urban areas speak it competently. In rural areas you'll need more effort — learning a few phrases in Chichewa is appreciated and opens doors considerably. *Moni* (hello), *zikomo* (thank you), and *muli bwanji?* (how are you?) will get you a long way.

About **three-quarters of the population is Christian**, split between various Protestant denominations and Roman Catholics. Muslims make up around 15–20 percent, concentrated mainly in the south and among the Yao. Traditional beliefs persist alongside both faiths.

Malawi has a well-earned reputation for being genuinely friendly to visitors. The "warm heart of Africa" label is used so often it's become a cliché, but most travellers find it rings true. People are curious rather than aggressive, hospitality is freely offered, and solo travel — including for women — is less fraught here than in many neighbouring countries. Petty crime exists (watch your phone in Lilongwe and Blantyre markets), but serious crime against tourists is uncommon.

Gule Wamkulu — "the great dance" — is the most significant traditional cultural expression of the Chewa people: a masked dance ceremony involving ancestral spirits, performed at initiations, funerals, and celebrations. It was inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2005.
