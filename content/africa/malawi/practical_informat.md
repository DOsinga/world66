**Visas**

Since November 2025, most nationalities require a visa to enter Malawi. You can get one on arrival at main entry points (Lilongwe airport, Mchinji, Songwe, Dedza) or in advance via the e-visa portal at evisa.gov.mw. A single-entry 90-day visa costs around USD 50; a 6-month multiple-entry visa is available for more. Check current requirements before travelling — visa policy has been changing.

**Currency**

The currency is the Malawian kwacha (MK or K). USD, GBP, and EUR can be exchanged at banks and licensed forex bureaux in Lilongwe, Blantyre, and Mzuzu. ATMs accepting Visa and Mastercard are available in the three main cities; don't count on them working in smaller towns. Declare all foreign currency on arrival. US dollars are widely accepted at lodges and some tourist businesses, but day-to-day spending requires kwacha.

The kwacha has devalued significantly in recent years. Check current exchange rates close to your travel date.

**Communications**

Mobile coverage is reasonable in towns and along main roads; rural areas are patchy. The main networks are Airtel and TNM. Local SIM cards are cheap and available at the airport and in town centres — bring an unlocked phone. Mobile data is affordable by regional standards.

Wi-Fi is available at most mid-range and upmarket lodges and guesthouses in tourist areas, though speeds vary.

**Electricity**

Malawi uses British-style 3-pin square plugs (Type G) at 230V/50Hz. Power cuts (loadshedding) are frequent and unpredictable; bring a power bank and adaptors.

**Time**

Malawi is UTC+2 (Central Africa Time) year-round. No daylight saving.

**Money-saving tips**

Malawi is not an expensive destination by international standards, but safari lodges and national park fees can add up. Entrance fees for Liwonde, Nyika, and other African Parks-managed reserves are in the USD 15–30 per person per day range. Budget accommodation around the lake costs USD 10–20 per night in a dorm or basic room; mid-range guesthouses run USD 40–80.
