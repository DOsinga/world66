---
title: "Food and Drink"
type: section
---

Maldivian food is built around fish and coconut, with strong influences from Sri Lanka and south India. The staple is tuna — specifically skipjack — prepared smoked, dried, or fresh. Coconut appears in almost everything, from curries to chutneys to sweet snacks.

**Mas huni** is the quintessential Maldivian breakfast: shredded smoked tuna mixed with grated coconut, onion, and chili, eaten with roshi (a thin flatbread similar to roti). It is simple, filling, and genuinely good. **Garudhiya** is a clear, fragrant tuna broth served with rice, lime, and chili paste — mild, comforting, and ubiquitous. **Gulha** are small fried dough balls stuffed with spiced tuna; **kuli boakibaa** is a denser fish cake. These are the kind of snacks found at any local tea shop (hoataa) or café.

Rice and curry is the default meal. Fish curries tend toward coconut milk and mild spice rather than the heat of Indian cooking, though that varies by household.

**Eating on local islands** is straightforward and cheap. Tea shops serve coffee or tea and snacks from early morning until late at night; a meal with a drink runs USD 3–8. Small restaurants offer rice, curry, and fish dishes for USD 5–15.

**Eating at resorts** is a different world. Resorts often operate on all-inclusive or full-board packages, and food is priced accordingly. An à la carte dinner can easily reach USD 50–100 per person. The food quality is generally high, with international menus alongside Maldivian options, but you are paying resort prices.

**Alcohol** is only available at resorts, liveaboards, and a small number of licensed venues. On all inhabited local islands, alcohol is not served or sold. If you are staying at a guesthouse rather than a resort and want to drink, factor this in when choosing your island.

**Fruit** is often imported and priced accordingly. Locally grown produce is limited by the thin, sandy soil of the islands. Breadfruit and coconut are the main local crops.
