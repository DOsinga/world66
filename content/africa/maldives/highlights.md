---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

The obvious draw is the water. Snorkeling off the house reef of almost any island will put you among parrotfish, triggerfish, turtles, and reef sharks within minutes. The diving is world-class: steep wall dives, drift dives through channels between atolls, and encounters with large pelagic species that few other places can match.

**Whale sharks and manta rays** are seasonal but reliable. South Ari Atoll is one of the best places in the world for year-round whale shark sightings. Manta rays concentrate at cleaning stations in Baa Atoll's Hanifaru Bay between June and November, sometimes in aggregations of hundreds — it is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve specifically because of this.

**Baa Atoll** is quieter and more ecologically focused than the busier central atolls. For diving and snorkeling without the resort price tag, local island guesthouses in North and South Male Atoll give easy access to good reefs.

**Malé** itself is worth half a day. For a capital city of fewer than 200,000 people, it is startlingly dense — one of the most crowded cities on earth by land area. The old Friday Mosque (Hukuru Miskiy), built in 1656 from coral stone, is the most significant historical site in the country. The fish market on the waterfront is lively in the mornings. The contrast with the resort islands, ten minutes away by speedboat, is striking.

**Local island stays** are the biggest change to Maldivian tourism in the last decade. Islands like Maafushi, Thulusdhoo, and Dhigurah have developed guesthouse industries that let travelers experience something of actual Maldivian life — fishing communities, mosques, tea shops, Friday markets — rather than just the engineered paradise of a resort.

**Liveaboard diving trips** remain the best way to reach the outer atolls, which see far fewer visitors and offer the most pristine reefs. A week on a liveaboard in the central atolls costs around USD 1,500; two weeks reaching the southern atolls can run to USD 3,500.
