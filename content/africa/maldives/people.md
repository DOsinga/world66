---
title: "People and Culture"
type: section
---

The Maldivian population is around 500,000, roughly half of whom live in Malé or on the densely developed islands close to the capital. The outer atolls have small, scattered communities that have traditionally lived from fishing, though tourism now shapes the economy of many islands.

Maldivians are South Asian in origin with a mixed heritage from south India, Sri Lanka, Arab traders, and East Africa. The language, Dhivehi, is an Indo-Aryan language with its own script and some Arabic loanwords. English is widely spoken in tourism contexts.

**Islam** defines Maldivian society at a constitutional level — the Maldives is officially an Islamic republic, and citizenship requires being Muslim. The call to prayer happens five times a day, and on Fridays the pace of life slows noticeably. Visitors are welcome but are expected to observe basic courtesies on inhabited islands: modest dress, no public drinking, no visible non-Islamic religious symbols.

Despite the restrictions that apply to visitors on local islands, Maldivians are generally warm and hospitable. The guesthouse industry has made genuine cultural exchange more possible than the old resort model allowed. Staying on a local island — joining fishing trips, eating at family-run tea shops, watching a boduberu drum performance — gives a sense of the actual country, not just its beaches.

**Boduberu** (literally "big drum") is the traditional music form: groups of drummers and singers perform at celebrations, with rhythms that build to a fast, ecstatic pace. It has West African roots, brought by enslaved people centuries ago, and is still performed at community events and occasionally for tourists.

The traditional occupation was fishing, and the wooden dhoni remains a cultural symbol even as fiberglass boats have largely replaced it. Many inhabited islands still have working fishing fleets, and tuna is central to the national diet and identity.

Tourism and the Maldivian way of life have long been kept deliberately separate by the resort island system. That separation is eroding as local island guesthouses grow. How this changes communities over the next decades — for better and worse — is one of the more interesting questions facing the country, alongside the more urgent one of whether those communities will have land to stand on.
