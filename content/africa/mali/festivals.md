*Africa > Mali > Festivals*

Mali has produced some of West Africa's most celebrated festivals. The security situation since 2012 has affected some, but others continue, and they remain among the best reasons to visit.

**Festival sur le Niger, Ségou** — held in February along the banks of the Niger in [Ségou](/africa/mali/segou). This is Mali's most accessible major festival and one of West Africa's best: three to four days of live music, theatre, dance, crafts markets, and pirogue processions on the river. Artists come from across the country and the region. The festival celebrates the Niger as Mali's lifeblood and draws a genuinely Malian (rather than tourist-oriented) crowd. It is the best time to be in Ségou.

**Festival au Désert** — originally held in the dunes near Essakane, north of Timbuktu, in January. A celebration of Tuareg culture and music that grew into an internationally famous event, drawing artists like Tinariwen, Robert Plant, and Damon Albarn. The festival was suspended after the 2012 conflict and has been held in exile (in Niger, in Europe) since then. It has not been held in Mali since the insurgency began. Check current status before planning around it.

**Festival sur le Toit du Monde, Bandiagara** — held on and around the Bandiagara Escarpment in [Dogon Country](/africa/mali/dogoncountry). Celebrates Dogon culture through traditional music, masked dances, and storytelling. Security concerns in the Mopti region have affected this festival in recent years; verify it is taking place before travelling.

**Sigui** — the great Dogon ceremony, held once every 60 years. The last one was in 1967–1973 (it runs over several years across different villages); the next is due around 2027. A remarkable event if you happen to be there.

**Mawlid (Prophet's Birthday)** — widely celebrated across Mali. In Bamako and other towns, processions, prayers, and music mark the occasion. Timing follows the Islamic lunar calendar.

**Tabaski (Eid al-Adha)** — the most important Muslim festival, marked by the slaughter of sheep and communal feasting. The whole country shuts down and families gather. An interesting time to be in Mali if you can manage the transport disruptions.
