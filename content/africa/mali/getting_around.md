*Africa > Mali > Getting Around*

Getting around Mali has always required patience. Roads outside the main southern corridor are mostly unpaved; buses and bush taxis run on African time; and the security situation has made certain routes genuinely off-limits.

## By bush taxi and bus

Bush taxis (taxi-brousse) are the backbone of internal transport. These shared vehicles — usually ancient Peugeot 504 wagons, minibuses, or 4WDs — leave when full from gare routières (bus stations) on the edge of town. There is no timetable. You show up, find the vehicle headed to your destination, pay for a seat, and wait until it fills. Journey times are long, breakdowns are common, and comfort is minimal. This is how most Malians travel.

Larger coaches (SOGEBAF and other companies) run routes between the main southern cities: Bamako–Ségou–Mopti is the most useful. These are more predictable than bush taxis and somewhat more comfortable.

The road from [Bamako](/africa/mali/bamako) to [Mopti](/africa/mali/mopti) via [Ségou](/africa/mali/segou) is paved and is the main travel axis in the country. Beyond Mopti, roads deteriorate and security risks increase.

## By river

The Niger River is one of the most beautiful ways to travel in Mali — and historically was the main route to [Timbuktu](/africa/mali/timbuktu). The Compagnie Malienne de Navigation (COMANAV) operates a large riverboat between [Koulikoro](/africa/mali/bamako) (near Bamako) and Gao, stopping at villages along the way. The full journey takes around two weeks. This service runs only during the high-water season, roughly August to November.

Outside that season, **pinasses** — large motorised wooden boats — take over. Chartering a pinasse from Mopti toward Timbuktu takes three to four days. It is slow, hot, and requires sleeping on deck, but the experience of waking to riverside villages in the morning light is hard to match. Smaller **pirogues** (dugout canoes) are available for short trips and river crossings.

Note that river travel through the northern reaches, beyond Mopti, now carries security risks.

## By air

Domestic flights link [Bamako](/africa/mali/bamako) to Mopti, Timbuktu, and Gao. Service is infrequent and schedules change. Flying is the only realistic way to reach Timbuktu without road or river risks, but flights from Bamako or Mopti are the only options and availability is limited.

## By car

Self-drive is possible but not recommended. Roads are rough, fuel can be scarce, navigation is difficult without local knowledge, and the security situation requires knowing which routes are currently passable. Hiring a vehicle with a driver who knows the terrain and has local contacts is a far better option.
