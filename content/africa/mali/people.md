*Africa > Mali > People*

Mali has a population of around 23 million (2024 estimate), making it one of the more populous countries in the Sahel. The population is young and growing fast; the UN projects it will double by 2050. Most people live in the south, along the Niger River valley.

## Ethnic groups

The **Bambara** (Bamanan) are the largest ethnic group, making up roughly 31% of the population. They occupy the heartland of southern Mali and their language, Bambara, serves as the national lingua franca even for people who speak other languages at home. [Ségou](/africa/mali/segou) was the seat of the historic Bambara kingdom.

The **Fulani** (Peul or Fula) — around 15% — are traditionally semi-nomadic herders, spread across the Sahel from Senegal to Chad. They dominate [Mopti](/africa/mali/mopti) and the inland delta region. In recent years, some Fulani communities have been caught up in the jihadist conflict, with accusations (sometimes leading to inter-community violence) that certain factions have joined Islamist groups.

The **Songhai** live along the Niger bend in the east, centred on [Gao](/africa/mali/gao) and Timbuktu. They were the dominant people of the Songhai Empire, which at its peak in the 15th and 16th centuries was the largest state in African history.

The **Tuareg** (Touareg) — sometimes called the "blue men of the desert" for their indigo robes — are Berber nomads of the Sahara. They live primarily in the north, in the regions of Kidal, Gao, and Taoudenni. Tuareg rebellions against the Malian state have occurred periodically since independence; the 2012 rebellion, which eventually led to the current conflict, was partly Tuareg in origin, though the jihadist groups that followed were a different matter.

The **Dogon** live on and around the Bandiagara Escarpment in central Mali. Their culture — architecture, religion, mask traditions, agricultural practices — is among the best-documented and most distinctive in Africa. See [Dogon Country](/africa/mali/dogoncountry).

The **Soninke** (or Sarakollé), **Malinké**, and **Bozo** (traditional river fishermen) make up other significant groups, along with the **Moors** (Maures) along the Mauritanian border.

## Religion

Around 90% of Malians are Muslim. Islam in Mali has historically been moderate and strongly influenced by Sufi orders, particularly the Tijaniyya and Qadiriyya brotherhoods. The imposition of strict Sharia law by jihadist groups in the north in 2012 — including banning music, destroying shrines, and enforcing extreme dress codes — was deeply alien to this tradition and was widely resisted. About 9% of the population follows indigenous beliefs; a small minority (around 1%) is Christian.

## The griot tradition

Mali's griots (djeli in Bambara) are hereditary praise singers, historians, and musicians. In a largely oral society, they are the keepers of genealogy and history. A griot can recite the ancestry of a family going back twenty generations, negotiate disputes, and provide music for ceremonies. This tradition produced the kora — a 21-string instrument that sounds like a cross between a harp and a lute — and underpins Mali's extraordinary musical culture. Families like the Kouyatés and Diabatés have been griots for centuries.
