Chinguetti is one of the most atmospheric towns in the Sahara, and Mauritanians consider it the seventh holy city of Islam. Whether or not that claim holds strictly to orthodoxy, the town did have a genuine reputation as a centre of Islamic learning, and the evidence is still very much present: a 13th-century mosque with a distinctive ostrich-egg-topped tower, a labyrinth of mud-brick houses slowly being swallowed by sand dunes, and a handful of private libraries preserving thousands of Arabic manuscripts on mathematics, astronomy, law, and theology.

The old city is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most evocative places in West Africa. Wandering its narrow lanes as the dunes encroach from the east is an oddly moving experience — you are watching a medieval city in very slow-motion decline, and the people here know it.

Beyond the town itself, Chinguetti is a base for desert exploration. The Adrar plateau surrounds it, and the sand dunes to the east are genuine Saharan dunes — photogenic, climbable, and navigable by camel. Most visitors combine Chinguetti with [Atar](/africa/mauritania/atar) and [Ouadane](/africa/mauritania/ouadane), and doing all three in a loop makes for an excellent introduction to the Mauritanian interior.
