---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

Morocco has an exceptionally rich literary tradition and has attracted an unusual density of foreign writers. Storytelling is an ancient tradition — visit a weekly souk or Djemaa el-Fna in Marrakech and you'll still find storytellers at work.

**The Sheltering Sky** by Paul Bowles (1949) is the defining novel of Morocco as experienced by outsiders — an American couple slowly unravelling in the Saharan south. Bowles spent decades in Tangier and also translated and transcribed oral stories by Moroccan writers including Mohammed Mrabet and Larbi Layachi.

**The Sand Child** and **The Sacred Night** by Tahar Ben Jelloun are the best-known works by Morocco's most celebrated contemporary author. Ben Jelloun writes in French and lives in exile; his novels cut deeply into questions of identity, gender, and Moroccan society.

**Dreams of Trespass** by Fatima Mernissi is an essential memoir of growing up in a Fez harem in the 1940s, and doubles as a study of the enclosed world of traditional Moroccan women. Mernissi is Morocco's most important feminist scholar and sociologist.

**Morocco That Was** by Walter Harris — a British journalist who covered Morocco at the turn of the 20th century — remains one of the best historical accounts of the old sultanate before the French Protectorate. Vivid and opinionated.

**The Spider's House** by Paul Bowles (1955) is set in Fez during the final years of French rule and captures the city and the independence movement with sharp clarity.

**In Morocco** by Edith Wharton (1920) is a travel account written after her visit in 1917 — dated in its colonial assumptions but wonderful for descriptions of the cities and courts as they were before mass tourism.

**Lords of the Atlas** by Gavin Maxwell is a gripping history of the Glaoui dynasty — the Berber warlords of the High Atlas who allied with France and dominated southern Morocco until independence in 1956.

For contemporary travel, the **Rough Guide to Morocco** and the **Lonely Planet Morocco** are both solid. The **Time Out Marrakech** guide is useful for that city specifically.
