Boumalne Dadès sits at the mouth of one of Morocco's most dramatic landscapes: the Dadès Gorge, a canyon of sculpted red rock that cuts north into the High Atlas. The town itself is small and unpretentious — a single main street, a few cheap hotels, and a market square — but its position makes it one of the more rewarding stops on the road between Ouarzazate and Tinghir.

The gorge is the main draw. The road climbs steeply through orange-and-red rock formations, past kasbahs clinging to cliff sides and terraced gardens fed by snowmelt streams. Even a short drive or hike into the lower gorge is worth the effort; those with more time can push further to Aït Oudinar or all the way to Msemrir. The Vallée des Oiseaux, a narrower side valley a few kilometres from town, is good for bird-watching and a quieter walk.

South of town, the Jbel Saghro massif offers several days of trekking through volcanic rock and nomad country. The mountains are less visited than the Atlas and have a raw, spacious character that rewards those who make the effort. The hotels in town can arrange guides and mule transport.

The oasis gardens surrounding Boumalne are pleasant to wander — date palms, fig trees, and irrigation channels that have supported life in this dry valley for centuries. The pace here is slow, and it is easy to stay longer than planned.
