About 550 km south of [El Ayoune](/africa/morocco/elayoune) on the Atlantic coast, Dakhla (ed-Dakhla) sits on a long peninsula jutting into the ocean. A former Spanish outpost, it has reinvented itself in recent years as a world-class destination for kitesurfing and windsurfing — the shallow lagoon on the peninsula’s inner side offers flat water and steady trade winds almost year-round.

Beyond the water sports, Dakhla is a place of isolated beauty. The town sits on a stunning bay, and empty beaches lie just 20 minutes away. If you have your own transport, you can find stretches of sand without a single footprint. The atmosphere is still distinctly military — this is disputed Western Sahara territory, and you should not be surprised to find yourself in the company of soldiers or asked to explain your presence.

The region has restrictions: some roads are blocked due to mine fields, and travelling south toward Mauritania requires joining an army convoy (departing Tuesdays and Fridays). Always cooperate with the authorities, and if they tell you an area is restricted, leave without argument. A place with so many restrictions is, paradoxically, filled with possibilities.
