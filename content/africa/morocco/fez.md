Fez is Morocco's spiritual and intellectual capital, and the most rewarding city in the country to lose yourself in. The walled medina of **Fes el-Bali** is the largest car-free urban area on earth — nine thousand alleys, dead ends, fondouks and squares packed into a bowl in the Saiss plain. The whole old city is a UNESCO World Heritage site and it has changed remarkably little since it was laid out in the ninth century.

There are really three towns. **Fes el-Bali** is the medieval medina, founded in 789 and built up by waves of Andalusian, Tunisian and Berber immigrants. **Fes el-Jdid** ("New Fez") is the comparatively young royal quarter, walled off by the Merinid sultans in the thirteenth century, with the Mellah (the old Jewish quarter) and the Royal Palace. **Ville Nouvelle** is the French-built grid to the south-west, where most of the modern hotels, restaurants and bars are.

The headline sights are inside the medina. The **Al-Qarawiyyin** mosque and university — founded in 859 by a Tunisian woman, Fatima al-Fihri — is generally cited as the oldest continuously operating university in the world. Non-Muslims can't enter, but you can peek in through the gates and visit the attached library after restoration. The **Bou Inania madrasa** is the masterpiece of Merinid architecture, all carved cedar and zellige tilework, and one of the few religious buildings in Morocco that is open to non-Muslims. The **Chouara tannery**, viewed from the leather-shop terraces above, is the postcard image of Fez: men working dye pits in primary colours that have not changed since the Middle Ages. Take a sprig of mint for the smell.

Stay in a **riad** — a restored old townhouse around a central courtyard — inside the medina. They are everywhere, vary wildly in price, and are the right way to experience the city. Hire a guide for the first day; the medina genuinely is a labyrinth and a guide will take you through workshops you would never find on your own. After that you should get lost on purpose. The general rule: walking downhill takes you towards the centre and the river, walking uphill takes you out.

Fez is less polished than Marrakesh and harder going for first-time visitors — touts can be relentless, the medina is exhausting, and good restaurants are mostly hidden behind blank doors. Stick with it. Three days is a minimum; a week is better.
