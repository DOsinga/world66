Kelaa M'Gouna sits at the heart of Morocco's rose-growing country, a small town in the Dadès Valley about 95 km east of Ouarzazate. The surrounding hillsides and valley floors are planted with thousands of damask rose bushes — *Rosa damascena* — brought here centuries ago, possibly along trade routes from Persia. Every May the roses bloom and the valley turns pink and fragrant.

The town itself is modest, but the landscape around it is the point. The valley is threaded by the Mgoun River and flanked by red-ochre kasbah walls and Berber villages. Beyond the roses, it sits at the meeting point of the Dadès and M'Goun valleys, which makes it a base for trekking into the High Atlas.

The Rose Festival (*Moussem des Roses*) takes place each May, usually over a long weekend, and draws crowds from across Morocco. There is a rose queen election, a parade, music, and stalls selling rose water, rose oil, and rose-petal jam. If you can time a visit to coincide with it, do — it is genuinely festive and not yet overwhelmed by tourism.

Outside festival season, Kelaa M'Gouna is quiet. The cooperative distilleries on the edge of town produce rose water and essential oils; a few will let you watch the distillation process in season. The town is also a good launching point for trekking in the M'Goun massif, one of the highest parts of the Atlas.
