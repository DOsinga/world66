Your jumping off point is Marrakesh. From there you can either hire a car and drive or head off to Ouazarzate by bus where you can arrange a tour or carry on your journey by public transport. Now while the public transport option is feasible, you've got to no way of knowing when you'll be arriving or coming back which is a problem if you are on a tight schedule or have hotels booked. Also, the tour or bus option means you don't get to stop off along the way to see and do what you want at your own pace. My advice - hire a car.

The journey is wonderful. Start off early in the morning, negotiate your way through the Marrakesh traffic and head off on the road to Fez until you get to the Ouarzazat turn off (about 10 minutes out of town). From here, you can't go wrong. The journey goes through some amazing scenery as you climb the windy twisty road and you'll want to stop frequently for photo op.s. I'm getting side tracked so all I'll say is don't stay in Ouarzazat (which you'll get to for a late lunch) as it isn't very interesting but stay further on either in Skoura or Boumane du Dades. Whilst in the area though, either on the way or way back make sure you visit Ait Ben Haddou (25 Kms before Ouarzazat) and the Kasbah in the city itself (one of the best in Morocco) and possibly the Atlas film studios if you're not pressed for time.

Anyway, the following day you can either drive straight to Merzouga or stop and visit the Dades and/or Todra gorges staying the night at Tinerhir.

So either on day two or day three, you'll be arriving in Merzouga. Up until recently, the last bit of the journey from Erfoud was 'piste' (as in a dirt road), they have recently finished tarmac-ing the road so there are no problems. There are plenty of options for staying the night but if you arrive by about 1pm, you'll probably be able to get yourself on a trip to the dunes without needing to sleep in Merzouga.I' recommend booking with Omar,can be reached at:omar@cameltrekking.com.( www.cameltrekking.com) If you do stay, the Timboctou is cheap & excellent but the best place is slightly outside town (along a dirt track - don't worry, even the cheapest Fiat Pallio hire car will make it) at a place called Auberge Derkaoua(Chez michel the Desert Inn).

Made to look like an old Kasbah, it is a true Oasis in the middle of the desert. The pool is one of the best in Morocco and the food is great. By the time you get there, you'll be desperate for an alternative to Tagine and here, you can even eat a burger. This is such a relaxing place to chill out. Make sure you make a reservation, as one of the most unique places in Morocco, it gets busy (but is always quiet as people are off at the dunes or quietly relaxing somewhere).

So depending on how fast you are going, either on day 2, 3 or 4 you'll be ready to organise your trip and at around 3pm, you'll head off in a caravan of camels (in our group, we had Omar,my wife and I and another couple). The journey takes about two hours. Initially you are on rockyish ground and are within site of the village but after 40 minutes or so you crest a dune and then all you see, for the next 16 hours are sand dunes. I couldn't stop smiling at the point where, no matter which direction I looked, all I could see was dunes - it is exactly how you imagine the desert to be. The light in the afternoon as the sun sets and the shadows grow is stunning and I have some great pictures of orange dunes with just the shadows of ourselves on the four camels and the shadow of the guide upahead.

You arrive at a very basic Tuareg camp. There are loads of blankets and little iron-frame fold-away beds for everyone (when we were there, the camp was almost empty, just us four and one other guy). After a spot of sand skiing and dinner around the camp-fire, we climbed this huge dune in the dark, which is one of the hardest things I've done (for some reason we couldn't stop laughing all the way up, like we'd taken laughing gas) as your feet just sink into the sand and you end up sliding back 2 metres for every one that you go forward. From the top, you can see the lights of merzouga twinkling in the distance. A sand storm whipped itself up whilst we were there so we sheltered in the lea of it before heading down.

We spent the night outside watching shooting stars - the sky is so clear here you can almost read by the light of those billions of stars.

The next day is an early start to catch the sun-rise over the dunes and then you are back on the camels and heading to the village.

By the afternoon, we were back in Oaurzazat and in theory, were we in a rush, could have made it
