Morocco sits at a crossroads. It is an hour by ferry from Spain, yet it can feel completely separate from Europe: an ancient Islamic civilization shaped by Berber, Arab, African, and Andalusian influences. The country rewards and challenges in equal measure.

In the imperial cities — [Fez](/africa/morocco/fez), [Marrakech](/africa/morocco/marrakesh), [Meknes](/africa/morocco/meknes), and [Rabat](/africa/morocco/rabat) — the medieval fabric is largely intact. Fez has the largest car-free medina in the world; Marrakech has Djemaa el-Fna, the most extraordinary public square in Africa. Both will disorient and delight you. Both will also, eventually, wear you out. Morocco's cities are intense, and persistent hustlers are a real irritant. The trick is knowing this in advance and not letting it sour the extraordinary things on offer.

Three mountain ranges run through the country: the Rif in the north (home to [Chefchaouen](/africa/morocco/chaouen), the famous blue-painted town), the High Atlas (North Africa's highest peaks, with serious trekking around Jebel Toubkal), and the Anti-Atlas in the far south. Mountain areas are generally more relaxed than the cities, and Berber hospitality in the villages is the real thing.

The pre-Saharan south — the Draa Valley, the Dades and Todra gorges, the oases near [Merzouga](/africa/morocco/merzouga) — is Morocco at its most cinematic. Mud kasbahs, palm groves, and then the Saharan dunes of Erg Chebbi. The desert is best visited in spring or autumn; summer heat is punishing.

On the Atlantic coast, [Essaouira](/africa/morocco/essaouira) offers a complete change of pace from Marrakech: wind, ramparts, grilled sardines, and a surfing and kitesurfing scene. [Agadir](/africa/morocco/agadir) is Morocco's main beach resort, rebuilt after a 1960 earthquake, with a more international and package-tourist character.

Travel in Morocco is genuinely intense. Young men will offer themselves as guides; women will receive more attention than they're used to; the pace of hassle in the medinas can be exhausting. None of this should stop you. Moroccan hospitality is genuine, the food is superb, the landscape is extraordinary, and the country is safe. Come prepared, take your time, and it will reward you.
