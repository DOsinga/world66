Taroudant sits in the Souss valley at the foot of the High Atlas, enclosed by some of the best-preserved medieval ramparts in Morocco. Locals call it "little Marrakesh" — it has the same ochre walls and Berber soul but a fraction of the tourists, and a great deal more calm.

The medina inside the walls is compact and walkable. The souks are genuine working markets rather than staged spectacles: you'll find Berber silver jewelry, leather goods, argan oil products, and the kind of spice stalls that actually supply local kitchens. Thursday and Sunday are the big market days, when traders come in from surrounding villages and the pace picks up. Arrive early.

The walls themselves are the main event. They run for about six kilometres, built by the Saadians in the 16th century when Taroudant briefly served as capital of their empire. They look best in the late afternoon, when the stone turns gold and orange in the low light. The best way to see them is by bicycle — rentals are available near the main square — or by horse-drawn carriage at sunset.

Beyond the walls, olive and orange groves stretch out in every direction, with the High Atlas and Djebel Toubkal as a backdrop on clear days. Taroudant makes a good base for trekking into the Western High Atlas or the Djebel Sirwa, and it sits on the road between Agadir and the mountains, making it an obvious stopping point rather than a detour.
