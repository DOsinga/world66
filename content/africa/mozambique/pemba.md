Pemba sits at the mouth of one of the largest natural bays in the world, a deep curve of the Indian Ocean on Mozambique's northern coast. The city is the capital of Cabo Delgado province, and for travelers willing to make the journey north, it rewards with some of the finest beaches and diving conditions in eastern Africa.

The bay itself is the draw. The waters are calm and brilliantly clear, sheltering coral reefs that see far fewer visitors than the better-known Mozambican islands further south. Snorkeling and scuba diving are excellent, and the town has operators who run trips out to the reefs and to nearby islands. Windsurfers and kayakers use the sheltered inner bay.

The town splits into two distinct parts. The old quarter, known as the Bairro, perches on a headland above the bay and retains some of the atmosphere of a Swahili trading port. The newer commercial centre sprawls inland. Down on the waterfront, the Wimbi beach strip has a handful of restaurants and bars that fill up on weekends with locals from the city.

Getting here takes effort — Pemba is a long way from anywhere — but that is precisely why the beaches are still beautiful and the reefs are still intact.
