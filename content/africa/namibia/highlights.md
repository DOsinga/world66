---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Namibia is one of the most spectacular countries on earth, and one of the least visited. The combination of extraordinary landscapes, excellent wildlife, and almost no crowds makes it hard to overstate how good a trip here can be.

**Sossusvlei and Deadvlei** — The red dunes of the Namib are the defining image of Namibia: enormous, ancient, and visually overwhelming. Getting to the top of Big Daddy dune at sunrise, looking down over the white clay pan of Deadvlei with its skeleton trees, is genuinely one of the great travel experiences in Africa. Stay at the Sesriem camp or lodge inside the park to access the dunes at dawn before the day-trippers arrive.

**Etosha National Park** — Etosha is the best place in southern Africa to see rhino, and the floodlit waterholes at the park's rest camps let you watch elephants, lions, and black rhino drinking in the dark just metres away. It's different from the Masai Mara style of game-watching — no rushing from sighting to sighting — but deeply rewarding. The park is entirely self-drive, which means it's accessible and affordable.

**Fish River Canyon** — The second-largest canyon in the world sits in Namibia's deep south, rarely visited, often overlooked. The 85km hiking trail through it is one of the great multi-day hikes in Africa (May to September only). If hiking isn't your thing, the viewpoints from the rim are dramatic enough on their own.

**Swakopmund** — An odd, charming colonial relic on the Skeleton Coast. Swakopmund functions as Namibia's adventure sports capital — sandboarding, quad biking, skydiving — and as a civilised base with good German-influenced restaurants and cafés. The Atlantic is too cold to swim in, but the light and the atmosphere are remarkable.

**The Skeleton Coast** — The remote, fog-shrouded coastline north of Swakopmund where the Namib meets the ocean. Shipwrecks, vast seal colonies, and a sense of genuine wilderness. The northern section is a restricted national park accessible only by fly-in safari.

**Damaraland and Twyfelfontein** — In the northwest, ancient rock engravings at Twyfelfontein sit alongside desert-adapted elephant, giraffe and rhino in a landscape of vast burnt-ochre plains. The Brandberg massif and Spitzkoppe granite inselbergs are highlights for photographers and hikers.

**Himba country (Kunene region)** — The far northwest is home to the Himba, semi-nomadic pastoralists who have maintained their culture intact. Visiting a Himba village requires sensitivity and a good guide; done properly, it is genuinely memorable rather than extractive.
