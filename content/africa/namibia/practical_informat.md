## Money

The local currency is the **Namibian Dollar (NAD or N$)**, which is pegged 1:1 to the South African rand. Rand is accepted everywhere. As of early 2026, roughly N$18-19 buys one US dollar, and N$19-20 buys one euro.

ATMs are reliable in Windhoek, Swakopmund, and larger towns but become sparse in rural areas. Carry enough cash before heading into the bush — particularly for fuel, campsites, and small lodges. Most lodges and restaurants in tourist areas accept Visa and Mastercard.

Tipping is expected and important: N$20-50 per night for lodge staff, 10% at restaurants, and a tip for your safari guide at the end of a stay is standard.

## Electricity

Namibia uses the **South African Type M plug** — three large round pins. Bring an adapter if you're coming from Europe or North America. Voltage is 220-240V, 50Hz.

## Communications

Mobile coverage (MTC and TN Mobile are the main operators) is decent in towns and along main roads, but expect nothing in the wilderness. Buy a local SIM at the airport or in Windhoek for data. Wi-Fi is available at most lodges and guesthouses.

## Health

Malaria is a risk in the north and northeast (Etosha, Kavango, Zambezi/Caprivi Strip) from November through April. Take prophylaxis if you're heading to these areas. The rest of the country — Windhoek, Sossusvlei, Swakopmund, Fish River Canyon — is generally malaria-free.

Tap water is safe to drink in Windhoek and most towns. In rural areas stick to bottled water. The sun is intense at altitude — use high-factor sunscreen even on cool days.

Yellow fever vaccination is required if you're arriving from a country with yellow fever risk. Always carry proof of vaccination.

Namibia has good private hospitals in Windhoek (Medi-City Hospital and Rhino Park Private Hospital are the main ones) but limited medical facilities in rural areas. Comprehensive travel insurance including medical evacuation is essential.

## Safety

Namibia is one of Africa's safest countries for travellers. The main risks are opportunistic petty theft in cities and, far more seriously, road accidents — gravel roads, free-roaming wildlife, and long distances are a dangerous combination. Drive slowly on gravel and never drive at night outside towns.

## Language

English is the official language and widely spoken in tourism and business. Afrikaans is the most common everyday language and useful to know a few words of. German is still spoken in Swakopmund and Lüderitz.
