Benin City is the capital of Edo State in southern Nigeria, and one of the oldest cities in West Africa. For centuries it was the seat of the Benin Empire, a powerful kingdom whose bronze-casting artisans produced some of the finest metalwork the world has ever seen. When the British sacked the city in 1897, they looted over 2,000 bronze sculptures — most now scattered across European museums.

Today Benin City is a sprawling, energetic place with rough edges and deep cultural pride. The Oba's Palace still stands near King's Square, and the National Museum houses what bronzes remain in Nigeria. The old city walls — among the largest earthworks ever built — are mostly gone, but traces survive in the landscape.

The city is not a polished tourist destination. Roads are chaotic, infrastructure is patchy, and there is little in the way of organized tourism. But for travelers interested in West African history and the living traditions of the Edo people, Benin City rewards curiosity. The annual Igue Festival in December brings the city's cultural heritage vividly to life.
