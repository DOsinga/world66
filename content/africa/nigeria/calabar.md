Calabar is a tropical river port in the far south-east of Nigeria, sitting on a ridge above the Calabar River just upstream of the Bight of Biafra. It is by some margin the most pleasant city in Nigeria — green, well-laid-out, cleaner and safer than most of the country, and with a long history as a trading centre stretching back to the 15th century, when Portuguese traders first arrived here. In the 19th century it was briefly the capital of the British Oil Rivers Protectorate (later the Niger Coast Protectorate) and the headquarters of the infamous Atlantic slave trade along this stretch of the West African coast. Presbyterian missionaries, most famously the Scot **Mary Slessor**, arrived in the 1840s and eventually helped put a stop to it; Slessor is buried in Calabar's hillside cemetery and still revered locally.

The best part of town is **Duke Town** and the neighbouring **Henshaw Town**, where the British-era colonial buildings, the old Presbyterian church, and the tin-roofed Efik houses look down onto the river. The **Calabar Museum**, housed in the old Government House (a prefabricated 19th-century building shipped out from Glasgow in pieces), has exhibits on the slave trade, the palm-oil trade, and the colonial period. On the outskirts, the **Drill Ranch**, run by a conservation charity, rehabilitates rescued drills — one of the most endangered primates in Africa.

The city is famous across Nigeria for its **Calabar Carnival**, held every December, which bills itself as "Africa's biggest street party" and draws hundreds of thousands of visitors in a week-long explosion of parades, costume bands and music. For the adventurous, Calabar is also the gateway to **Cross River National Park** — one of the last strongholds of the Cross River gorilla, the world's rarest great ape — reached via Ikom, three to four hours inland.
