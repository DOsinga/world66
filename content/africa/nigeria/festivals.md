---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Nigeria's festival calendar is one of the richest in Africa, spanning Islamic celebrations, Yoruba masquerades, Igbo new year ceremonies, and sheer carnival excess.

**Durbar (Northern Nigeria)** — Held during Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha, the Durbar is the most spectacular public event in the north. In [Kano](/africa/nigeria/kano), Katsina, and other emirate cities, elaborately costumed horsemen parade through the streets in honour of the Emir, performing mock cavalry charges and acrobatics. The riders wear chain mail and robes of brilliant colour; the horses are decorated with elaborate headdresses. Kano's Durbar is the largest and most theatrical — arrive early to get a good position.

**Calabar Carnival (December)** — [Calabar](/africa/nigeria/calabar) hosts what is billed as Africa's biggest street party every December. The month-long festival features costumed band competitions, live music, dance, and parades through the city centre. Up to two million visitors attend. It has been held every year since 2004 and the scale keeps growing. If you are in Nigeria in December, plan around it.

**Eyo Festival (Lagos)** — The Eyo Masquerade is one of the oldest Yoruba ceremonies and is held on Lagos Island, usually to mark the passing of a prominent chief. Eyo masquerades appear in long white robes and wide-brimmed hats, moving in procession through the streets with staffs called *opambata*. Dates are not fixed in advance — the festival is announced at short notice. If you happen to be in Lagos when one is called, don't miss it.

**Argungu Fishing Festival (Kebbi State)** — Held on the Sokoto River in the town of Argungu, this four-day festival dating to 1934 sees thousands of fishermen enter the water simultaneously to catch the largest fish using only hand nets. The spectacle is extraordinary — men wading chest-deep in a frenzy of competition, while crowds watch from the banks. Cultural events, music, and swimming competitions accompany it. Usually held in late February or early March.

**New Yam Festival (Igbo communities)** — Celebrated across Igboland in the southeast, usually in August, the New Yam Festival (*Iri Ji*) marks the harvest of new yams with masquerade performances, communal feasting, and thanks to the earth. Each community has its own version; the celebrations in Enugu and Anambra states are large and publicly accessible.
