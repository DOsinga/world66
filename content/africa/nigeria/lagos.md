Lagos is one of the fastest-growing cities in the world — some 21 million people now, give or take a few million nobody is sure about — and easily the most exhausting. It sprawls across islands and lagoons at the mouth of the Ogun River, a commercial engine that runs on traffic jams, generator fumes, Nollywood, and Afrobeats. You do not come here for ease. You come because it is the closest thing Africa has to a New York, and because the energy, music, food, and hustle of the place are unlike anywhere else.

Most visitors stay on **Victoria Island** or **Ikoyi**, the gated green islands where the embassies and expat hotels are. **Lagos Island**, just across the bridge, is the old business and trading heart; **Marina** and **Broad Street** still have the old colonial buildings, though most visitors find the density and hustle too intense without a guide. **Lekki**, further east, is the newer upmarket strip.

The city is not really about sights. The **Nigerian National Museum** on Lagos Island has a decent collection of bronzes and masks. **Lekki Conservation Centre** has a canopy walkway through swamp forest. **Bar Beach** and the beaches east at **Tarkwa Bay** (ferry only) and **Lekki** are the local escape on weekends.

What Lagos really does better than anywhere in Africa is nightlife and live music. **The Shrine** in Ikeja, the venue run by Femi and Yeni Kuti in memory of their father Fela, is the single best club experience on the continent. **Freedom Park** on Lagos Island hosts regular concerts in a former colonial prison. Ask, go late, stay later.

A note on Lagos traffic: plan for hours on the road. A driver and decent air conditioning change the experience entirely.
