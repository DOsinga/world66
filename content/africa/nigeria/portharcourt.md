Port Harcourt is the capital of Rivers State in southern Nigeria and the centre of the country's oil industry. The city was founded by the British in 1913 as a deep-water port on the Bonny River and named after Lewis Harcourt, Secretary of State for the Colonies; before that, the area was small fishing villages at the edge of the Niger Delta. It is still sometimes called the "Garden City" for its colonial-era planned avenues and green spaces, though the reality of modern Port Harcourt is more sprawling and chaotic than that nickname suggests.

Most visitors are here for the oil and gas industry rather than tourism. The security situation in the wider Niger Delta has been unstable for decades, with kidnapping risk in the outer creeks and occasional militant activity. Stay in the city centre, use reputable hotel security and drivers, and check Foreign Office advice before planning any trips outside the city.

Within Port Harcourt itself, the **Isaac Boro Park**, the **Port Harcourt Zoo**, and the **Pleasure Park** are the main green spaces. The older streets around the Bonny Waterside give you a sense of the early colonial city. Nigerian, Chinese, and Lebanese restaurants cluster around Old GRA (the original Government Reserved Area), and Trans Amadi Industrial Layout is where most of the oil-company compounds are based.

For those with the time and the right arrangements, the **Niger Delta** itself is extraordinary — mangrove channels, stilt villages, and wildlife (the Andoni Elephant Reserve still holds a small population of forest elephants). Trips into the delta should be organised through reputable tour operators with local security connections.

The climate is equatorial: hot, humid, and wet. The rainy season runs from March to October with heavy daily downpours; November to February is drier and marginally cooler.
