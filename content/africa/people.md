title: "People"
type: section
---

Africa is home to roughly 1.5 billion people and over 3,000 distinct ethnic groups speaking more than 2,000 languages. No other continent comes close to this human diversity. Understanding even a little of it before you travel transforms what you see on the ground.

**North Africa** is predominantly Arab-Berber. The Berbers — or Amazigh — are the continent's original North African inhabitants, and though Arabization and Islam reshaped the region over centuries, Berber culture, language, and identity remain very much alive in [Morocco](/africa/morocco) and [Algeria](/africa/algeria). In Egypt, the Coptic Christian community is one of the oldest continuous Christian traditions in the world, making up perhaps 10 percent of the population.

**West Africa** is home to some of the continent's largest and most historically powerful ethnic groups. The Hausa and Fulani dominate northern [Nigeria](/africa/nigeria) and the Sahel. The Yoruba of southwestern Nigeria built one of West Africa's great pre-colonial civilizations; their culture, religion, and diaspora spread across the Atlantic during the slave trade and took deep root in Brazil, Cuba, and the Caribbean. The Akan peoples of [Ghana](/africa/ghana) and [Ivory Coast](/africa/ivorycoast) gave the world kente cloth and a matrilineal social structure that still shapes daily life. The Wolof of [Senegal](/africa/senegal) are known for their elaborate system of social castes and a tradition of griots — hereditary oral historians, musicians, and storytellers who carry the community's memory.

**East Africa** is the homeland of the Maasai, whose red-cloaked warriors and cattle-herding culture have become one of Africa's most recognized images — though the Maasai themselves are a small group within a much larger mosaic. [Ethiopia](/africa/ethiopia) alone has over eighty distinct ethnic groups, including the Oromo (the largest), the Amhara (historically dominant), and smaller highland and lowland peoples with completely different languages and traditions. The Swahili coast — stretching from [Somalia](/africa/somalia) through [Kenya](/africa/kenya) and [Tanzania](/africa/tanzania) — has a distinct culture shaped by a thousand years of Indian Ocean trade, blending African, Arab, Persian, and Indian influences into the Swahili language and way of life.

**Southern Africa** is where the San (Bushmen) survived longest as hunter-gatherers, and where their ancient rock art still covers canyon walls across [Botswana](/africa/botswana), [Namibia](/africa/namibia), [Zimbabwe](/africa/zimbabwe), and [South Africa](/africa/southafrica). The Zulu and Xhosa are the largest groups in South Africa and the most prominent in contemporary politics. [South Africa](/africa/southafrica)'s eleven official languages give a sense of the complexity — though English tends to function as the common tongue in cities. [Madagascar](/africa/madagascar) is a world apart: its people descended from Austronesian seafarers who arrived from Southeast Asia around 1,500 years ago, plus Bantu and Arab settlers, producing a culture and language unlike anything on the African mainland.

Religion runs deep across the continent. Islam predominates in North Africa and the Sahel, and is growing rapidly in West and East Africa. Christianity — in dozens of forms, from ancient Orthodox churches in [Ethiopia](/africa/ethiopia) and [Egypt](/africa/egypt) to evangelical Pentecostalism in [Nigeria](/africa/nigeria) and [Kenya](/africa/kenya) — is the majority religion across most of sub-Saharan Africa. Traditional religious beliefs persist everywhere, often layered beneath or alongside the major faiths rather than replaced by them.

Hospitality is perhaps the most consistent thread across African cultures, despite everything else that varies. Showing up with a little curiosity about where you are, learning a few words of the local language, and being willing to sit and eat with people will take you further than any guidebook.
