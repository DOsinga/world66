---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Réunion's Creole cuisine is one of the best reasons to visit the island. It draws from French, African, Malagasy, Indian, and Chinese cooking traditions — the result is something distinct and very good.

The central dish is the **cari** (curry): meat, fish, or vegetables cooked with onion, garlic, ginger, turmeric, and masalé (a local spice blend), served with rice, red lentils (grains), and a side of rougail — a fresh relish of tomato, onion, and chilli. Cari poulet (chicken), cari thon (tuna), and cari requin (shark) are the most common. A full cari plate at a local restaurant typically costs €10–14.

**Rougail saucisse** — smoked sausages in a spiced tomato sauce — is the other dish you will find everywhere. Simple, robust, and very satisfying. It often appears as the daily special (plat du jour) at lunch.

The **Chinese community** has a significant presence in the island's food culture: Chinese restaurants and snack stalls are common in Saint-Denis and Saint-Pierre, and Chinese-influenced dishes have worked their way into Creole cooking.

For a quick and cheap meal, look for **bouchons** — small street-side or market stalls selling Creole takeaway, typically rice, cari, and grains for €5–8. The Saturday morning market in Saint-Paul is a good place to eat like a local.

Réunion is also the only Indian Ocean island to produce **wine and spirits** of note. The Cilaos wine (made from the Isabelle grape) is unusual — rough and sharp to wine-educated palates, but worth trying. Local rum arrangé (rum infused with tropical fruits and vanilla) is everywhere and very good.
