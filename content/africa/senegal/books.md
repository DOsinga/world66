---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

Senegal has a rich literary tradition in French, and several essential works are short enough to read before or during your trip.

**So Long a Letter** by Mariama Bâ (1979) is the place to start. Written as a letter from a Dakar woman to her friend, it addresses polygamy, grief, and women's autonomy in Senegalese society with a directness that still cuts. It remains the best-selling novel in West African literary history.

**Ambiguous Adventure** by Cheikh Hamidou Kane (1961) follows a young Fulani man torn between his Islamic upbringing and a French colonial education. Spare, melancholy, and philosophically serious — one of the great African novels of the twentieth century.

**God's Bits of Wood** by Ousmane Sembène (1960) is a novelization of the 1947–48 Dakar–Niger railway strike. Sembène, who was also Senegal's greatest filmmaker, writes about collective resistance with panoramic scope. A vivid portrait of the country before independence.

**Xala** by Ousmane Sembène (1973) is shorter and funnier — a satirical take on the Senegalese bourgeoisie after independence. He adapted it into a film the following year.

**Longing to Belong: An Outsider at Home in Senegal** by Pamela Spencer Hambrick is a readable memoir of expatriate life in Dakar, good for a sense of daily rhythms and social texture.

For birdwatchers, **Birds of Senegal and the Gambia** by Nik Borrow and Ron Demey is the standard field guide. Indispensable if you are heading to Djoudj or the Sine-Saloum.
