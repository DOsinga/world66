## Ile de Gorée

Less than three kilometres off the Dakar waterfront, [Ile de Gorée](/africa/senegal/iledegoree) was for nearly three centuries one of the main staging posts for the Atlantic slave trade. The Maison des Esclaves — a coral-pink building with the famous "Door of No Return" — is the most visited site, though historians debate the exact scale of its role. What is undeniable is the island's gravity. Walk past it in the afternoon light, with the sea visible through the doorway and the city of Dakar behind you, and you will feel it. The rest of the island is beautiful in a melancholy way: bougainvillea over crumbling walls, car-free lanes, and cats everywhere.

## Djoudj National Bird Sanctuary

Located 60 km north of [Saint Louis](/africa/senegal/saintlouis), Djoudj is one of the world's great birdwatching sites — a UNESCO World Heritage Site that hosts up to three million birds during the northern winter. Pelicans, flamingos, African spoonbills, herons, waders, and dozens of migrant species from Europe gather here in numbers that can be overwhelming. The best way to see the park is by pirogue, which gets you close to nesting colonies without disturbing them. November to March is peak season.

## Saint Louis

[Saint Louis](/africa/senegal/saintlouis) was the capital of French West Africa for two centuries, and it still looks the part. Its island centre — a narrow strip between the Senegal River and an ocean-facing spit — is packed with tall colonial houses, wooden balconies, and horse-drawn carriages. The colonial ambition is still visible in the faded grandeur of the buildings. The annual Saint-Louis Jazz Festival in May or June brings musicians from across the continent and beyond; the concerts spill into the streets and last until morning.

## The Sine-Saloum Delta

South of Dakar, the Sine and Saloum rivers spread into a labyrinth of mangrove channels, islands, lagoons, and baobab forests. This UNESCO Biosphere Reserve is one of the finest places for wildlife in West Africa: dolphins, manatees, saltwater hippos, and over 400 bird species. Villages of stilt-raised houses and huge ancient shell middens (some 2,000 years old) are scattered through the delta. Explore by pirogue; base yourself in Ndangane or the island lodge of Mar Lodj.

## Casamance

Senegal's southern region, separated from the rest of the country by the Gambia, is a different world. Lush, green, and forested where the north is sandy and dry, Casamance has mangroves, rice paddies, and the kind of beaches — at [Cap Skirring](/africa/senegal/capskirring) especially — that genuinely justify the description. The Diola people here have a culture distinct from the Wolof-dominated north, including palm wine and a more relaxed relationship with alcohol. After years of separatist tension, the region is now largely peaceful and welcoming to visitors.

## Mbalax and the Music Scene

Senegalese music is one of the genuinely great things about being here. Mbalax — a driving, percussive genre built around the sabar drum and made globally famous by Youssou N'Dour — is everywhere. [Dakar](/africa/senegal/dakar)'s live music scene is outstanding: bars, clubs, and open-air venues with bands that play until 3am and audiences that know every word. The Saint-Louis Jazz Festival and the Dak'Art Biennale of contemporary art (even years) are international-calibre events worth planning around.
