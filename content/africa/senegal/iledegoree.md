Gorée lies just 3 km off the Dakar waterfront, a 20-minute ferry ride from the Dakar port terminal. It is one of the first places European traders settled on the West African coast — the Portuguese arrived in the fifteenth century, followed by the Dutch, English, and French — and it played a significant role in the Atlantic slave trade from the sixteenth to the nineteenth centuries.

The most visited site is the **Maison des Esclaves**, an eighteenth-century house with holding cells and the "Door of No Return" opening directly onto the sea. Historians debate the exact numbers who passed through Gorée specifically, but the building carries the weight of the broader history. It is sobering and worth the visit.

The rest of the island is beautiful. No cars, bougainvillea cascading over stone walls, children playing in car-free lanes, and a string of small restaurants by the waterfront. The **Musée Historique** traces Senegal's past from prehistoric times through the slave era. Several artists have studios here and there is a small community of residents who have chosen island life over the capital's noise.

The ferry from Dakar runs regularly throughout the day from the port terminal near Place de l'Indépendance. Avoid arriving in the midday heat if you can; early morning and late afternoon are more pleasant and less crowded.
