Touba is Senegal's second city and its holiest. Located about 200 km east of [Dakar](/africa/senegal/dakar), it is the spiritual capital of the Mouride Brotherhood, the most powerful Islamic Sufi order in West Africa. The city was founded in 1887 by Cheikh Amadou Bamba, who was later exiled by the French colonial authorities — an act that only deepened his followers' devotion. Today, Touba is a city shaped entirely by faith, and it operates by its own rules: alcohol is banned, smoking is banned, and the authority of the Khalife General carries more weight than the Senegalese state.

The scale of the place surprises most visitors. Touba is prosperous in a way that is hard to explain from the outside. A remarkable number of Mercedes cars cruise the streets, testimony to the global reach of the Mouride network, whose members run trade operations across West Africa, Europe, and the United States. Much of that wealth flows back to Touba.

The city's centerpiece is the Grand Mosque, the largest in Senegal. Its main minaret stands over 86 metres tall, and from the top you get a sweeping view of a city that seems to exist in its own universe. The mosque is open to non-Muslim visitors outside of prayer times, though you should dress conservatively and ask permission before taking photographs.

The annual Magal pilgrimage, held roughly 48 days after the Islamic New Year, draws several million people to Touba to commemorate Cheikh Amadou Bamba's return from exile. It is one of the largest religious gatherings in Africa. If you plan to visit during Magal, expect enormous crowds, impassable roads, and no available rooms — but also extraordinary scenes of collective devotion. If you just want to see the mosque and the city, time your visit for any other time of year.
