---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Seychellois food is called Creole, but it's a creature of its own — shaped by French, African, Indian, and Chinese influences, and built around the freshest possible fish and seafood. Octopus curry is the dish most Seychellois would call their own: slow-cooked with coconut milk, turmeric, and ginger, it's rich without being heavy. Grilled tuna and parrotfish are staples. Kari koko — fish or chicken curry in coconut milk — is another local favourite. Most meals come with rice, breadfruit, or lentils.

Outside the hotels, you'll find authentic Creole food at small family-run restaurants called *table d'hôte* — usually a set meal with several dishes at a fair price. These are where the cooking is best.

Fruit is excellent throughout the year: mangoes, papayas, bananas, jackfruit, and the coconuts that grow everywhere. Fresh juices are commonly available at local stalls.

**Marie Antoinette** on Mahé is the most established Creole restaurant on the islands — a grand old wooden house on the hillside above Victoria. The fixed-price Creole spread covers parrotfish, tuna, barracuda, fish curry, chicken curry, aubergine fritters, rice, and salad. It's a meal rather than just a dinner, and worth the trip.

At the beach-facing end of the scale, Anse Lazio on Praslin has a good restaurant right on the sand — grilled fish, cold beer, and a view that makes everything taste better. On La Digue, most guesthouses prepare Creole meals that are among the best eating experiences on the island.
