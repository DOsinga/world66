title: "People"
type: section
---

Seychelles has no indigenous population. The islands were uninhabited when the French established the first permanent settlement in 1770, bringing with them enslaved Africans and labourers from South Asia. Today's Seychellois are primarily the descendants of those African, French, Indian, and Chinese ancestors — a blend that produced a distinct Creole culture found nowhere else in the world.

Around 93% of the population identifies as Seychellois Creole, with small communities of European, Indian, and Chinese descent. About 90% of the roughly 100,000 people live on [Mahé](/africa/seychelles/mahe). Praslin and La Digue have settled communities; the outer islands are sparsely populated or uninhabited.

Seychellois Creole — a French-based language with African, English, and Asian influences — is the language of daily life for nearly everyone. English and French are both official languages and widely used in government, schools, and business. Most Seychellois move comfortably between all three.

The predominant religion is Roman Catholic, observed by around 80% of the population. The pace of life is distinctly relaxed, particularly on the smaller islands. Music is central to Seychellois culture — Sega and Moutya, both rooted in African traditions, remain popular alongside contemporary African and international styles.
