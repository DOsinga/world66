Sherbro Island sits in the mouth of the Sherbro River in southern Sierra Leone, separated from the mainland by a broad estuary. It is one of the larger islands off the West African coast and has been inhabited for centuries by the Sherbro people, who gave it their name. The island has a history intertwined with the slave trade — it was a major transit point for enslaved people in the 18th and early 19th centuries, and the town of Bonthe on the island was once a significant colonial settlement.

Bonthe is the main town, with some faded colonial-era buildings still standing. The surrounding mangrove channels and offshore waters are good for fishing and birdwatching. The island sees very few tourists, which means facilities are basic and independent travel requires patience. The journey from Freetown typically involves a combination of road and water transport. For travellers willing to put in the effort, Sherbro offers a genuinely off-the-beaten-track experience in a country that has relatively little tourism infrastructure overall.
