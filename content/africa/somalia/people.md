Somalia's population is estimated at around 18 million, though reliable census data is difficult to compile given the decades of displacement and the large nomadic population. The country is ethnically one of the most homogeneous in Africa — around 85% are ethnic Somali, a Cushitic people who have inhabited the Horn for millennia. A Bantu-speaking minority lives primarily in the agricultural south, descendants of communities that predate the Somali expansion.

## The Clan System

Somali society is organized around a clan structure that functions as the primary unit of identity, loyalty, and dispute resolution. The four major clan families are the Darod, Hawiye, Dir, and Rahanweyn (Digil-Mirifle), each subdivided into dozens of sub-clans and lineage groups. A person's clan affiliation shapes everything from whom they marry to who provides security to which political faction they support.

The clan system is neither inherently violent nor backward — it is a sophisticated social contract that managed conflict and cooperation across a vast, difficult landscape for centuries. It also became a vehicle for the post-1991 civil war, as competing clan militias carved up the country. Understanding the basics of clan politics is essential for anyone working seriously in Somalia.

## Nomadic Traditions

Despite urbanization, the nomadic and pastoralist heritage remains central to Somali identity. Camels, goats, and cattle are not just livestock — they are measures of wealth and social standing. Somalia has more camels than any other country in the world. The traditional portable dwelling, the aqal (a dome-shaped structure of bent sticks and woven mats), is still used by nomadic families across the interior.

Somali oral culture is exceptionally rich. Poetry — gabay, geeraar, and jiifto — has for centuries been the primary vehicle for political commentary, love, philosophy, and social critique. A well-crafted line from a respected poet can shift public opinion the way a speech might elsewhere.

## Religion

Islam arrived in Somalia in the seventh century and is now the bedrock of society. Virtually all Somalis are Sunni Muslim. Religion and daily life are inseparable — prayer times structure the day, Islamic law shapes social conduct, and the mosque is the community center. Sufi brotherhoods (particularly the Qadiriyya and Ahmadiyya) have deep roots and coexist, sometimes uneasily, with more austere Salafi currents that grew in influence from the 1990s onward.

## Language

Somali is the official language, written in a Latin script adopted in 1972. Arabic is used in religious contexts and widely understood. Italian is a legacy of the colonial period and is still spoken by some older Somalis in the south. English is increasingly used in government, business, and education.
