Berbera is a coastal town on the Gulf of Aden in the northwestern corner of Somaliland — hot, port-dominated, and quietly important to the economy of the Horn of Africa.

The port was built up by the Somali government in the 1970s and 1980s, when it served as a deep-water hub capable of handling massive oil tankers and — briefly — a port of call for the USS Enterprise. The civil war stripped it bare: fuel storage tanks, oil pipelines, water purification systems, communications equipment — all went. What was left in the early 1990s was derelict.

Recovery came slowly but purposefully. Berbera received a significant boost when Ethiopia lost sea access following Eritrean independence, and began routing trade through Somaliland. By the time Ethiopian interest turned serious, international humanitarian agencies and the EC had already invested heavily — repairing the road to the capital Hargeisa (cutting the journey to under three hours on tarmac), rehabilitating port equipment, training drivers and operators, and modernising communications. From the mid-1990s, Berbera evolved from a ruin into a functioning regional port once more.
