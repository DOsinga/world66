Borama is Somaliland's second city, sitting in the highlands near the Ethiopian border at an altitude that keeps the heat manageable. It has a reputation as one of the calmer, safer cities in the region — less frenetic than Hargeisa, more of a university town than a trading hub. Amoud University, one of the first higher education institutions to reopen in post-war Somaliland, gives the city a younger, more studious feel than you might expect this far off the beaten track.

The city is compact and walkable, built on a plateau with the Golis Mountains rising to the east. The surrounding landscape is rugged highland scrub — dramatic at dusk when the mountains catch the light. Locals will tell you to stay inside after dark, not because of violence but because hyenas roam the outskirts and occasionally wander close to residential areas. It is an unusual safety warning, and a genuine one.

Borama sees very few foreign visitors, which means you will attract curiosity and, typically, generous hospitality. The market is worth a morning: livestock, imported goods, and the informal commerce that keeps a border town moving. For anyone traveling overland between Somaliland and Ethiopia, Borama is a logical stop — close enough to the Tog Wajaale crossing to make it a base, and considerably more interesting than the crossing itself.
