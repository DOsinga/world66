---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

South Africa has produced extraordinary writing — history, fiction, and memoir — that gives real texture to a place with a complicated past.

**Long Walk to Freedom** by Nelson Mandela is the obvious starting point. Mandela's autobiography is a patient, generous account of his life from rural Transkei to Robben Island to the presidency. It is also an account of South Africa's 20th century from the inside.

**Disgrace** by J.M. Coetzee (1999) is the most important South African novel of the post-apartheid era — bleak, precise, and deeply uncomfortable. It won the Booker Prize and is set partly in Cape Town, partly in the Eastern Cape.

**An Act of Terror** by André Brink is a long, politically charged thriller set in late-apartheid South Africa. Brink was one of the Afrikaner writers who broke with the establishment, and the novel is also a vivid portrait of the country and its tensions.

**The Power of One** by Bryce Courtenay — set in the 1930s and 1940s, following an English-speaking South African boy navigating a world of Afrikaner nationalism and black suffering. It has been criticised for sentimentality, but as a portrait of the era it is compelling.

**A Human Being Died That Night** by Pumla Gobodo-Madikizela is a psychologist's account of her interviews with Eugene de Kock, the apartheid-era police death-squad commander. Difficult and important.

**The Washing of the Spears** by Donald R. Morris is the definitive account of the rise and fall of the Zulu nation, particularly the Anglo-Zulu War of 1879. Necessary reading before visiting KwaZulu-Natal.

**The Covenant** by James Michener — a long, novelistic history of South Africa from its earliest inhabitants to the apartheid era. Excellent for getting a sweep of the country's history on a long flight.

**Triomf** by Marlene van Niekerk is a darkly comic novel about a poor white Afrikaner family in the Johannesburg suburb built on the ruins of Sophiatown. Confronting and brilliant.
