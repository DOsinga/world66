Cape Town's most photogenic neighbourhood tumbles down the slopes of Signal Hill in a riot of colour — lime green, cobalt blue, bubblegum pink. The candy-coloured houses date back to the 18th century, when the area was home to enslaved people brought from Southeast Asia, and later to freed Muslims who established the Cape Malay community that still defines Bo-Kaap's character.

Wander the cobblestone streets of Wale and Chiappini for the best facades, then visit the Bo-Kaap Museum on Wale Street for context on the neighbourhood's history. The Auwal Mosque, founded in 1794, is the oldest in South Africa. Up the hill, the Tana Baru cemetery holds the graves of political exiles and religious leaders who were imprisoned on Robben Island in the early colonial period.

The spice shops and local eateries serve Cape Malay staples — bobotie, samoosas, koeksisters — that you won't find done better anywhere else in the city.
