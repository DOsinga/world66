The Northern Drakensberg is the highest and most dramatic section of the Drakensberg range, rising to over 3,000m along the escarpment that forms the border between KwaZulu-Natal and the mountain kingdom of Lesotho. The Zulu name is Ukhahlamba — "Barrier of Spears." The basalt peaks divide the watershed: rain falling west flows towards the Atlantic, while on the eastern side it tumbles down frequently vertical slopes into KwaZulu-Natal, towards the Indian Ocean.

The Tugela Falls, the second highest waterfall in the world, plunges 950m off the Mont-aux-Sources plateau. The Amphitheatre — the semicircular cliff face above Royal Natal National Park — is the most photographed feature of the range, an extraordinary wall of rock that dominates the horizon. In winter the escarpment is regularly covered in snow.

The Northern Drakensberg contains the Natal Drakensberg Park, a serious wilderness area. Hikers regularly encounter bushbuck, eland, mountain reedbuck, and baboons; lammergeier (bearded vultures) soar overhead. Spring brings carpets of wildflowers, autumn fills the lower slopes with cosmos. There are 24 snake species here — not all of them will move aside for you.

The mountains also hold thousands of San rock painting sites, the legacy of people who lived here for millennia. The oldest paintings date back about 800 years; the finest were produced between 200 and 400 years ago.

The Northern Drakensberg consists of two main areas:
- Mont-aux-Sources, Royal Natal National Park, the Amphitheatre, Rugged Glen Nature Reserve
- Singati Valley, Ifidi, Mnweni and Ntonjelana valleys, the Mnweni cutback, Mponjwane, the Saddle
