South African food is a direct expression of its history — Dutch, Malay, Indian, British, and African influences that have been cooking together for three centuries.

## The Braai

The braai — Afrikaans for barbecue, though the word barely does it justice — is a national institution. It is done over wood, not gas, and it is more of a social ritual than a cooking method. Boerewors (a coiled, spiced beef and pork sausage flavoured with coriander, cloves, and nutmeg) is the centrepiece, alongside lamb chops and chicken. At a proper braai, opinions are offered loudly and freely. Consider attending one if invited.

## Biltong

Cured, air-dried meat — usually beef, but also game (kudu, springbok, ostrich). Unlike jerky it is not heat-dried, which gives it a softer, more complex texture. You will find it everywhere: filling bowls on bar counters, sold in plastic packets at petrol stations, and sliced to order at specialist shops. Get it with a bit of fat, which carries more flavour. Also try droëwors — spiced dried sausage that travels well.

## Cape Malay Cooking

Cape Town's oldest culinary tradition, brought by enslaved people from the Indonesian archipelago in the 17th and 18th centuries. The defining dish is **bobotie**: minced meat (usually beef or lamb) spiced with curry, turmeric, and dried fruit, topped with an egg custard and baked. It is mildly spiced and unexpectedly good. **Bredie** (a slow-cooked meat and vegetable stew) and **sosaties** (meat skewers marinated in a sweet curry sauce) are other classics.

## Durban Curry

KwaZulu-Natal has its own culinary identity, shaped by the Indian community that has been here since the 1860s. The curries in Durban are famous across South Africa — richer and hotter than the Cape Malay versions. **Bunny chow** — a hollowed-out half-loaf of white bread filled with curry — is the city's signature street food, born in the Indian community and now beloved by everyone. Order a "quarter bunny" (quarter loaf) to start.

## Pap

The staple starch for most of the country: a stiff maize porridge similar to polenta. It is served alongside everything from braai meat to stews. **Umngqusho** (Xhosa: samp and beans, slow-cooked) is a variation that is deeply satisfying. Nelson Mandela's favourite dish, by his own account.

## Chakalaka

A spiced vegetable relish — beans, onions, peppers, carrots, chilli — that turns up alongside braai meat and pap everywhere. It is one of those simple things that is almost impossible to stop eating.

## Wine

South Africa makes genuinely world-class wine, especially from the Winelands region around [Stellenbosch](/africa/southafrica/capetown), [Franschhoek](/africa/southafrica/franschhoek), and [Paarl](/africa/southafrica/paarl). Chenin Blanc (locally called Steen) is the variety the Cape does best — bone-dry versions of real elegance. Pinotage is the local invention: a cross of Pinot Noir and Cinsaut that divides opinion sharply. Try it at a serious estate before writing it off.

## Rooibos

Grown only in the Cederberg mountains of the Western Cape, rooibos ("red bush") tea is caffeine-free, mildly sweet, and excellent. Drink it with milk or without. It is now sold globally, but it is best here, fresh.
