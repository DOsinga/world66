---
title: "People"
type: section
---

South Africa has a population of around 62 million (2022 census), making it one of Africa's more populous nations. What makes it remarkable is the diversity packed into those numbers.

Black Africans make up about 76% of the population. The Zulu are the largest single ethnic group, concentrated in KwaZulu-Natal. The Xhosa are the second largest, predominant in the Eastern Cape; both Nelson Mandela and Archbishop Desmond Tutu were Xhosa. The Sotho, Tswana, Pedi, Tsonga, Venda, and Swazi peoples each have their own distinct languages and traditions.

White South Africans are around 9% of the population, largely of Dutch, German, French Huguenot, and British descent. The Afrikaners — descendants of the earliest European settlers — speak Afrikaans, a language that evolved here from 17th-century Dutch. Their cultural identity runs deep: the braai (barbecue), the Afrikaans language, and the complex legacy of apartheid are all bound up in it.

The Coloured community, about 9% of the population, has roots in the indigenous Khoisan people, freed and escaped slaves from East Africa and the Indonesian archipelago, and early European settlers. The Cape Malay culture — still visible in Cape Town's Bo-Kaap neighbourhood — traces directly to Muslim slaves brought here in the 17th century.

South Africans of Indian descent (about 2.5%) are concentrated mainly in KwaZulu-Natal, where their ancestors arrived as indentured labourers for the sugar industry from the 1860s onward. Durban has the largest Indian population of any city outside of India.

South Africa has twelve official languages. In practice, people switch fluidly between languages — a conversation in Johannesburg might move through English, Zulu, and Afrikaans in the space of a few minutes. The "Rainbow Nation" ideal, articulated by Desmond Tutu at the end of apartheid, captures the aspiration if not always the reality. Inequality remains stark — South Africa is one of the most unequal societies on earth — and the legacy of apartheid shapes daily life more than thirty years after its end.

What strikes most visitors is how frank and direct South Africans are about all of this. People will discuss race, history, and politics openly and without embarrassment. It's one of the things that makes the country genuinely fascinating.
