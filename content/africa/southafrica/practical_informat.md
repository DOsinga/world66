## Visas

Citizens of most Western countries — including the US, UK, EU member states, Australia, Canada, and New Zealand — do not need a visa for stays of up to 90 days. Your passport must be valid for at least 30 days after your planned departure, and you need two consecutive blank visa pages on arrival. Check requirements carefully if you hold a different passport, and check whether your situation has changed close to your travel date.

## Currency

The South African Rand (ZAR, symbol R) is the currency. ATMs are widely available in cities and larger towns; Visa and Mastercard are accepted almost everywhere. In smaller towns and rural areas, carry cash. At the time of writing, R1 is worth roughly US$0.055 or €0.050 — useful as a rough multiplier.

Tipping is customary and expected: 10–15% at restaurants, a few rand for petrol station attendants (who fill your tank for you), and similar small amounts for hotel porters and guides.

## Safety

South Africa has high crime rates, and you should take it seriously without letting it paralyse you. Street crime — theft, mugging, carjacking — is the most common risk. Keep valuables out of sight in a car. Don't use your phone on the street in city centres. Avoid walking alone at night, even in seemingly safe areas. Never leave bags visible in a parked car.

Cape Town and Johannesburg city centres are fine to walk in daylight, but their surroundings include areas you should stay out of unless with a local guide. Consult local advice on specific neighbourhoods before exploring.

South Africa still has some areas where malaria is present, particularly in low-altitude parts of Limpopo, Mpumalanga, and northern KwaZulu-Natal — which includes the Kruger area. Consult a travel health clinic about prophylaxis before you go.

## Electricity

South Africa uses 220–240V, 50Hz. The standard plug is the South African Type M (large, three-round-pin), though the smaller Type C is also common. Bring an adaptor.

## Load Shedding

South Africa has experienced scheduled power cuts ("load shedding") for years. These are typically announced in advance in rotating blocks (Stage 1–8). Download an app like **EskomSePush** to track schedules. Hotels usually have generators; many restaurants and shops do too.

## Health

Tap water is safe to drink in most South African cities and towns. In rural areas, check locally.

## Communications

Mobile coverage is good in cities and along main highways. Buying a local SIM card (Vodacom, MTN, Cell C, or Telkom) is easy and cheap — you'll need your passport.

## Language

South Africa has twelve official languages. English is widely spoken in cities, tourism infrastructure, and business. Afrikaans is common in the Western Cape and parts of the Northern Cape. isiZulu and isiXhosa are the most widely spoken mother tongues nationally.

## Time Zone

South Africa Standard Time (SAST) is UTC+2, year-round. South Africa does not observe daylight saving time.
