An unusually grand Victorian villa, built in 1886 for George Heys, who made a fortune running the stagecoach lines that served the Transvaal before the railway arrived. The house is a mix of English Victorian and Cape Dutch styles, lavishly decorated with stained glass, Carrara marble fireplaces and original William Morris wallpapers. During the **Second Anglo-Boer War** (1899–1902) it was requisitioned as the residence of the British commanders-in-chief Lord Roberts and then Lord Kitchener, and on **31 May 1902 the Treaty of Vereeniging** — which ended the war and led, eight years later, to the creation of the Union of South Africa — was signed in the dining room. The house is preserved as a museum almost exactly as it was when the treaty was signed, and has rooms full of original furniture and period pieces.
