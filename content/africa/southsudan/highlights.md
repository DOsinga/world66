---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

South Sudan's highlights are mostly theoretical — things that exist and matter but are accessible to almost no one right now. Name them anyway, because conditions do change.

**The Great Nile Migration.** Boma and Bandingilo national parks host what an aerial survey in 2024 confirmed is the world's largest land mammal migration — around six million antelope, including white-eared kob, Mongalla gazelle, tiang, and reedbuck. This is not a misprint. It surpasses the Serengeti. African Parks took over management of both parks in 2022. The migration is real; getting there safely is the problem.

**The Sudd.** One of the world's largest wetlands, fed by the White Nile, stretching for hundreds of kilometers through the interior. From the air it looks like the Amazon — an endless green maze of channels, floating vegetation, and waterbirds. There is no easy access from the ground.

**Nilotic cultures.** The Dinka, Nuer, Shilluk, and other Nilotic peoples have some of the most distinctive pastoral cultures in Africa. Cattle are central to everything — wealth, marriage, ritual, identity. Tall, lean, elaborately scarified: these are ancient ways of living that have survived centuries of outside interference, though not without enormous cost.

**Juba itself.** The capital is not pretty, but it is alive. The riverside at dusk, the Konyo Konyo market, the restaurants serving goat stew and sorghum porridge — there is a city here trying to exist despite everything. For travelers who end up here, it is more than a transit stop if you let it be.
