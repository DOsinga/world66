---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Sudanese food is simple, satisfying, and often very good. The cuisine is built on a few staple ingredients — sorghum, lentils, beans, dried meat, and vegetables — with strong influences from the Nile Valley, the Arabian Peninsula, and sub-Saharan Africa. The variety is more regional than it might first appear.

**Ful medames** is the national breakfast: slow-cooked fava beans mashed with oil, lemon, and often tomato and onion. In Sudan it is eaten with flat kisra bread or with a round wheat bread called gurrasa. You will find it everywhere, from roadside stalls to hotel dining rooms.

**Kisra** is the Sudanese staple bread, a thin, slightly sour flatbread made from fermented sorghum. It is used to scoop up stews and sauces the way injera is used in Ethiopia. It is an acquired taste for some — slightly tangy and very thin — but it grows on you.

**Aseeda** is a stiff porridge of sorghum or wheat, served with a sauce (marara, a bitter offal-based sauce; or a vegetable or meat stew). This is proper peasant food in the best sense — filling, cheap, nutritious.

**Shorba** (soup) is common — clear broths with vegetables or meat, sometimes with a little chili. Sudanese food is not generally spicy, but pili pili (chili sauce) is usually available on the side.

**Shaiyah** is grilled meat — chunks of beef or lamb cooked over charcoal at roadside restaurants. In Khartoum's market areas, the air in the evenings was thick with the smell of it.

**Tea and coffee** are taken seriously. Sudanese tea (shai) is sweet and sometimes spiced with ginger or cinnamon. Jebena coffee — brewed in a clay pot, served in small cups with ginger or cardamom — is a proper ritual in many homes and tea stalls.

Alcohol has been officially banned since 1983 under Sharia law. Home-brewed merissa (a sorghum beer) remains common in non-Muslim communities, particularly in the south, but is not served publicly.
