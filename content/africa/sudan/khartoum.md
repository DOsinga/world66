Khartoum sits at the confluence of the Blue Nile and White Nile, the spot where two great rivers merge into one. It is one of those rare capitals whose geography you can actually see working — stand on the bridge at the right time of day and you can watch the darker Blue Nile water push against the lighter White Nile in a visible seam.

Founded in 1821 as an Egyptian army camp, the city grew into a trade hub and, infamously, a slave market. The name means "elephant's trunk," likely a reference to the narrow strip of land between the two rivers. The British siege and fall of Khartoum in 1885, when General Gordon was killed on the palace steps, made the city front-page news worldwide and shaped Sudan's colonial history.

The most rewarding part for visitors is the colonial downtown, where crumbling but handsome buildings line dusty streets. The National Museum holds remarkable antiquities from the ancient kingdom of Kush, and the confluence itself is worth the trip. Beyond the centre, Khartoum is a sprawling, low-rise city that blends into its twin cities of Omdurman across the White Nile and Khartoum North across the Blue Nile. Omdurman is the older, more characterful of the three — its souk is one of the largest in Africa.

Sudan is not an easy country to travel in, and Khartoum reflects that. Infrastructure is limited, the heat can be brutal, and alcohol is banned. But the welcome is genuine, the tea is sweet, and the city rewards those who take the time to look past the dust.
