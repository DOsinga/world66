Sudan is a country of extraordinary history and extraordinary suffering. Along the banks of the Nile, the ancient Nubian kingdoms built more pyramids than Egypt and left behind temples and royal cities that still stand in the desert. The people are, or were, among the most famously hospitable in Africa. The landscapes — from the Sahara in the north to the Red Sea hills in the east — are genuinely beautiful.

None of this can currently be visited. Since April 2023, Sudan has been engulfed in a civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), a paramilitary group that grew out of the Janjaweed militias responsible for the Darfur genocide. The fighting has killed more than 150,000 people and displaced over eleven million — the largest displacement crisis on earth. Famine conditions have spread across Darfur and Kordofan. Khartoum, once a city of several million, was occupied, looted, and devastated. The RSF controls all five Darfur regional capitals and most of the west. The SAF retook Khartoum in early 2025, and the military government returned in January 2026, but the city remains badly damaged with basic services barely functioning.

Every major government — the United States, United Kingdom, European Union, Australia, Canada — advises against all travel to Sudan. This is not routine caution. Sudan is an active war zone with no functioning tourist infrastructure, widespread unexploded ordnance, and ongoing airstrikes and ground combat across much of the country.

This page documents what Sudan was and what it may become. Come back when the war is over.

**Area:** 1,861,484 sq km
**Capital:** Khartoum (recaptured by SAF, March 2025; government returned January 2026)
**Population:** approximately 48 million (pre-war estimate)
**Language:** Arabic (official); over 70 additional languages spoken
**Religion:** Sunni Islam predominant, particularly in the north; Christianity and traditional beliefs in some communities
**Currency:** Sudanese pound (SDG)
