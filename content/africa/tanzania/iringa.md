Iringa is a provincial capital in the Tanzanian Southern Highlands, perched on an escarpment at about 1,600 metres above sea level with views over a wide valley. It is a cool, pleasant town rarely visited by travellers, which is part of its appeal. The surrounding region is excellent for day trips.

The most significant excursion from Iringa is the **Isimila Stone Age Site**, about 20 km south, where hand axes and tools dating back 60,000–100,000 years were found in an eroded gully — one of the most important Palaeolithic sites in Africa. **[Ruaha National Park](/africa/tanzania/ruaha_national_par)** is about three hours' drive; its great size and relative inaccessibility mean far fewer visitors than the northern parks while still having superb wildlife. **[Udzungwa Mountains National Park](/africa/tanzania/udzungwa_national)** is accessible from the western approaches.

In town, **Neema Crafts** (www.neemacrafts.com) is a social enterprise employing more than 60 disabled people making handicrafts — woven goods, paper products made from elephant dung, recycled glass jewellery. The attached cafe serves excellent local coffee, ice cream, and food. **Gangilonga Rock** is worth the climb for views over the town — go with a group. The weekly market is among the best in southern Tanzania; look for the traditional woven Iringa baskets.
