---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Togolese food is filling, flavourful, and built around a few key staples. Fufu — a dense, dough-like paste made from pounded yams or cassava — is the foundation of most meals in the south, served alongside soups and stews. In the north, millet and sorghum take over, often shaped into tô, a thick porridge eaten with sauces.

Palm oil flavours almost everything. Soups are often based on tomato and onion, enriched with palm oil and fish or meat. Gboma dessi is a spinach stew common in the south, simmered with palm oil, fish, and spices. Djenkoume is a denser cornmeal dish, steamed and served with sauce — look for it at market stalls.

Ablo is a lightly fermented rice bread, steamed into small cakes, and eaten throughout the day as a snack or with sauces. It is particularly popular in Lomé. Along the coast, fresh fish is grilled on the beach and in roadside stalls — simple, good, and cheap.

In [Lomé](/africa/togo/lome) you will find a wide range of options, from street food around the grand marché to proper restaurants serving Lebanese, French, and Chinese food alongside local dishes. Outside the capital, your options narrow considerably. In smaller towns, look for the women cooking over charcoal near the bus station — that is usually where the best and cheapest food is found.

Togolese beer (Flag and Béninoise are the most common) is cold and drinkable. Sodabi — a palm wine spirit that is sometimes distilled twice — is the local firewater. Treat it with respect.
