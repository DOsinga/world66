**Visas**

Most nationalities can obtain a visa on arrival at Lomé's airport, valid for up to seven days. It can be extended for free — but you will need to surrender your passport to the authorities for several days while the extension is processed. If you plan to stay longer, arrange a visa through a Togolese embassy before you travel. Your passport must be valid for at least six months beyond your planned departure date.

**Money**

The currency is the West African CFA franc (XOF), shared by most former French colonies in the region. Notes from neighboring countries using the same currency (Benin, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire, etc.) are accepted. The economy is primarily cash-based, especially outside Lomé. ATMs exist in the capital but are less reliable elsewhere — carry enough cash when heading north. Credit cards are accepted at larger hotels and some restaurants in Lomé.

**Language**

French is the official language and is widely spoken in towns. In the south, Ewe and Mina are common. In the north, Kabre dominates. English is not widely spoken, though you will find some in the tourism sector. Knowing a few words of French goes a long way.

**Safety**

Lomé and most of the country are reasonably safe for travelers. Petty theft — pickpockets and bag-snatching — is the main concern in crowded areas like markets and bus stations. Lomé's beach road at night has a poor reputation; avoid walking it alone after dark.

The northern border areas near Burkina Faso are a different matter. Armed groups operating in the Sahel have carried out attacks and kidnappings in the border regions. Exercise caution if traveling to the far north, stay informed about current conditions, and consider whether travel to areas immediately bordering Burkina Faso is necessary.

**Health**

Yellow fever vaccination is mandatory for entry. Malaria is present throughout the country — take prophylactics and use insect repellent. Medical facilities outside Lomé are limited; bring a basic medical kit. Drink bottled or purified water.

**Electricity and practicalities**

Power uses 220V with French-style two-pin plugs. Power cuts are common outside Lomé. Mobile coverage is reasonable along the main corridor; data is slow in rural areas.
