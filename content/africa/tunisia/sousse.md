**Sousse** is Tunisia's third-largest city and the main beach resort on its central coast — a city of about 270,000 people about 140 km south of Tunis, with a long sandy Mediterranean beach fringed by resort hotels and a **UNESCO-listed medieval Medina** at its heart. It is the easiest beach destination in Tunisia for European travellers (flights direct from several European cities to nearby **Monastir Airport**) and is the country's best combination of classic Mediterranean beach resort and proper Arab-Islamic old town.

The **Medina**, surrounded by 9th-century walls, is one of the best-preserved in North Africa. At its heart stand the two defining buildings of early Islamic Sousse: the **Great Mosque**, begun in 851 CE in a severe fortified style that borrows directly from the earlier **Ribat** next door — a Byzantine-era coastal watchtower converted into an Aghlabid monastery-cum-fortress for Muslim warrior-monks guarding the coast against Christian raids. The Ribat can still be climbed for a fine view of the Medina and the harbour. A short walk away, the **Sousse Archaeological Museum**, housed in the old Kasbah, holds the second-finest collection of **Roman mosaics** in Tunisia after the Bardo in Tunis — a reminder that Sousse is the modern successor of the Roman city of Hadrumetum, once one of the most important cities of Roman Africa.

Beyond the Medina, Sousse is a working Tunisian port and university town, with a pleasant Corniche running out to the resort strip of **Port El Kantaoui** 10 km north — a mock-Andalusian marina village built from scratch in the 1980s that is now the main concentration of upmarket hotels. The beaches in both the city and the resort are good: long sandy stretches and shallow warm water. From Sousse you can easily day-trip to **[Kairouan](/africa/tunisia/kairouan)** (Islam's fourth holiest city, and home to one of the oldest mosques in North Africa), to **[Mahdia](/africa/tunisia/mahdia)** (a quieter old port town further down the coast), to **[Monastir](/africa/tunisia/monastir)** (another Ribat and the birthplace of Habib Bourguiba), and even to **El Jem** with its spectacular surviving Roman amphitheatre — the third-largest in the Roman world after the Colosseum and Capua.
