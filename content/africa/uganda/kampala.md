Kampala is Uganda's capital, a sprawling, hilly, noisy city of around 1.7 million people a few miles north of Lake Victoria. Built across seven hills — the colonial-era joke that it was another Rome was taken seriously by town planners — it is the busy, matatu-clogged hub of a country that has rebuilt itself from the wreckage of Idi Amin's rule and the decades of civil war that followed.

The city has few conventional sights, but the ones it has are worth a morning each. The Kasubi Tombs, burial place of the Buganda kings, are a UNESCO World Heritage Site (rebuilt after a 2010 fire) and a window into the pre-colonial kingdom that still dominates life in central Uganda. The Lubiri (the Kabaka's Palace) and the haunting Idi Amin torture chambers in the old military compound beneath it give a sobering counterweight. The Uganda Museum in Kamwokya is a slightly dusty but fascinating collection of ethnographic and musical-instrument displays from across the country.

Most travellers use Kampala as a one- or two-day stop before heading out to the real draw: the national parks of the west and the south (Bwindi, Queen Elizabeth, Murchison Falls) for gorilla trekking and safaris. But spend a night in the city itself — the food is lively, the matoke (plantain) and nyama choma (grilled meat) stalls are good, and the Ugandans you meet will be among the friendliest on the continent.
