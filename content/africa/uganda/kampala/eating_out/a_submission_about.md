**DCQ's guide to 10 must-eat things in Uganda**

We travel to see new places, to learn new cultures, to make new friends – and to taste local foods. OK, the 3 rd world is different: the water can’t be trusted, the dishes may not be washed properly for lack of running water, “organic” may be taken a bit too far, as when you get some vaguely-identifiable particles in your salad. So, yes, you will avoid the open-air fellow that makes the best mandazi in town or the one with a novel way of cooking up barbequed chicken. But not eat local food altogether? That would be a waste. Sure, Kampala has some world-class eating palaces and most foreigners will eat there only, but some variation is possible, without ruining your digestive system for days on end. Here’s the list:

1. Matooke and luwumbo . In a traditional restaurant matooke will be cooked – steamed – in matooke leaves and with matooke stem inserted for good measure. The finished colour will be a lovely light yellow. The texture will be soft – but only for 10 minutes or so, so you have to eat fast. You eat matooke with g-nut (ground nut; p-nut) sauce and a stew. The best is a beef luwumbo. The meat is cooked in matooke leaves with very little oil. In a traditional place the beef may be a bit too tough for foreign taste. Also – so much for eating street food! – not all local places can be trusted for their hygiene, so the choice boils (!) down to the innumerable traditional-food buffet joints on Kampala Road . Try them, you’ll get the whole array of local staples to sample, all boiled – cassava, sweet potatoes, yams, millet (as porridge; mind the grits!). As a vegetable you’ll find nakati (spinach; red is the best), and if you’re lucky, ntula , a very small oval aubergine that is nicely bitter in taste and hence full of goodies, otherwise it would be extinct by now, right?

2. Fried tilapia . Tilapia is our favoured fish from the lake. The pre-1972 small version that was indigenous to Uganda got eaten up by the Nile Perch and the tilapia we get now is a new introduction – very large. Of course people want a big one. Over in Bwaise there is a whole lane where they fry up tilapia pieces at 500 shillings, but no-one would want to go that far through the mud. Try it at Gaba Beach at 6-7,000 shillings. Take your own chilies and bread. In town the massala tilapia gives better value. Only 2-3 places make it.

3. Samosa – also spelt as Sambusa, known by trendies as S’M’S. A ubiquitous snack, consisting of a crisp pastry filled with a filling of mince meat, vegetable, or chicken. Was once a preserve of Asians and a bride was judged on how crisp her sms pastry was. Now sambusa can be found on the street for 200 shillings, but the fill will be beans and the oil overused (yes, I know, so much for street food!). The secret of a good sms – my cousin taught me – is slow frying and lots of coriander. The filling should be full otherwise the pastry will sag. There’s a thick-pastry thing done by Punjabis, filled always with potatoes. Is that a samosa?

4. Pillao . Eaten in Asian homes as celebration food or guest-come food. Now a preferred lunch dish of Kampalaites – but not as much as once projected by a Vancouver-chef-turned-aspiring-pillao-entrepreneur (10k per day!). Muslims like it; the blue-blooded stick to matooke day in day out. What passes off as pillao pisses off Asians: it’s just stock-flavoured rice topped up by a piece of KFC chicken. For the authentic, you have to get yourself invited as a spouse to the Aga Khan mosque on big occasions or to Asian homes. café Viva’s is unbeatable in restaurants. Biriyani – that’s quite another thing: that’s like for 4 times a year.

5. Nyama Choma – barbequed meat, sold on wooden sticks. Ubiquitous food near markets and bus stops upcountry. A bit too dry or else too fatty, although fat from the hump of the cow is the tastiest, cholesterol-laden (of course, there is always a price to pay!) food for your 300 shillings. Many upmarket places in Nakasero – one rung below the Nakasero places – feature BBQ meats. A great variation is Nyama Karanga – fried goat with onion and spices. Pork variations are done in the outskirts of town.

6. Rolex. Uganda ’s answer to take-away fast food. It’s an omelette wrapped in chapatti. Tomatoes are added. Street food par excellence, particularly for late-night reveling students. The best are done from a wagon at Wandegeya and you have to line up. Off rush-hour if you come with your own onion and coriander, then, voila, you have a gourmet omelette. The chapatti tends to be on the lumpy side, so for a price tell him to fry it up a little more. Of course you can always get an omelette and chapatti at a medium-level restaurant in the city, but Rolex is, well, different.

7. 8,9. Fried cassava . But only if the cassava is preboiled. Nobody on the street does it. An Asian joint in Old Kampala fills the niche. Roasted g-nuts . Uganda ’s are tiny, but tiny are the best. They are sold rolled up in plastic wrap, which is now banned, so expect the vendors to revert to making a cone from newspapers. It tastes even better. And if you are a peanut- butter fan, try our local version. It’s sold in supermarkets and it’s great. Sigiri-roasted maize . By the roadside where maize is actually grown. How much closer can you get to our food?

10? Well, yes, there has to be a 10. Come November, the rains will return and with that Uganda ’s favourite protein food – the ensenene. You see huge funnels of bright light in the suburbs. You see winged insects hovering around them and then dropping into the bright light. They die, slightly singed. (Friends assure me it’s a less painful death than that inflicted on live crabs dropped into boiling water!). In the morning you see them by the bagful at the markets, green. Buy ‘em, fry ‘em lightly in oil, add some spicing. Down them with chilled beer or at fashionable sundowners with fizzy wine. Mmm…What? Never had fried scorpions in China ? Never ate curried crab on the street in Thailand ?

BUT what do people actually eat in Uganda ?

Well, all of the above – but not all the time, of course, and you remember the list was pitched to the adventurous visitor.

Breakfast

Katogo. It’s boiled matooke where you still see the fingers – i.e. it’s not mashed. The accompaniment is a sauce - for the common man (us all), sauce made of beans, groundnuts or just tomatoes; for the better-off a sauce made from offals and beef.

Lunch

Kikomando – chapatti and beans at 500/-. For the upmarket people there’s the luwumbo of anything from groundnut to prime beef. Northerners go for their preferred cereal – millet. It’s boiled and comes as a lump, called kalo. The westerners have their own specialty – the eshabwe, a sauce made from petrified milk.

Snacks

Kabalagala (yes, as in the red-light strip). It’s made from cassava flour, baking powder and mashed yellow bananas. Mandazi – deep-fried sweetened dough. Tend to be very hard. Ensenene (grasshoppers). Roasted maize, roasted groundnuts . Mpaffu (olives) are delicious, eaten with a chilli sauce. Dried coffee beans ( mwanyi ) are chewed by taxi drivers to ward off fatigue and sleep; also mulondo roots, which are reputed to have other stimulatant properties! In the evening people go out to eat kigele , aka mulokoni – cow’s hooves, usually served with cassava. Stays in the stomach for a while so you can still have your supper (it’s always supper here: we were under the British!).

Supper

Any of the above.

Around 65 per cent of daily calories are derived from starchy foods – matooke in the south, millet in the north. Cassava is ubiquitous. The starchy staples are boiled and mashed and eaten with a sauce. The sauce varies with one’s income – from just tomatoes to chicken. Isn’t that how it is everywhere in the world? Wheat and rice are the staples. Rice is boiled, wheat baked. Both are eaten with sauces and as people get richer sauces become more proteinous. At the start of the 20 th century almost two-thirds of the daily intake in the United States was in the form of bread; now the ratio is under 20 percent. The Japanese at all income levels still stay loyal to rice, but don’t expect rice at a formal banquet in China . It is there, but it comes last and after three days of nonstop proteinous foods your stomach craves for starchies.
