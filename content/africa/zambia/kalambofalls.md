Kalambo Falls drops 221 metres over a single sheer cliff on the Kalambo River, which forms the border between Zambia and Tanzania at the southern end of Lake Tanganyika. It is the second highest uninterrupted waterfall in Africa, and one of the least visited major falls on the continent — which is precisely why it is worth the effort.

The falls were the site of some of Africa's most important archaeological discoveries. Stone tools and organic material found here pushed back the evidence of human fire use and complex behaviour far beyond what was previously known. UNESCO inscribed Kalambo Falls as a World Heritage Site in 2023 for its archaeological significance.

Access is from the Zambian side via Mbala, a small town about 40 km away. The track to the viewpoint is rough and 4WD is recommended in the wet season. There are no facilities at the falls themselves. The best view is from the Zambian rim; with a boat from local fishermen, it is sometimes possible to cross the river to the Tanzanian side for a different perspective.
