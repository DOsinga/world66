The Bamiyan valley sits at 2,590 metres in the Hindu Kush, roughly 230 km northwest of [Kabul](/asia/afghanistan/kabul). It is a place of stunning natural beauty — surrounded by dramatic cliffs and the turquoise Band-e Amir lakes — and carries one of the most poignant stories in world archaeology: the two giant Buddha statues that once dominated the valley cliffs, carved in the 6th and 7th centuries AD, were dynamited by the Taliban in March 2001 in an act of deliberate cultural destruction that shocked the world.

The Buddhas are gone, but the niches remain — vast empty alcoves cut into the sandstone cliffs, which still draw visitors trying to understand the scale of what was lost. Chinese pilgrims Fa-hsien (around 400 AD) and Xuanzang (630 AD) both described the statues in their travel diaries: the larger stood 55 metres high, the smaller 38 metres, both finished with plaster and painted, gleaming with gold leaf and jewels when Xuanzang saw them. The surrounding cliffs contain hundreds of ancient cave dwellings and meditation cells used by Buddhist monks — many are still accessible.

Beyond the archaeological sites, the valley is remarkable for its landscape. The Band-e Amir national park, about 75 km to the west, holds a series of six deep-blue crater lakes separated by natural travertine dams — one of Afghanistan's most extraordinary natural sights. In summer, the valley itself is green and cool, a welcome contrast to the heat of Kabul.

Travel to Bamiyan requires careful planning given security conditions. The Hazara population of the valley has historically been more welcoming to visitors than other parts of Afghanistan, and the road from Kabul — though long and rough — passes through dramatic mountain scenery.
