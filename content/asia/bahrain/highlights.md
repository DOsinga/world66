---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**Bahrain Fort (Qal'at al-Bahrain).** A UNESCO World Heritage Site on the northern coast, this ancient fort sits on a tell that contains layers of occupation going back to the Dilmun civilization around 2300 BC. The fort itself is Portuguese-era, but the archaeological site beneath it tells a story spanning four thousand years. The on-site museum is excellent. Visit at sunset for stunning views across the sea.

**Bahrain National Museum.** The best museum in the country, covering everything from ancient Dilmun burial mounds to pearl diving traditions to modern Bahraini art. Well-curated and air-conditioned -- a good refuge from the heat.

**Al-Fateh Grand Mosque.** One of the largest mosques in the world, capable of holding 7,000 worshippers. Non-Muslim visitors are welcome outside prayer times and free guided tours are available. The dome is made of fiberglass and weighs over 60 tonnes.

**The Tree of Life.** A solitary 400-year-old mesquite tree standing alone in the southern desert, kilometers from any obvious water source. Nobody is quite sure how it survives. It is a popular excursion, though some visitors find it underwhelming -- manage your expectations and enjoy the surreal landscape.

**Pearl diving heritage.** Bahrain was the pearl capital of the Gulf for centuries, and the Pearling Path in [Manama](/asia/bahrain/manama) is a UNESCO-listed walking trail through the old pearl merchant quarter. The trail connects traditional houses, shops, and the seafront where divers once set out.

**Al-Khamis Mosque.** Believed to be one of the oldest mosques in the Arabian Peninsula, dating from the 7th century. The twin minarets are distinctive and the ruins are atmospheric, though small.

**Formula 1 Bahrain Grand Prix.** Held at the Bahrain International Circuit in Sakhir, usually in March. The night race atmosphere is electric. Even if you are not a motorsport fan, the spectacle is worth experiencing.

**Manama Souq.** The old market near Bab al-Bahrain is a tangle of narrow alleys selling spices, textiles, gold, and perfumes. It is touristy in parts but still has genuine character. Haggling is expected.
