---
title: "Shopping in Cox's Bazar"
type: section
---

The beautiful tourist resort haven in Bangladesh can also be good for some **Cox Bazar shopping**. Here are some of the places where shopping can be done.

**Hotel Motel zone**

The Hotel Motel zone is the main shopping district of Cox&rsquo;s Bazar. This shopping area is the most touristy, given that the main industry of the town is tourism. This is the best place to pick up the little souvenirs that are customary for any tourist wanting to bring a little piece of Cox&rsquo;s Bazar home; from key rings and T-shirts, to seashells and shawls, they sell everything here. Those who are not enticed by the need to bring something home will find this place a little too touristy, and might want to avoid shopping here. Another thing to note is that all prices are negotiable, and haggling is a standard practice here.

**Burmese Market**

The Burmese Market, as its name suggests, caters to the rather sizable Burmese population living in the area surrounding Cox&rsquo;s Bazar. The place is run by locals, mostly, or Burmese who have been living around the place for generations and have assimilated entirely into the local way of life. Still, this is a good place to check out the cultural melting pot that Cox&rsquo;s Bazar is, being the meeting point of various cultures with tourists thrown into the mix. This is also a good place to pick up some local beauty products, such as sandal wood soap and shampoo, as well as home items, such as bed sheets and towels that are hand woven by the women who run the stalls at the market.

**Ramu**

The village of Ramu is located about 10 kilometers away from Cox&rsquo;s Bazar, a mere 10 minute drive away from the resort and beach haven that the town is. The village of Ramu is famous for its monasteries and Buddhist relics, having a large Buddhist population living in the village. It is also famous for its homemade cigars, of which some varieties are very well regarded in the world. Given the religious nature of the village, it is rare to see the villagers themselves smoking away. Although, during the colder months, it is fairly common to see even the monks have a puff or two in order to warm their bodies.

Cigar making in this village is more of a means of survival and a way of sustaining the culture in the place, and smoke is not a large part of the people&rsquo;s lives. However, cigar lovers will find this place a wonder, considering how each cigar is wrapped differently from family to family, even though the same varieties of leaves are used.
