There are two ways into Bhutan: by air to [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) or overland from India through [Phuntsholing](/asia/bhutan/phuntsholing).

**By air** -- Paro International Airport is the only international airport and one of the most dramatic in the world, with the runway wedged between mountains at 2,200 metres. Only two airlines serve Bhutan: Druk Air (the national carrier) and Bhutan Airlines. Between them, they fly to Bangkok, Delhi, Kathmandu, Kolkata, Dhaka, Singapore, and a handful of other cities. Flights are weather-dependent and delays or cancellations are common, especially in winter and monsoon season. Book early in peak season -- seats fill fast.

**Overland** -- The most common land entry point is [Phuntsholing](/asia/bhutan/phuntsholing) on the Indian border in the southwest. From there, a spectacular but gruelling 6-hour drive climbs through subtropical forest to [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu). Other land crossings at Gelephu and Samdrup Jongkhar are open to tourists but far less convenient. Indian nationals can enter more freely through these border points.

**Visa requirements** -- All visitors except citizens of India, Bangladesh, and the Maldives need a visa. You cannot get one yourself -- your licensed tour operator or approved hotel submits the application on your behalf. The visa costs $40, and you will receive an e-visa clearance letter that you must present to board your flight. Allow at least a week for processing.

**The Sustainable Development Fee** -- On top of the visa, every international visitor (except citizens of India, Bangladesh, and the Maldives) must pay a Sustainable Development Fee of $100 per adult per night ($50 for children aged 6 to 12, free for under-6s). This fee does not include accommodation, food, transport, or guide services -- those are additional costs arranged through your operator. The SDF is Bhutan's way of ensuring high-value, low-impact tourism. It is non-negotiable and has been confirmed through at least 2027.

**Booking** -- You must arrange your trip through a licensed Bhutanese tour operator or an approved hotel. The Tourism Council of Bhutan maintains a list of licensed operators. Many international travel agents can arrange bookings, but the on-the-ground arrangements will always be handled by a Bhutanese company.

Bhutan has one international airport: Paro (PBH), about 6 km from Paro town and 54 km from Thimphu. The approach — threading between steep Himalayan ridges — is considered one of the world's most dramatic landings. Only a handful of pilots are certified to fly it.

Two airlines operate all international flights to Paro: **Drukair** (the national carrier) and **Bhutan Airlines** (private). Both fly from Bangkok, Delhi, Kolkata, and Kathmandu. Drukair also connects from Dhaka, Singapore, and Dubai. There are no direct flights from Europe or North America; you'll connect through one of these hubs.

The alternative is overland entry through India. The main border crossing for tourists is **Phuntsholing**, on the southern edge of Bhutan opposite the Indian town of Jaigaon in West Bengal. There are also crossings at Gelephu and Samdrup Jongkhar for those with specific regional itineraries. From Phuntsholing, road trips to Thimphu take 4-5 hours on mountain roads.

### Visa and entry

All visitors except Indian nationals need a Bhutanese visa. The process is managed online at the Tourism Council of Bhutan's portal (bhutan.travel). You cannot simply show up and get a visa on arrival in the traditional sense — your entry requires pre-approval.

The steps: apply online, pay the non-refundable US$40 visa application fee, and pay the Sustainable Development Fee (see below) in advance. Your visa approval comes as a letter you present on arrival, where the actual stamp is issued.

Indian nationals enter with a national ID card or passport and do not need a visa, but still must pay the SDF.

### The Sustainable Development Fee

Bhutan charges a **Sustainable Development Fee (SDF) of US$100 per person per night** for most foreign visitors. Indian nationals pay INR 1,200 per night. Children aged 6-12 receive a 50% discount; children under 6 are exempt. This rate is guaranteed through August 2027.

The SDF replaced the old "minimum daily tourist spend" system in 2022. Previously, tourists were required to book a full package covering accommodation, meals, guide, and transport at a fixed daily rate of US$200-250. The new system separates the SDF from your actual travel costs. You still need to book through a licensed Bhutanese tour operator, but accommodation, food, and tours are now priced separately and competitively.

The SDF funds free healthcare and education for Bhutanese citizens and environmental conservation projects. Critics argue it makes Bhutan expensive; supporters say it keeps tourist numbers manageable and the environment intact.

### Booking through a tour operator

You cannot travel independently on a tourist visa — all visitors (except Indian nationals and certain other permitted categories) must book through a licensed Bhutanese tour operator. The operator handles your visa application, internal transport, guide, and accommodation. You can arrange this through a Bhutanese operator directly or through an international travel agent who works with one.
