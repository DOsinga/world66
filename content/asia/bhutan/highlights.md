**Tiger's Nest Monastery (Taktsang)** -- Bhutan's most iconic sight clings to a cliff face 900 metres above the Paro Valley floor. The hike up takes about two hours through blue pine forest, with a teahouse halfway offering views that will stop you in your tracks. The monastery itself, rebuilt after a fire in 1998, is a working temple where monks still meditate. Even if you have seen a thousand photos, nothing prepares you for the scale of it in person. Allow a full half-day from [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro).

**Punakha Dzong** -- The most beautiful fortress-monastery in Bhutan sits at the confluence of the Pho Chhu and Mo Chhu rivers. Built in 1637 by the country's unifier Shabdrung Ngawang Namgyal, it serves as the winter residence for the central monk body. In spring, jacaranda trees frame the dzong in purple. The interior is magnificent -- towering whitewashed walls, ornate woodwork, and a sense of deep calm. See [Punakha](/asia/bhutan/punakha).

**Dochula Pass** -- The mountain pass between [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) and [Punakha](/asia/bhutan/punakha) sits at 3,100 metres and is crowned by 108 memorial chortens. On a clear day, the panorama of snow-capped Himalayan peaks stretching across the horizon is one of the finest views in Asia. Best in early morning during autumn and spring when the air is sharpest.

**Thimphu Weekend Market** -- The biggest market in the country comes alive on Fridays and Saturdays along the banks of the Wang Chhu. Farmers from surrounding valleys bring in red rice, yak cheese, dried chili, fresh vegetables, and handwoven textiles. It is the best place to see everyday Bhutanese life and pick up affordable souvenirs.

**Bumthang Valley** -- The spiritual heartland of Bhutan, where Buddhism first took root in the 8th century. The [Bumthang](/asia/bhutan/bumthang) area is a cluster of four valleys dotted with ancient temples, apple orchards, and buckwheat fields. Jambay Lhakhang and Kurjey Lhakhang are among the oldest temples in the country. The locally made apple juice, cheese, and honey are excellent.

**Gangtey Valley and Phobjikha** -- A wide glacial valley in central Bhutan that feels like the end of the world. In winter (late October to February), endangered black-necked cranes migrate here from Tibet, and the annual Black-Necked Crane Festival in November draws visitors from across the country. The Gangtey Goemba monastery overlooks the entire valley.

**The Tsechu Festivals** -- Bhutan's masked dance festivals are held at dzongs and monasteries throughout the year, with the biggest at [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) (spring) and [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) (autumn). Monks in elaborate costumes and painted masks perform sacred dances that have been unchanged for centuries. The festivals are genuine religious events, not tourist shows, and attending one is the single best way to understand Bhutanese culture.

### Tiger's Nest Monastery (Paro Taktsang)

The most iconic image in Bhutan: a cluster of white and gold buildings clinging to a granite cliff 900 metres above the Paro Valley. Guru Rinpoche is said to have flown here on the back of a tigress in the 8th century to meditate in a cave, establishing Buddhism in the region. The monastery itself was built in 1692.

The hike takes 2-4 hours return, starting from a car park and climbing through pine forest to a viewpoint, then descending and re-climbing to the entrance. It's steep but well-marked. Horses are available for the lower section. Bring water and give yourself the morning — go early before the clouds come in.

### Punakha Dzong

The most beautiful fortress-monastery in the country, sitting at the confluence of the Pho Chhu (male river) and Mo Chhu (female river). Built in 1637, it served as Bhutan's capital until 1955 and still functions as the winter seat of the Central Monastic Body. In spring, jacaranda trees bloom purple around its whitewashed walls. The interior is richly decorated and worth spending time in.

### Thimphu

The capital and largest city, with a population under 200,000 — one of the smallest capitals on Earth. Visit Tashichho Dzong (the fortress housing the government and royal offices), the National Memorial Chorten, the Folk Heritage Museum, and the weekend market at the river. The city has an increasingly good cafe and restaurant scene. Don't miss the takin enclosure — Bhutan's strange national animal, sometimes described as a gnu-wildebeest cross.

### Dochula Pass

On the road between Thimphu and Punakha, at 3,100 m, the Dochula Pass offers some of the finest views of the eastern Himalayas on a clear day — a sweep of peaks from Masang Gang to Gangkar Puensum (at 7,570 m, the world's highest unclimbed mountain). The pass is marked by 108 memorial chortens and a small monastery.

### Gangtey Valley (Phobjikha)

A broad glacial valley in central Bhutan, home to a population of Black-Necked Cranes that migrate here from Tibet each winter (mid-October to February). The valley is strikingly different from the narrow river valleys elsewhere — open, flat, ringed by forested ridges. The Gangtey Goenpa monastery looks out over the whole bowl. Relatively few tourists make it this far, and the slower pace rewards those who do.

### Bumthang

The cultural heartland of Bhutan: four valleys containing some of the oldest temples in the country, including Jambay Lhakhang (7th century) and Kurjey Lhakhang. The pace is noticeably slower than the western valleys. Bumthang has its own micro-brewery (Red Panda beer) and is known for its buckwheat noodles and red rice. The drives to get here — crossing the Pelela and Yotong La passes — are beautiful in themselves.

### Trekking

The classic routes include the **Druk Path Trek** (5-6 days, Paro to Thimphu through alpine lakes and forest) and the **Jomolhari Trek** (8-10 days, approaching the sacred 7,329 m peak along the Tibetan border). The **Snowman Trek** is one of the hardest multi-week treks in the world, crossing passes above 5,000 m and reaching remote communities in the north. All trekking requires a guide arranged through your tour operator.
