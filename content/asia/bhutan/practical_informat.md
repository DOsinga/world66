**Money** -- The national currency is the Ngultrum (Nu.), pegged at par with the Indian Rupee. Indian Rupees are accepted everywhere, but only in denominations of 100 or less -- larger notes are often refused. US dollars are useful for paying the SDF and visa fees but not widely accepted in shops. Credit cards work at some upscale hotels in [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) and [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) but expect a surcharge of 5-7%. ATMs exist in the main towns but are unreliable. Bring enough cash in US dollars or Indian Rupees for your trip and exchange at the Bank of Bhutan.

**Language** -- Dzongkha is the national language, but English is widely spoken in towns and by anyone in the tourism industry. In remote eastern villages, you may struggle without some Dzongkha. A few useful phrases: *Kuzu Zangpo* (hello), *Kadrin chhe* (thank you), *Ga de bay ye?* (how are you?).

**Internet and phones** -- Mobile coverage exists in the main valleys but drops out quickly in rural areas. Wi-Fi is available at hotels in [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) and [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) but tends to be slow. You can buy a Bhutanese SIM card (B-Mobile or TashiCell) on arrival in Paro, which is useful for basic communication. Do not expect to work remotely from Bhutan.

**Electricity** -- 230V, 50Hz with Indian-style round pin plugs (Type D and Type G). Power cuts happen, especially outside the main towns. Bring a universal adapter and a portable charger.

**Safety** -- Bhutan is one of the safest countries in Asia. Violent crime against tourists is virtually unheard of. Petty theft is rare but not impossible in border towns like [Phuntsholing](/asia/bhutan/phuntsholing). The main safety risks are road accidents on mountain highways and altitude sickness above 3,000 metres.

**Altitude** -- [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) sits at 2,300 metres and [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) at 2,200 metres, which is enough to cause mild symptoms in some people. If you are heading higher on treks, take acclimatisation seriously. Drink plenty of water and ascend gradually.

**What to wear** -- Dress modestly, especially when visiting dzongs and monasteries. Long sleeves and long trousers or skirts are required. Remove your shoes before entering temples. Hats must be removed inside dzongs. Outside of religious sites, casual dress is fine, but Bhutanese appreciate visitors who make an effort.

**Tipping** -- Not traditionally expected but increasingly common in tourism. A tip of $5-10 per day for your guide and $3-5 for your driver is appreciated at the end of a trip.

### Currency

The national currency is the **Ngultrum (Nu. / BTN)**, pegged at par with the Indian Rupee. Both currencies are accepted everywhere. US$1 is roughly Nu. 85 (check current rates before travel). Ngultrum is useless outside Bhutan, so don't exchange more than you need.

Exchange cash at Paro Airport on arrival, or at Bank of Bhutan and Bhutan National Bank branches in Thimphu and Paro. ATMs are available in Thimphu and Paro but may not accept all foreign cards — bring cash as backup.

Bhutan is still largely cash-based outside major cities and high-end hotels. Visa and Mastercard are accepted in smarter hotels and some restaurants in Thimphu; smaller towns and rural areas are cash-only. Indian Rupees in denominations of Rs 200 and below are widely accepted; Rs 500 and Rs 2,000 notes are often refused.

### Health

No vaccinations are specifically required for Bhutan, but standard travel immunisations are sensible (hepatitis A and B, typhoid, tetanus). There is no malaria risk in Thimphu or the high valleys, but the southern lowlands carry some risk during monsoon season.

Altitude is the main health concern. Thimphu sits at 2,300 m, Paro at 2,200 m. Passes between valleys can reach 3,000-3,700 m. Most visitors have no problems, but take it easy on the first day and drink plenty of water. If you're going on high-altitude treks, allow time to acclimatise.

The healthcare system is basic outside Thimphu. Serious emergencies may require evacuation to India. Good travel insurance including medical evacuation cover is essential.

### Communications

Mobile coverage (SIM cards from B-Mobile or TashiCell) works in towns and along main roads. Buy a local SIM at the airport or in Thimphu — they're inexpensive and work with a passport. Coverage thins out in valleys and disappears entirely on remote treks.

Wi-Fi is available at most hotels and some cafes in Thimphu and Paro. Connections are decent in the capital, slower elsewhere.

### Electricity

220-240V, 50Hz. Plug types vary — you'll find both the Indian round-pin (Type D/M) and the flat-blade Type C/F European style. Bring a universal adapter.

### Time

Bhutan Standard Time is UTC+6. There is no daylight saving.

### Photography

Ask before photographing people, especially monks and monks in ceremonies. Photography inside dzongs and temples is often restricted or requires a permit. Respect the rules — your guide will advise.

### Dress and conduct

Traditional dress (the gho for men, kira for women) is required when visiting dzongs and government offices. Shoes must be removed before entering temples. Bhutanese society is conservative; dress modestly in religious sites.
