---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's"
type: section
---

**1. Hike to Tiger's Nest Monastery** -- The climb to Taktsang through blue pine forest above the [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) valley is the quintessential Bhutan experience. The trail is well-maintained but steep, gaining 900 metres over roughly two hours. Pause at the teahouse halfway for views of the monastery clinging impossibly to the cliff face. Inside, the temple rooms are intimate and deeply sacred. Go early in the morning for the best light and fewer people.

**2. Attend a Tsechu Festival** -- Plan your trip around one of Bhutan's great masked dance festivals and you will see something that exists nowhere else on earth. Monks in silk costumes and wooden masks perform centuries-old dances in the courtyards of massive dzongs while the entire community watches in their finest dress. The Paro Tsechu in spring and Thimphu Tsechu in autumn are the most accessible, but smaller valley festivals can be even more powerful.

**3. Drive over Dochula Pass at dawn** -- Leave [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) before sunrise and arrive at the 3,100-metre pass as the first light hits the Himalayan peaks. The 108 chortens catch the golden light while the entire eastern horizon fills with snow-capped mountains. On a clear morning, this is one of the great views in Asia. Combine it with the drive down to [Punakha](/asia/bhutan/punakha), which is spectacular in its own right.

**4. Eat ema datshi in a farmhouse** -- Ask your guide to arrange a meal at a traditional Bhutanese farmhouse. You will sit cross-legged on the floor around a wood-burning stove, eating the national dish of chili and cheese with red rice, drinking butter tea or ara, and getting a glimpse of daily life that no hotel buffet can match. The warmth of Bhutanese hospitality is at its best in these settings.

**5. Walk through the Bumthang temples** -- The four valleys of [Bumthang](/asia/bhutan/bumthang) are the spiritual heart of Bhutan, scattered with temples dating back to the 7th and 8th centuries. Walk between Jambay Lhakhang, Kurjey Lhakhang, and Tamshing Lhakhang through buckwheat fields and apple orchards, with prayer flags overhead and monks chanting in the distance. It is the closest you will come to understanding why Bhutan calls itself the Land of the Thunder Dragon.
