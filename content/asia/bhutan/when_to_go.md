Bhutan has two peak seasons and they are peak for good reason. **Spring (March to May)** brings warm days, clear skies, and entire hillsides of rhododendron in bloom -- pink, red, and white. The Paro Tsechu festival falls in spring and is one of the country's most spectacular events. **Autumn (September to November)** offers crisp air, brilliant blue skies, and the best mountain views of the year. The Thimphu Tsechu happens in early autumn. Both seasons are ideal for trekking.

**Summer (June to August)** is monsoon season. Roads flood and landslides block highways, sometimes for days. The southern lowlands become oppressively hot and humid. That said, the high-altitude meadows are carpeted with wildflowers, including Bhutan's national flower, the blue poppy. If you can handle the rain and the occasional road closure, you will have the place almost to yourself and pay lower prices.

**Winter (December to February)** is cold, especially at altitude. Passes above 3,000 metres may be snowed in, and some eastern routes become impassable. But the western valleys around [Paro](/asia/bhutan/paro) and [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) remain accessible, festival-goers attend the dramatic Punakha Drubchen and Trongsa Tsechu, and visitor numbers drop sharply. If you dress warmly and stick to the western circuit, winter can be a rewarding time to visit.

Temperatures vary enormously with altitude. [Thimphu](/asia/bhutan/thimphu) at 2,300 metres sees daytime highs of 25 degrees Celsius in summer and lows around minus 5 in winter. The southern border town of [Phuntsholing](/asia/bhutan/phuntsholing) can hit 35 degrees in summer. Always bring layers, even in the warm months -- evenings cool down fast in the valleys.

Bhutan has four distinct seasons, and when you visit makes a real difference.

**Spring (March to May)** is the most popular time to visit, and for good reason. The weather is mild and clear, rhododendrons are in bloom, and the Paro Tshechu — one of the country's biggest festivals — falls in April. Mountain views are excellent before the summer haze sets in. Book flights and accommodation months in advance.

**Autumn (September to November)** is equally good, possibly better for mountain views. The post-monsoon air is crystal clear. The Thimphu Tshechu runs in late September or early October. October and November are the driest months of the year. Crowds are comparable to spring.

**Winter (December to February)** is cold but has its own appeal. Snowfall in the higher valleys adds drama; Thimphu and Paro can get frost. Fewer tourists mean easier bookings and sometimes lower tour costs. The Black-Necked Crane Festival at Gangtey in late November draws birdwatchers. Some high-altitude treks and passes close.

**Monsoon (June to August)** is the one season most visitors avoid. The rain is heavy and persistent, landslides regularly close roads, and views are obscured. The landscape is intensely green, however, and this is when the Haa Valley festival takes place. Prices are lower. If you're flexible and don't mind the rain, it has a certain character.

### Festivals as a reason to visit

Timing your trip around a Tshechu festival — Paro in spring, Thimphu or Punakha in autumn — is one of the best reasons to visit Bhutan when you do. These multi-day festivals feature masked dances, religious ceremonies, and the unfurling of enormous thangka paintings at dawn. They're genuinely extraordinary and not staged for tourists.
