Cambodia is one of Southeast Asia's most captivating destinations, a country where ancient temple ruins rise from jungle canopy and modern cities hum with the energy of a nation reinventing itself. For most visitors, the temples of Angkor are the draw, and rightly so -- there is nothing else quite like them on earth. But Cambodia has far more to offer than its famous ruins.

The country sits between Thailand, Laos, and Vietnam, with a short coastline on the Gulf of Thailand. The Mekong River runs through its heart, and the vast Tonle Sap lake -- Southeast Asia's largest freshwater lake -- swells to five times its dry-season size each year, sustaining fishing communities that live entirely on water.

[Phnom Penh](/asia/cambodia/phnompenh), the capital, is a chaotic, charming city on the confluence of the Mekong and Tonle Sap rivers. Its riverfront buzzes with restaurants and bars, while the Royal Palace and Silver Pagoda gleam in the tropical sun. The city also confronts its dark recent history head-on at the Tuol Sleng Museum and the Killing Fields of Choeung Ek, experiences that are harrowing but essential to understanding modern Cambodia.

[Siem Reap](/asia/cambodia/siemreap) is the gateway to the Angkor temple complex, but has grown into a destination in its own right, with a lively restaurant and bar scene centred on Pub Street and the Old Market area. Beyond the main temples, the floating villages on Tonle Sap and the remote jungle temple of Beng Mealea are well worth the trip.

[Battambang](/asia/cambodia/battambang) is Cambodia's second city and arguably its most charming, with well-preserved French colonial architecture along the riverfront and a thriving arts scene. [Sihanoukville](/asia/cambodia/sihanoukville) is the jumping-off point for Cambodia's southern islands, including Koh Rong and Koh Rong Sanloem, which offer some of the region's best beaches. The quieter coastal town of [Kep](/asia/cambodia/kep) is famous for its crab market and faded seaside grandeur.

Off the beaten track, [Kratie](/asia/cambodia/kratie) on the Mekong is a pleasant river town where you can spot rare Irrawaddy dolphins. [Kampong Cham](/asia/cambodia/kampongcham) has impressive pre-Angkorian ruins nearby, and the remote northeast province of Ratanakiri offers volcanic lakes, waterfalls, and hill-tribe communities.

Cambodia's recent history casts a long shadow. The Khmer Rouge regime of 1975-1979 killed an estimated two million people and devastated the country's infrastructure, culture, and educated class. The recovery has been remarkable but uneven, and poverty remains widespread outside the main cities. Cambodians are overwhelmingly warm and welcoming, and even basic interactions reveal a resilience and good humour that is deeply moving.

The country is affordable, easy to get around, and rewarding for travelers willing to look beyond the main tourist circuit. Just be prepared for the heat.
