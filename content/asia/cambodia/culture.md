## Religion

Cambodian religion is a layered thing. Today almost 95 percent of the population practices Theravada Buddhism, which entered the country in the 13th century and gradually displaced the earlier Hindu cults promoted by the Angkor kings. Before that, the Khmer kings had largely followed Brahmanist traditions, worshipping Shiva and Vishnu and conceiving of themselves as god-kings (devaraja). Hindu cosmology shaped Angkor's entire architectural vocabulary — the temple as cosmic mountain, the moat as primordial ocean.

Buddhism sits alongside older animist practices that have never fully disappeared. Territorial spirits and ancestor worship remain common; small spirit houses (neak ta) stand at roadsides and in pagoda courtyards, and most important ceremonies — weddings, funerals, coming-of-age — incorporate both Buddhist and animist elements. Cambodian Buddhism is generally tolerant and easy-going about this.

## Art and Architecture

The height of Khmer art coincides with the Angkor period (roughly the 9th to 15th centuries). Everything built then was religious — all stone, all sacred — and governed by elaborate cosmological rules. The central sanctuary represented Mount Meru, the towers its peaks, the enclosing walls the world's edge, and the moat the ocean beyond.

The defining sculptural motifs are apsaras (celestial dancing women) and nagas (the sacred serpent, source of water and fertility). Both appear constantly at Angkor, carved with extraordinary finesse — the apsaras in particular display an astonishing variety of expression and adornment across thousands of individual carvings. The naga balustrades at temple entrances have become one of the most recognizable images of Southeast Asian art.

Angkor Wat, the largest religious building on earth, was conceived by Suryavarman II in the 12th century and took an estimated 30 years to build. Its five towers, stylized on the Cambodian national flag, have become a symbol of Khmer identity. The temple has been continuously occupied by Buddhist monks and is in remarkable condition.

## Music and Dance

Classical Khmer dance (robam kbach boran) was nearly wiped out under the Khmer Rouge, who killed the majority of the country's traditional performers. The tradition has been slowly reconstructed from those who survived. Performances are staged for tourists in Siem Reap and Phnom Penh, but they are not purely a tourist product — dance carries deep cultural and religious meaning for Cambodians and is still taught seriously.

Shadow puppetry (sbaek thom) is another ancient art form surviving in the country. The large leather puppets depict scenes from the Ramayana and were traditionally performed at religious ceremonies.
