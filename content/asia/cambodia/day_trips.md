Cambodia's main bases — [Phnom Penh](/asia/cambodia/phnompenh) and [Siem Reap](/asia/cambodia/siemreap) — both have worthwhile day trips that take you out of the city without requiring a major logistics effort.

## From Phnom Penh

**Oudong** (35km north) was Cambodia's royal capital before Phnom Penh. The hillside site has a collection of royal stupas from the 17th–19th centuries and good views across the rice plains. Half a day is plenty; it's easy by tuk-tuk or taxi.

**Tonle Bati** (about 30km south) has a 12th-century Khmer temple (Ta Prohm — not to be confused with the more famous one at Angkor) beside a peaceful lake. The site sees few tourists and makes for a relaxed afternoon. Combine with the Phnom Tamao Wildlife Rescue Centre nearby, which is one of the better wildlife sanctuaries in Southeast Asia and worth supporting.

**Choeung Ek** (15km southwest of Phnom Penh) is technically not a day trip but the Killing Fields memorial site, best visited in conjunction with the Tuol Sleng museum in the city. The journey takes about 30 minutes by tuk-tuk.

## From Siem Reap

**Banteay Srei** (37km northeast) is an outlying Angkor-era temple built in the 10th century from pink sandstone and covered in some of the finest decorative carving in all of Khmer art. Tiny and exquisite. Combine with a stop at the landmine museum nearby.

**Beng Mealea** (70km east) is a vast jungle temple in the same era as Angkor Wat, left largely unrestored and partly consumed by forest. It feels genuinely remote, and you'll often have it nearly to yourself. Half a day from Siem Reap.

**Tonle Sap Lake** — the floating villages on the lake are a short drive and boat trip from Siem Reap. The scale of the lake (it grows to five times its dry-season size during the monsoon) is hard to comprehend from the shore; a boat trip gives a sense of it. The village visits can feel staged in the more touristed spots — ask about less-visited communities.

**Kampong Phluk** is one of the more authentic floating villages, accessible year-round (though the approach route changes with the season). Houses on stilts rise 6–8 meters above the ground during the dry season; in the wet season the whole settlement floats.
