China is not one country so much as a continent pretending to be one. It stretches from subarctic forests in Manchuria to tropical beaches on Hainan, from the Tibetan Plateau at 4,500 meters to the neon canyons of Shanghai. The sheer scale is hard to grasp until you are sitting on a train for twenty hours and realize you have not left the same province.

The east coast is where most visitors start, and for good reason. [Beijing](/asia/china/beijing) has the Forbidden City, hutong alleyways, and the best Peking duck on the planet. [Shanghai](/asia/china/shanghai) is China at its most futuristic -- a skyline that changes every year, French Concession lane houses, and dumpling shops that have been open since your grandparents were born. Further south, [Guangzhou](/asia/china/guangzhou) and [Shenzhen](/asia/china/shenzhen) hum with Cantonese energy and some of the best food in the country. And then there is [Hong Kong](/asia/china/hongkong), still very much its own world.

Head inland and China gets wilder. [Xi'an](/asia/china/xian) has the Terracotta Warriors and a Muslim Quarter that alone is worth the trip. [Guilin](/asia/china/guilin) and [Yangshuo](/asia/china/yangshuo) deliver the karst mountain scenery you have seen on every Chinese painting. [Yunnan province](/asia/china/yunnanprovince) in the southwest is staggeringly diverse -- snow mountains, rice terraces, Tibetan villages, and a food scene influenced by Southeast Asia. [Tibet](/asia/china/tibet) remains one of the most extraordinary places on earth, though getting a permit requires patience and a registered tour.

The contrast between old and new China hits you constantly. You can take a 350 km/h bullet train to a village where farmers still plow with water buffalo. A monk in saffron robes scrolls through WeChat. A 2,000-year-old city wall has a Starbucks built into it.

Be prepared for some friction. The language barrier is real outside major cities -- learn a few Mandarin phrases or keep a translation app handy. The Great Firewall blocks Google, WhatsApp, Instagram, and most Western social media, so download a VPN before you arrive. Visa rules change frequently and bureaucracy can be slow. But China rewards persistence. The food alone is worth the hassle, and once you get past the tourist trail, you will find a warmth and curiosity from ordinary people that is genuinely disarming.

China is not an easy country to travel. It is an extraordinary one.
