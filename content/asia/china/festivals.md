China has a rich calendar of traditional festivals, all based on the lunar calendar, plus the modern public holidays that bring the country to a halt three times a year. Timing your visit around the right festival — or deliberately avoiding the wrong one — makes a big difference.

## Spring Festival (Chinese New Year)

The most important holiday in China. The date shifts each year (late January or February), marking the start of the lunar new year. Families travel home from across the country in what is the world's largest annual migration. Cities empty out as workers return to their home villages; rural areas fill up with people who've spent the year working in factories or construction.

For travelers, the run-up to Chinese New Year (two weeks before) brings spectacular temple fairs, fireworks, and red lanterns everywhere. It is also when transport becomes virtually impossible to book. Many restaurants and shops close for a week or more. If you want to experience the celebrations, go to a city that keeps things lively — Xi'an, Chengdu, or any city with a lively temple fair. The ice sculptures in [Harbin](/asia/china/harbin) peak in January and make a striking backdrop to the season.

## Lantern Festival

Fifteen days after Chinese New Year, marking the end of the Spring Festival period. Cities hang out elaborate lantern displays, and people solve riddle games attached to lanterns. A good, lower-key festival to catch if you're still in the country.

## Qingming Festival (Tomb Sweeping Day)

Early April. Families visit the graves of ancestors to clean them and make offerings. A significant cultural moment and a public holiday — expect busy transport.

## Dragon Boat Festival (Duanwu)

Fifth day of the fifth lunar month, usually falling in June. Teams race long wooden boats decorated as dragons on rivers and lakes; the best races to watch are in Guangzhou, [Hangzhou](/asia/china/hangzhou), and the towns of Hunan province. The traditional food is zongzi — sticky rice wrapped in bamboo leaves, filled with pork, egg, or red bean.

## Mid-Autumn Festival (Moon Festival)

Fifteenth day of the eighth lunar month, usually September or October. Families gather to watch the full moon and eat mooncakes — dense pastries filled with lotus paste, red bean, or salted egg yolk. More intimate than the Spring Festival; a good time to see the communal, family-oriented side of Chinese culture.

## National Day Golden Week

October 1–7 marks the founding of the People's Republic of China. This is not a traditional festival but has the practical effect of a massive national holiday. Everyone travels. Every tourist site is packed. Prices spike. If you're in China during this week, either stay in one place or book everything months in advance.

## Minority Festivals

Beyond the Han calendar, China's ethnic minorities celebrate their own festivals with extraordinary color. The Water Splashing Festival of the Dai people in [Yunnan](/asia/china/yunnanprovince) (mid-April) involves several days of water fights and celebration. The Miao people of Guizhou hold elaborate silver-adorned festivals in autumn. The Tibetan New Year (Losar) is celebrated with masked dances, ceremonies, and yak butter sculptures in monasteries across [Tibet](/asia/china/tibet).
