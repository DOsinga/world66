title: "Food"
type: section
---

Chinese food as you know it abroad is a shadow of the real thing. The regional diversity is staggering — eight major cuisines, each with centuries of tradition, and they taste nothing alike.

**Sichuan** food is the one that gets all the attention, and fairly so. The mala numbing-spicy combination of Sichuan peppercorns and dried chilies is addictive. Hotpot in [Chengdu](/asia/china/chengdu) or Chongqing is a communal ritual — a bubbling cauldron of chili oil where you cook your own meat, vegetables, and offal. Dan dan noodles, mapo tofu, and twice-cooked pork are everyday dishes here that would be star attractions anywhere else.

**Cantonese** cooking in [Guangzhou](/asia/china/guangzhou) and [Hong Kong](/asia/china/hongkong) is more subtle — dim sum, roast goose, wonton noodle soup, fresh steamed fish. Breakfast dim sum with tea (yum cha) is one of the great meals on earth.

**Peking duck** in [Beijing](/asia/china/beijing) is carved tableside with surgical precision — crispy skin, thin pancakes, spring onions, and hoisin sauce. The best places roast with jujube wood and you will taste the difference.

In the northwest, **Lanzhou hand-pulled noodles** are an art form — the cook stretches and folds the dough until it becomes thin strands, dropped into beef broth. Xinjiang food brings lamb kebabs, naan bread, and cumin-heavy flavors that feel more Central Asian than Chinese.

Street food is everywhere. Jianbing (savory breakfast crepes), baozi (steamed buns), stinky tofu, and skewers of everything imaginable grilled over charcoal. Eat where the locals queue and you will rarely go wrong.

Tea culture runs deep. Green tea in the east, pu'er in Yunnan, jasmine in the north. A tea house visit is one of the most relaxing things you can do in China.
