## High-Speed Rail

China has built the largest high-speed rail network in the world, and it shows. Bullet trains (G and D trains) connect Beijing, Shanghai, Xi'an, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Hangzhou, and dozens of other cities at speeds of 250–350 km/h. The journey from Beijing to Shanghai takes about 4.5 hours; Beijing to Xi'an about 5. Tickets are reasonably priced and the trains are clean and punctual.

Book in advance on Trip.com or the official 12306 app (which has an English version). Bring your passport — it's required to buy a ticket and pass through security at the station.

## Slower Trains

Regular (non-high-speed) trains cover routes the bullet network doesn't reach, including Xinjiang and much of the west. They're cheaper and slower, but overnight sleeper trains remain a practical way to cover long distances while saving on a night's accommodation. Hard sleepers (6 berths per compartment) are fine; soft sleepers (4 berths) are more comfortable.

## Air

For long distances — especially to [Tibet](/asia/china/tibet), [Yunnan](/asia/china/yunnanprovince), and the far west — flying is often faster and sometimes cheaper than the train. Domestic airlines include Air China, China Eastern, China Southern, and several budget carriers. Book on Trip.com or Ctrip. Airports in major cities are modern and well-connected by metro.

## City Metro

Most Chinese cities of any size now have metro systems. They're fast, cheap, clean, and well-signed in English. Buy single-journey cards from machines at the station; reloadable transit cards are available in bigger cities and work on buses too.

## Buses

Intercity buses fill the gaps the rail network misses, particularly in rural areas, Sichuan mountain routes, and the southwest. They're cheap but variable in comfort and safety. Within cities, buses are usable if you know your stop — useful for reaching places not on the metro.

## Taxis and Ride-Hailing

Taxis are metered and relatively cheap in most cities. Didi (the Chinese equivalent of Uber) is widely used and has an English interface accessible through WeChat. Didi is often easier than hailing a taxi, and cheaper too. Tip: have your destination written in Chinese before you get in — most drivers don't speak English.

## Renting a Car

Foreigners cannot drive in China on an international driving licence; you need a Chinese licence. Hiring a driver — either through a tour agency or informally — is the practical option for reaching remote areas.

## Payments on the Go

Cash (yuan, also called RMB) is accepted everywhere but the country runs overwhelmingly on digital payments: WeChat Pay and Alipay. Foreign visitors can now link a foreign Visa or Mastercard to Alipay. Set this up before you arrive or shortly after — it will save a lot of friction.

## Avoid Golden Week

Avoid traveling during Chinese New Year (late January or February), the May Golden Week (around May 1), and National Day Golden Week (first week of October). Trains, planes, hotels, and major tourist sites become extremely congested. If your trip overlaps, book everything weeks in advance.
