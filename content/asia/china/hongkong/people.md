---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Hong Kong has a population of around 7.5 million, one of the most densely packed places on earth. About 92 percent are ethnic Chinese, predominantly Cantonese-speaking and with roots in Guangdong province, though significant communities trace ancestry to Shanghai, Chiu Chow, and Hakka regions. The remainder includes a long-established South Asian community (mainly Filipino and Indonesian domestic workers, and older-generation Indian and Pakistani families), as well as a substantial expatriate population from Europe, North America, and Japan.

The dominant language is Cantonese, which is distinct enough from Mandarin that speakers of each cannot understand the other. English remains widely used in business, law, and government — street signs are bilingual, and younger Hongkongers typically speak reasonable English. Mandarin has become increasingly useful and is widely understood, particularly since 1997, but Cantonese remains the language of daily life and cultural identity.

Hong Kong people have a reputation for being hardworking, impatient, and commercially sharp. They are also intensely proud of their city and its particular culture — the food especially — and will tell you at length that Hong Kong dim sum is superior to anything on the mainland. Religion is a practical, eclectic mix: Buddhist and Taoist temples are everywhere and well-attended, particularly for Lunar New Year and the Ching Ming grave-sweeping festival. A substantial minority is Christian, a legacy of the colonial era's missionary schools.
