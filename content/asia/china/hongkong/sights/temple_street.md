**The Yaumatei District**
Heading
North on Kownloon's Canton or Jordan road, you will get to the Yaumatei
District. The Yaumatei District is famous for its large typhoon shelter
where Hong Kong's boat people anchor their floating homes. This
district is also famous, although some might say infamous, for the
floating brothels ( sampans in Cantonese). The Temple Street is located right in the heart of the Yaumatei District.
**

Temple Street's Night Market**

Originally
famous for its temples, Temple Street is now renowned for its night
market that lights up after the sun goes down. Right in the middle of
Jordan Road and Kansu Street, bargain shoppers will find an amazing
night market that starts just after sunset. Some locals might point out
that the market does officially start at 2 p.m., however it is only
after dark (and after work!) that people come out to shop. It is a
gathering of colour and activity with endless rows of market goods.
If you
want a sneak peek of what Temple Street's night market looks like, look
for the 1990's Hong Kong film called "Queen of Temple Street", the
1992's Hong Kong film called "The Prince of Temple Street" and director
Stephen Chow's famous "The God of Cookery".
We highly recommend the local eateries that present various seafood dishes and local delicacies from the Cantonese cuisine.

**Temple's Street Temples**
Temple
Street wouldn't be called Temple Street without the 4 temples that are
grouped together here at Public Square Street. The colony's main Tin
Hau Temple was built on the shores more than 100 years ago. Land
reclamation has forced it to move inland, but Yaumatei's boat people
hike to it very often to worship their sea gods, particularly Tin Hau,
patron and protector of fisher men.
Another
attraction of Temple Street are the fabulous temples built in honor of
Shing Wong, a spiritual magistrate well known around Hong Kong, and the
temples dedicated to Tei and Kuan Yim, the Chinese gods of earth and
mercy.
