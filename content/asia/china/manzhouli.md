Manzhouli sits in the far northeast of Inner Mongolia, a small modern city on the main border crossing from China into Russia. It is an unlikely place to visit, but that is exactly what makes it interesting — a Chinese town where Russian is widely spoken, Russian cafes line the streets, and the Mongolian grasslands start just outside the city limits.

The main draw is the landscape. The grasslands surrounding Manzhouli are easily accessible and genuinely spectacular — vast, open steppe stretching to the horizon. Dalai Lake, about 30 kilometres from the city, is one of the largest lakes in northern China and a good base for a night or two in a yurt during the summer months. The lake area has seen some tourist development, but the countryside beyond it remains wide open and tranquil.

The town itself is compact and centered on a main square, with avenues of shops and malls radiating outward. The Russian influence is everywhere — many Russians cross the border to buy Chinese goods, and the result is a town where you will find as many signs in Cyrillic as in Chinese. The Russian restaurants and bars are worth seeking out.

Getting to Manzhouli takes some effort. The most common route is by train via Harbin, a journey of about sixteen hours. Once in town, you will need to hire a taxi to reach the grasslands or Dalai Lake — fares are negotiable but tend to run high since there is little competition.
