An hour by high-speed train from Shanghai, Suzhou has been China's city of gardens for a thousand years. The classical gardens here — ten of them listed as UNESCO World Heritage Sites — are the reason most people come, and they are worth the journey. Packed into small plots behind whitewashed walls, each one is an exercise in illusion: miniature hills, reflecting pools, and moon gates that make a quarter-acre feel like a landscape.

The city itself is built on a grid of canals cut during the Tang and Song dynasties. Much of that infrastructure survives in the old town, where narrow lanes run alongside water and stone bridges cross at regular intervals. It's quieter than people expect, especially away from the main tourist circuits around Guanqian Street. The pace is leisurely in a way that Shanghai, forty minutes away, emphatically is not.

Suzhou has been prosperous for most of its history — famous for silk, tea, and the refinement of its scholars and merchants. That prosperity shows in the quality of the old buildings, the number of intact gardens, and the general sense that the city has always had money to spend on aesthetics. It also shows in the food, which is sweeter and more delicate than northern Chinese cuisine, with particular strengths in fresh fish from Taihu Lake and seasonal vegetables.

The modern city beyond the old canal district is large and industrial, and not worth much attention. Stay focused on the historic core and you have a city that more than justifies the detour from Shanghai.
