Ayia Napa sits on the southeastern tip of Cyprus, a former fishing village that transformed into one of Europe's most famous party destinations. In summer the population swells from under a thousand to tens of thousands, drawn by turquoise water, white sand beaches, and a nightlife scene that rivals Ibiza.

But there is more here than clubs. The coastline east toward Cape Greco is spectacular -- rocky coves, sea caves, and crystal-clear water that feels more like the Caribbean than the Mediterranean. The 16th-century monastery in the town centre is a quiet counterpoint to the noise of The Square. And just up the road, Protaras offers a slightly calmer version of the same beaches.

The main hub is The Square, where bars, restaurants, and shops cluster together. Nissi Beach and Makronissos Beach are the big draws during the day, while the strip comes alive after dark. The area around Paralimni, 5 km north, serves as the actual administrative centre of the district.

Most visitors come between June and September, when the weather is hot and the town is at full tilt. Spring and autumn are quieter and still warm enough for swimming -- a good bet if you want the beaches without the crowds.
