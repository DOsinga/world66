---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Timor-Leste is not a country you visit for its infrastructure. You come for the diving, the mountains, and the chance to see a place that most travellers have never heard of. What it offers is real.

**Diving and marine life** are the headline attraction. The waters off Dili and Atauro Island are part of the Coral Triangle, with some of the highest marine biodiversity on earth. You can dive directly off Dili's beach at sites like K41 and Pertamina Pier, encountering soft corals, pygmy seahorses, nudibranchs, and large pelagics within minutes of entry. Atauro Island, 25 km offshore by boat, has been called one of the richest reef environments ever studied. Whale sharks, dolphins, and manta rays are regularly spotted.

**Atauro Island** is worth a trip on its own merits beyond the diving — a relaxed volcanic island with fishing villages, good hiking, cold Sagamore beer, and guesthouses run by local families. Day trips are easy; staying a few nights is better.

**Jaco Island**, at the country's eastern tip, is an uninhabited protected island accessible only by local fishing boat. The beach and water are genuinely stunning. Entry requires a permit and coordination with the local community at Tutuala — arrange this in advance or on arrival at Tutuala village. Swimming across the narrow channel to the island is not recommended due to currents.

**Mount Ramelau (Tatamailau)** at 2,963 m is the country's highest peak. The standard approach is a night hike from the highland village of Hatubolu, arriving at the summit for sunrise over the surrounding ranges and, on clear mornings, a view stretching to the coast. The trail is manageable for fit walkers; guides are available in Hatubolu.

**Coffee country** in the highlands around Ermera and Aileu is where the majority of Timor-Leste's coffee is grown — mostly organic Arabica, cultivated on small family farms since Portuguese times. The Timor hybrid, a natural cross between Arabica and Robusta discovered here in the 1920s, is now used in breeding programmes worldwide. Plantation visits and cupping sessions can be arranged, and buying directly from farmers or cooperatives supports communities directly.

**Dili** itself rewards a day or two. The Cristo Rei statue (similar to Rio's, on a headland east of town), the Portuguese-era waterfront, the Resistance Museum documenting the independence struggle, and the central market are the main draws. The diving from Dili's shoreline is surprisingly good.

**Baucau**, the second city, has a different character — a hilltop Portuguese-built upper town with a large colonial pousada (now a hotel), a natural swimming pool, and a more temperate climate than the coast. The drive from Dili along the north coast road is scenic.
