---
title: "Languages"
type: section
---

Timor-Leste has two official languages: **Tetum** and **Portuguese**. Both are required in government, courts, and education, though in practice Tetum is the language most Timorese use daily and the one that actually binds the country together.

Tetum developed as a lingua franca between the island's many indigenous language groups, and its importance grew further during the Indonesian occupation when it became a marker of national identity — the one language that was neither the coloniser's nor the occupier's. It has absorbed Portuguese loanwords over the centuries, and more continue to arrive as the official language policy takes hold.

Portuguese is spoken fluently by older Timorese who were educated before 1975, and is now taught in schools again since independence. The younger generation is rebuilding this connection, though progress is uneven — outside Dili, Portuguese is rare in everyday conversation.

**Bahasa Indonesia** remains widely understood, particularly by people aged 30 and above, educated during the occupation. It's useful in informal contexts and in areas close to the West Timor border.

**English** is spoken at hotels, dive centres, NGOs, and among younger professionals in Dili. Outside the capital, don't count on it.

At least 16 indigenous languages are spoken in Timor-Leste, several of them mutually unintelligible. Mambai (highlands), Makasae (east), Kemak (northwest), and Bunak (south-centre) are among the most widely spoken. In rural areas, you will often be in a community where neither Tetum nor Portuguese is anyone's first language, though most adults will have some Tetum.

Learning a few words of Tetum — *obrigadu* (thank you, male speaker), *obrigada* (female speaker), *bondia* (good morning), *hau hakarak* (I would like) — will be appreciated and opens doors.
