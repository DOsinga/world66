---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Timor-Leste's population is around 1.3 million, one of the youngest demographics in Asia — more than half the population is under 25. The country is ethnically diverse: the majority are Austronesian and Papuan peoples, broadly grouped under the Timorese identity. A small Chinese community has been present for centuries, mainly in trade.

Catholicism is central to daily life. Over 97% of the population is Catholic, a legacy of Portuguese colonisation and — during the Indonesian occupation — a faith that served as a marker of identity and resistance. Churches are full on Sundays; religious festivals punctuate the calendar. The relationship with the Church is deep and genuine, not merely inherited.

Tetum is the national language alongside Portuguese. Most Timorese in Dili can manage some English, especially younger people who have grown up post-independence. In rural areas, you'll encounter one of the country's 16 indigenous languages — Mambai, Makasae, and Kemak among the most widely spoken. Indonesian (Bahasa Indonesia) is understood by the generation educated under occupation, which remains useful in some contexts.

The occupation left marks that haven't fully healed. Families lost members; communities were displaced. Timorese talk about this period directly when it comes up, without bitterness toward individual visitors but with clarity about what happened. Be a good listener.

Culturally, traditional animist beliefs persist underneath the Catholic surface — sacred houses (uma lulik), ancestral objects, and ritual observances remain important in village life. Tais, handwoven cloth with distinctive geometric patterns, is the most visible cultural expression: worn at ceremonies, sold in markets, and given as gifts. Each region has its own patterns and colour combinations.
