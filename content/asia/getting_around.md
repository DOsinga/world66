---
title: "Getting Around in Asia"
type: section
---

Asia is enormous, and how you get around depends entirely on which part you are in. The good news is that transport options have exploded in the last two decades. The bad news is that border crossings can still be unpredictable, and distances that look short on a map often take much longer than you expect.

**Flying** is the most practical way to cover long distances. Budget carriers have transformed Asian travel — AirAsia, Scoot, IndiGo, and Lion Air connect hundreds of cities at prices that can beat a bus ticket. The major regional hubs are [Singapore](/asia/singapore), Bangkok, Kuala Lumpur, and Dubai. For Northeast Asia, [Tokyo](/asia/japan/tokyo), Seoul, and Shanghai serve as gateways. Book early for the best fares, and watch your baggage allowance — that is where budget airlines make their money.

**Trains** are some of the best in the world, and some of the worst. The Trans-Siberian from Moscow to [Beijing](/asia/china/beijing) or Vladivostok remains one of the great rail journeys. [Japan](/asia/japan)'s Shinkansen network is fast, punctual, and a genuine pleasure. [China](/asia/china)'s high-speed rail now covers over 40,000 kilometers and can get you from Beijing to Shanghai in four and a half hours. [India](/asia/india)'s railway network is vast and cheap, though often slow and crowded — it is also one of the best ways to see the country. Southeast Asian trains are generally slower but scenic, particularly the routes through [Sri Lanka](/asia/sri_lanka) and [Vietnam](/asia/vietnam).

**Buses** fill the gaps trains do not reach. In [Turkey](/asia/turkey), [Iran](/asia/iran), and much of Southeast Asia, long-distance buses are comfortable and affordable. In places like [Laos](/asia/laos), [Nepal](/asia/nepal), and parts of Central Asia, they may be the only overland option — and the roads can be rough. Overnight buses save on accommodation but vary wildly in quality; the VIP buses in [Thailand](/asia/thailand) are excellent, while others are best endured with low expectations.

**Boats** are essential in island Southeast Asia. Ferries connect the islands of [Indonesia](/asia/indonesia), the [Philippines](/asia/philippines), and [Thailand](/asia/thailand). Speed boats and slow boats run the Mekong between [Laos](/asia/laos) and [Thailand](/asia/thailand). In some places, particularly remote Indonesian islands, boats run on loose schedules — flexibility helps.

**Driving yourself** is straightforward in [Japan](/asia/japan), [South Korea](/asia/south_korea), and the Gulf states, but in most of South and Southeast Asia it ranges from challenging to inadvisable. Traffic in [India](/asia/india), [Vietnam](/asia/vietnam), and [Indonesia](/asia/indonesia) follows its own logic, and a hired driver is often cheaper and safer than a rental. Ride-hailing apps — Grab in Southeast Asia, Careem in the Middle East — have made getting around cities far easier than it used to be.
