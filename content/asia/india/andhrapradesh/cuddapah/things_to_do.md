Cuddapah's sights are modest in scale but varied in character — colonial-era graves, Sufi shrines, a Gothic cathedral, and a park fill out a half-day of wandering. The Seven Roads junction in the town centre is itself a landmark worth orienting from.

### Seven Roads Junction

The city's defining landmark is the point where seven streets converge in the middle of town. Roads arrive from the old bus stand, the Chennur bus stand area, Palem Papaiah Street (the building materials bazaar), the government hospital road, and several others. The junction marks the boundary between the old bazaar quarter and the newer Madras Road development. Vijaya Hotel, a popular vegetarian restaurant, occupies a clock-tower building right at the split.

### Shrines of Cuddapah

Several Sufi dargahs in Cuddapah draw visitors of all faiths. The most visited are the Hazrath Amin Pir Saab Dargah Complex and the Hazrath Shah Mir Saab Dargah. The Ali Murad Saab Dargah was historically a focus of communal prayer during cholera outbreaks — residents regarded the saint as a protector of the town. The Rafeeq Shah Vali Saab Dargah rounds out the main cluster. These shrines are woven into the social fabric of Rayalaseema well beyond Cuddapah itself.

### St Mary's Cathedral

A large Gothic-style Catholic cathedral in the Mariapuram neighbourhood, St Mary's is one of the more architecturally striking churches in Andhra Pradesh. It opens around 9am and is worth a visit even if you aren't Catholic — the stonework and vaulted interior are well maintained. Address: St Mary's Cathedral, Mariapuram.

### Nehru Park

A family park with lawns and play equipment, busiest in the early morning and late afternoon. A good place to rest if you are passing through with children.

### Old Cemetery

Near Nirmala Convent, a colonial-era British cemetery known locally as *Dorala Gorilu* ("Europeans' graves"). It holds the graves of British officers stationed in Cuddapah during the Raj, including some killed during a Pathan uprising centred on the area now called BKM Street. The headstones are weathered but legible, and the site gives a quiet sense of the town's history under British administration.
