Lepakshi is a small village in the Anantapur district of southern Andhra Pradesh, about 120 kilometres north of Bangalore and a short detour off the main highway to Hyderabad. It would be easy to drive past — but doing so would mean missing one of the finest examples of Vijayanagara architecture in India.

The centrepiece is the Veerabhadra Temple, built in the 16th century under the patronage of two brothers, Virupanna and Veeranna, who served as governors under the Vijayanagara Empire. The temple is a masterpiece of stone carving: its pillars are covered in intricate sculptures of gods, celestial musicians, and mythological scenes, and the ceilings carry some of the largest and best-preserved Vijayanagara-era murals in existence. One chamber's ceiling spans an enormous area in a single painting — extraordinary by any measure.

Before you reach the temple, you pass a monolithic Nandi bull, one of the largest in India, carved from a single granite boulder and sitting in calm attendance at the edge of the village. The main temple complex holds a massive Shivalinga sheltered beneath the hood of a seven-headed stone serpent, set in the open air rather than enclosed in a shrine.

Lepakshi is easy to visit as a day trip from Bangalore, and most people do exactly that. But the place rewards slowness — arriving early before the tour groups, sitting with the carved columns, noticing the details. There are no crowds to fight, no queues, and the entry is free.
