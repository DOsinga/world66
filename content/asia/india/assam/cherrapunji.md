Cherrapunji (also known as Sohra) holds the record for the world's highest annual rainfall — over 11,000 mm in a typical year, with a single-year record of 22,987 mm — making it one of the wettest places on earth. Despite technically being in [Meghalaya](/asia/india/meghalaya) rather than Assam, it is easily visited from Guwahati and is a common add-on for travellers in the region.

The town sits on a plateau in the Khasi Hills, with dramatic views south over the plains of Bangladesh. Warm, moisture-laden air off the Bay of Bengal hits the hills and is forced rapidly upward, generating near-constant cloud and rain for much of the year. The best time to visit is October to May, when skies occasionally clear and the waterfalls fed by the monsoon are still flowing strongly.

The most unusual attraction is the living root bridges — footbridges made by training the aerial roots of rubber fig trees across rivers and gorges over many decades. The Khasi people of the area have built dozens of these across the valleys below the plateau; the double-decker root bridge near the village of Nongriat is the most celebrated. A hike to see them involves a steep descent and around 3,500 steps each way.

Cherrapunji was established as a British administrative centre and later as a hill station, before being largely superseded by Shillong. The Welsh missionary presence left a strong Christian imprint on the Khasi community that persists today.
