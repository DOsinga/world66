Hansi is a small city in Haryana's Hisar district, sitting about 26 km east of Hisar on National Highway 10. It's not a major tourist destination, but it has more history packed into it than you'd guess from the highway. The town was once grander than Hisar itself — a fortified centre that mattered to empires from the Tomars and Prithviraj Chauhan through the Mughals, Marathas, Sikhs, and finally the British.

The main draw is Asigarh Fort, a 30-acre square fort that gave the city its older name. It's said to date back to Prithviraj Chauhan, and was later used as a sword-manufacturing base — blades from here reportedly reached Arab markets. The entry gate was built by George Thomas, an Irish sailor who improbably became an independent ruler of the region in the late 18th century, with Hansi as his capital. The fort was declared a Protected Monument of National Importance in 1937 and remains in reasonable shape.

The town also has Jain connections: a large hoard of Jain bronze idols was discovered here in 1982, some dating to the Gupta period (4–6th century CE), apparently buried before a medieval invasion. Jain statues of Mahavira and the Buddha stand in front of the fort.

Hansi is known locally for its *pera*, a milk-based sweet sold across the region. Worth trying if you pass through.

The city has five historic entry gates — Delhi Gate (east), Hisar Gate (west), Gosain Gate (northwest), Barsi Gate (south) and Umra Gate (southwest) — an unusual feature that gives the old town some navigational character. The terrain is semi-arid, receiving about 46 cm of rain a year.

Most visitors come as a day trip from Hisar or while passing through on NH-10. There's no real reason to stay overnight unless you're doing deep heritage research, but the fort is worth an hour if you're in the area.
