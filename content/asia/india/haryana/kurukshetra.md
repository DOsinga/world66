Kurukshetra is one of the most sacred sites in Hinduism — the battlefield where the Pandavas and Kauravas fought the war at the heart of the Mahabharata, and where Lord Krishna delivered the Bhagavad Gita to Arjuna. Few places carry such concentrated spiritual weight, and pilgrims arrive from across India to walk ground that ancient tradition regards as the origin of dharma itself.

The district lies in the northern state of Haryana, about 150 km northwest of Delhi. Its flat agricultural plains hold more than 360 temples and 860 pilgrimage sites connected to the Mahabharata and the Gita. The region is also known as Dharmakshetra — Land of Dharma — a name given in the very first verse of the Bhagavad Gita. According to the Vedas, Kurukshetra once stretched across an area of roughly 48 by 128 km, encompassing dozens of sacred tanks and shrines.

The town is compact and unhurried, oriented almost entirely around religious life. The great Brahma Sarovar tank — one of the largest sacred reservoirs in India — draws enormous crowds during solar eclipses, when a dip in its waters is believed to be especially auspicious. Beyond the temples, the Archaeological Museum holds artefacts from excavations across the region, offering a quieter counterpoint to the devotional energy outside.
