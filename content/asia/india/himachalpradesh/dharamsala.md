Dharamsala is a hill town in the **Kangra Valley** of Himachal Pradesh, stretched up the forested slopes of the **Dhauladhar** range at elevations between around 1,250 and 1,800 metres. Before 1960 it was a sleepy British-era cantonment and tea-growing district headquarters; since the **Dalai Lama** and some 80,000 Tibetan refugees settled here after fleeing the 1959 uprising in Lhasa, it has become one of the most important centres of Tibetan Buddhism outside Tibet itself — and one of the most unusual small towns in India.

The town splits into two quite distinct parts. **Lower Dharamsala**, down in the valley, is a dusty, unremarkable Indian administrative town. Almost everything a traveller comes for is in the upper settlement, **McLeod Ganj**, about 10 km (and 500 metres of altitude) further up the hill, where the **Tibetan government-in-exile** is based and where the Dalai Lama's private residence and the **Tsuglagkhang temple complex** sit on a hilltop at the centre of town. A visit to the main temple, the **Namgyal Monastery** and the small but excellent **Tibet Museum** below it gives a quick introduction to Tibetan Buddhism and to the history of the 1959 exodus. If the Dalai Lama is in residence and gives public teachings, visitors can attend — in normal times, these are free, and word gets out well in advance.

Beyond McLeod Ganj, the surrounding hills offer some of the best short walking in northern India. The popular **Triund** trek climbs steeply up to a grassy ridge at about 2,975 metres for extraordinary views of the Dhauladhar peaks — a long day out from McLeod or an overnight camp; more ambitious walkers continue to **Lahesh Cave** and the snow line. Closer to town, the **Bhagsu waterfall**, **Dharamkot** village, and **Dal Lake** are all reachable on foot. Below the hills, the **Church of St John in the Wilderness** — an 1850s neo-Gothic chapel in the pine forest that holds the grave of Lord Elgin, a Viceroy of India — is one of the strangest and loveliest colonial leftovers in the region. Further afield, the medieval **Masroor rock-cut temples** and the flaming-rock Hindu pilgrimage site of **Jwalamukhi** are rewarding day trips.

McLeod Ganj is also one of the main centres in India for **Tibetan Buddhism courses**, **meditation retreats**, **yoga**, and cheap but excellent Tibetan and Indian food. Expect backpackers, monks in red robes, and a cosmopolitan café culture that feels closer to Kathmandu than to anywhere else in India.
