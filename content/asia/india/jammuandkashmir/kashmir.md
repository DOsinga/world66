The Kashmir Valley is a broad, lake-studded bowl between the Himalayas and the Pir Panjal range — one of the most beautiful landscapes in Asia, and one of the most politically complicated. At around 1,600 m, the valley runs roughly 135 km from northwest to southeast, its floor intensively cultivated and its edges rising steeply to snow-covered peaks. The Mughal emperors called it Paradise on Earth and built gardens here to prove the point. It is hard to argue with them.

[Srinagar](/asia/india/jammuandkashmir/srinagar) is the valley's capital and the main base for visitors. It sits on Dal Lake, ringed by mountains, with wooden mosques, Mughal gardens, and the famous houseboats giving the city a character completely unlike anywhere else in India. Most travellers spend several days here and use it as a jumping-off point for the wider valley.

The day trips from Srinagar are genuinely excellent. [Gulmarg](/asia/india/jammuandkashmir/gulmarg), 55 km west at 2,690 m, offers India's best skiing in winter and extraordinary mountain panoramas year-round from the Gondola cable car. [Pahalgam](/asia/india/jammuandkashmir/pahalgam), 96 km east at 2,130 m, is the main trekking base — pine forests, alpine meadows, and the Lidder river, with routes up to Kolahoi Glacier and beyond.

The valley has been disputed between India and Pakistan since Partition in 1947, and the 1990s insurgency effectively shut down tourism for a decade. Visitors have returned in large numbers since the 2000s, but it is worth checking the current security situation before travelling and staying aware of developments while you are there.
