Ladakh — "land of high passes" — is a high-altitude desert region sandwiched between the Karakoram to the north and the Himalaya to the south. Elevations rarely drop below 3,000 m, and the landscape has more in common with the Tibetan plateau than with the rest of India. The region is called "Little Tibet" for good reason: 82% of the population is Buddhist, prayer wheels and chortens mark every village, and monasteries built on rocky outcrops dominate the valley views.

[Leh](/asia/india/jammuandkashmir/ladakh/leh), the capital, sits at 3,521 m in the Indus Valley. It is the base for most travellers — a compact old town with a bazaar, guesthouses, trekking operators, and a palace overlooking everything. The monasteries scattered along the Indus and its tributaries are the main draw: Hemis (the richest), Thiksey (the most photogenic), Alchi (the most historically significant for its medieval murals), and Lamayuru (the oldest, in a dramatic moonscape setting) are the classics.

Beyond the monastery circuit, Ladakh offers some of the best adventure travel in Asia. The Markha Valley Trek and the Zanskar routes are among the finest multi-day walks in the Himalayas. The Indus and Zanskar rivers offer serious whitewater rafting. Nubra Valley (via the 5,359 m Khardung La pass), Pangong Lake (the extraordinary blue lake on the India-China border), and Tso Moriri are standard jeep excursions.

The accessible season runs from roughly June to October, when both overland roads are open. Outside this window, the only way in is by air. Altitude sickness is a genuine concern — acclimatise properly before exerting yourself.
