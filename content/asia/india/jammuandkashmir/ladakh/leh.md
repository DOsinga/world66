Leh is the capital of Ladakh, a high-altitude desert town sitting at 3,521 m in the Indus Valley, ringed by mountains exceeding 6,000 m. It was once a major stopping point on the Silk Road between Central Asia and India; today it is the base for exploring one of the most dramatic landscapes on earth.

The old town climbs steeply from the main bazaar to Leh Palace, a nine-storey fortified palace modelled on the Potala in Lhasa that dominates the skyline. The lanes below are full of Buddhist shrines, chortens, and prayer wheels. The new town stretching south has all the practical infrastructure — guesthouses, restaurants, trekking agencies, jeep hire.

The immediate surrounding area is extraordinary: monasteries perch on every ridge and hilltop along the Indus Valley, from the vast complex at Hemis (40 km east) to the medieval murals of Alchi (70 km west) and the ancient monastery at Lamayuru (125 km west). The rivers offer serious whitewater rafting. The high passes and valleys are among the finest trekking terrain in the Himalayas.

The altitude hits most visitors immediately. Plan to rest for at least the first two days, drink water constantly, eat lightly, and avoid alcohol until acclimatised. Rushing into physical activity before adjusting properly can bring on serious altitude sickness.

Leh is accessible by air from Delhi (highly recommended) or by two spectacular overland routes — the Manali–Leh Highway and the Srinagar–Leh Highway — both open only from roughly June to October.
