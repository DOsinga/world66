Madhya Pradesh — "the middle state" — is the geographic heart of India and, after the split-off of Chhattisgarh in 2000, still its second-largest state. For all its size it is strangely under-visited, which is part of the appeal. Two of India's best tiger reserves, a World Heritage Site of erotic temple carvings, and the stupas at Sanchi — one of the oldest standing structures in the subcontinent — are all here, with a fraction of the crowds of Rajasthan or Uttar Pradesh.

The star attraction is [Khajuraho](/asia/india/madhyapradesh/khajuraho), whose cluster of 10th-century temples is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Only a small portion of the carvings are the famous erotic friezes, but they are extraordinary — the rest is equally fine Chandela-era stonework in a garden setting. Khajuraho is isolated but has its own airport and is easily reached as a two- or three-day stop.

The capital [Bhopal](/asia/india/madhyapradesh/bhopal), with its parks, lakes, and Taj-ul-Masajid (one of the largest mosques in India), is a good base for exploring [Sanchi](/asia/india/madhyapradesh/sanchi) — a Buddhist site where Emperor Ashoka built the Great Stupa in the 3rd century BC. The site remains one of the most atmospheric historic landscapes in India.

For wildlife, **Bandhavgarh** and **Kanha** national parks in the east of the state have the highest tiger-spotting success rates in India. Jeep safaris run in winter (October–June); accommodation near the park gates ranges from basic forest rest-houses to very expensive lodges. Bandhavgarh is smaller and busier; Kanha is larger and feels wilder.

In the west, the abandoned hill-fort city of **Mandu** — a 15th-century Afghan-architecture capital on a plateau above the Narmada valley — is one of the great off-the-beaten-track sights of India. The fort of **Gwalior** in the north and the ruined temples of **Orchha** are also worth the detour.
