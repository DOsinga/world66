Alibag is a small coastal town about 100 km south of Mumbai on the **Konkan coast**, the long narrow belt of palm-fringed shoreline that runs between the Arabian Sea and the Western Ghats. For most of its history it was a minor fishing port; in the late 17th century it was developed by **Sarkhel Kanhoji Angre**, the famously effective Maratha admiral who harassed European shipping along the Konkan coast for decades. Today it is best known as Mumbai's favourite weekend beach escape — a cluster of low-key beaches, old Portuguese and Maratha forts, and the sort of second-home culture that sees private jets landing on neighbouring airstrips.

The town's signature sight is the **Kolaba Fort**, built by Shivaji in 1662 on a small tidal island just off Alibag's main beach. At low tide you can walk out to it across the wet sand; at high tide it is surrounded by water. South of Alibag, the more impressive **Janjira Fort** at Murud sits on a rock island out in the sea — the only Maratha coastal fort never captured, despite repeated sieges — and can be reached by small sailing boat from the mainland. The Alibag coast itself is strung with a series of quiet beaches: **Alibag Beach** and **Nagaon** closest to the town, **Kihim** and **Akshi** with cleaner sand further north, **Kashid** (the prettiest) halfway to Murud.

The easiest way to reach Alibag from Mumbai is by **RoRo ferry** from the Gateway of India to Mandwa (about an hour), followed by a short bus ride. The overland route via Panvel is longer (2–3 hours by road). Avoid the full monsoon (June to September): the sea is rough, ferries stop, and most of the beaches become unswimmable.
