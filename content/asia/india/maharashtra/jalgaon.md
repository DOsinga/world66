Jalgaon is a prosperous market town in the Khandesh region of northern Maharashtra, best known as the most convenient base for visiting the Ajanta Caves — one of India's supreme artistic and archaeological treasures. The town sits on the main Mumbai–Kolkata rail line, making it easy to reach from both coasts, and a steady stream of travellers passes through on their way to the caves.

The town itself is a working commercial centre rather than a tourist destination. Jalgaon district produces around two-thirds of Maharashtra's banana crop, and the markets along the main bazaar streets reflect that agricultural wealth. It's a functional, unpretentious place — somewhere to arrive, rest, and head out to the caves early the next morning.

The Ajanta Caves lie about 60 km northeast of town, a UNESCO World Heritage Site comprising 30 rock-cut Buddhist sanctuaries carved into a horseshoe-shaped gorge. The paintings inside — some of the finest surviving examples of ancient Indian art — date from the 2nd century BCE to the 6th century CE. The caves were abandoned and forgotten for over a millennium before a British officer stumbled upon them in 1819. Allow a full day; the site requires serious walking and deserves unhurried attention.

Ellora, with its even larger complex of Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain cave temples, lies about 150 km to the south near Aurangabad — a longer excursion but worth it if time allows.
