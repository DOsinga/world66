Nashik is one of Hinduism's holiest cities, 170 kilometres northeast of Mumbai in the foothills of the Western Ghats. The Godavari River rises in the nearby hills and flows through the city's ghats, and this is one of the four sites where the Kumbh Mela — the world's largest religious gathering — takes place every twelve years. Even in quieter years, the ghats are busy with pilgrims bathing, priests performing rituals, and ashes being scattered.

The old town around the Ram Kund tank and the Panchavati quarter is where Nashik's religious significance concentrates. The Kalaram Temple and Sundar Narayan Temple are the most striking of the many temples here. Hindus believe Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana spent part of their exile at Nashik, which gives the Panchavati area its special resonance.

Outside the religious sphere, Nashik has become India's unofficial wine capital. The surrounding hills have a climate suited to viticulture, and Sula Vineyards (India's largest winery) and a growing number of others run tours and tastings within easy reach of the city. The annual SulaFest in January is the country's best-known wine festival.

Nashik is also a practical gateway — to Shirdi (the Sai Baba pilgrimage town, a day trip northeast), Trimbakeshwar with its Jyotirlinga temple, and the 2,000-year-old Pandavleni cave complex a few kilometres south of the city.
