Nagaland is a small hill state in India's far northeast, wedged between Assam, Manipur, and Myanmar. It is home to more than a dozen Naga tribes, each with distinct languages, weaving traditions, and histories — and the differences between them are real and visible, not just a brochure claim. The landscape is steep and green: forested ridges, terraced fields, and valley towns connected by mountain roads that take longer than the map suggests.

Kohima, the capital, sits at around 1,500 metres and has a colonial-era war cemetery that is genuinely moving — the site of one of the most brutal battles of the Second World War, where Allied forces stopped the Japanese advance into India in 1944. The cemetery is immaculate and the inscriptions on the headstones are worth reading slowly. Nearby, the Nagaland State Museum gives a good overview of tribal cultures.

Dimapur, down in the lowlands, is the state's commercial hub and the only point with a rail connection to the rest of India. It's not a destination in itself but it's where most visitors arrive. The ruins of the medieval Kachari kingdom — stone pillars scattered through the town — are a quiet reminder that this region had complex histories long before colonialism.

Dzukou Valley, behind Japfu Peak south of Kohima, is one of the best treks in the northeast. The valley blooms with lilies in summer and is cool and uncrowded even by regional standards. The trail up takes about three hours; basic huts allow overnight stays.

The state requires an Inner Line Permit for all non-Naga Indian citizens and a Protected Area Permit for foreigners — both are obtainable online or at state entry points.
