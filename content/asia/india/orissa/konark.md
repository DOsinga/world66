**Konark** is famous for its magnificent 13th-century Sun Temple, constructed by Narasimha Deva I of the Ganga dynasty. Standing in solitary splendour amongst the sand dunes near the Bay of Bengal, the temple is the most striking example of Kalinga architecture and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The temple was conceived as a giant chariot with 24 intricately carved wheels at its base and seven horses pulling it — a symbolic representation of the Sun God's chariot crossing the sky. The wheels themselves function as sundials, and the carvings across every surface depict scenes from daily life, mythology, and nature with astonishing detail. Three images of the sun — morning, midday, and setting — are positioned so that actual sunlight falls on them at the corresponding time of day.

The erotic sculptures that adorn parts of the temple walls attract plenty of attention, but they are only a fraction of the whole. The bulk of the carving depicts elephants, musicians, dancers, hunting scenes, and geometric patterns of extraordinary precision. What survives today is the audience hall and a detached dancing hall; the main tower collapsed centuries ago, but even in ruin the scale and ambition of the place is breathtaking.
