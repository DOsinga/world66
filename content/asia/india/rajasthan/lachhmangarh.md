Lachhmangarh (also spelled Laxmangarh) is a small town in the Shekhawati region, founded in 1862 by Rao Raja Laxman Singh of Sikar. It is part of the string of Shekhawati towns known for their painted havelis -- merchant mansions decorated with elaborate frescoes depicting everything from mythology to European trains and telephones.

The town's hilltop fort, built on scattered boulders, is privately owned and closed to visitors, but you can climb the ramp to a temple near the entrance for good views over the town. The real attractions are at street level: the Char Chowk Haveli has well-preserved paintings including some explicit erotic imagery, and the Rathi Family Haveli near the clock tower features delightfully over-the-top paintings of European women and British soldiers. Lachhmangarh fans of modern fiction may recognize it as the model for the hometown of Balram Halwai in Aravind Adiga's Booker Prize-winning novel The White Tiger.
