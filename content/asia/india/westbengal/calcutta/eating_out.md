---
title: "Eating Out in Calcutta"
type: section
---

Calcutta has one of the most interesting food scenes in India — not for innovation but for depth. Bengali cooking, Mughlai cuisine, and the city's own hybrid traditions (Chinese-Indian food was invented here) all coexist alongside a café culture that goes back to the colonial era.

**Bengali food** is the thing to seek out. Kewpie's Kitchen in Elgin Park is a local favourite for authentic home-style Bengali cooking — mustard fish, kosha mangsho (slow-cooked mutton), and mishti doi. You won't see many tourists there. Oh Calcutta! at The Forum on Elgin Road is the upscale version — beautifully prepared bekti meuniere and rice pudding with custard apple — with decor that matches. Aheli in the Peerless Inn does similar work in a hotel setting.

**Mughlai food** reaches its peak at Shiraz on Park Street and Park Circus: the biriyani is famous, the tikkas and kebabs cooked over charcoal. Zaraang near New Market on Sudder Street is more upscale, serving both Indian and French food; the food is excellent, the service occasionally snobby.

**Chinese** — proper Tangra Chinese, not the Indianised version — can be found in the Tangra neighbourhood east of the city centre, which remains the last functioning Chinatown in India. Kim Fa and Tung Nam are reliable. The Chinese-Indian hybrid of momos (Tibetan dumplings, called dim sum locally) is a Calcutta street food institution; there are clusters of momo stalls along Lee Road and Elgin Road.

**Bakeries:** Flury's on Park Street, open since 1927, is still the city's best patisserie — come for breakfast or afternoon tea. The Black Forest pastry is the classic order.

**Street food:** Puchka (the Calcutta version of pani puri, filled with spiced mashed potato and tamarind water) is the defining street snack. The puchkawallas around Victoria Memorial and Dalhousie Square are the most reliable.
