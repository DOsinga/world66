*Asia > India > West Bengal > Darjeeling*

Darjeeling sits at around 2,050 metres in the foothills of the eastern Himalayas, with the Kanchenjunga massif — at 8,586 metres the world's third-highest peak — dominating the northern horizon on clear days. The British built it as a hill station to escape the Bengal plains in summer, and the town retains more of that colonial character than almost anywhere else in India: Victorian buildings, church spires, a racecourse, and the famous narrow-gauge railway that winds up from the plains.

The town itself is compact and walkable. The Mall and Chowrasta, a wide promenade at the ridge top, is where everyone comes in the morning for views toward Kanchenjunga and in the evening to walk and talk. The surrounding hills are carpeted in tea gardens — Darjeeling tea is one of the world's great teas and the best first-flush pickings command extraordinary prices. You can visit estates and watch the picking and processing; Happy Valley Tea Estate is the closest to town.

The Toy Train — the Darjeeling Himalayan Railway, a UNESCO World Heritage Site — is as much an attraction as any building. The steam locomotives that still run on special services date from the 1880s. Even if you don't do the full journey from New Jalpaiguri (7 hours), riding one station up from Darjeeling to Ghoom and back is a pleasure.

**Tiger Hill**, 11 km from town, is where visitors go before dawn for the famous sunrise view over Kanchenjunga — on clear days, Everest is visible 225 km away. The road passes through Ghoom, home to one of the most venerable Tibetan Buddhist monasteries in India, the Yiga-Choling gompa. The Batasla Loop, an engineering curiosity where the toy train makes a spiral to descend 300 metres in altitude, is just below Ghoom.

The **Padmaja Naidu Himalayan Zoological Park** has the best collection of high-altitude Himalayan wildlife in India: snow leopard, red panda, Siberian tiger, Himalayan black bear, and many bird species. The adjacent **Himalayan Mountaineering Institute** traces the history of Himalayan climbing and houses an Everest museum.

October and November, and March through May, are the best times to visit: clear skies, mild temperatures, and the landscape at its most vivid. July and August bring heavy monsoon rains and cloud that obscures the mountains. December through February is cold but often beautifully clear.
