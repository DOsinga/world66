Batam is an industrial island in the Riau Archipelago, just 20 kilometres south of Singapore and reachable by ferry in about an hour. Carved out as a special economic zone in the 1970s and turbo-charged in the 90s, it has spent the last few decades absorbing factories, ports, and weekend Singaporeans escaping the high prices and tight rules of home.

For travellers, that mix produces something a bit unusual: a heavily developed island with traffic-clogged Nagoya at its centre, scattered beach resorts and golf courses on its northern and western coasts, and a string of quiet small islands hanging off the south, connected by the photogenic **Barelang Bridges**. The shopping is genuinely cheap — Batam is a free-trade zone — and the seafood at the kelong restaurants near the bridges is excellent.

Be honest about what Batam is, though. It is not a beach paradise; the better stretches of sand are at Nongsa, and the resort strips closer to town can be grim. The Nagoya Entertainment District is loud, smoky and openly oriented around the sex trade — worth knowing before you book a hotel there. There are no significant historic sites beyond the moving Vietnamese refugee camp on Galang Island, a relic of the boat-people era still tended by visiting pilgrims.

If your priority is beaches and quiet, skip Batam and go straight to **[Bintan](/asia/indonesia/bintan)** next door — better sand, more relaxed atmosphere. If you want cheap shopping, a round of golf, fresh seafood, and a quick hop across the Strait, Batam delivers exactly that and not much more.
