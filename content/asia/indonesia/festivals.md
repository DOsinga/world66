---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Indonesia's festival calendar is rich and varied — a reflection of its many cultures and religious traditions. Many of the most spectacular events are tied to specific regions.

**Nyepi** (Bali, March/April) is the Balinese Hindu New Year, and uniquely, it is celebrated in complete silence. For 24 hours the entire island shuts down — no cars, no noise, no lights after dark. Tourists are expected to stay in their accommodation. The night before, *ogoh-ogoh* — giant demonic effigies — are paraded through the streets with great noise and fire before being burned.

**Galungan** (Bali, every 210 days by the Balinese calendar) marks the victory of dharma over adharma. For ten days, the gods are believed to descend to earth. Families make offerings and tall bamboo poles called *penjor*, decorated with fruit and young coconut leaves, arch over every road. The final day, Kuningan, is for prayers and remembering ancestors.

**Kasada** (East Java, June/July) is the annual ceremony of the Tenggerese people at Mount Bromo, when offerings of vegetables, livestock, and money are thrown into the active crater. It draws thousands of Tenggerese in traditional dress and is one of the most dramatic ceremonies in Java.

**Pasola** (Sumba, February/March) is a ritual war festival on the island of [Sumba](/asia/indonesia/sumba), in which two teams of horsemen hurl blunted spears at each other. It is connected to the rice planting cycle and conducted as a way of reading omens for the harvest. Genuine, not staged for tourists.

**Waisak** (Java, May) commemorates the birth, enlightenment, and death of Buddha and is celebrated at Borobudur with candlelight processions and the release of lanterns into the sky. One of the great spectacles of Central Java.

**Independence Day** (17 August, nationwide) is celebrated across the country with ceremonies, parades, and neighbourhood games — including the greasy pole (*panjat pinang*) and tug-of-war competitions that take over village streets.

**Idul Fitri** (dates shift by the lunar calendar) marks the end of Ramadan and is the biggest holiday of the year for Indonesia's Muslim majority. Cities empty as millions travel home to their family villages; if you need to travel during this period, book transport well in advance.
