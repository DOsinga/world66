Jakarta is the capital of Indonesia and one of the largest cities in the world — a sprawling, chaotic, sinking megacity of more than 10 million people (30 million in the wider metropolitan area) on the north-west coast of Java. It is not a place that most travellers go to for pleasure. It is hot, traffic-choked, flood-prone, and short on the kind of concentrated sightseeing that rewards a day or two. But anyone spending time in Indonesia will pass through, and Jakarta has a few genuinely interesting things hidden in the noise.

The old colonial quarter, **Kota Tua** (Old Town), is the most atmospheric part of the city: the Dutch East India Company's former headquarters at **Fatahillah Square** now houses the Jakarta History Museum, the Fine Arts Museum, and the Wayang (puppet) Museum, all set around a handsome cobbled square with colonial warehouses and a draw-bridge. The **National Museum** (Museum Nasional), on Merdeka Square, is one of the best in Southeast Asia, with a world-class collection of Hindu-Buddhist sculpture, ceramics, and ethnographic material from across the archipelago. **Monas** (the National Monument), a 132-metre marble obelisk in the centre of Merdeka Square, gives a panoramic view of the city from its observation deck and houses a small diorama museum of Indonesian history in the basement.

Jakarta's real energy is in its food. The city is a melting pot of every regional Indonesian cuisine: Padang restaurants on every corner, Javanese warungs, Betawi (native Jakartan) dishes like soto betawi and kerak telor, and a booming modern café and restaurant scene in the south of the city around Senopati and Kemang. Go to a night market or a street-food cluster in Pecenongan (famous for late-night Chinese-Indonesian food) and eat your way through the options.

The Indonesian government is in the process of moving the official capital to **Nusantara** in East Kalimantan, though Jakarta will remain the country's commercial and cultural centre for the foreseeable future. The city is also sinking — parts of northern Jakarta are subsiding at up to 25 cm per year, and flooding is a regular occurrence in the wet season (November–March). Travel by taxi or ride-hail (Grab and Gojek are ubiquitous); the relatively new MRT and LRT lines have made some routes faster, but traffic remains Jakarta's defining challenge.
