Jakarta is one of the great megacities of Asia — sprawling, chaotic, relentlessly energetic, and far more rewarding than its reputation suggests. As the capital and commercial engine of the world's fourth most populous nation, it is a city that runs on ambition. Skyscrapers and colonial Dutch warehouses sit within a few kilometres of each other; luxury malls neighbour street food markets where a bowl of soto betawi costs less than a dollar.

The old city of Kota is the place to start. This was Batavia, the Dutch East India Company's Asian headquarters, and the cobblestoned Fatahillah Square still holds the bones of the colonial era: the Jakarta History Museum in the old town hall, the Fine Arts and Ceramic Museum in the former courthouse, and the Maritime Museum along the waterfront. A short walk takes you to Chinatown (Glodok), one of the oldest in Southeast Asia, where temples, spice merchants and noodle stalls crowd the narrow lanes.

Central Jakarta is defined by Merdeka Square, a vast open plaza anchored by the National Monument (Monas), whose viewing platform offers a panorama of the entire city. The surrounding precinct holds several of the city's best national museums. To the south, the upmarket neighbourhoods of Menteng and Kebayoran Baru give way to shopping districts and Jakarta's formidable restaurant scene.

The city is also a gateway. Just offshore, the Thousand Islands (Kepulauan Seribu) offer a complete change of pace — coral reefs, empty beaches and clear water within two hours of the city by boat. Inland, the hill town of Bogor and the volcanic landscapes of West Java are easy day trips.

Jakarta's infrastructure has been improving steadily: the MRT now connects the south of the city to the centre, making navigation far easier than even a few years ago. The heat is constant and the traffic legendary, but for travellers willing to engage, Jakarta delivers a visceral and distinctly Indonesian urban experience that few other cities in the region can match.
