Pontianak is the capital of West Kalimantan province on the island of Borneo, sitting almost exactly on the equator. The Tugu Khatulistiwa (Equator Monument) a few kilometres north of the city centre marks the line, and on the equinoxes in March and September you can watch your shadow disappear entirely at noon.

The city sprawls along the confluence of the Kapuas and Landak rivers, and the waterfront is still its most interesting area. Wooden boats line the riverbanks, and the old Malay kampung houses on stilts give a sense of what the whole city once looked like. The Keraton Kadriah, the old sultan's palace, sits on the riverbank and is open to visitors — it is a modest wooden structure but holds royal artefacts and gives context to Pontianak's founding in 1771.

For most travellers, Pontianak is a transit point on the way to or from the Kalimantan interior. Boats head upriver along the Kapuas — at over 1,100 kilometres, it is the longest river in Indonesia — toward Sintang and the Dayak longhouse communities deeper in Borneo. The city also sits on the overland route between the Malaysian border at Entikong and the rest of West Kalimantan. Supadio Airport has flights to Jakarta, Kuching, and other Indonesian cities.

The food here is excellent. Pontianak is known for its Chinese-Indonesian cuisine, particularly bubur pedas (spiced rice porridge) and chai kwe (fried dumplings). The night food stalls along Jalan Gajah Mada are the best place to sample the range.
