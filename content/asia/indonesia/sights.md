title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Indonesia's sights range from ancient temple complexes to active volcanoes, from orangutan forest to coral reefs. The country is too large for any single overview to do justice, but a few things stand out above the rest.

**Borobudur** is the largest Buddhist monument in the world, a 9th-century stone pyramid on the plains of Central Java near [Yogyakarta](/asia/indonesia/yogyakarta). At sunrise, when mist lies in the valleys below and the silhouettes of stupas emerge from the light, it is genuinely one of the great sights of Southeast Asia. Go early, before the tour buses arrive.

**Prambanan** temple complex, 18 kilometres east of Yogyakarta, is the Hindu counterpart — a cluster of soaring stone towers dedicated to Shiva, Vishnu, and Brahma, built around the same time as Borobudur. The Ramayana ballet is performed here on full-moon nights against the illuminated temple backdrop. Both Borobudur and Prambanan are UNESCO World Heritage Sites.

**Mount Bromo** in East Java is the most visited active volcano in Indonesia. The classic view — the smoking caldera inside a vast sand sea ringed by higher peaks — is reached before dawn by jeep and then on horseback or foot. The Tengger Massif around it is worth exploring beyond the crater rim.

**Komodo National Park**, between [Flores](/asia/indonesia/flores) and [Sumbawa](/asia/indonesia/sumbawa), is the only place on earth where the Komodo dragon survives in the wild. These are large, genuinely intimidating animals — up to three metres long — and the national park visit, with its rangers and guided walks through dry savanna, is memorable. The surrounding waters also have exceptional diving.

**Kelimutu**, on [Flores](/asia/indonesia/flores), is a dormant volcano with three crater lakes that each hold water of a different colour — turquoise, dark green, and black-brown, depending on chemical conditions. The colours change over years. It is quiet, beautiful, and completely unlike anything else in the country.

**Tana Toraja**, in the highlands of [Sulawesi](/asia/indonesia/sulawesi), is known for its elaborate funeral ceremonies, in which the body of the deceased is kept for months or years while the family saves enough money for a proper send-off. The funerals involve buffalo sacrifice, traditional music, and communal feasting; if one is happening when you visit, you will likely be welcome to observe.

**Raja Ampat**, off the northwest tip of Papua, is considered by many divers to be the richest marine environment on earth — an extraordinary density of species, extraordinary visibility, and relatively few visitors. It is remote and expensive to reach, but the diving and snorkelling here is in a different league from anywhere else in Indonesia.

**Ubud**, in central [Bali](/asia/indonesia/bali), remains the island's cultural heartland despite the boutique hotels and wellness retreats. The rice terraces around the town, particularly at Tegallalang, the temples, and the dance performances are the reason people keep coming back. The Pura Besakih temple complex on the slopes of Gunung Agung is Bali's holiest site.
