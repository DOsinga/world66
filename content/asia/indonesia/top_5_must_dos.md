---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's in Indonesia"
type: section
---

**1. Watch Sunrise from Borobudur**

Get there before 5am and climb to the upper terrace before the light comes up. As the sky turns from black to orange, the silhouettes of 72 stupas emerge from the mist, and the volcanoes on the horizon take shape. Borobudur is the largest Buddhist monument in the world, built in the 9th century from two million stone blocks, and nothing prepares you for the scale of it. Go on a clear morning in the dry season. Skip the afternoon tour buses.

**2. Dive or Snorkel Raja Ampat**

In the remote northwest corner of Papua, Raja Ampat holds what many marine biologists consider the most biodiverse reef system on earth. The fish density is extraordinary — sharks, rays, schooling fish, pygmy seahorses, walking sharks. You can snorkel straight from the guesthouses on Arborek or Kri island and see more in an hour than most reef dives anywhere else. It is not cheap and not easy to get to, but it is genuinely unlike anywhere else in Indonesia.

**3. Attend a Funeral in Tana Toraja**

The highlands of [Sulawesi](/asia/indonesia/sulawesi) around Rantepao are home to the Toraja people, whose funeral ceremonies can last for days and involve buffalo sacrifice, traditional music, thousands of guests, and the body of the deceased presiding over the feast. These are genuine community events, not performances, and visitors are usually welcome if they dress appropriately and bring a small gift. The June–August dry season is when most funerals are held. Nothing else in Indonesia is quite so frank about death, or so communal about grief.

**4. Trek to the Crater Rim of Mount Bromo**

The approach is theatrical: a pre-dawn jeep ride across the Sand Sea, then a walk up 253 steps to the crater rim as the volcano belches sulphurous smoke into the cold air. Mount Bromo sits inside the Tengger caldera in East Java, surrounded by the higher peaks of the Tengger Massif. The hike to the viewpoint at Penanjakan above gives the full picture — Bromo, Batok, and Semeru (Java's highest peak, still active) laid out before you in the dawn light.

**5. Take the Slow Boat through the Banda Sea**

The Pelni ferry from Ambon to Banda Neira — the old spice islands of [Maluku](/asia/indonesia/maluku) — is a journey of about 14 hours through some of the most beautiful sea in Indonesia. Banda Neira itself is a small island with ruined Dutch forts, nutmeg trees, excellent snorkelling, and almost no tourists. From there you can continue on to the Kei Islands, which have white sand beaches so fine they feel unreal. This is eastern Indonesia at its best: remote, unhurried, and extraordinary.
