The tomb of Cyrus the Great is a simple, almost austere structure — a gabled chamber on a six-stepped plinth of white limestone, about 11 metres tall in total. It's been standing since roughly 530 BCE. Alexander the Great reportedly visited and found it stripped of its furnishings; he ordered repairs. Local Zoroastrians and later Muslims venerated it for centuries under different attributions. Today it stands alone on the plain, surrounded by nothing, and its plainness is the point. There are no reliefs, no inscriptions visible from the outside. Just the proportions, and the age.
