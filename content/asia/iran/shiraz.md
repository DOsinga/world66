Shiraz is Iran's city of poets, roses, and wine — the wine long gone, the roses and poetry very much intact. At 1,600 metres on a fertile plain ringed by mountains, the climate is unusually kind: mild winters, warm but not brutal summers, and air that carries the scent of orange blossom in spring.

The city's claim on the Iranian imagination runs deep. Hafez and Saadi, two of the greatest poets in the Persian language, were born and died here. Their tombs draw a steady stream of Iranians who come to sit, recite verses, and lay a hand on the marble. The gardens around both mausoleums are among the nicest places to spend an afternoon in Iran.

Beyond the tombs, Shiraz rewards slow exploration. The old bazaar — the Bazaar-e Vakil — is a working market, not a tourist trap: vaulted brick corridors selling spices, carpets, copperware, and dried fruit. The nearby Masjed-e Vakil has a forest of twisted stone columns that is one of the more striking interiors in the country.

Shiraz is also the natural base for visiting [Persepolis](/asia/iran/persepolis), the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Empire, about 60 kilometres to the northeast. Most travellers spend at least two days here.
