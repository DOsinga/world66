---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's in Iran"
type: section
---

**1. Stand in Naqsh-e Jahan Square, Isfahan**

The Imam Square in [Isfahan](/asia/iran/isfahan) is not merely the world's second-largest public square — it is a complete and functioning work of art. Built in the early 1600s by Shah Abbas I, it is surrounded on all four sides by monuments of the Safavid era: the Imam Mosque to the south, its dome clad in turquoise and gold tile visible from across the city; the intimate Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque to the east, with an interior so perfectly proportioned it draws an involuntary silence from visitors; the Ali Qapu Palace to the west, its six-story facade overlooking the old polo ground; and the vaulted entrance to the Grand Bazaar to the north. In the evenings, families spread picnic blankets on the grass, children chase pigeons, and the whole space fills with the kind of life that makes you realize a great city is meant to be lived in as much as admired.

**2. Walk through Persepolis at dawn**

[Persepolis](/asia/iran/persepolis), 60 km northeast of [Shiraz](/asia/iran/shiraz), was the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Empire, founded by Darius the Great around 515 BCE. Alexander the Great burned it in 330 BCE — whether in a drunken rage or deliberate political act, historians still argue — but what survived is staggering. The Apadana staircase reliefs show delegation after delegation of subject peoples bringing tribute to the king: Egyptians, Babylonians, Indians, Ethiopians, each dressed in their own clothing. The Gate of All Nations, its stone doorposts carved with the enormous winged bulls called lamassu, is still standing. Come early, before the coaches arrive, and you will have the vast stone terrace almost to yourself in the early light.

**3. Lose yourself in Yazd's old city**

[Yazd](/asia/iran/yazd) is built almost entirely from mud brick the same color as the desert around it, making the city seem to grow organically from the landscape. The old city is a labyrinth of covered lanes, wind towers (badgir), and mosques with brilliant blue tile work. The Friday Mosque is extraordinary — its twin minarets, at 48 meters, are the tallest in Iran, and the tilework in the entrance portal is a lesson in how far geometric abstraction can go. Just outside the city, the Tower of Silence (Dakhme) sits on a bare hilltop — the old Zoroastrian funerary tower where the dead were exposed to the sky — and the fire has been burning at the Zoroastrian Fire Temple without interruption since 470 CE.

**4. Visit the Golestan Palace in Tehran**

It is easy to pass through [Tehran](/asia/iran/tehran) quickly, treating it as a transit point, but that is a mistake. The Golestan Palace complex — a UNESCO World Heritage site — is the most concentrated display of Qajar-era opulence you will encounter anywhere. The Hall of Mirrors is the standard highlight: thousands of fragments of mirror covering walls and ceiling in patterns that fracture light into something dizzying. But the Marble Throne (Takht-e Marmar) in the open pavilion, the decorative tilework and stucco, and the sheer accumulation of fine craftsmanship make this a genuine stop. Walk through the Grand Bazaar nearby — a city under a roof, selling everything from saffron to gold — and lunch on ash reshteh (herb and noodle soup) at one of the traditional tea houses.

**5. Spend a night in the Dasht-e Kavir or Dasht-e Lut desert**

Iran contains two of the world's great deserts. The Dasht-e Kavir (Great Salt Desert) stretches across the center of the country; the Dasht-e Lut in the southeast is so hot it has recorded surface temperatures higher than anywhere else on Earth and was declared a UNESCO Natural Heritage site in 2016. You do not need to be a serious desert traveler to experience this landscape: from [Yazd](/asia/iran/yazd) or [Kerman](/asia/iran/kerman), day trips reach the caravanserai ruins and sand dunes of the desert fringe. For something more immersive, stay a night in one of the small desert villages — Kharanaq, Chak Chak, Meybod — where the architecture and the silence are unlike anything in the cities.
