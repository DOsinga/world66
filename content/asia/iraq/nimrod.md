Nimrud (also spelled Nimrod) was Assyria's second capital, lying about 37 km south-east of [Mosul](/asia/iraq/mosul), on the eastern bank of the Tigris. It was designated as the Assyrian capital by Ashurnasirpal II in 879 BC and was known in ancient times as Kalhu (mentioned in Genesis as Calah). When Sargon II came to power it was replaced by Khorsabad as the new capital around 720 BC, and in 612 BC it was destroyed by the Medes, at the same time as the fall of [Ninevah](/asia/iraq/ninevah).

The site was excavated in the 19th century by the British archaeologist Henry Layard, and subsequent work by Sir Max Mallowan uncovered the vast palaces of the Assyrian kings. The most impressive is the palace of Ashurnasirpal II, the city's founding monarch. The entrance was once guarded by colossal human-headed winged lions — lamassu — in the distinctive Assyrian style. Many of these were removed to the British Museum and the Louvre, but the site retained some remarkable pieces.

ISIS systematically destroyed much of Nimrud in 2015, using sledgehammers and bulldozers to demolish what they could not truck away. The destruction was filmed and released as propaganda. Restoration and excavation work has resumed since 2017 and the site is once again accessible from Mosul.
