---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

The Jewish calendar is lunar, so festival dates shift against the Gregorian calendar from year to year. Check specific dates before planning around them.

**Purim** (February or March) is the most festive of Jewish holidays — costumes, street parties, and public revelry. Tel Aviv's Purim street party is one of the biggest outdoor celebrations in the region. Jerusalem marks the holiday a day later than the rest of Israel (Shushan Purim). A fun, chaotic time to be in the country.

**Passover / Pesach** (March or April) is an eight-day holiday commemorating the Exodus from Egypt. During Passover, leavened bread (chametz) disappears from Jewish shops and restaurants, replaced by matzah. Families gather for the seder, a ritual dinner. The intermediate days (Hol Hamoed) are when Israelis travel domestically — expect crowds at national parks, the Dead Sea, and Eilat.

**Independence Day / Yom Ha'atzmaut** (April or May, by Hebrew calendar) is preceded by Yom HaZikaron, the solemn memorial day for fallen soldiers. The transition from mourning to celebration — within 24 hours — is one of the most emotionally vivid things to experience in Israel. Fireworks and street barbecues mark independence night.

**Shavuot** (May or June) marks the giving of the Torah at Sinai. Streets fill with all-night Torah study and, in secular Tel Aviv, all-night parties. A one-day public holiday.

**Rosh Hashanah** (September or October) is the Jewish New Year — a quieter, more introspective holiday than secular new year celebrations. The country slows, businesses close.

**Yom Kippur** (September or October), the Day of Atonement, is the most significant day of the Jewish year. The country comes to a complete halt — no traffic, no flights, no TV broadcasts. Streets fill with cyclists. Even secular Israelis generally fast. A profound and unusual day to be in Israel.

**Sukkot** (September or October) is a week-long harvest festival. Sukkot (booth-like structures covered with palm fronds) appear on balconies and in public spaces. The last day, Simchat Torah, involves dancing with Torah scrolls in synagogues.

**Hanukkah** (December) is a minor Jewish holiday but a highly visible one — menorahs burn in windows and public spaces. Not a public holiday; shops and businesses remain open.

**Israel Festival** (Jerusalem, May or June) brings top international theater, music, and dance to Jerusalem venues. One of the country's premier arts events.

**Karmiel Dance Festival** (Karmiel, July or August) is one of the largest folk dance festivals in the world, drawing performers and audiences from across Israel and abroad.

**Red Sea Jazz Festival** (Eilat, August) is an annual international jazz event that draws artists and audiences to Israel's southernmost city.
