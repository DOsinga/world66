A UNESCO World Heritage Site and the most accessible of the Nabataean cities along the ancient incense route. The ruins — temples, a bathhouse, wine presses, and Byzantine churches built over Nabataean foundations — stand on a flat-topped ridge with wide desert views. The Nabataeans engineered elaborate water-harvesting systems to farm this arid land 2,000 years ago; the remains of their channels and cisterns are still visible. Combined entry with Ein Avdat National Park is available.
