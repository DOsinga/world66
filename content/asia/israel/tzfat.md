Tzfat (Safed) sits high in the mountains of the Upper Galilee, at around 900 meters — cool in summer, occasionally snowy in winter. It has been one of the four holy cities of Judaism for centuries and was the center of Jewish mysticism (Kabbalah) in the 16th century, when Rabbi Joseph Karo compiled the Shulchan Aruch here and the mystic Isaac Luria developed Kabbalistic doctrine that spread across the Jewish world.

The old city is an artists' quarter now, with galleries, studios, and workshops filling the arched stone lanes that once housed rabbinical academies. It still functions as a place of religious study; several of the ancient synagogues remain active congregations. The Ari Ashkenazi synagogue and the Abuhav synagogue are the most visited.

Walking around Tzfat is the main activity — the streets are steep, atmospheric, and rewarding. The views over the Galilee on clear days extend as far as the Sea of Galilee and Mount Hermon. In summer the town fills with visitors; weekday mornings off-season are peaceful.

There is a small artists' colony in the lower part of town, established in the 1950s, with a different, more secular atmosphere than the religious quarter above. A good place to find original Israeli art at reasonable prices.

Tzfat is 35 km from Tiberias and about 40 km from Acre. Public buses connect to both.
