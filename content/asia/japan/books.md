**Travel writing**

*Hokkaido Highway Blues* by Will Ferguson — a witty, affectionate account of hitchhiking the length of Japan from south to north, following the cherry blossom. One of the best travel books about Japan.

*The Roads to Sata* by Alan Booth — a writer walks the entire length of Japan on foot, from Hokkaido to Cape Sata on Kyushu's southern tip. Unhurried, observant, and deeply fond of the country.

**Fiction**

*The Makioka Sisters* by Junichiro Tanizaki — four sisters in prewar Osaka, navigating marriage, family, and a changing Japan. Long, but repays the investment.

*Norwegian Wood* by Haruki Murakami — melancholic coming-of-age story set in Tokyo in the late 1960s. The novel that made Murakami internationally famous.

*Snow Country* by Yasunari Kawabata — a short novel about a Tokyo dilettante and a geisha in a remote hot-spring resort. Nobel Prize winner, and one of the most evocative portraits of Japan's traditional world.

*Silence* by Shusaku Endo — the story of Portuguese Jesuit missionaries in 17th-century Japan during the persecution of Christians. Harrowing and morally serious.

**History and culture**

*Hiroshima* by John Hersey — the definitive account of the atomic bombing, told through six survivors. Published in 1946, still essential.

*The Chrysanthemum and the Sword* by Ruth Benedict — an anthropological study of Japanese culture written during World War II. Some elements are dated, but its insights into honor, obligation, and social form remain illuminating.

*Dogs and Demons* by Alex Kerr — a critical examination of Japan's postwar development, focusing on construction, bureaucracy, and the destruction of the natural landscape. Not a comfortable read, but an important counterpoint to the usual rosy picture.

*Memoirs of a Geisha* by Arthur Golden — historical fiction set in the world of Kyoto's geisha. Hugely popular and engaging, though its accuracy has been disputed by actual geisha.

**For children**

*Sadako and the Thousand Paper Cranes* by Eleanor Coerr — the true story of a young Hiroshima survivor who folded paper cranes in hospital. Quietly devastating.
