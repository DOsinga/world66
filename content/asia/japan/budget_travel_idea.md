---
title: "Budget Travel Ideas"
type: section
---

Japan has a reputation for being expensive, and parts of it are. But traveling on a budget is genuinely possible with some planning.

**Where to sleep**

Hostels are widespread in all major cities and tourist towns, ranging from ¥2,500–4,500 per night in a dorm. Japan's network of youth hostels (affiliated with Hostelling International) covers even smaller towns. Capsule hotels — small individual sleeping pods stacked in rows — cost ¥3,000–5,000 and are clean, well-designed, and perfectly comfortable for a night or two. Many capsule hotels now serve all genders. Budget business hotels (Toyoko Inn, Super Hotel, APA) offer private rooms from around ¥5,000.

**Eating cheap**

Convenience stores (konbini) — 7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson — are where budget travelers eat in Japan, and not reluctantly. Onigiri (rice balls), sandwiches, hot noodles, and prepared meals cost ¥100–400 and are genuinely good. Gyudon (beef bowl) chains like Yoshinoya and Sukiya serve filling meals for ¥400–600. Ramen shops and soba restaurants rarely charge more than ¥800–1,000 for a bowl. Lunch sets at restaurants that would be expensive at dinner are often ¥900–1,300 — make lunch your main meal.

**Getting around cheaply**

The Japan Rail Pass sounds expensive but can save significant money on a multi-city itinerary involving Shinkansen. Do the maths before you buy. Within cities, IC cards (Suica, Pasmo) make buses and subways seamless. Long-distance overnight buses (Willer Express and others) cost a fraction of the Shinkansen and save you a night's accommodation if you can sleep on buses.

**Free and cheap attractions**

Many of Japan's best experiences are free or very cheap: wandering Kyoto's Higashiyama district, crossing the famous scramble crossing in Shibuya, walking in any city's covered shopping arcade (shotengai), visiting most Shinto shrines, or climbing a mountain. Temple and museum admission fees are usually modest (¥500–1,000).

**Timing**

Avoid Golden Week (late April–early May), O-bon (mid-August), and New Year. Prices for accommodation rise sharply and availability falls. Outside these periods, and especially in winter (except ski areas), prices are considerably lower.
