---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Japan has a dense calendar of festivals (matsuri) — Shinto shrine ceremonies, Buddhist observances, seasonal celebrations, and local peculiarities going back centuries. These are not tourist events put on for visitors; they are living culture.

**New Year (Shogatsu) — January 1–3**

The most important holiday in the Japanese calendar. The country essentially stops: shops close, families gather, and millions of people make their first shrine visit of the year (hatsumode). Major shrines like Meiji Jingu in Tokyo and Naritasan Shinshoji in Chiba receive several million visitors in the first few days of January. Women wear kimono, vendors sell hot amazake (sweet rice wine) and traditional sweets, and there is an atmosphere of quiet optimism about the year ahead. It's wonderful to witness but book accommodation and transport well ahead.

**Sapporo Snow Festival (Yuki Matsuri) — February**

Held annually in Sapporo's Odori Park over about a week in early February, this is one of Japan's most famous winter festivals. Professional and amateur sculptors build enormous, sometimes building-sized, sculptures from snow and ice. A participatory area invites visitors to try their hand. Over two million people attend each year; Sapporo's hotels fill up fast. See [Hokkaido](/asia/japan/hokkaido).

**Cherry Blossom (Sakura Matsuri) — March to May**

Not a single event but a nationwide season. As cherry trees bloom from south to north, people gather under them for hanami — picnics, sake, and conversation beneath falling pink petals. Ueno Park and Shinjuku Gyoen in [Tokyo](/asia/japan/tokyo), Maruyama Park in Kyoto, and Hirosaki Castle in Aomori are among the most celebrated spots. The timing shifts year to year; websites track the blossom forecast from January onwards.

**Gion Matsuri — July (Kyoto)**

One of Japan's three greatest festivals, Gion Matsuri runs throughout July in Kyoto, culminating in the Yamaboko Junko parade on July 17 when enormous decorated floats, some of them centuries old, are wheeled through the city center. The evenings in the lead-up week (Yoiyama) are particularly atmospheric: the old streets of central Kyoto are closed to traffic and fill with food stalls and people in yukata (summer kimono).

**O-Bon — mid-August**

A Buddhist festival honoring the spirits of ancestors, O-bon sees families return to hometowns and visit graves. Bon Odori dances take place in neighborhoods across the country — participatory, informal, and a genuinely good time. Fireworks displays (hanabi taikai) are a feature of the whole summer season, with the biggest in Tokyo in late July and August.

**Jidai Matsuri — October (Kyoto)**

Held on October 22, the Festival of the Ages is a historical procession through Kyoto featuring costumes from the Meiji era back to the Heian period. Less energetic than the Gion Matsuri but visually impressive.
