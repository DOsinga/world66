**By air**

Almost all international visitors arrive by air. Japan's main international gateways are:

- **Narita (NRT)**, 65 km northeast of Tokyo. The Narita Express (N'EX) runs to Tokyo Station in about 55 minutes (¥3,070); the cheaper Keisei Skyliner reaches Ueno in around 40 minutes (¥2,520). Limousine buses serve major hotels across the city.
- **Haneda (HND)**, much closer to central Tokyo — about 30 minutes by rail. Increasingly popular with international carriers; check if your airline flies here as it's more convenient than Narita.
- **Kansai International (KIX)**, serving Osaka, Kyoto, and Kobe. The Haruka express runs to Kyoto in 75 minutes; Osaka's Namba is just 29 minutes by Nankai Line. Worth choosing if your main destination is the Kansai region.
- **Fukuoka (FUK)**, the main gateway to Kyushu. Well connected internationally, especially to other Asian cities.
- **New Chitose (CTS)**, serving Sapporo and Hokkaido.

Flight times: London to Tokyo around 12 hours nonstop; New York around 14 hours; Los Angeles around 11 hours; Sydney around 10 hours; Singapore around 7 hours.

Japan Airlines (JAL) and All Nippon Airways (ANA) are the main Japanese carriers. Budget carriers including Jetstar Japan and Peach operate within Asia.

**By sea**

Ferry services connect Japan with South Korea (Fukuoka–Busan, around 3 hours by high-speed ferry) and with China (Osaka–Shanghai takes about 48 hours). Slow but scenic, and popular with overland travelers completing an Asian loop.

The trans-Siberian route — London to Moscow by rail, then across Russia to Vladivostok, then ferry to Japan — remains a classic long-haul journey for those with time and curiosity. It takes the better part of two weeks from western Europe.

**Departure tax**

International departure tax is included in the price of most airline tickets.
