title: "Highlights"

---

**Kyoto**

Japan's old imperial capital holds the country's greatest concentration of temples, shrines, and traditional streetscapes. Walking the stone lanes of Higashiyama at dawn, before the tour buses arrive, through wooden machiya townhouses and past moss-covered garden walls, is one of the best experiences in Asia. [Kyoto](/asia/japan/honshu/kyoto) repays multiple visits across different seasons.

**The Shinkansen**

Taking a bullet train is not just transport — it's a statement about what Japan values: precision, efficiency, and a certain quiet pride in doing things well. Watch Mount Fuji appear for a few seconds on the right side of the southbound train from Tokyo. Buy a station bento box before you board.

**An onsen ryokan**

Soaking in a hot spring in a mountain town, then eating a twelve-course kaiseki dinner in a yukata robe in your tatami room — this is the Japan that visitors remember longest. It doesn't have to be expensive; a modest ryokan in a smaller town often delivers the full experience for a fraction of the price of Kyoto's famous inns.

**Hiroshima's Peace Memorial**

The A-bomb Dome — the ruin of the Hiroshima Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall, left standing at the epicenter of the 1945 bombing — is one of the most powerful historic sites in the world. The museum beside it is honest, moving, and essential. [Hiroshima](/asia/japan/honshu/hiroshima) is a thriving modern city today; visiting it feels important rather than morbid.

**Nara's deer**

The deer that wander freely through Nara Park are sacred in Shinto tradition and perfectly accustomed to humans. Watching a deer bow to receive a cracker, or nonchalantly push past a toddler, is charming in a way that no photograph quite captures. The giant bronze Buddha inside Todai-ji temple — the world's largest — is equally hard to photograph convincingly, and equally worth seeing.

**Cherry blossom season**

If you have any flexibility in timing, visiting during cherry blossom (late March to early May depending on latitude) is worth arranging your trip around. The combination of the delicate flowers, the light, and the whole country deciding to sit outside under the trees with food and drink, is genuinely joyful.

**Hokkaido's outdoors**

In a country that is 73% forested mountain, [Hokkaido](/asia/japan/hokkaido) is where the outdoors is largest and least touched. Daisetsuzan National Park is bigger than some European countries and takes days to explore. Shiretoko is the kind of wilderness where brown bears outnumber tourists. In winter, the powder snow is some of the best in the world.
