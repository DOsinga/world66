Kobe sits between the Rokko mountains and Osaka Bay, a port city that has been open to foreign influence longer than almost anywhere else in Japan. Together with [Osaka](/asia/japan/honshu/osaka) and [Kyoto](/asia/japan/honshu/kyoto) it forms the heart of the Kansai region, and it is about three hours and twenty minutes from [Tokyo](/asia/japan/tokyo) by Shinkansen.

The Kitano district is where foreign traders settled in the late 1800s, and their "ijinkan" — Western-style houses with Japanese touches — still line the hillside streets. Some are open to the public, others have been converted into boutiques and restaurants. The area has a pleasantly un-Japanese atmosphere, with wrought-iron balconies and bay windows looking out over the harbour.

Nada, the eastern part of the city, is one of Japan's great sake-producing districts. The breweries use water from the Rokko mountains, and several are open to visitors for tours and tastings. Nada-no-ki-ippon is among the most celebrated brands.

The Arima Onsen, tucked into the mountains behind the city, is one of Japan's oldest spa resorts. Its mineral-rich waters come in two types: "gold" (iron-rich, rust-coloured) and "silver" (clear, carbonated). The town around the springs is compact and pleasant, with traditional ryokan, souvenir shops, and the Zuihoji Park where the warlord Hideyoshi Toyotomi once held tea ceremonies.

Kobe beef needs no introduction, but it is worth noting that the city is also excellent for Chinese food — the Nankinmachi Chinatown is one of Japan's three great Chinatowns — and for Indian and Southeast Asian cuisine brought by the port's long trading connections.
