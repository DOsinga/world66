A thumb of land jutting north into the Sea of Japan from Ishikawa Prefecture, the Noto Peninsula is one of rural Japan's most compelling detours. Rolling hills, rugged coastlines, and terraced rice paddies give way to small fishing villages where little has changed in decades. The pace here is resolutely slow — and that is precisely the point.

The main town is [Wajima](https://www.world66.com/asia/japan/honshu/wajima), an old fishing port at the northern tip of the peninsula, famous throughout Japan for its lacquerware (Wajima-nuri) and its morning market, which has operated daily for centuries. The market runs along the main street every morning except the 10th and 25th of each month and is the best place to buy lacquerware directly from the craftspeople who make it.

Public transport on the peninsula was already sparse and was further reduced after the 2024 earthquake that caused significant damage to parts of Ishikawa. A car or bicycle is the practical way to explore. Cyclists in particular love the Noto Peninsula for its relatively quiet roads, spectacular coastal scenery, and a well-established cycling route that circles the peninsula.
