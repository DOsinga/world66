The third-largest city in Kyushu after Fukuoka and Kitakyushu, Kumamoto sits on the central plains of the island with the ancient volcanic plateau of **Aso-san** rising to the east. The city suffered devastating earthquakes in 2016, but has rebuilt with impressive determination — and the ongoing restoration of its great castle has become a symbol of that resilience.

Kumamoto's most famous landmark is **Kumamoto-jo**, one of Japan's three great castles. Destroyed by fire during the Satsuma Rebellion of 1877, the current keep is a 1960 reconstruction, but its predominantly black exterior remains striking and the hilltop setting commands the city. The castle district is pleasant to walk, even mid-restoration.

The second essential stop is **Suizenji-joju-en**, a 17th-century strolling garden in the southeastern suburbs. Its miniature landscape recreates the 53 stages of the old Tokaido highway, with a small replica of Mount Fuji at its centre. It ranks among Japan's finest traditional gardens.

Kumamoto's most famous culinary speciality is *basashi* — raw horse meat served thin-sliced with ginger and garlic soy sauce. Most restaurants in the centre offer it, and it is worth trying at least once.
