Japan rewards the traveler who looks beyond the obvious — though the obvious is pretty good too.

**Kyoto**

Japan's ancient capital for over a thousand years, [Kyoto](/asia/japan/honshu/kyoto) holds the densest concentration of temples, shrines, and traditional culture anywhere in the country. The golden pavilion of Kinkaku-ji, the thousands of torii gates marching through the forest at Fushimi Inari, the Zen rock garden at Ryoan-ji — these are justifiably famous, but they sit alongside quieter precincts, traditional machiya townhouses, and the geisha district of Gion that are equally compelling. Visit on weekdays and arrive early at the popular sites.

**Mount Fuji**

Fuji-san (3,776 m) is Japan's highest peak and its most recognizable image. About 200,000 people climb it each year, mostly in July and August. The climb itself is not technical but is long, cold at the top, and often crowded — many people start at night to reach the summit at dawn. For the best views without the climb, Hakone or the Fuji Five Lakes (Fujigoko) to the north offer classic panoramas.

**Hiroshima and Miyajima**

[Hiroshima](/asia/japan/honshu/hiroshima) is a city worth visiting on its own merits, but the Peace Memorial Park and Museum — built around the shell of the Hiroshima Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall, the building closest to the epicenter that remained standing — is one of the most powerful historic sites in Asia. A short ferry from nearby Miyajimaguchi reaches Miyajima Island, where the famous "floating" torii gate of Itsukushima Shrine stands in the bay.

**Nara**

Just 45 minutes from both Kyoto and Osaka, [Nara](/asia/japan/honshu/nara) was Japan's first permanent capital. The Todai-ji temple contains the world's largest bronze Buddha statue. Wild deer wander freely through Nara Park, taking crackers from visitors' hands; they are sacred according to Shinto tradition but very much their own animals — don't startle them.

**Nikko**

In northern [Honshu](/asia/japan/honshu), Nikko is where the Tokugawa shoguns are buried in mausoleums of baroque extravagance rare in Japanese architecture. The Tosho-gu shrine complex is gilded, carved, and vividly coloured — quite unlike the austere Zen aesthetic of Kyoto. The surrounding mountains and lakes are fine hiking country.

**Hokkaido's national parks**

[Hokkaido](/asia/japan/hokkaido) is Japan for people who like the outdoors. Daisetsuzan National Park is the island's wild interior — vast, volcanic, and excellent for hiking in summer, skiing in winter. The Shiretoko Peninsula, a UNESCO World Heritage Site on the northeast tip, is one of the few places in Japan where you might genuinely see brown bears. In February, the Sapporo Snow Festival fills Odori Park with elaborate ice sculptures.

**Kyushu and Yakushima**

[Kyushu](/asia/japan/kyushu) has Nagasaki, Kumamoto Castle, and Mount Aso — the world's largest volcanic caldera, still active, where you can drive to the crater rim. Just offshore, Yakushima island is a place of otherworldly ancient cedar forests, some of the trees several thousand years old, and said to have inspired the landscapes in Studio Ghibli's Princess Mononoke.
