**Hiking**

Japan has extraordinary hiking, mostly overlooked by visitors sticking to the city circuit. The Japanese Alps in central Honshu have serious mountain routes; [Hokkaido's](/asia/japan/hokkaido) Daisetsuzan is remote and spectacular. Shikoku's 88-temple pilgrimage (Shikoku Henro) is a 1,200 km circuit that people walk over several months, or dip into for shorter stages. Kumano Kodo, a network of ancient pilgrimage trails through the forested Kii Peninsula south of Osaka, is a UNESCO World Heritage trail and increasingly popular with foreign walkers.

**Skiing and snowboarding**

Japan's powder snow — light, dry, and deep — has earned a global reputation. Niseko in Hokkaido draws the most international visitors, but Furano, Hakuba, and Nozawa Onsen are also excellent. The ski season runs roughly December to March; conditions in Hokkaido are often best in January and February.

**Onsen bathing**

Hot spring bathing is a Japanese institution. Towns built around onsen include Hakone (easy from Tokyo), Kinosaki (in northern Hyogo), Beppu (Kyushu), and Noboribetsu (Hokkaido). Tattoos are traditionally prohibited at most onsen, though some establishments now waive this rule for foreign visitors — check ahead if relevant.

**Sumo**

Six official sumo tournaments (basho) are held each year, three in Tokyo (January, May, September) and one each in Osaka, Nagoya, and Fukuoka. Tournament tickets can be bought on arrival in Japan; same-day tickets for upper sections are available at the door. The ritual and atmosphere of a full day at the tournament is worth experiencing even if you know nothing about sumo.

**Traditional arts**

Kabuki theatre — stylized, noisy, visually extraordinary — is performed at the Kabuki-za in Ginza, Tokyo, and the Minami-za in Kyoto. Single-act tickets are available for those who don't want to commit to a full four-hour performance. Noh is more austere and often staged at shrines. Tea ceremony (chado) experiences for visitors are available throughout Kyoto and elsewhere.

**Cycling**

The Shimanami Kaido, a series of bridges and islands connecting Honshu and Shikoku across the Seto Inland Sea, is Japan's most celebrated cycling route — 70 km of traffic-light scenery through small islands and fishing villages. Bikes are rentable at each end.

**Day trips from Tokyo**

[Nikko](/asia/japan/honshu/nikko) (2 hours north), Kamakura (1 hour south, famous giant Buddha), and Hakone (90 minutes, views of Fuji, hot springs) are the classic escapes. All are easy on public transport and can be done in a day.
