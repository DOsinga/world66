---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's"
type: section
---

**1. Sleep in a ryokan and use the onsen**

A traditional ryokan is not just a hotel — it's the closest thing Japan has to a total cultural experience. Rooms are tatami-floored with futon beds laid out by staff; dinner is a kaiseki meal of small seasonal dishes served in your room or a communal dining room; breakfast is grilled fish, miso soup, and rice. The centrepiece, in most ryokan, is the onsen: a hot spring bath, usually separated by gender, where you soak in silence. Prices range from modest to extravagant; even a mid-range ryokan in a mountain or coastal town will be memorable.

**2. Ride the Shinkansen**

The bullet train is fast (up to 320 km/h), perfectly punctual, and a pleasure to ride. Taking it from Tokyo to Kyoto or Hiroshima is not just efficient — it's part of the experience. Watch Mount Fuji appear for a few seconds on the right side of the train (southbound from Tokyo, about 45 minutes in) if the sky is clear. Reserve a window seat and buy an eki-ben (station bento box) from the platform vendors before you board.

**3. Wander Kyoto's temple and shrine district**

[Kyoto](/asia/japan/honshu/kyoto) was the imperial capital for over a thousand years, and despite mass tourism and some heavy-handed restoration, it remains extraordinary. The best way to experience it is on foot, early in the morning before the tour buses arrive. Walk the stone-paved lanes of Ninenzaka and Sannenzaka, take the Philosopher's Path through gardens and along a canal, and duck into smaller temples and shrines that most visitors walk past. Save the big sites — Kinkaku-ji, Fushimi Inari, Ryoan-ji — for late afternoon or on a weekday.

**4. Eat your way through a covered market**

Nishiki Market in Kyoto and Kuromon Market in Osaka are the obvious examples, but almost every Japanese city has a covered shotengai (shopping street) or market with food stalls serving local specialities. Try things you can't name. The Japanese approach to food — the precision, the seasonality, the sheer variety — is best encountered in these everyday settings rather than in tourist restaurants.

**5. Get into the countryside**

Japan is densely urbanized but the countryside is often surprisingly close. The Japanese Alps run through central Honshu; [Hokkaido](/asia/japan/hokkaido) is vast and rural; [Shikoku](/asia/japan/shikoku) has the ancient 88-temple pilgrimage circuit that people still walk today. Even a short detour from the standard Tokyo–Kyoto circuit — to the mountain town of [Takayama](/asia/japan/takayama), or to the Noto Peninsula in Ishikawa, or to the valleys of Kumano — shows a completely different Japan from the big-city experience. Slow down, take a local bus, and eat at the one restaurant in the village.
