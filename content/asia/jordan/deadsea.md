The Dead Sea is one of the world's most extraordinary natural phenomena — a vast, hypersaline lake sitting more than 400 metres below sea level, making it the lowest point on Earth's surface. Fed by the Jordan River but with no outflow, the water is nearly ten times saltier than the ocean, creating a density so high that anyone who enters simply floats. Lying back on the glassy surface, newspaper in hand if you choose the cliché, is a genuinely surreal experience.

The shoreline straddles Jordan and Israel, but the Jordanian side offers the more relaxed approach — a string of resort hotels set against bare, mineral-streaked cliffs that glow amber and ochre at sunset. The therapeutic reputation is ancient: Cleopatra is said to have imported Dead Sea mud for her skin, and King Herod built a palace nearby to benefit from the climate. Today the mud baths and spa treatments draw visitors from around the world, and the dense, oxygen-rich air is said to be gentler on the lungs than anywhere else.

Beyond the sea itself, the surrounding region holds more than most visitors expect. Nearby Wadi Mujib cuts a dramatic canyon to the waterline. The baptism site at Bethany beyond the Jordan, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, lies only a short drive north. And the ancient mosaics at Madaba and the summit of Mount Nebo — where Moses is said to have seen the Promised Land — are both easily reached in a day.

The sea has been shrinking for decades, its levels dropping as water is diverted from the Jordan River. What exists today is still magnificent, but it is not the same sea that travellers encountered a generation ago. Visit while you can.
