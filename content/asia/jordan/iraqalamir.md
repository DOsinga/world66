Iraq al-Amir is a small village in the Wadi Seer valley, about 30 kilometres west of [Amman](/asia/jordan/amman). It makes an easy half-day trip from the capital and offers one of the few Hellenistic sites in [Jordan](/asia/jordan) — a reminder that this part of the world was once ruled by the successors of Alexander the Great.

The centrepiece is Qasr al-Abd (Castle of the Slave), a partially reconstructed 2nd-century BC palace built by a member of the powerful Tobiad family. The building is remarkable for its carved stone lions and eagles, and for the sheer ambition of its construction — massive blocks fitted together without mortar. It was never finished, likely because its builder fell out of favour with the Ptolemaic rulers.

The surrounding valley is green and pleasant, a sharp contrast to the arid plateau above. There are caves in the cliffs that were once used as storerooms and stables. A few simple restaurants near the site serve tea and lunch. The drive from Amman winds down through increasingly rural scenery and is part of the appeal.
