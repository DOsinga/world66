Aksu-Zhabagly is the oldest nature reserve in Central Asia, established in 1926 at the foot of the Western Tien Shan mountains in southern Kazakhstan. The reserve ranges from 1,100 to 4,236 metres and shelters snow leopards, Tien Shan white-clawed bears, and over 1,200 plant species — including the wild Greig tulips that are the ancestors of the cultivated tulips in your garden.

The gateway is Zhabagly village, where a community-based ecotourism programme offers homestay accommodation, guided treks, horseback trips, dombra concerts, and kumiss (fermented mare's milk) tastings. It is a genuine, low-key place — no resort infrastructure, just local families opening their homes.

The best treks from Zhabagly include Red Hill (a day trip to see the wild tulip fields in late April and a stalactite cave with an underground lake), the Kshi-Kaindy waterfalls and petroglyphs (one to three days), and the Aksu Gorge — over 500 metres deep, with mulberry trees and wild grapes at the bottom and snow-covered peaks visible from the rim.

The Chokpak bird ringing station, on the migration flyway, is worth a detour during the spring or autumn migrations. The Mausoleum of Kozha Ahmet Yassaui in Turkestan and the 13th-century ruins of Otrar are also within reach as day trips.
