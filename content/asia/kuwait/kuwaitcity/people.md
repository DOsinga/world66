---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Kuwait City is one of the most cosmopolitan cities in the Gulf. Kuwaiti nationals are a minority in their own capital -- the majority of residents are expatriate workers from India, Egypt, the Philippines, Bangladesh, Pakistan, and other Arab countries.

This diversity is visible everywhere: Indian restaurants outnumber Kuwaiti ones, Filipino workers run many of the service businesses, and Arabic, English, Hindi, and Tagalog are all heard on the streets. The different communities tend to cluster in particular neighbourhoods -- Hawalli and Salmiya have large South Asian populations, while wealthier Kuwaiti families live in suburbs like Mishref and Rumaithiya.

Kuwaiti culture is rooted in tribal and family bonds. The diwaniya -- a regular social gathering held in reception rooms of Kuwaiti homes -- is the backbone of social and political life. Kuwaitis are known for their warmth and hospitality, and visitors who engage with locals often find themselves invited to share tea, coffee, or a meal.

The city has a young population and a growing arts and culture scene. Galleries, cultural centres, and the Sheikh Jaber Al-Ahmed Cultural Centre (the national opera house) reflect a society that is investing in its cultural identity alongside its economic development.
