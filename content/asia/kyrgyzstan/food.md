Kyrgyz cuisine is nomadic food: hearty, meat-heavy, and designed to sustain people through cold winters at altitude. It is not subtle, but it is honest and filling, and there are genuine pleasures to be found if you approach it with an open mind.

## The Essentials

**Beshbarmak** is the national dish -- flat noodles topped with slow-cooked lamb or horse meat in an onion broth. The name means "five fingers" because you eat it with your hands. It is served at celebrations and gatherings, and being offered beshbarmak in someone's home is an honor.

**Laghman** is everywhere. These thick hand-pulled noodles come in two styles: as a hearty soup (guiru laghman) or stir-fried (boso laghman). Every cook has their own version, and the best ones are superb.

**Plov** (called paloo in Kyrgyz) is the Central Asian rice pilaf -- rice cooked with lamb, carrots and onions in a massive cast-iron qazan. It is the default celebratory dish and sold at markets across the country.

**Manti** are steamed dumplings filled with lamb and onion, sometimes pumpkin. They show up as a starter or a meal in themselves. **Samsa** are baked pastries with similar fillings, often triangular, sold hot from tandoor ovens at bazaars.

**Kuurdak** is one of the oldest Kyrgyz dishes -- roasted meat with onions, offal and potatoes. It is simple, rich and satisfying.

## Street Food

At any bazaar, look for samsa fresh from the oven, shashlik (grilled meat skewers, usually lamb), and tandyr nan -- round flatbread baked in clay ovens and served at every meal. The bread is considered sacred; never place it upside down or throw it away.

## Drinks

**Kymyz** (fermented mare's milk) is the iconic Kyrgyz drink, available everywhere in summer. It is sour, slightly fizzy and mildly alcoholic. You either love it or you don't, but you should try it. **Maksym** is a milder fermented grain drink, refreshing in hot weather. Tea is served constantly -- green tea in the south, black tea with milk in the north.

## Ashlyamfu

A cold noodle dish with vinegar and chili, originally from the Dungan (Chinese Muslim) community. It is spicy, tangy and refreshing on a hot day. Karakol is the best place to try it, where the Dungan population has perfected the recipe.

## Vegetarian Warning

Kyrgyz food revolves around meat. Vegetarians will find laghman without meat, bread, salads and dairy, but options are limited outside [Bishkek](/asia/kyrgyzstan/bishkek), where a growing number of cafes cater to plant-based diets. In yurt camps and rural homestays, expect meat at every meal.
