Getting around Kyrgyzstan is half the adventure. The country is mountainous, distances are long, and the road network ranges from decent highways to bone-rattling tracks. Plan for slow travel and you will enjoy the scenery instead of cursing it.

## Shared Taxis and Marshrutkas

The backbone of transport between cities is the shared taxi and the marshrutka (minibus). Both operate on a "leave when full" basis from bus stations. Marshrutkas are cheap -- a ride from Bishkek to Cholpon-Ata on Lake Issyk-Kul costs around 300-400 KGS ($3-4) -- but they are cramped and the driving can be alarming. Shared taxis cost two to three times more but are faster and marginally more comfortable.

On popular routes like Bishkek to Karakol or Bishkek to Osh, departures are frequent. On less-traveled routes, you may wait hours or need to pay for empty seats to get the vehicle moving. In remote areas, hitching a ride with passing vehicles is common and expected.

## Private Taxis

In [Bishkek](/asia/kyrgyzstan/bishkek) and other cities, private cars double as taxis. Flag one down, agree on a price before getting in, and have your destination written in Cyrillic if you can. Ride-hailing apps like Yandex Go work in Bishkek and Osh.

## Domestic Flights

Asman Airlines and Tez Jet operate domestic flights, mainly between Bishkek and Osh. This route saves you a grueling 12-hour drive over two 3,000-meter passes and costs from around $50. Karakol's airport reopened in late 2024 with seasonal flights from Bishkek.

## Driving

You can hire a car, but driving yourself requires confidence. Roads outside major highways are rough, signage is minimal, and mountain passes can close without warning due to rockfalls or snow. Hiring a car with a driver is a better option for most travelers. Expect to pay $80-150 per day depending on the vehicle and route. A cheaper alternative is to find a driver at the bus station and negotiate a price.

## Horse Trekking

In the high pastures, horses are still the main form of transport. Multi-day horse treks to places like Son-Kul Lake or the Suusamyr Valley are one of the best ways to experience the country. Community-based tourism organizations in Karakol, Kochkor and other towns arrange treks with local guides and yurt accommodation.
