## Son-Kul Lake

A vast alpine lake at 3,016 meters, ringed by mountains and accessible only in summer. From June to September, Kyrgyz herders set up yurt camps on the surrounding jailoo (summer pastures) and you can stay with them, ride horses across the plateau, and fall asleep to absolute silence. Getting there requires a half-day drive and a final stretch over a rough mountain track, which is exactly why it feels so remote.

## Lake Issyk-Kul

The world's second-largest alpine lake, 182 kilometers long and deep enough to swallow the Eiffel Tower twice over. The north shore has beach resorts popular with Kazakh and Russian holidaymakers; the south shore is quieter and more scenic. The lake never freezes despite sitting at 1,600 meters -- its name means "warm lake" in Kyrgyz. Use [Karakol](/asia/kyrgyzstan/karakol) or Cholpon-Ata as your base.

## Ala-Kul Trek

A three-to-four-day trek from [Karakol](/asia/kyrgyzstan/karakol) that takes you through the Jeti Oguz Valley (known for its red rock formations) up to Ala-Kul, a turquoise glacial lake at 3,500 meters, then down through the Altyn-Arashan hot springs. It is the most popular multi-day trek in the country, and for good reason.

## Osh Bazaar and Sulaiman-Too

[Osh](/asia/kyrgyzstan/osh) has been a trading post for over 3,000 years, and its bazaar still feels like it belongs on the Silk Road. Sulaiman-Too, the sacred mountain that rises above the city, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and offers panoramic views over the Fergana Valley. The combination of ancient market and sacred peak in one city is unique in Central Asia.

## Jeti Oguz Valley

Seven dramatic red sandstone formations -- the "Seven Bulls" -- mark the entrance to this valley south of Karakol. Beyond them, the valley opens into rolling green pastures backed by snow-capped peaks. It is easy to reach as a day trip from Karakol and stunning enough to justify several days of hiking.

## Eagle Hunting

Kyrgyzstan is one of the last places where eagle hunting (berkutchi) is a living tradition rather than a tourist performance. In the Issyk-Kul and Naryn regions, you can meet hunters who train golden eagles to hunt foxes and hares. The World Nomad Games, held every two years, feature eagle hunting competitions alongside horseback games like kok-boru (polo with a goat carcass).

## Arslanbob Walnut Forest

In the south, near Jalal-Abad, the village of Arslanbob sits in the world's largest natural walnut forest. Community-based tourism here is well organized, with homestays and guided hikes to waterfalls and holy lakes. The forest is spectacular in autumn when the leaves turn gold.
