---

Lebanon's turbulent history and rich culture have inspired some remarkable writing. Here are books worth reading before or during your trip.

**The Prophet** by Khalil Gibran -- Lebanon's most famous literary export, a collection of poetic essays on love, work, freedom and death. Gibran was born in [Bcharre](/asia/lebanon/bcharre) in 1883 and emigrated to the United States, but his work is deeply rooted in Lebanese landscape and spirituality. You can visit the Gibran Museum in Bcharre.

**Pity the Nation** by Robert Fisk -- The definitive account of Lebanon's civil war (1975-1990), written by the longtime Middle East correspondent of The Independent. Dense but essential reading for understanding modern Lebanon.

**Beirut Hellfire Society** by Rawi Hage -- A darkly comic novel set during the civil war, following a young man who joins a secret society that buries the dead rejected by religious authorities. Captures the absurdity and horror of wartime Beirut.

**The Story of Zahra** by Hanan al-Shaykh -- A landmark novel about a young Lebanese woman navigating family, sexuality and war. One of the most important works of modern Arabic fiction.

**An Unnecessary Woman** by Rabih Alameddine -- A reclusive 72-year-old woman in Beirut secretly translates great works of literature into Arabic. A love letter to books, Beirut and solitude.

**The Art of Syrian Cookery** by Helen Corey -- Despite the name, this classic cookbook covers Lebanese and Syrian cuisine thoroughly. Useful if you want to recreate the flavours at home.

**De Niro's Game** by Rawi Hage -- Two friends in 1980s Beirut, one choosing the militia and the other trying to escape. Won the IMPAC Dublin Literary Award.

**Beyrouth** by Samir Kassir -- A sweeping history of Beirut from antiquity to the 21st century by the Lebanese journalist and historian who was assassinated in 2005. Available in French and English.
