A cedar tree graces Lebanon's flag, but few of the fabled trees that once covered the mountains remain. The best place to see them is the grove on Mount Makmal, about 8 km east of [Bcharre](/asia/lebanon/bcharre), at an altitude of 1,800 metres. Bring a warm layer -- it is noticeably cooler up here.

The Cedars of God (Arz al-Rab) is a UNESCO World Heritage site, a grove of ancient trees, some estimated to be over 3,000 years old. The grove is small but atmospheric -- these are gnarled, massive trees with enormous spreading branches, and they carry the weight of millennia. A French artist carved a sculpture from the trunk of a dead cedar rather than let it be cut down, and you can also look for "Adam and Eve" -- two trees whose trunks are joined at the base.

There are two entrances to the grove: one off the main road near the souvenir stands, and another further up the road toward the ski resort, which tends to stay open later.

The cedars combine naturally with a visit to [Bcharre](/asia/lebanon/bcharre) and the Qadisha Valley. The drive from Beirut takes about two hours along mountain roads. Note that the road leading east from Bcharre over the mountains into the Bekaa Valley (and to [Baalbek](/asia/lebanon/baalbek)) is only open during summer.
