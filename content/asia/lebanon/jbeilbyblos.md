Byblos (Jbeil in Arabic) is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world, with a history stretching back over 9,000 years. Located 37 km north of [Beirut](/asia/lebanon/beirut), it has been occupied by Phoenicians, Egyptians, Assyrians, Greeks, Romans, Arabs, Crusaders and Ottomans, each leaving traces that make it one of the richest archaeological sites in the Middle East.

The city became an important trading port during the 3rd millennium BC and the commercial and religious capital of the Phoenician coast. Situated at the foot of Lebanon's timber-bearing mountains, it was an ideal port for exporting cedar wood and oil, traded with Egypt for gold, alabaster, linen and papyrus. The Greeks called this port "Byblos" after their word for papyrus -- and from Byblos comes the word "Bible." It was here that an ancestor of the Greek alphabet was developed.

The old part of town is dominated by the 12th-century **Crusader castle**, one of the main attractions. From its towers you look down over the excavated remains of the ancient city -- temples, fortifications and houses spanning thousands of years. The **Temple of the Obelisks** is notable for the many miniature obelisk stones found within it. Nearby, a Greco-Roman amphitheatre overlooks the sea, and the tombs of Phoenician kings yielded the oldest alphabetic inscription ever discovered.

The **old harbour** has been restored and is one of the most charming spots on the Lebanese coast -- small, picturesque and lined with seafood restaurants. The souk behind it is pleasant for browsing, with shops selling handicrafts, antiques and local products.

Byblos is an easy half-day or full-day trip from Beirut, and is one of the most rewarding destinations in Lebanon.
