Tripoli (Trablos in Arabic) is Lebanon's second city, about 85 km north of [Beirut](/asia/lebanon/beirut), and arguably the most underrated destination in the country. While most visitors focus on the capital, Tripoli has extraordinary Mamluk architecture, the best souks in Lebanon, and a grittier, more traditional atmosphere that feels a world away from Beirut's cosmopolitan scene.

The city's name comes from "triple city" -- it was the centre of a Phoenician confederation with Sidon, Tyre and Arados Island. The medieval old town, spreading from the foot of the Crusader-era Citadel of Raymond de Saint-Gilles (Castle of St. Giles), is the real draw. The covered souks are still organised by trade -- jewellers, spice merchants, soap makers, goldsmiths -- and feel genuinely unchanged in centuries.

The Mamluk monuments are outstanding. The Mosque of Taynal and the Mosque of Al-Attar are beautifully decorated, and the Islamic schools (madrassas), khans, towers and hammams scattered through the old town testify to Tripoli's importance in the Middle Ages. The city is also famous for its sweets -- Hallab is the most renowned sweet shop, but there are many others.

Tripoli is predominantly Sunni Muslim and more conservative than Beirut. It is poorer too, and shows fewer signs of the reconstruction money that has flowed into the capital. But it is safe, welcoming and full of character. The seafood in the Mina (port) district is excellent, and the city makes a good base for exploring northern Lebanon and the coast toward Batroun.
