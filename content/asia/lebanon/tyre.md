Tyre (Sour in Arabic) was one of the great cities of the ancient world -- a Phoenician powerhouse that defied Nebuchadnezzar for 13 years and held out against Alexander the Great for seven months before he built a causeway from the shore to the island fortress. That causeway silted up over the centuries, turning the island into a peninsula. In 1980, UNESCO designated Tyre a World Heritage site for its impressive Roman and Phoenician remains.

The archaeological sites are spread across two areas. Al-Bass, near the main road, has a monumental Roman hippodrome, one of the largest ever found, along with an elaborate necropolis and a triumphal arch. Al-Mina, in the old city, has the remains of Crusader buildings, Roman colonnades and a road of columns leading to the ancient harbour.

Beyond the ruins, Tyre has something rare in Lebanon: a long, sandy public beach. It is one of the best beaches in the country -- clean, free and uncrowded by Beirut standards. The old city retains a working fishing-port atmosphere with narrow streets, colourful boats and a slower pace of life.

Tyre is about 80 km south of [Beirut](/asia/lebanon/beirut). The southern region was heavily affected by the 2024 conflict, and travelers should check current security conditions before visiting. When accessible, Tyre can be visited as a day trip from Beirut, though it deserves more time if you can spare it.
