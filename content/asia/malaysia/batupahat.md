Batu Pahat is the second-largest town in Johor State, a regional commercial centre on the west coast of the Malaysian peninsula about 110 km northwest of Johor Bahru. The name means "chiselled stone" in Malay — a reference to an ancient well in the coastal village of Kampung Minyak Beku, said to have been cut by Siamese craftsmen and associated in legend with the warrior Hang Tuah. The town is not a major tourist destination but functions as the market and service hub for a wide agricultural and industrial hinterland.

The town's economy has long been based on rubber, palm oil, and textiles. Batu Pahat has developed a reputation across Malaysia as a textile centre, with fabric and garments available at lower prices than in the big cities — Jalan Penggaram's row of shophouses is the place to look. The large Chinese community has deep roots and maintains a strong kopitiam (coffee shop) culture; the pasar malam (night markets) that rotate through different streets each evening are among the best in southern Johor for hawker food.

One genuinely unusual draw is the population of Lesser Adjutant storks (burung botak) that roost and feed in the wetlands around Taman Parit Labis on the town's edge. These large endangered wading birds — nearly as tall as an adult human — are rarely seen in Malaysia, and Batu Pahat is one of the best places in the country to observe them. Bird watchers make specific trips here for this reason.
