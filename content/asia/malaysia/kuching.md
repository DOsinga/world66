Kuching is the capital of Sarawak, the Malaysian state that occupies the northern third of Borneo, and it is by a considerable margin the most pleasant and interesting city on the island. The name means "cat" in Malay, though the origin of the name is disputed. The city's character is shaped by its long history as the seat of the Brooke family — the White Rajahs who ruled Sarawak as a private kingdom from 1841 to 1941 — and by its layering of Malay, Chinese, and indigenous Dayak cultures that coexist with unusual ease.

The Sarawak River divides the city. On the south bank, the Kuching Waterfront esplanade runs past colonial-era buildings, food stalls, and river views across to the Astana — the White Rajahs' palace, now the Governor's residence — and Fort Margherita, which guarded the river approach to the city. Bumboats cross on request. The Main Bazaar, running parallel to the waterfront, is lined with shophouses selling handicrafts, antiques, and Sarawakian textiles, many of them run by Chinese families who have traded here for generations.

The Sarawak Museum, housed in a Victorian building a short walk from the waterfront, is one of the best ethnographic museums in Southeast Asia. Its collection covers the material culture of Borneo's indigenous peoples with genuine seriousness. The city's food scene rewards exploration: kolo mee (dry noodles), laksa Sarawak (a complex spiced broth), and midin (wild jungle fern) are all worth seeking out at the open-air coffee shops and hawker stalls around Padungan and Carpenter Street.

Kuching is also an excellent base for Sarawak's national parks. Bako — Sarawak's oldest park, reached by road and boat about 37 km north — is exceptional for wildlife: proboscis monkeys, bearded pigs, and multiple forest types in a compact area. Semenggoh Wildlife Centre, 25 km south, is the closest place to see semi-wild orangutans. Further out, Gunung Gading hosts the giant rafflesia flower, and Annah Rais longhouse offers a genuine introduction to Bidayuh community life.
