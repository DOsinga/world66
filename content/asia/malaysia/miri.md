Miri is a city of about 300,000 on the northern coast of Sarawak, Malaysian Borneo, close to the Brunei border. It was built on oil — the first oil well in Malaysia was drilled here in 1910 on Canada Hill, which now has a small petroleum museum — and the city retains traces of its colonial-era boom in its waterfront and older districts. Today Miri is primarily known as the gateway to two of Borneo's most spectacular natural destinations.

Gunung Mulu National Park, a 30-minute flight from Miri, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site containing the world's largest cave chamber (Sarawak Chamber), the Deer Cave with its nightly exodus of millions of bats, and the dramatic limestone Pinnacles reached by a two-day jungle trek. Niah National Park, three hours by road, has prehistoric caves where human remains dating back 40,000 years were found and where locals still harvest edible birds' nests. For visitors interested in Borneo's indigenous cultures, Miri is also the departure point for flights to Bario in the Kelabit Highlands.
