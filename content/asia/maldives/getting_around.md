---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

The Maldives has no road network connecting islands, so everything moves by boat or plane. Getting from the airport to your resort or guesthouse is the first logistics puzzle — and often the most expensive part of the trip.

**Seaplanes** are the most scenic option and reach the greatest number of resorts. Trans Maldivian Airways, the world's largest seaplane operator, serves over 80 resorts. The flight itself — skimming over turquoise atolls — is genuinely spectacular. The catch: seaplanes only operate in daylight (roughly 6am to 4:30pm). If your international flight lands at night, you'll need to overnight in Malé or a transit resort and take the seaplane the following morning. One-way fares to distant atolls typically run US$200–400 per person, usually pre-arranged through your resort.

**Speedboats** are the workhorse of short- and medium-distance travel. Resorts within an hour or two of Malé almost always use them — faster, cheaper than seaplanes, and operable at any hour. Shared speedboat transfers between the airport and nearby resorts cost roughly US$50–100 per person each way; private charters cost more. Atoll Transfer and similar operators run scheduled public speedboat services between islands.

**Public ferries** connect Malé to the inhabited islands of each atoll on fixed schedules. They're cheap — a few dollars per journey — and perfectly comfortable, but slow and infrequent. They don't go to resorts. If you're doing guesthouse tourism on local islands, the ferry system is your friend; build in flexibility for your schedule.

**Domestic flights** serve airstrips on several of the outer atolls, including Addu (Gan), Hanimaadhoo, and Kaadedhdhoo. Maldivian (the national carrier) and FlyMe operate these routes. A domestic flight typically runs 40–60 minutes and costs US$100–200 one way.

In Malé itself, taxis are available and there are bicycles for hire, though the island is small enough to walk across in 20 minutes.
