Malé is one of the most densely packed capitals on earth — roughly 200,000 people crammed onto an island barely two kilometres across. It is noisy, crowded, and unlike anything you will see on the resort brochures. That is exactly why it is worth a visit.

The city sits a short ferry ride west of Velana International Airport. Most visitors pass through without stopping, which is a shame. A few hours walking the narrow streets gives you a feel for actual Maldivian life that no resort island can offer.

The Old Friday Mosque (Hukuru Miskiy), built in 1658 from coral stone, is the oldest mosque in the country and well worth seeing. Nearby, the Islamic Centre's golden dome dominates the skyline — you can spot it from islands across the atoll. The fish market on the waterfront is loud, pungent, and fascinating, especially in the late afternoon when the dhonis come in with the day's catch. Tuna gets butchered on the spot.

The artificial beach on the south side of the island is where locals go to swim and socialise in the evenings. Sultan Park, the only real green space, offers a quiet break from the concrete. The National Museum inside the park has a small but interesting collection of royal artefacts and pre-Islamic coral carvings.

Malé has had a turbulent political history, and security can be tight in certain areas — pay attention to any photography restrictions, particularly around government buildings.
