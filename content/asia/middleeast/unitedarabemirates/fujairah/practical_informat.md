One of 7 emirates in the UAE, Fujairah entered the UAE in 1971 and is currently the smallest emirate. Covering nearly 440 square miles, Fujairah is almost entirely mountainous. The higher elevation brings about more rainfall, making it a perfect location for agricultural pursuits.

**Population

**The current population of Fujairah is around 130,000 inhabitants. The gender ratio in the UAE is 2.743 male to female. This statistic was taken from an age group of males and females in the 15 to 65 age range.
**
Weather

**The UAE does experience seasons but it remains warm the majority of the year. However, this is still something to consider when planning a trip. March is the coolest month out of the year with daytime temperatures averaging around 77 degrees. In the summer, temperatures regularly climb over 100 degrees F. Pack cool clothes and plenty of sunblock. **

Language

**The main language spoken in Fujairah is Arabic. Schools are beginning to teach English as a second language which is spoken in the majority of businesses, as well.

**Religion

**Over 80 percent of the population in Fujairah, as well as the UAE, practices Islam. Because of the influence of other cultures in recent years, religions such as Christianity, Hinduism and Sikhism have begun to pop up all over the country. The local government allows the practice of other religions as long as it doesn't interfere or influence Islamic religious practices in any way.
**
****Currency

**The currency of Fujairah, as well as the rest of the UAE, is the Emirati Dirham which is commonly referred to as the UAE dirham.
**
Dress and Etiquette

**The majority of men and women in the UAE wear the traditional garb of a abaya for women (a black over-garment covering all but the eyes) and for men, a kandura which is a ankle-length white linen shirt. Western-style attire is becoming more and more popular in places such as bars and clubs but in definitely not in public places where Islamic culture is rampant. As this is a law that is highly enforced and can lead to arrest, visitors are expected to respect and conform to this tradition.
