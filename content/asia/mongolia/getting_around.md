---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Getting around Mongolia is slow, often uncomfortable, and can be genuinely difficult. This is part of the experience. The country is vast, the roads are mostly dirt tracks, and distances are enormous.

**By jeep** is how most of Mongolia gets visited. Russian-built furgons (minivans) and Toyota Land Cruisers are the standard vehicles. Almost every traveler outside Ulaanbaatar ends up in one — either hired privately with driver, or sharing a public jeep with locals. Expect breakdowns, river crossings, and navigation by GPS and memory. A private jeep with driver and guide is expensive by Mongolian standards but often the only practical option for remote areas.

**By domestic flight** is the fastest way to cover long distances. MIAT and Aero Mongolia serve most provincial capitals. Flights are cheap, schedules change regularly, and the aircraft are small props. Seats sell out fast in summer — book early. Foreigners used to pay more than Mongolians, though this gap has narrowed. There is no online booking for some routes; you may need to book in person or through a local agent.

**By bus and shared transport** between towns is possible and cheap. Buses connect Ulaanbaatar with most major towns; minibuses and shared jeeps are faster and nearly as cheap. Services depart when full rather than on a fixed schedule. For long distances — Ulaanbaatar to Olgii in the far west, for example — expect 24+ hours on rough roads or a combination of short flights.

**By train** within Mongolia, there is only one main line: the north-south Trans-Mongolian corridor from Sukhbaatar (Russian border) through Ulaanbaatar to Zamyn-Uud (Chinese border). A handful of towns along this line are reachable by train. It is cheap, reliable, and limited.

**By horse** is impractical for first-time visitors to organize independently, but tour operators in Ulaanbaatar can arrange horse trekking expeditions ranging from a few days to several weeks.

**By car independently** is technically possible with an international driving licence, but not recommended for first-timers. There are few paved roads, petrol stations are scarce outside major towns, and navigation without local knowledge is genuinely hazardous.

Only about 2,400 kilometres of Mongolia's roads are paved. Plan your trip with realistic time estimates — 100 kilometres of steppe track can take four hours.
