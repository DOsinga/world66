Stretching across southern Mongolia and northern China, the Gobi is one of the world's great deserts — and one of its most surprising. It's not the sea of sand you might picture: much of it is rocky steppe, dramatic cliffs, and gravel plains, with dunes appearing only in certain pockets like the famous Khongor Els. Summer days are scorching and nights are cold; winters are brutal. Spring brings fierce sandstorms.

For travelers, the Mongolian Gobi — especially the Gobi Gurvansaikhan National Park — is the main draw. The park holds the best of it: dinosaur fossil sites, ice-filled canyons that persist year-round, vast singing dunes, and the chance to stay with nomadic families in gers. Wildlife includes Bactrian camels, snow leopards, and the rare Gobi bear.

Most visitors fly from Ulaanbaatar to Dalanzadgad, the regional capital, and then hire a jeep and guide to reach the sites. Self-driving is technically possible but demanding — tracks are unmarked and GPS is essential. A typical visit runs four to seven days.
