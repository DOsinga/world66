**Visas**

Citizens of around 60 countries can enter Mongolia visa-free. Americans, Germans, French, British, Canadians, and most other Western nationals can stay for 30 days without a visa (some nationalities get 90 days). Mongolia has extended its visa exemption policy through the end of 2026. Others need to obtain a visa in advance — visas are no longer reliably available on arrival. Apply through your nearest Mongolian embassy or the official e-visa portal. Processing typically takes 3–5 working days; apply at least two weeks before you travel.

If you plan to stay more than 30 days, register with the Mongolia Immigration Agency in Ulaanbaatar within the first seven days of arrival. Failure to do so results in fines and delays when leaving.

**Money**

The currency is the Mongolian Tugrik (MNT). ATMs are widely available in Ulaanbaatar — look for Khan Bank, Golomt Bank, and Trade and Development Bank. Outside the capital, ATMs exist in larger towns but become increasingly unreliable. In rural areas, assume cash only and bring enough from the city. Most ATMs accept Visa and Mastercard. Exchange rates are better in the city than at the airport; currency exchange offices in Ulaanbaatar offer competitive rates. Do not exchange money on the street.

Cards are accepted at upmarket hotels and some restaurants in Ulaanbaatar. Everywhere else, cash is king.

**Language**

Mongolian (Khalkha dialect) is the official language. The Cyrillic script is used everywhere — learning to read a few basic characters helps with navigation. English is spoken at tourist-oriented businesses in Ulaanbaatar and barely anywhere else. Russian is understood by older Mongolians; Mandarin is increasingly spoken by younger people near the Chinese border. Hire a guide if you plan to travel independently in the countryside — communication without one is genuinely difficult.

**Safety**

Mongolia is relatively safe. Violent crime against tourists is rare outside Ulaanbaatar. In the capital, pickpocketing is common on public transport and in crowded areas, particularly around the State Department Store and Sukhbaatar Square. Be cautious of people posing as police demanding to see documents — they may be attempting robbery. Avoid walking alone late at night in central Ulaanbaatar.

In the countryside, the main dangers are practical: broken-down vehicles, river crossings, and extreme weather. Always travel with a guide, carry sufficient water and food, and let someone know your itinerary.

**Health**

Mongolia is at altitude — Ulaanbaatar sits at 1,350 metres, and much of the country is higher. This is not a serious concern for most people but worth noting if you have heart or breathing conditions. The Gobi in summer is brutal heat; carry more water than you think you need.

Medical facilities in Ulaanbaatar are adequate but not excellent. Outside the capital, facilities are very limited. Travel insurance with medical evacuation cover is strongly recommended.

Tap water in Ulaanbaatar is technically safe but bottled water is cheap and widely available. In the countryside, treat water or carry your own.

**Climate**

Expect extreme temperature variation. Summer (June–August) days reach 25–35°C; nights drop to single figures. Winter temperatures regularly hit -30°C or below. Come prepared for cold even in summer, especially in the north and at altitude.

**Tipping**

Not a strong local custom, but appreciated at tourist-oriented restaurants in Ulaanbaatar. Round up or leave 10% if the service was good. For guides and drivers, a cash tip at the end of a trip is expected and important — it forms a significant part of their income. A common rule of thumb is USD 10–15 per day for a guide and USD 5–10 per day for a driver.
