Ulaanbaatar — UB to everyone who goes there — is the capital of Mongolia and home to about half the country's entire population: 1.5 million people in a basin at 1,350 metres, surrounded by four sacred mountains and some of the emptiest steppe on earth. It is a strange, compelling city — part Soviet concrete, part glass-and-steel boom town, part ger (yurt) district where nomadic families who have moved to the city live in fenced compounds of traditional felt tents within sight of the high-rises. In winter it is one of the coldest capitals in the world, regularly hitting -30°C; in summer it is warm, dusty, and alive with the preparations for the Naadam festival.

Most visitors to Mongolia pass through UB on the way to the countryside — the Gobi, the Khentii mountains, the Orkhon valley — and the city works best as a base for organising those trips. But it has a few things worth a day or two. The **Gandantegchinlen Monastery** (Gandan), in the west of the city, is the most important Buddhist monastery in Mongolia — destroyed under the Soviets, rebuilt since 1990, and now home to a 26-metre gilded statue of the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara and a community of working monks. The **National Museum of Mongolia** gives a strong overview of the country's history from Stone Age to Soviet era. The **Chinggis Khaan Square** (formerly Sükhbaatar Square) is the ceremonial centre of the city, with the Parliament House and a large seated statue of Genghis Khan.

UB's food scene has improved enormously: Mongolian cuisine (buuz — steamed dumplings, khuushuur — fried meat pies, airag — fermented mare's milk) alongside a growing number of Korean, Japanese, and international restaurants. The **Naran Tuul** (Black Market) is the city's vast outdoor bazaar where you can buy anything from a saddle to a satellite dish, and it is the best people-watching in the city.

The **Naadam Festival** (11–13 July) is the reason to time a visit: three days of the "three manly games" — wrestling, horse racing (with child jockeys riding 30 km across the steppe), and archery — in and around the national stadium. It is Mongolia's defining cultural event and one of the great spectacles in Central Asia.
