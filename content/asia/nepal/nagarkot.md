Nagarkot is a small hilltop resort village on the eastern rim of the **Kathmandu Valley**, about 32 km from Kathmandu and reached by a winding two-hour drive up through terraced rice fields and rhododendron forest. At 2,175 metres it is one of the highest points accessible by road on the edge of the valley, and its reason for existing is a single extraordinary feature: on a clear morning, the view from Nagarkot takes in eight of the world's highest mountain ranges, including the snow walls of **Manaslu**, **Ganesh Himal**, **Langtang**, **Jugal**, **Rolwaling**, and — weather permitting — even a distant glimpse of **Everest** itself to the north-east.

There is not much to the village. A few dozen lodges and resorts of varying quality cling to the ridge, and most visitors come for a single night: up in the afternoon, dinner as the sun sets on the Himalayas, an early wake-up call at 5 am to bundle up on a rooftop and watch the peaks turn pink and then gold in the first light, then breakfast and back to Kathmandu. The **sunset** and **sunrise** over the mountains are the main attractions; the **view tower** at the top of the village is the traditional gathering point. Clear views are much more reliable in autumn (October–November) and spring (February–April) than in the monsoon months; winter can also be excellent but is cold at night.

For those who want to stretch their legs, a good **day hike** runs from Nagarkot down through forest and villages to the old Newari town of **Changu Narayan**, with its UNESCO-listed 4th-century temple — about 4–5 hours downhill, ending at one of the most important ancient sites in the valley. Bus or taxi back to Kathmandu from there. Nagarkot is also easily combined with the sacred hilltop temple of **Shivapuri** and the medieval Newari city of **Bhaktapur** on the same trip.
