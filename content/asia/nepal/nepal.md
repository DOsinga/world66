Nepal is one of those places that changes you. Sandwiched between India and China, this small Himalayan kingdom holds eight of the world's ten tallest mountains, a staggering variety of cultures and ethnic groups, and some of the most dramatic trekking terrain on earth. It is also one of the poorest countries in Asia — something you'll feel immediately in [Kathmandu](/asia/nepal/kathmandu)'s chaotic streets and in the worn faces of porters carrying impossible loads up mountain trails.

The country divides roughly into three zones. The Terai, a flat subtropical strip along the Indian border, is warm, humid, and home to Chitwan National Park's rhinos and tigers. The Middle Hills are a patchwork of terraced rice fields, forests, and market towns where most Nepalis live. The high Himalaya begins above 3,500 metres — the trekking country that draws visitors from around the world.

[Kathmandu](/asia/nepal/kathmandu) is the entry point for almost everyone, and despite its notorious traffic and pollution, it rewards patience. The old city's medieval squares — Durbar Square, Swayambhunath, Boudhanath, Pashupatinath — contain temple architecture and living culture that can keep you busy for days. [Pokhara](/asia/nepal/pokhara) is the gateway to the Annapurna region and the most relaxed place in the country to recharge between treks.

Nepal is not an easy destination. Roads are rough, power cuts are common, altitude can knock you flat, and the bureaucracy around trekking permits is genuine. But the hospitality of ordinary Nepalis and the sheer scale of the landscape make it worthwhile for almost everyone who comes.

Nepal was the world's only Hindu kingdom until 2008, when it became a federal democratic republic after a decade-long civil war. It remains deeply religious — Hindu and Buddhist traditions intertwine throughout the country, and the festival calendar is packed year-round.
