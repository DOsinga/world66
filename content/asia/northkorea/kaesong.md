Kaesong is the most historically interesting destination in North Korea after Pyongyang, and the easiest to justify on purely cultural grounds. The city was the capital of the Koryo dynasty (918–1392), which unified the Korean peninsula and gave the country its name — "Korea" is a Western rendering of Koryo.

The old city centre retains a handful of well-preserved sites. The **Koryo Museum** occupies a former Confucian academy (Sungkyunkwan) and houses a good collection of celadon pottery, bronze bells, and other Koryo-era artefacts. The **Sonjuk Bridge** nearby is where the scholar Chong Mong-ju was assassinated in 1392, ending the dynasty. A red stain on the bridge is said to be his blood — or the iron content of the stone, depending on who you ask. Either way, locals treat it as sacred.

The **Tomb of King Kongmin** outside the city is a UNESCO-recognised site: twin burial mounds for the 31st Koryo king and his Mongol queen, flanked by carved stone animals and guardians. The setting is peaceful and the stonework worth examining.

Kaesong sits just north of the DMZ, about 160km south of Pyongyang. Most visits are day trips from the capital. The city also hosts the Kaesong Industrial Complex, a joint inter-Korean economic zone that operated from 2004 until South Korea suspended it in 2016 — the kind of geopolitical detail that gives Kaesong an additional layer of interest for anyone following Korean affairs.
