---
title: "People"
type: section
---

North Koreans are Koreans — culturally, linguistically, ethnically the same people as those across the 38th parallel — shaped by 75-plus years of isolation, state ideology, and entirely different history. Figuring out what you are actually observing when you interact with them is one of the more intellectually challenging aspects of visiting.

Contact is limited. Your guides are your primary North Korean interlocutors. They are selected and trained for this role, fluent in English, and deeply embedded in the state apparatus. This does not make them robots. Most visitors find their guides to be genuinely engaging, quick-witted, and willing to discuss a surprising range of topics — carefully. Friendships of a kind form. Some guides have been doing this work for decades and take real pride in it.

Ordinary citizens encountered on the street, in parks, at cultural performances, will typically ignore foreign visitors or smile politely if you make eye contact. There is no hostile response to foreigners. There is very little spontaneous conversation. This is both the product of caution and, in many cases, genuine disinterest — tourists are not uncommon in Pyongyang by local standards.

The Arirang Mass Games, when they run, offer an unexpected window. The performers — drawn from schools, factories, and the military — are doing something technically extraordinary, and whatever its ideological framing, the experience of being in an audience of 100,000 watching 30,000 people move as one is genuinely moving.

Pyongyang is not representative of the country. The capital is home to the privileged, the loyal, and those who have earned the right to live there. The people you see on wide boulevards cycling to work, shopping in modern malls, or strolling through Moranbong Park are the beneficiaries of the system. What life is like for the 25 million people you do not see is a different question entirely, and one the country's literature, defector memoirs, and human rights reports document at length.
