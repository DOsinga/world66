**Visas**

 Visas are becoming increasingly easy to get, especially for nationals of "advanced" countries. The Omani government is mostly interested in high-end tourists from Europe, Australia, Japan, and North America, so those nationals have an easy time of it. Indeed, it is now possible to get a 14-day tourist visa on arrival, either at the airport or at one of the land border crossings from the United Arab Emirates. It's not cheap, however: It currently costs R.O. 20 (1 Omani rial = US $2.58) although that is slated to be halved in the near future. You can, of course, apply for the visa in advance and get it at an Omani embassy abroad.

 **Air**

The national airlines of Oman are *Gulf Air* *(GF)* * *which it jointly owns with the governments of Abu Dhabi Bahrain and Qatar; and *Oman Air (WY)*. Other carriers serving Muscat include Lufthansa, Air France, Swissair, KLM, British Airways, Emirates, Royal Jordanian, Kuwait Airways, Saudia, Air India, Pakistan International, Air Lanka, and Thai International.

 **Nonstop flights**

 There are nonstop flights to Muscat from the following cities: London, Zurich, Amsterdam, Paris, Colombo, Bombay, Nairobi, Zanzibar, Dar-es-Salam, and Cairo.

Many flights to Muscat will have an intermediate stop in the Gulf, Dubai being the most common.

**Approximate flight times**

From Muscat to *London* is 8 hours 10 minutes to *Singapore* 6 hours 30 minutes and to *Sydney* 16 hours. Nairobi is 5 hours away, Bombay about 3, and Cairo 3 3/4.

**International airport:**

*Muscat (MCT) *(Seeb International) 40km (25 miles) west of the city (travel time – 15-30 minutes). Airport facilities include 24-hour bank/bureau de change 24-hour duty-free shops 24-hour bar and light refreshments and car hire *(Avis, Budget*,*Thrifty,* and *Hertz)*. There are several reliable local companies as well. Costs range from an absolute low of around R.O. 14 per day with insurance to R.O. 80-100 or so for luxury sedans. A 4x4, which you need to explore the many wonderful and beautiful *wadis* (dry water courses), will cost you around R.O. 40 (ca. US $110) per day.

Taxis and buses to the city are available.

Departure tax

OR5 for all departures. Children under 12 years old are exempt. On tickets purchased in Oman, however, the airport tax is included in the fare.

**Sea**

The main ports are Sultan Qaboos and Mina Raysut (near Salalah); a new port is being built in Sur. Traffic is mainly commercial. However, *effective this coming June (2001),* a new passenger ferry service between Muscat, Bombay (Mumbai) and Cochin is being introduced. This was just announced a few days ago, so the information is brand new.

This new ferry service, run by Bahrain-based **Tylos Ferry** will actually ply the following route: Umm Qasr (Iraq!) - Bahrain - Doha (Qatar) - Dubai (UAE) - Muscat - Mumbai - Cochin and return.

**Road**

Overland travel into Oman has been greatly simplified since the introduction  of reciprocal visa validities for holders of Omani or UAE visas. If you have one visa, you may enter the other country without further formality. This also  makes it possible to rent a car in Dubai, let's say, and drive into Oman.

**Road Conditions and Main Routes**

 Omani highways are generally excellent. There is a four-lane motorway that runs along the coast from the UAE border all the way to Muscat with a new branch being built to Nizwa in the interior. At present this spur road is only complete as far as Bidbid, but this will soon change.

 The road to Salalah, in Oman's southernmost province of Dhofar, is two-lane, however. Although the surface is excellent, there is comparitively little traffic, which means that drivers tend to take a lot of unnecessary chances, barreling along at horrific speeds. The route is especially dangerous at night, not only because of wild drivers, but because camels and other animals that may be wandering along it -- or simply sleeping on it.
