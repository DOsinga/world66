Dera Ismail Khan — commonly abbreviated to D.I. Khan — is a city on the west bank of the Indus River in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, [Pakistan](/asia/pakistan). Founded in the late 15th century by the Baloch adventurer Ismail Khan, it sits 200 miles west of Lahore at the edge of the tribal areas, where the plains meet the mountains.

The city is a commercial centre for the surrounding agricultural region, known especially for the Dhakki Date, a prized variety grown in nearby villages and exported to the Middle East, Europe, and the United States. Sohan Halwa, a dense sweet made from wheat sprouts and ghee, is the city's famous delicacy.

Tourist attractions are modest but include the pre-Islamic fort of Bilot, on a hill near the Chashma highway, and the archaeological ruins at Handeray, about 45 kilometres along the Indus Highway. A historic Sikh shrine in the old bazaar marks the spot where Guru Nanak is said to have stayed. The city's bazaars — Topanwala, Bhatiya, Muslim, and Kalan — converge at the Chowaglla intersection and are worth exploring for the local crafts, including lacquered woodwork, glass and ivory ware, and woven mats.

D.I. Khan is connected by road to Bannu and Peshawar via Kohat, and to Mianwali and Punjab via the Chashma Barrage bridge. The airport is 10 kilometres from the city centre.
