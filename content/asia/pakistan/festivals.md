---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Pakistan has a rich calendar of religious and cultural festivals. Dates for Islamic festivals shift each year according to the lunar calendar.

**Eid ul-Fitr** marks the end of Ramadan with three days of prayer, feasting, and family gatherings. Cities come alive at night during Ramadan itself — stalls appear on the streets after sundown, and the atmosphere in places like [Lahore](/asia/pakistan/lahore) old city is extraordinary. Eid is worth experiencing but book accommodation and transport well in advance.

**Eid ul-Adha**, the Festival of Sacrifice, comes roughly 70 days after Eid ul-Fitr. Animals are slaughtered and the meat distributed to family, neighbors, and the poor. It's a major event across the country.

**Shandur Polo Festival** (usually early July) is one of the great spectacles of the country. Teams from [Chitral](/asia/pakistan/chitral) and Gilgit-Baltistan play three days of free-style polo at the Shandur Pass at 3,734 metres — reputedly the world's highest polo ground. Folk music, bonfires, and dancing accompany the matches. Getting there requires planning but it is memorable.

**Nauroz** (March 21), the Persian New Year, is celebrated in [Gilgit](/asia/pakistan/gilgit), [Hunza](/asia/pakistan/hunzavalley), Skardu, and [Chitral](/asia/pakistan/chitral) with polo matches, traditional sports, music, and dancing. It marks the start of spring in the mountains.

**Mela Chiraghan** (Festival of Lamps, late March) takes place outside the Shalimar Gardens in [Lahore](/asia/pakistan/lahore). Lamps are lit, music is played, and it draws a large crowd — a rare urban festival in the Punjab.

**Lok Mela** (early October, Islamabad) runs for a week and showcases crafts, folk music, dancing, and traditions from across the country. A good opportunity to see the cultural diversity of Pakistan in one place.

**Kalash Festivals** — the [Kalash people](/asia/pakistan/chitral) of the Chitral valleys celebrate three major festivals: Chilam Joshi (spring, May), Uchal (summer, August), and Choimus (winter solstice, December). These are genuinely unusual events — colorful, participatory, and one of the few glimpses of a pre-Islamic Dardic culture still alive. Visitors are welcome but numbers are growing; be respectful.

**Urs** (death anniversaries of Sufi saints) are observed throughout the year at shrines across the country. The Urs of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar in Sehwan (Sindh) and Shah Abdul Latif Bhitai in Bhit Shah draw enormous crowds of devotees and feature intense nights of qawwali and dhamal — ecstatic devotional music and dance.
