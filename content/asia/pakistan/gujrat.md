Gujrat is a small city in Punjab province, about 120 kilometres southeast of Islamabad on the main road between Lahore and the capital. It sits between the Chenab and Jhelum rivers, the middle of the three great rivers of the Punjab, and has an old agricultural and trading history — it was an important stop on the Grand Trunk Road for centuries.

The city is famous within Pakistan for two things: pottery and fans. Gujrat pottery, particularly its blue-glazed earthenware, has been produced here for generations, and the city is one of the largest producers of ceiling fans in South Asia. Neither is a major tourist draw on its own, but it's a useful glimpse of Pakistan's industrial north.

For travellers, Gujrat is mainly a stopover. The old quarter around the shrine of Shah Daula, with its small alleys and busy bazaars, is worth an hour or two. Two annual fairs — the Mela Shah Jehangir (said to mark the spot where Mughal emperor Jahangir's internal organs were buried on the way to Lahore) and the Shah Daula festival — animate the city briefly. For most visitors, though, Gujrat is a place to pause between Islamabad and Lahore rather than a destination in its own right.
