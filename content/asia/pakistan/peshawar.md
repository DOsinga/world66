Peshawar is one of the most colourful cities in Pakistan — and that is saying a lot. Twenty kilometres from the mouth of the Khyber Pass, it has been the gateway between South and Central Asia for two thousand years, and that frontier character still runs through its bazaars, its cuisine, and its streets. Pashto is the language of the city. Most men wear the *shalwar kameez* and a *pakol* or turban. The city is proudly Pashtun.

The old city — the **Qissa Khwani Bazaar**, the "Storytellers' Bazaar" — is still one of the great markets of the subcontinent, piled with dried fruit, pomegranates from the Khyber, and brass. The **Bala Hisar Fort**, built by Babur in the 16th century, dominates the city skyline. The **Peshawar Museum** holds one of the world's finest collections of Gandharan Buddhist sculpture — the artistic legacy of the centuries when this region mixed Greek, Persian, Indian, and Buddhist influences into something genuinely original.

The security situation has improved since the Taliban-era violence of the late 2000s, but travel in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa still requires care. Foreigners have generally been welcome, though the [Khyber Pass](/asia/pakistan/khyberpass) itself now requires a permit and an armed escort. Check local conditions before you go.

The food is a reason to come. **Chapli kebab** — a flat minced-meat kebab with pomegranate seeds — was invented here; the best are on Namak Mandi street. **Peshawari naan** is a sweet stuffed bread eaten hot from the tandoor.
