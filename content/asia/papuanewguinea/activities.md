**Diving and Snorkelling**

PNG is world-class for diving. Kimbe Bay on New Britain has some of the highest coral biodiversity on the planet, with over 400 coral species and 900 fish species recorded, alongside plunging walls, seamounts, and strong current dives. Tufi on the northeast mainland sits at the head of a series of fjords with pristine reefs dropping into deep water. Milne Bay, in the far southeast, is known for its extraordinary variety of nudibranchs and was where the manta ray birostris was first scientifically recorded. Around Rabaul, WWII wrecks — Japanese freighters, fighters, and submarines — lie in shallow, clear water. Most dive operators also work as live-aboard operators for multi-day expeditions.

**Trekking**

The Kokoda Track is the flagship — see [Highlights](/asia/papuanewguinea/highlights) — but there are other excellent trekking routes. The climb to the summit of Mount Wilhelm, PNG's highest peak at 4,509 metres, takes two days from the village of Keglsugl. The Black Cat Track between Wau and Salamaua follows another WWII route through rugged terrain. Most serious trekking requires a local guide, and most trekkers go through organised operators.

**Birdwatching**

PNG is one of the world's great birdwatching destinations. The country has 38 species of birds of paradise, more than anywhere else on earth. Varirata National Park above Port Moresby and Ambua Lodge near Tari in the Southern Highlands are two reliable spots. The Huon Peninsula is particularly rich in endemic species. A local guide is essential — they hear and identify birds that visitors would otherwise miss entirely.

**Rafting and Kayaking**

Several rivers in the Highlands, including the Watut and the Jimi, offer whitewater rafting. The Tufi fjords are excellent for sea kayaking, with calm water, village stops, and reef snorkelling along the way.

**Cultural Tourism**

Village stays, guided walks to *haus tambaran* (spirit houses), and visits to traditional markets in the Highlands are available through local operators and lodge networks. The sing-sing festivals in August (Mount Hagen) and September (Goroka) are the most accessible introduction to Highland culture and are well worth timing a trip around.
