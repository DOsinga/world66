---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Eating well in Papua New Guinea depends largely on where you are. Port Moresby has a reasonable range of restaurants — Chinese (Cantonese and Szechuan), Korean, Japanese, Indian, and a handful of Pacific-influenced places — mostly concentrated in the Waigani, Boroko, and Harbour City areas. Outside the capital, options narrow quickly, and in remote areas you eat what is available locally: rice, tinned fish, sweet potato, and whatever the market has that day.

Local cuisine centres on starchy staples — kaukau (sweet potato), taro, sago, and cassava — combined with vegetables, coconut milk, and whatever protein is available. On the coasts, fish and seafood feature prominently. In the Highlands, pork and chicken are the main proteins. The national dish, *mumu*, is a slow-cooked feast of pork or chicken, sweet potato, taro, and greens, wrapped in banana leaves and cooked in an underground oven for special occasions.

Worth trying if you get the chance: *kokoda*, raw fish marinated in lime juice and coconut milk (PNG's version of ceviche), is fresh and delicious on the coast. *Saksak* is a sago dumpling steamed with banana and coconut. Highlands Arabica coffee, grown at altitude in the Waghi Valley and elsewhere, is genuinely excellent — rich and low-acid. You can buy fresh-roasted beans at markets in Goroka and Mount Hagen.

The betel nut (*buai*) is chewed constantly across the country; red-stained pavements are a universal feature of PNG towns. It is mildly stimulating and perfectly legal. Alcohol is widely available in towns.
