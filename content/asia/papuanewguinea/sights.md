---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Papua New Guinea's major sights are scattered across an enormous country with very limited infrastructure. Most require flying to a regional hub and then arranging local transport or a guided trip. This is part of what makes them so rewarding.

**The Highlands**

The Highlands run down the spine of the mainland at altitudes between 1,500 and 4,500 metres. This is the most densely populated part of the country and the region with the most extensive road network, though "extensive" is relative. Towns like Mount Hagen and Goroka are bases for exploring the valleys, traditional markets, and the communities that make the Highlands culturally distinctive.

Mount Gahavisuka Provincial Park near Goroka has orchid gardens and forest walks. Lake Kutubu, one of PNG's largest lakes, sits in the Southern Highlands and is reachable by small plane from Mendi; the Foi people who live around it are known for their bark cloth art.

**The Sepik Region**

The Sepik River and its tributaries flow through lowland rainforest in the north of the country. This region produces some of PNG's most celebrated art — carved spirit figures, ancestor poles, and crocodile masks — and its *haus tambaran* (spirit houses) are architectural wonders. Chambri Lake, the Maprik area (home of the Abelam people and their elaborate yam cults), and the coastal town of Wewak are all access points.

**Rabaul and East New Britain**

Rabaul was devastated by volcanic eruptions in 1994 and again in 2014, but it is still worth visiting. The harbour is set inside a caldera — you are surrounded by active volcanoes — and the floor of the bay holds dozens of Japanese warships from World War II, now spectacular dive sites. The Kokopo Museum nearby documents the Japanese wartime occupation and the island's colonial history.

**Madang**

On the north coast, Madang is often called the prettiest town in PNG. It sits on a peninsula surrounded by lagoons, and the diving and snorkelling here are excellent. Kranket Island is a short boat ride away and has traditional villages and easy reef snorkelling.

**Port Moresby**

The capital is not a beautiful city, but it has some genuine sights: the National Museum and Art Gallery has good collections of traditional art, masks, and WWII material. The National Parliament building, designed in traditional Sepik style, is worth seeing. Varirata National Park on the plateau above the city has forest walking and good birdwatching, including birds of paradise.
