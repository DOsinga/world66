The Trobriand Islands are a small coral archipelago in the Solomon Sea, about 100 kilometres north of the eastern tip of Papua New Guinea's mainland. Made famous by the anthropologist Bronisław Malinowski, who spent years here during World War I studying the Kula ring exchange system, the islands retain a strong traditional culture that sets them apart from much of PNG.

Kiriwina, the largest island, is flat and fertile — a landscape of yam gardens, coconut palms, and villages built around open plazas where communal life plays out. The Trobrianders are matrilineal, and yam harvests remain central to social prestige. Cricket matches, introduced by missionaries but transformed into something entirely local — with elaborate dances, chanting, and teams of up to 50 players — are a highlight if your timing is right.

Getting here requires a flight from [Port Moresby](/asia/papuanewguinea/portmoresby) or Alotau to Losuia airstrip. Accommodation is basic — village guesthouses are the main option. Bring cash, as there are no ATMs. The diving and snorkelling around the reefs is excellent, and the islands see very few visitors.
