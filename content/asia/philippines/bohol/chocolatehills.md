In the dry season the Chocolate Hills are exactly what the name suggests: over a thousand chocolate-brown, cone-shaped mounds spread across the interior of Bohol. In the wet season they turn a vivid green and lose their chocolate resemblance, but they are still a remarkable sight. The hills are probably of volcanic origin, though local legend claims they are the tears of giants who once lived on the island.

To get there, take a bus from [Tagbilaran](/asia/philippines/bohol/tagbilaran) to Carmen, from where you can reach the viewing complex by motorbike or on foot — it is only about four kilometres. The trip through the interior is as interesting as the hills themselves. Along the way you can stop at the church in Baclayon, one of the oldest in the Philippines (built in 1595), cross a hanging bridge over the Loboc River, and watch local artisans making basketware and other native products. The rice fields along the road are intensely green and worth the drive on their own.
