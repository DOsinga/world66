Calamba is best known as the birthplace of Jose Rizal, the national hero of the Philippines. The Rizal Shrine, a reconstructed version of the house where he was born in 1861, sits in the town center and is a genuinely worthwhile stop. The museum inside holds original manuscripts, personal effects, and family photographs that bring Rizal's story to life in ways that Manila's larger monuments do not quite manage.

Beyond the Rizal connection, Calamba's main draw is its hot springs. The city sits at the foot of Mount Makiling, and the geothermal activity in the area feeds dozens of resorts ranging from simple concrete pools to elaborate water parks. The springs along the national highway in Pansol are the most accessible, and you can rent a private pool by the hour for very little money. The water temperature varies from pleasantly warm to genuinely scalding, so test before you leap.

Calamba also serves as a gateway to Los Banos, home to the University of the Philippines campus and the International Rice Research Institute, and to the hiking trails on Mount Makiling itself. The local food scene benefits from the Laguna tradition of buko pie (coconut pie) and espasol (rice flour treats), both sold at roadside stalls along the highway.
