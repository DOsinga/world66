Caloocan is one of the oldest cities in Metro Manila, and the one that started a revolution. On 26 August 1896, Andres Bonifacio tore up his cedula — his Spanish colonial registration paper — in what became known as the Cry of Pugad Lawin, the opening act of the Philippine Revolution against Spain. That moment happened here, and Caloocan wears it with pride.

Divided into two non-contiguous parts by the cities of Malabon and Navotas, Caloocan is a densely populated working-class city that few foreign tourists visit. The northern section, sometimes called North Caloocan, is the larger and more residential half; the southern section near the old city centre is where the historical landmarks cluster. The LRT Line 1 runs through the southern part, making it easy to reach from central Manila.

For most travelers, Caloocan is a day trip from Manila rather than a base. The Bonifacio Monument at Rizal Avenue Extension is the city's defining landmark — a dramatic bronze statue group honoring the Katipunan's founding father. The Manila North Cemetery, one of Southeast Asia's largest, sits partly within Caloocan and is a genuinely extraordinary place: a city within a city where families live alongside the dead in elaborate mausoleums.

The city is poor and congested, with traffic as chaotic as anywhere in Metro Manila. But it is not without energy or character, and its historical significance to Philippine nationhood gives it a weight that the tidier tourist districts lack.
