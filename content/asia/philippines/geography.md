---
title: "Geography"
type: section
---

The Philippines consists of around 7,100 islands and islets scattered across the western Pacific, between Taiwan to the north and Indonesia and Malaysia to the south. The total land area is slightly larger than Italy, but the coastline — at roughly twice the length of the United States' — reflects just how fragmented the territory is.

The archipelago is usually divided into three main island groups. **Luzon** in the north is the largest island and contains [Manila](/asia/philippines/manila), the capital, as well as the Cordillera highlands, the Ilocos coast, and the rice terraces of Ifugao. **The Visayas** are a cluster of islands in the centre — [Cebu](/asia/philippines/cebu), [Bohol](/asia/philippines/bohol), Leyte, Negros, Panay, and Samar among them — connected by a web of ferry routes. **Mindanao** in the south is the second largest island and the most culturally diverse, with significant Muslim and indigenous communities in its highlands and interior.

[Palawan](/asia/philippines/palawanisland) sits apart from the three main groups, stretching southwest toward Borneo in a long, narrow archipelago that has some of the most unspoiled marine environments in the country.

The terrain is almost universally mountainous. Jungle covers much of the interior; rice terraces cut into the Cordillera slopes; and 21 active volcanoes dot the landscape, including Mayon (whose near-perfect cone is visible from [Legazpi](/asia/philippines/legaspi)) and Taal, which sits inside a lake south of Manila and erupted most recently in 2020.

The country sits on the Pacific Ring of Fire and experiences frequent earthquakes. Typhoons (locally called *bagyos*) track across Luzon and the Visayas from the Pacific between June and November, sometimes causing severe damage.
