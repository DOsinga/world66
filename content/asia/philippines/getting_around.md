---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Getting around the Philippines requires accepting that almost everything routes through Manila or Cebu. Fly direct from Manila to the wrong island and you may still have to backtrack. Plan your route with this in mind.

**By air** is the fastest and often most practical way to cover large distances. Cebu Pacific and AirAsia dominate domestic routes, with fares that are remarkably cheap if booked ahead. Philippine Airlines flies the same routes with more legroom and reliability. Smaller propeller services reach Busuanga (for Coron), El Nido, and other island destinations that larger planes cannot.

**By ferry** connects most major islands and is an experience in itself on longer routes. 2GO Travel operates large overnight vessels between Manila, Cebu, Davao, Zamboanga, and many island ports — not fast, but cheap and sociable. For shorter hops, OceanJet and other fast-craft operators cut crossing times significantly. On smaller islands, the *banca* (outrigger boat) is the universal inter-barangay transport.

**Jeepneys** are the iconic fixed-route vehicles found in every city and town — elongated, decorated, and cheap. The government has been pushing modernized e-jeepneys with air conditioning and USB ports, which are steadily replacing the old diesels in many cities. Fares are typically ₱13–25 for short urban routes.

**Tricycles** (motorcycles with a sidecar) fill in where jeepneys don't reach. They're ideal for the last mile between town and beach, but negotiate the fare beforehand — a short ride should cost ₱20–50, though tourists often get quoted much more.

In Manila, **Grab** (the regional Uber equivalent) works well and avoids metered taxi uncertainty. Most major cities now have Grab coverage.

Roads between major cities on Luzon are reasonable, and renting a car or hiring a private van with driver makes sense for multi-stop trips through the highlands. On other islands, road quality drops sharply, and many travellers rent motorbikes — useful freedom, but exercise real caution on unmarked mountain roads.
