Kalinga is a mountainous province in the Cordillera region of northern Luzon, known for its dramatic river gorges, rice terraces, and the living traditions of the Kalinga people. The name comes from the Ibanag word for "headhunters" — a practice long abandoned but still part of the province's fierce identity. Elderly Kalinga women still bear the traditional tattoos (*batok*) that once marked warriors and their wives.

The main draw for visitors is the whitewater rafting on the Chico River, some of the best in the Philippines. The capital Tabuk is the jumping-off point for rafting trips and for treks into the remote villages where the *bodong* peace pact system — an indigenous conflict resolution institution — still governs intertribal relationships.

Getting here takes some effort. Buses run from Manila to Tabuk (about 10–12 hours), or you can fly to Tuguegarao and take a jeepney (about 90 minutes). The climate is wet from May to October, with typhoons common from July to October.
