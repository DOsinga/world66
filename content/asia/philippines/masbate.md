**Masbate** is one of the least-visited provinces in the Philippines — an island group in the Bicol region, at the centre of the Philippine archipelago, made up of three main islands (Masbate itself, **Ticao**, and **Burias**) and a number of smaller islets. It is the country's cattle-ranching capital, a quiet, sparsely populated place of rolling pastures and long empty beaches, and a place most Filipino travellers know about but hardly anyone actually goes to. That, of course, is the main reason to visit.

Masbate's two best-known attractions reflect its unusual character. The annual **Rodeo Masbateño** — held every April in Masbate City — is the largest rodeo in the Philippines, a genuine cattle-country event with bronc riding, bull wrestling, and lasso competitions, and is the closest thing the country has to an American western. And **Ticao Island**, off the north-east coast, has become a serious destination for divers in search of **manta rays**: the shallow reefs around **Ticao Pass** attract large populations of mantas that come in to feed on plankton, and a few small dive lodges have opened to cater to them. The waters around Masbate and Ticao are also known for **whale sharks** (seasonal, mostly March–June) and some of the less-disturbed coral reefs in the country.

Otherwise, Masbate is a place for quiet beaches, fishing villages and long empty drives along the coast. **Buntod Sandbar** near the capital is a strip of white sand that appears at low tide; **Bituon Beach** and **Bitu-on Beach** on the west side are worth the ride out. Getting there takes some effort — small planes fly from Manila to Masbate City, and there are ferries from Bulan in Sorsogon — but the reward is a corner of the Philippines that still feels untouched by mass tourism.
