Zamboanga City sits at the southwestern tip of Mindanao's Zamboanga Peninsula, fronting the Sulu Sea. Known as "Asia's Latin City" for its Chavacano-speaking population and Spanish colonial heritage, it has a distinct character unlike anywhere else in the Philippines — a blend of Catholic, Muslim, and indigenous Yakan cultures that reflects its history as a trading crossroads.

The most photographed sight is **Rio Hondo**, a water village on stilts at the mouth of the river, where the houses are painted in vivid colours. **Fort Pilar**, built by the Spanish in 1635 and still standing near the waterfront, houses a museum of history and culture. The town's market is lively and sells goods from nearby Sulu and Tawi-Tawi. The offshore Santa Cruz Islands have good snorkelling and diving.

Zamboanga has a history of conflict — the southern Philippines has long been subject to tension between the government and Moro separatist groups — and travellers should check current security conditions before visiting. The city itself has been relatively stable in recent years and has a functioning international airport with connections to Manila.
