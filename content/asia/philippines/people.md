---
title: "People"
type: section
---

The Philippines has a population of around 115 million, making it one of the most populous countries in Asia. Filipinos are predominantly Malay in descent, with significant Chinese ancestry in the cities and traces of Spanish, American, and indigenous Austronesian heritage woven throughout the culture. Over 180 languages are spoken across the archipelago; the main regional languages include Cebuano (dominant in the Visayas and Mindanao), Ilocano (northern Luzon), Hiligaynon, and Waray, in addition to Filipino and English.

Catholicism is central to Filipino life in a way that is hard to overstress: roughly 80% of the population is Roman Catholic, and the Church permeates everything from the school calendar to local politics. Fiestas in honor of patron saints are celebrated in virtually every barangay, and Good Friday is observed with a seriousness — including, in some towns, actual crucifixions — that surprises first-time visitors. In Mindanao, around 5% of the population is Muslim, concentrated in the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao.

Filipinos are widely regarded as among the most hospitable people in Asia. Hospitality (*pagpapahalaga sa panauhin* — the value placed on guests) is a genuine cultural value, not a tourism slogan. Visitors are often offered the best seat, the best food, and invited to join family occasions with little prior acquaintance. The Filipino sense of humor is quick, self-deprecating, and very much present even in difficult circumstances.

Overseas Filipino workers (OFWs) form a huge part of the social fabric — roughly 10 million Filipinos work abroad at any one time, mainly in the Middle East, Hong Kong, Singapore, and the United States. The remittances they send home are a pillar of the national economy. The result is a country deeply connected to the outside world and generally comfortable with foreigners.

Filipino society places family (*pamilya*) at the center of everything. Extended family networks provide economic support, childcare, and social identity. This gives life in the Philippines a warmth and communal quality that visitors frequently remark on.
