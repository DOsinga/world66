---
title: "Practical Information in Asia"
type: section
---

**Visas** vary enormously across the continent. Many Southeast Asian countries — [Thailand](/asia/thailand), [Malaysia](/asia/malaysia), [Indonesia](/asia/indonesia), [Vietnam](/asia/vietnam) — offer visa-free or visa-on-arrival entry for most Western passport holders. [Japan](/asia/japan) and [South Korea](/asia/south_korea) are similarly easy. Others require advance planning: [China](/asia/china) still requires a pre-arranged visa with supporting documents, [India](/asia/india) has an e-visa system that works well but must be sorted before you fly, and Central Asian countries like [Turkmenistan](/asia/turkmenistan) and [Uzbekistan](/asia/uzbekistan) range from bureaucratic to genuinely difficult. Always check current requirements — they change frequently.

**Money** is where Asia shows its range. Southeast Asia remains one of the most affordable regions on earth — you can eat well in [Vietnam](/asia/vietnam) or [Cambodia](/asia/cambodia) for a few dollars a day. [Japan](/asia/japan), [Singapore](/asia/singapore), and the Gulf states are expensive by any standard. ATMs are widespread in cities and tourist areas, though rural parts of [Laos](/asia/laos), [Myanmar](/asia/myanmar), and Central Asia may require you to carry cash. [Japan](/asia/japan) is still more cash-dependent than you would expect. In much of [China](/asia/china), cash has practically disappeared — everyone pays by phone through WeChat Pay or Alipay, and as a foreign visitor, setting these up can be tricky. Carry US dollars as backup in countries with unstable currencies.

**Languages** across Asia span dozens of families, from Indo-European in South Asia to Sino-Tibetan, Austronesian, and Turkic elsewhere. English gets you surprisingly far in [India](/asia/india), [Singapore](/asia/singapore), the [Philippines](/asia/philippines), and [Malaysia](/asia/malaysia). In [Japan](/asia/japan) and [South Korea](/asia/south_korea), English signage is common but conversational English is limited outside tourist areas. In [China](/asia/china) and much of Central Asia, very little English is spoken — a translation app on your phone is essential. Learning even a few words in the local language makes a real difference everywhere.

**Safety** across Asia is generally good for travelers, though it pays to stay informed. Petty theft and scams targeting tourists are common in busy cities across South and Southeast Asia — the usual street smarts apply. Some regions have active travel advisories: border areas in [Myanmar](/asia/myanmar), parts of southern [Thailand](/asia/thailand) and the [Philippines](/asia/philippines), and [Afghanistan](/asia/afghanistan). Natural disasters — typhoons in East and Southeast Asia, earthquakes across the Pacific Ring of Fire, monsoon flooding in South Asia — are a bigger practical concern than crime for most visitors. Register with your embassy and check current conditions before heading to remote areas.

**Connectivity** is one of Asia's strengths. Mobile coverage is excellent across most of the continent — often better than in Europe or North America. Local SIM cards are cheap and easy to buy in most countries. In [China](/asia/china), the internet sits behind the Great Firewall: Google, WhatsApp, and most Western social media are blocked, so install a VPN before you arrive. WeChat is essential for daily life there. Grab and Gojek have replaced taxis across much of Southeast Asia. Free WiFi is common in cafes and hotels, though speeds in rural areas can test your patience.
