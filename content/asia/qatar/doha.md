Doha has grown up fast. Sixty years ago it was a pearl-diving port of perhaps fifteen thousand people; today it is the capital of one of the richest countries on earth, a city of three million where roughly 90 per cent of residents are foreign workers, and the skyline of West Bay competes with Dubai's and Abu Dhabi's for glossy 21st-century statement.

The city has done one thing better than either Gulf rival: spent some of its oil money on serious cultural institutions. I.M. Pei's **Museum of Islamic Art**, on its own island at the end of the Corniche, is one of the best museums of its kind anywhere. Jean Nouvel's **National Museum of Qatar**, the great stacked "desert rose" that opened in 2019, is the natural follow-up. **Mathaf**, the Arab Museum of Modern Art, fills out the trilogy.

The old city has mostly been demolished and replaced, but **Souq Waqif** — rebuilt in traditional style in the 2000s — is the liveliest place in town after dark. The **Corniche**, the seven-kilometre crescent along Doha Bay, is where you walk in the cool of the evening. For the beach, head out to the **Pearl** or further down the coast to the new developments at **Lusail**.

Summer (May–September) is brutal: 45°C-plus with high humidity. Come between November and March, when evenings are pleasant and the Corniche fills with families. Qatar grants visa-on-arrival to most Western passports; alcohol is served in hotel bars but not otherwise.
