---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Qatari cuisine is built on the flavors of the Gulf — dried limes, cardamom, saffron, rosewater — with strong influences from India, Persia, and the East African coast, legacy of centuries of trade. The traditional dishes are rich, slow-cooked, and made for sharing on large platters.

**Machboos** (also spelled majboos) is the national dish: spiced rice cooked with chicken, lamb, or hammour (a local grouper), flavored with dried lime and a warming blend of spices. It is not complicated food, but done well it is deeply satisfying. **Quzi** is a whole roasted lamb on spiced rice, typically served at feasts and celebrations — you will find it in traditional restaurants. **Madrouba** is a creamy broken-rice porridge cooked with meat and spices, comfort food from before Qatar had central heating. **Luqaimat** are fried dough balls soaked in date syrup, the classic Ramadan street dessert.

The best place to find traditional Qatari food is **Souq Waqif** in Doha, where several restaurants serve machboos and quzi to both tourists and locals. **Bayt Sharq** in the Al Khulaifi Heritage House does a classic machboos. **Saasna** in Msheireb is a smaller, more contemporary cafe focused on preserving Qatari recipes.

Doha received its first Michelin Guide in 2025, with 33 restaurants recognized including two one-star venues. The restaurant scene is diverse — Indian, Lebanese, Japanese, and Western cuisines are all well represented. Prices at good restaurants are broadly comparable to Western European cities.

Alcohol is not served at most restaurants, only at hotel bars and licensed hotel restaurants. Tea and coffee are universal. Arabic coffee (qahwa) — lightly brewed with cardamom and saffron, served in small cups — is the traditional hospitality drink. If offered, drink it; declining is considered impolite.
