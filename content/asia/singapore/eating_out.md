---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Eating is Singapore's primary cultural activity and its greatest pride. UNESCO inscribed Singapore's hawker culture on its Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2020 — the first street food tradition to receive that recognition. Whether or not you think UN agencies should be making pronouncements about food courts, the hawker centres genuinely are outstanding, and they are cheap.

A hawker centre is an open-air complex of individual food stalls, each specialising in one or two dishes. The food is cooked to order, served on plastic trays at communal tables, and costs S$3–8 for a full plate. Reserving a table is done by leaving a packet of tissues on it (this custom is known locally as "choping"). Bring cash; many stalls don't take cards, though this is slowly changing.

**Dishes to eat.** Hainanese chicken rice — poached or roasted chicken with fragrant rice cooked in chicken stock, served with chilli sauce, ginger paste, and dark soy — is widely considered Singapore's national dish, and it is exceptional when well made. Char kway teow is stir-fried flat rice noodles with cockles, Chinese sausage, egg, and bean sprouts; look for woks blackened with years of use. Laksa is a coconut curry noodle soup with deep roots in Peranakan culture. Bak kut teh (pork rib herb soup) is a traditional breakfast or supper dish. Roti prata — flaky flatbread cooked on a griddle, served with curry sauce — is the best cheap breakfast in the city, found at any Indian Muslim stall.

**Where to eat.** Maxwell Food Centre in Chinatown is probably the most famous hawker centre, home to Tian Tian chicken rice. Old Airport Road Food Centre in Geylang is larger and more local in feel, and many Singaporeans consider it the best. Lau Pa Sat (Telok Ayer Market) is in the business district and gets busy with office workers at lunch. Newton Food Centre is tourist-heavy but still good.

**Kopitiam.** The traditional coffee shop — a grid of basic tables, a few stalls selling kaya toast, soft-boiled eggs, and strong local coffee brewed through a sock-like filter — is the backbone of Singapore breakfast culture. Ya Kun Kaya Toast in Far East Square is the famous one; any neighbourhood kopitiam is just as good and cheaper.

**Beyond hawker centres.** Singapore has excellent restaurants at every price point. The Peranakan (Straits Chinese) cuisine found at restaurants in Joo Chiat and Katong is worth seeking out — dishes like ayam buah keluak (chicken with black Indonesian nut) and kueh pie tee have no equivalent elsewhere. The cluster of restaurants and bars in Tanjong Pagar, Keong Saik Road, and Duxton Hill is where the more interesting local chefs are working.

Alcohol is expensive by Asian standards — a beer in a bar typically costs S$10–15. Hawker centres generally don't sell alcohol or have a stall that does; drink Milo, fresh sugarcane juice, or the excellent local barley water instead.
