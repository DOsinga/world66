---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Singapore's festival calendar is dense and genuinely multicultural — the three main ethnic communities each bring their own major celebrations, and most are public holidays. Timing a visit around one of them significantly changes the experience of the city.

**Chinese New Year** (January or February, depending on the lunar calendar) is the biggest event of the year. The lead-up is weeks of preparation: Chinatown is strung with lights and lanterns, street markets open on Terengganu and Pagoda Streets, and families spring-clean and stock up on mandarin oranges to exchange as gifts. On the eve and first two days, the city is quieter as families gather for reunion dinners. The Chingay Parade — a spectacular street procession of floats, acrobats, and dancers — takes place on Marina Bay in the days after New Year. The celebration lasts fifteen days.

**Thaipusam** (January or February) is one of the most arresting public spectacles in Southeast Asia. Hindu Tamil devotees — having fasted and prayed in preparation — carry kavadis (ornate metal frames pierced into the skin with hooks and skewers) in a procession from Sri Srinivasa Perumal Temple on Serangoon Road to Sri Thendayuthapani Temple on Tank Road. It is an act of devotion and endurance. Spectators are welcome; dress respectfully and do not disrupt the procession.

**Hari Raya Puasa** (date shifts annually with the Islamic calendar) marks the end of Ramadan. The Malay community celebrates with new clothes, family visits, and open houses. Geylang Serai — the traditional Malay quarter in the east — fills with a large bazaar in the weeks before, selling traditional food, textiles, and decorations. The atmosphere is warm and welcoming.

**Deepavali** (October or November) lights up Little India. For several weeks, the streets are strung with elaborate light installations and the shops and stalls overflow with sweets, flowers, and saris. The Festival of Lights commemorates the triumph of good over evil in Hindu tradition; in Singapore it is a genuinely public celebration that draws visitors from all communities.

**National Day** (9 August) marks Singapore's independence in 1965. The National Day Parade at the Padang or the floating platform at Marina Bay is a major production with military displays, mass performances, and fireworks. The rehearsals in the week before are almost as spectacular and are free to watch from the waterfront.

**Singapore Food Festival** (July) celebrates what many consider the country's greatest contribution to world culture. Hawker stalls, special menus, and food tours run across the city for several weeks.
