Sentosa is Singapore's playground island, a 500-hectare resort destination just off the southern coast connected to the mainland by a short causeway, a cable car, and a pedestrian boardwalk. What was once a British military fortress and later a quiet fishing village has been engineered into one of Southeast Asia's most concentrated entertainment zones. Whether that sounds like paradise or an overmanaged theme park depends on what you are looking for.

The island's biggest draw is Universal Studios Singapore, the anchor of the Resorts World Sentosa complex on the northern end. Around it cluster an aquarium (S.E.A. Aquarium, one of the largest in the world), a water park, a casino, and a stretch of restaurants and shops. If you have children, you can easily spend a full day here without seeing anything else on the island.

The southern side of Sentosa is quieter and more interesting. Three beaches — Palawan, Siloso, and Tanjong — run along the coast, all artificial but well-maintained, with calm water and beach bars. Siloso is the liveliest, with volleyball courts and weekend crowds; Tanjong is the most secluded. Fort Siloso, near the western tip, preserves a coastal gun battery from the 1880s that saw action during the fall of Singapore in 1942 — it is worth a visit for the history alone.

Getting to Sentosa is easy. The Sentosa Express monorail runs from VivoCity mall at HarbourFront MRT station, or you can walk across the boardwalk for free. Once on the island, free shuttle buses, a beach tram, and walking paths connect the main attractions. Go on a weekday if you can — weekends bring heavy crowds, especially at Universal Studios and the beaches.
