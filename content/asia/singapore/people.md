---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Singapore has a population of around 6.1 million, of whom about 3.7 million are citizens and the rest permanent residents or foreign workers on various passes. The proportion of foreign-born residents is unusually high — roughly one in three — which gives the city an internationally mobile quality alongside its deep-rooted local communities.

The ethnic Chinese community makes up about 75% of residents, descendants of migrants who came mostly from Fujian and Guangdong provinces in southern China during the 19th and early 20th centuries. Malay Singaporeans, roughly 15%, are indigenous to the region and the broader Malay Archipelago. Indian Singaporeans, around 8%, are mostly Tamil in origin, with smaller Punjabi, Malayali, and other South Asian communities. The remainder includes Eurasians with mixed European and Asian heritage, and a significant expatriate population from around the world.

Four languages carry official status: English, Malay, Mandarin, and Tamil. English is the main language of government, education, business, and everyday interaction across ethnic groups. Malay is the national language — the anthem is sung in Malay — reflecting the nation's position within the broader Malay world. In practice, most Singaporeans are bilingual or trilingual. Singlish, the informal creole that blends English with Malay, Hokkien, and Cantonese vocabulary and grammar, is the genuine vernacular, and it is very much alive despite official efforts over the years to promote standard English instead.

Religion is taken seriously. The Malay community is almost entirely Muslim. Chinese Singaporeans are predominantly Buddhist or Taoist, with a substantial Christian minority. The Indian community is largely Hindu, with significant Muslim and Sikh minorities. The government invests considerable effort in managing inter-ethnic and inter-religious relations, and the coexistence is mostly peaceful and genuine — though the boundaries between communities remain real in daily life, from language to neighbourhood to food.

What binds Singaporeans across ethnic lines is a shared pride in what the country has achieved and a collective memory of where it came from. The independence generation watched a modern state built from scratch in one generation. The tensions of that story — authoritarian governance, the suppression of political dissent, ethnic management from above — are more openly discussed now than they used to be.
