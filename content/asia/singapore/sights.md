---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Singapore is a small island, and its sights are concentrated and easy to get between. The great dividing line is between the historic ethnic districts — which reward slow walking — and the newer waterfront developments, which are spectacular but designed for tourists.

**Gardens by the Bay.** Built on reclaimed land and opened in 2012, this is the most spectacular thing Singapore has built in recent years. The Supertrees — towering vertical gardens lit up each night in a sound-and-light show — are extraordinary. The two conservatories (the Flower Dome and the Cloud Forest, with its indoor waterfall) are worth the admission fee, especially on a hot afternoon. Free to enter the grounds; conservatories charge separately.

**Marina Bay Sands.** The three towers topped by a surfboard-shaped SkyPark are the defining image of modern Singapore. The observation deck on the 57th floor (SkyPark Observation Deck) gives the best panoramic view of the city and harbour. The casino, hotels, and mall below are deliberately overwhelming in scale.

**Chinatown.** The shophouses along Pagoda, Trengganu, and Sago Streets preserve the look of old Singapore, and the area genuinely functions as a commercial and religious hub. The Buddha Tooth Relic Temple on South Bridge Road is a modern building housing what is claimed to be a tooth of the historical Buddha; the rooftop garden is open to visitors. The Chinatown Heritage Centre gives good context on the lives of early Chinese migrants.

**Little India.** The streets around Serangoon Road and the Tekka Centre market are among the most sensory parts of the city — flower garlands, incense, spice shops, and the smell of fresh roti. Sri Veeramakaliamman Temple (on Serangoon Road) is one of the most vivid and active Hindu temples in Singapore; photography is allowed outside, ask before shooting inside. Mustafa Centre is the 24-hour department store famous for everything, particularly cheap electronics and good exchange rates.

**Kampong Glam (Arab Quarter).** The neighbourhood around Arab Street and Haji Lane has the Sultan Mosque, Malay heritage shophouses, and an improbable number of good cafes and boutiques in converted buildings. Haji Lane in particular is worth an hour of wandering. The Malay Heritage Centre (in the former Istana Kampong Glam palace) covers the history of Malay communities in Singapore.

**The Colonial District.** The area around the Padang contains the best surviving colonial architecture: the Victoria Theatre, the National Museum, the Supreme Court, St Andrew's Cathedral, and Raffles Hotel. The hotel's Long Bar — where the Singapore Sling cocktail was invented — is a tourist institution; have one drink there and move on.

**Bukit Timah Nature Reserve.** Singapore has preserved a patch of genuine primary rainforest in the middle of the island. Bukit Timah is the highest point in the country (163 metres), and the trails are good for two to three hours of walking. Macaques are common; do not feed them or carry food openly. A useful escape from the city.

**Sentosa Island.** A resort island just off the southern coast, connected by cable car, monorail, and a pedestrian bridge. Universal Studios Singapore is the main attraction for families. The beaches are artificial but pleasant enough. The whole island is built for tourism, which is either fine or dispiriting depending on your mood.
