For most of Singapore's history, the river was its commercial heart — lined with godowns (warehouses), crowded with bumboats and tongkangs, and frankly quite smelly. A massive cleanup effort starting in the late 1970s transformed it into the leisure corridor it is today.

The river divides neatly into three stretches. Boat Quay, closest to the mouth, packs dozens of restaurants and bars into a row of conserved shophouses along the south bank. Clarke Quay, further upstream, is louder and more tourist-oriented, with clubs and themed bars in brightly painted buildings. Robertson Quay, at the far end, is the quietest section — good cafes, galleries, and a pleasant morning walk.

Walking the full length from the Merlion at the river mouth to Robertson Quay takes about 40 minutes along shaded promenades. The bumboat river cruises (about 25 minutes) cover the same stretch from the water and run until late evening. The best time to walk is early morning or after dark when the quays light up.
