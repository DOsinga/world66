---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's"
type: section
---

**1. Eat at a hawker centre**

Go to Maxwell Food Centre in Chinatown or Old Airport Road in Geylang, find a seat at a communal table, and work your way through the stalls. Order Hainanese chicken rice from the Tian Tian stall (arrive before noon; it sells out). Eat char kway teow from somewhere with a blackened wok. Drink iced barley water or fresh sugarcane juice. Pay S$15–20 for a full meal. This is what Singapore does better than anywhere else in the world.

**2. Walk the ethnic districts at street level**

Start in Kampong Glam at the Sultan Mosque in the morning, walk south through the Arab Street textile shops to Haji Lane, then catch a bus or MRT to Little India and follow Serangoon Road south through the flower and spice traders. Then on to Chinatown — the Buddha Tooth Relic Temple, the back lanes off Pagoda Street, the dim sum places. Do this all in one day. By the end you understand the layering that makes Singapore genuinely interesting.

**3. Gardens by the Bay at night**

Come in the evening, walk the free outdoor grounds, and watch the Supertree light show (it runs at 7:45pm and 8:45pm nightly). The trees — towering steel and plant structures covered in ferns and bromeliads, lit from within — are remarkable. If it is your first time in Singapore, this is the one thing that announces the city is attempting something genuinely new.

**4. The Night Safari**

Singapore Zoo's night zoo, in the Mandai area in the north, is one of the best wildlife experiences in Southeast Asia. A tram carries you past open enclosures of civets, tapirs, and slow lorises; walking trails get you closer. The animals are nocturnal and active in a way zoo animals rarely are. Worth booking ahead on weekends.

**5. A kopitiam breakfast**

Before the tourist Singapore swallows your day, find a neighbourhood kopitiam — a traditional coffeeshop — and sit down for kaya toast, two soft-boiled eggs with soy sauce and white pepper, and a cup of kopi (coffee brewed through a flannel filter, served with condensed milk). This meal costs around S$5 and has barely changed in seventy years. Ya Kun Kaya Toast in Far East Square is the famous version; any kopitiam in any housing estate is just as good.
