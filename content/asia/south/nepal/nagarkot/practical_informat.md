When visiting Napal and the city of **Nagarkot, **this **practical information** guide help you become familiar with the lifestyle and traditions of the local people. Being well-informed will help you negotiate travel details and get the most out of your trip.

**Population**

A small village consisting of less than 700 houses, Nagarkot is home to 3000 people. The residents here descend from the Newar people—a community with Tibeto-Burman and Indian-Aryan roots. The Newar ethnic group is the sixth largest ethnic group in Nepal, with its population being 5 percent of the whole country's population. Newars have strong traditions in music, art, dance, cuisine and architecture.

**Language**

The people here speak the Nephal Bhasa language, also referred to as Newah Baye or Newari. This is one of the major languages in Nepal with over 800 000 people using it and it can be also heard in some parts of India, although in a dialect form. An interesting fact is that Nephal Bhasa is the only language from the Tibeto-Burman familiy, which is written in the Devanagari writing system. Nephal Bhasa has a rich history regarding literature, with its literature being the 4th oldest from the Sino-Tibetan languages.

**Religion**

The Newars worship both Buddhism and Hinduism, with most of them following the latter religion. However, feasts of both religions are being celebrated. In addition the Newar people have their own specific rituals and organize a number of festivals tied to their beliefs. Some of the more interesting rituals are related to the stages of life and mark important changes in man&rsquo;s life cycle.

**Economy
**

Nagarkot&rsquo;s beautiful mountain landscape makes the village an attractive tourist destination and many of the resident villagers are engaged in the tourism business. Nagarkot is often referred to as the Gateway to Himalaya Mountain. However, tourism isn&rsquo;t the only source of income for the locals, as the people here are also involved in farming and breeding.

**Currency
**

Finally, here is some important information about the local currency. The official currency in Nepal is the Nepalese Rupee (or Nepali Rupayiaah as the locals call it). One rupee is divided into 100 paise, with $1 being equal to about 75 Nepalese rupees. Since Nagarkot is a small village, you&rsquo;d better change your money in Katmandu, where you&rsquo;ll find a large number of government and private money exchange centers.
