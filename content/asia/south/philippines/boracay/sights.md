---
title: "Sights in Boracay"
type: section
---

**Boracay Sights **provide the ideal getaway, with their tranquility and unique landscape, wrapped in unspoiled, natural surroundings. Boracay isn't a very big island; its coastline is just about four kilometers long. However, its pristine waters and white, powdery beaches make it an ideal vacation spot. Further, the upper reaches of the Boracay Island are lined with small, lush-green hills. Boracay has often been voted among the most scenic of Asian tourist destinations. Some of the most popular Boracay Sights include:

**Mount Luho**

While most mountains in Boracay can be easily accessed through daytime treks, Mount Luho is an exception. This mount is the highest natural elevation in Boracay. This is one of the few mountains in Boracay that is remote and totally uninhabited. Since trekking towards the highest point of Luho isn't feasible for most tourists, special arrangements can be made. There are mountaineering camps where professional trekkers use a cable system and other gadgets to help visitors climb up Boracay. It is generally believed that the peak of Boracay offers the most comprehensive sight of mainland Philippines and its surrounding islands. However, visitors need to be guarded against inclement weather conditions, as rains are often intermittent and violent in Boracay. The lower reaches of Boracay have been converted into a resting spot for tourists. Here, visitors can lounge for hours, suspended in hammocks.

**Dead Forest**

The name is spooky, to say the least, but the Dead Forest isn't as frightening as it seems to be. Located along the southern part of Boracay Island, this forest sprawls across a unique network of small fish-ponds. The word &ldquo;dead&rdquo; is used because these trees have long been lifeless. However, their silhouettes have somehow managed to survive. Some people believe that evil spirits are behind the ability of these dead trees to stand tall. The more scholarly believe that the nutrition carried by the surrounding ponds and high level of humidity help the trunks of the trees to remain suspended long after their life cycle ends.

**Bat Caves**

These caves are now a globally popular destination. They are visited by tourists from across the world, particularly student groups from Asian nations. Located along the northern part of the eastern coast of Yapak, these caves are home to a mindboggling number of bats. The bats can make the weak-hearted edgy, with their wingspan of over four feet. These are the giant fruit bats of Philippines, also called the "golden flying foxes" or the "Philippine flying foxes." The Bat Caves are known to have been one of their most historic breeding spots. Visitors here can expect to meet a naturalist, photographer or inquisitive student conducting a survey or collecting samples. The path to the Bat Caves is a bit demanding, as visitors need to trek through small, unsystematically marked jungle roads. but usually, the nearby villagers are eager to lend their guidance in return for a token amount of money. The best time to visit the caves is around the afternoon, when visitors can watch the bats take flight during the sunset.
