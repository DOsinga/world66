Seoul is one of the biggest, densest, and most relentlessly modern cities in the world. Home to roughly half of South Korea's population in its wider metropolitan area, it is a place of endless neon, twenty-four-hour street food, and 5G on the subway — and yet, tucked between the office towers, it still contains five Joseon-era palaces, a city wall, and the shrines and hanok villages that have been there for six centuries.

The city fills a valley on the Han River, ringed by mountains (Bukhansan to the north is a national park right at the city limits). North of the river, Jongno and [Jung-gu](/asia/southkorea/seoul/junggu) are the old city — Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung palaces, the craft-and-tea alleys of Insadong, the hanok lanes of Bukchon, and the market chaos of Gwangjang and Namdaemun. Myeongdong is the tourist shopping heart, brash and crowded. South of the river, [Gangnam](/asia/southkorea/seoul/gangnam) is the money — glass skyscrapers, plastic-surgery clinics, and the Starfield mall. Itaewon is the old foreign quarter, Hongdae the student and indie-music district, and Seongsu the current hipster favourite for third-wave coffee and craft beer.

Food is a reason in itself to come. Korean barbecue and Korean fried chicken are obvious pleasures; the deeper ones are the late-night pojangmacha tents, the 24-hour seolleongtang joints, the temple-cuisine restaurants, the dakgalbi dens, and whatever new fried-rice-cake combination has caught on this month. A day trip north to Panmunjom and the DMZ is the strangest excursion any capital can offer — you can look directly into North Korea from a few kilometres inside the Republic of Korea.
