This capital city (pop. 10 million) set in a pretty valley divided by the Han River covers an area as large as Los Angeles and is home to more than a fifth of the country’s population. Abuzz with traffic and energy it has become one of the best places in the world for those who are there to buy and sell: Its large hotels target the business traveler with impressively equipped executive suites.

Seoul which seems obsessed with modernity is divided into an old and new city. The old city proclaimed the nation’s capital in 1392 was walled until the early 1900s. Some of the gates remain: The Great South Gate which has been designated the premier national treasure was built in 1394 as the principal entrance to Seoul. Unfortunately both it and the Great East Gate aren’t easily inspected—they’re located in the center of traffic roundabouts. To get a true feel for Seoul visit the markets that still operate just inside these gates.

Seoul has emerged as a prime shopping destination in East Asia—you can get better deals than in Bangkok and it’s markets are more interesting than those you’ll find in Hong Kong or Singapore. Some of the best bargains (and people watching) can be found at the South Gate Market. It’s a wholesale as well as retail district and you’ll find sizable shops specializing in just one specific item (we found for instance a store that had nothing but thousands of designs of gift-wrapping paper). The food stalls also give insight into the local culture. East Gate and Tongdaemun are other popular shopping areas (the latter specializes in locally made items). For antiques head to Changan-dong and Insa-dong markets (the latter has unbelievable finds but you won’t see many bargains).

Two other interesting shopping areas are Myong-dong (right downtown it’s where the hip and fashionable youth hang out at night) and Itaewon a suburb with many hotels for Westerners. Itaewon is very much geared to tourists—the shopkeepers all speak English (and know a thing or two about high-pressure sales). There’s a lot of entertainment for tourists in this area as well. And finally one last shopping district deserves mention: Beneath the streets in the downtown area is a huge air raid shelter/pedestrian walkway/shopping mall; because the traffic makes crossing the street above somewhat hazardous the underground passageway is a good way for pedestrians to get around (it also connects to the subway system).

Although an increasing number of people are heading to Seoul simply because of its shopping the city has much more to offer. Some old palaces are right in or adjacent to downtown. Toksu Palace a large parklike area with several fine old structures stands in the shadows of high-rise hotels on the west side of downtown.

On the northern edge of the city center is another complex of museums and palaces. The National Folklore Museum is a must-see: it’s located within Kyongbok Palace which was built in 1395 and renovated in the 19th century. The palace has beautifully designed buildings gardens and pavilions. Directly in front of it is the five-story National Museum of Korea. (Visit the museum sooner rather than later—the Japanese built it in the 1920s and it is blamed by some for disturbing the natural energy flows of the area and causing 75 years of bad luck. The Korean government has considered demolishing the structure.)

Among the city’s other attractions are the Tonggunung tombs Chonsugongbang (traditional artists at work) Changkyong Palace (spectacular lake and pavilion landscaping) Pomun-sa Temple (unique building material) the railroad station (1920s Renaissance style) the National Art Gallery (which is in the southern portion of the city next to the Seoul Grand Zoo) Toksugung Palace (it shows definite Western influence in its architecture) and the Korea House (where you can see demonstrations of traditional lifestyles and building techniques as well as entertainment and restaurant facilities).

The city has a number of beautiful gardens and parks which come alive in the spring and summer. The 78-acre/32-hectare Secret Garden in the Changdok Palace complex (built in 1406) has particularly striking pavilions and landscaped grounds (the palace itself holds beautiful furniture and art treasures; entrance is restricted to group tours). (Near Changdok is Insa-dong the antiques and arts area which is sprinkled with lovely old teahouses along the back alleys.)

For a beautiful view of the city head to the Seoul Tower set atop 900-ft/275-m Namsan Mountain; the tower can be reached by road cable car or on foot.

If time permits take one of the relaxing boat cruises along the Han-gang River (1-2 hours) and visit the Independence Museum. The latter located south of town is anything but relaxing: It displays a catalogue of horrors from the brutal Japanese occupation during the first half of this century. Allow at least four days for Seoul itself; excursions from the city can be made to Panmunjom (to see the DMZ) or Suwon (to see the Korean Folk Village).
