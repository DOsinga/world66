---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

South Korea's sights span millennia, from Silla-era tombs and Joseon palaces to the eerie theatre of the DMZ. The country rewards travelers who get out of Seoul.

**Gyeongju** — once the capital of the Silla Kingdom, which unified Korea in the 7th century, Gyeongju is sometimes called "the museum without walls." The town is surrounded by grassy burial mounds (tumuli) containing royal tombs, and the surrounding hills hold some of the finest Buddhist art in Asia. The Bulguksa Temple and the nearby Seokguram Grotto — a stone rotunda containing a seated granite Buddha of remarkable serenity — are UNESCO World Heritage Sites. The Cheomseongdae observatory, built around 647 AD, is the oldest existing observatory in East Asia and quite small in person, which only makes it more impressive.

**Seoraksan National Park** — in the northeast corner of the country near Sokcho, Seoraksan is probably Korea's most beautiful national park. Granite peaks rise from dense forest; in autumn the foliage is extraordinary. The main trail system is well-maintained, and a cable car provides access for those who prefer not to hike. The Sinheungsa temple at the park entrance dates to the 7th century. Plan at least two days.

**The DMZ** — the Demilitarized Zone, straddling the 38th parallel between South and North Korea, is as strange a sight as you will find in Asia. Half theme park, half Cold War monument, it attracts over a million visitors a year to Panmunjom and the JSA (Joint Security Area), where soldiers of both countries stand metres apart across the border. Tours depart from Seoul and include the Third Infiltration Tunnel, dug secretly by the North. The atmosphere is a combination of genuine geopolitical tension and extraordinary absurdity.

**Haeinsa Temple** — deep in the mountains of South Gyeongsang Province, Haeinsa is one of the three "Jewel Temples" of Korean Buddhism and home to the Tripitaka Koreana — over 80,000 carved wooden printing blocks that preserve the Buddhist canon. They were completed in the 13th century and are still in almost perfect condition. The approach through forested mountain valleys is beautiful.

**Jeju Island** — the volcanic island south of the peninsula is its own world. Hallasan, the highest mountain in South Korea, dominates the interior. The Manjanggul lava tube is one of the world's longest. The Haenyeo, traditional female divers who free-dive for seafood without breathing equipment, represent a culture that goes back centuries and is now UNESCO listed. The west and south coasts have good beaches; Seongsan Ilchulbong (Sunrise Peak) at the eastern tip is a dramatic volcanic tuff cone.

**Gyeongbokgung Palace (Seoul)** — the largest and most impressive of Seoul's five main palaces, built in 1395 and twice destroyed by Japanese invasions before being restored in the 19th century. The changing of the royal guard at the Gwanghwamun Gate is held twice daily. The National Folk Museum is within the palace grounds and gives a useful overview of Korean life through the centuries.
