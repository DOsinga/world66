---
title: "Top 5 Must Do's"
type: section
---

**1. Walk the DMZ**

The Demilitarized Zone north of Seoul is one of the strangest places in Asia — a 4km-wide strip of land where the Korean War never officially ended, watched over by soldiers on both sides. Day tours from Seoul take you to Panmunjom and the Joint Security Area, where you stand metres from North Korean guards across the border. The Third Infiltration Tunnel, secretly dug by the North in the 1970s, you can walk into. The whole experience is part history lesson, part theatre, entirely unforgettable.

**2. Spend a morning at Gyeongbokgung Palace**

The largest of Seoul's royal palaces was built in 1395 and has been twice burned and twice rebuilt. Arrive early on a weekday to avoid the crowds. The changing of the guards at the Gwanghwamun Gate happens at 10am and 2pm daily. Wander through the halls and courtyards, then walk ten minutes to Bukchon Hanok Village to see traditional tile-roofed houses on a hillside between palaces. The contrast with the glass towers visible over the walls is the whole country in miniature.

**3. Hike Seoraksan in autumn**

Korea's finest national park, in the northeast near Sokcho, is extraordinary in September and October when the granite peaks rise above forests in full colour. The main valleys are accessible and well-signed; the upper ridges are harder work but reward you with views across to the East Sea. The Sinheungsa Temple at the park entrance is a good place to start. Stay overnight in Sokcho and eat raw fish at the seafood market.

**4. Eat your way through a Korean barbecue**

*Samgyeopsal* — pork belly grilled at the table, wrapped in lettuce with garlic and chilli paste — eaten with a group over multiple rounds of soju is one of the great eating experiences in Asia. The ritual matters: you grill your own meat, you pour drinks for others rather than yourself, you pile everything into little lettuce parcels. Find a busy restaurant that is full of Korean families and do exactly what they are doing.

**5. Do a temple stay**

Many Buddhist temples throughout Korea accept overnight guests as part of the temple stay programme. You participate in the daily routine — early morning prayer at 4:30am, meditation, a tea ceremony, simple vegetarian meals, 108 bows. The 108 prostrations are harder than they sound. Temples in mountain settings like Haeinsa or Magoksa are particularly good. It is a genuine window into a tradition that has been continuous in these mountains for over a thousand years.
