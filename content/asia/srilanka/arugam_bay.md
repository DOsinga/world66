Arugam Bay is one of the world's top surfing destinations — a laid-back village on Sri Lanka's southeastern coast where the Indian Ocean delivers long, consistent right-hand breaks from April through August. It sits about 320 km from Colombo, deep in the island's dry zone, which means sunshine when the rest of Sri Lanka is being rained on.

The bay itself is small and unpretentious. Three villages merge into one strip of beach shacks, guesthouses, and tuk-tuks. The surf crowd that washes up here every season gives the place an easy, international vibe without turning it into a resort town. Outside surf season — roughly November to March — it becomes very quiet.

The three surf breaks that made the bay famous are Arugam Point (the main break, right out front), Pottuvil Point (about 10 km north, longer and less crowded), and Crocodile Rock (south of the bay, with its name doing most of the work in keeping beginners away). In 2004 the ISA staged the bay's first international surfing competition here. The nearest town is Pottuvil, 4 km north — a workaday Muslim trading town with a bus station and the services the bay lacks.

Beyond the surf, the surrounding area rewards exploration. Kumana National Park, just south of the bay, is one of Sri Lanka's best wildlife reserves and sees far fewer visitors than Yala. Crocodiles are genuinely present in the lagoon between the bay and the park. The Pottuvil Lagoon to the north is good for birdwatching by canoe.
