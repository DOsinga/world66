Galle is the best-preserved colonial town in Sri Lanka, dominated by its massive Dutch Fort — a UNESCO World Heritage Site built in the seventeenth century and still remarkably intact. The fort occupies a rocky headland at the southern tip of the city, its ramparts enclosing a grid of quiet streets lined with Dutch colonial buildings, old churches, mosques, and increasingly stylish boutique hotels and cafes.

Walking the ramparts at sunset, with the Indian Ocean crashing against the walls and cricket matches on the green below, is one of the most atmospheric experiences in Sri Lanka. Inside the fort, the Dutch Reformed Church (1755), the lighthouse, and the old warehouse district reward slow exploration. The streets are narrow, car-free in practice, and filled with art galleries, gem shops, and small restaurants.

The town outside the fort is a busy commercial centre with a large bus station and a lively fish market. Galle is on the main coastal railway and road from Colombo (about 2.5 hours by train), making it an easy destination to reach.

The surrounding south coast has some of Sri Lanka's best beaches — [Unawatuna](/asia/srilanka/unawatuna) is just 5 km east, and the coast stretches on through Mirissa and Tangalle.
