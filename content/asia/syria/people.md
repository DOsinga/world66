---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Syria's population before the 2011 civil war was around 22 million. The war caused one of the largest displacement crises in modern history — roughly 6 million Syrians fled abroad (mainly to Turkey, Lebanon, Jordan, and Europe) and another 7 million were internally displaced at the conflict's peak. Since Assad's fall in December 2024, some diaspora Syrians have begun returning, but the country's population is still a fraction of what it was.

**Ethnicity.** Arabs make up the majority of the population, but Syria has always been more mixed than that description suggests. Kurds, concentrated in the northeast (the Jazira region) and in some Damascus suburbs, make up around 10 percent. Armenians, many descended from survivors of the 1915 genocide, settled in Aleppo and other cities and maintain distinct communities. Assyrians, Circassians, Turkmens, and Druze each have their own presence.

**Religion.** Syria's religious diversity was both its richness and, under Assad, a source of deliberate manipulation. Sunni Muslims are the majority, around 70 percent. Alawi Muslims — the sect to which Assad and much of the military leadership belonged — make up roughly 10-12 percent, concentrated on the coast around Latakia. Christians of various denominations account for around 8-10 percent; they are one of the oldest Christian communities in the world — Paul was converted on the road to Damascus, and Aramaic is still spoken in the village of Maloula. Druze live mainly in the Hauran hills of the south.

**Language.** Arabic is the official language. Syrian Arabic has its own accent and vocabulary, distinct from Egyptian or Gulf Arabic, though educated Syrians understand Modern Standard Arabic. Kurdish (Kurmanji) is widely spoken in the northeast. Armenian remains in use in some Aleppo communities. In Maloula, northeast of Damascus, Western Aramaic — the language spoken by Jesus — is still used by a small community, one of the last places on earth where it survives as a living tongue.

**Character.** Syrians have a reputation for hospitality that is not exaggerated. Invitations to homes, insistence on paying for your meal, tea from strangers — these are genuine expressions of culture, not performance for tourists. The years of war and displacement have been brutal, but the social warmth survives.
