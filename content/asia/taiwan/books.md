title: "Books"
type: section
---

**Taiwan: A New History** edited by Murray Rubinstein is the standard academic survey — thorough, dense, and essential reading if you want a serious grounding in the island's past, from the Aboriginal era through Dutch colonialism, the Qing, Japanese occupation, and the Republic of China period.

**The Generalissimo: Chiang Kai-shek and the Struggle for Modern China** by Jay Taylor is the most balanced biography of the man whose regime dominated Taiwan for decades. Required context for understanding the Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall and much else.

**Wild Fire: Sparks of Taiwan's Social Movement** edited by Hanchao Lu — a collection of essays from the 1980s democracy movement. The passion still comes through in translation.

**The Stolen Bicycle** by Wu Ming-Yi is a novel that ranges across Taiwan's history through objects, memory, and the island's butterfly and insect life. Wu is Taiwan's most celebrated contemporary literary novelist and this is a good starting point. His earlier **The Man with the Compound Eyes** uses a piece of oceanic garbage to explore Taiwan's relationship with the sea.

**Formosa Betrayed** by George Kerr (1965) is a first-hand account by a US diplomat who witnessed the 1947 February 28 Massacre. Uncomfortable and essential — it explains a wound in Taiwan's history that still hasn't fully healed.

For practical travel reading, the **Lonely Planet Taiwan** guide is reliable for infrastructure and logistics. Supplement it with the Round Taiwan Round blog (rtaiwanr.com) for current, on-the-ground detail.
