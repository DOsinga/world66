---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

Taipei is one of the easier Asian cities to sightsee. Distances are manageable, the MRT is cheap and clean, and the major sights are concentrated in a handful of districts. Start with the **National Palace Museum**, home to the greatest collection of imperial Chinese art in the world — over 700,000 pieces brought out of mainland China by Chiang Kai-shek's retreating forces in 1949, of which a small fraction is on display at any time. From there, **Taipei 101** in the Xinyi district was the tallest building in the world from 2004 to 2010, and the observation deck still offers the best view in the city. The **Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall** is a grand ceremonial complex with hourly changing-of-the-guard ceremonies; the **National Theatre** and **National Concert Hall** face each other across the same plaza.

In the older, working-class part of Taipei west of the station, **Longshan Temple** in Wanhua is the most atmospheric working temple in the city, with clouds of incense, crowds of worshippers, and a constant hum of chanting; the surrounding streets still contain some of the oldest neighbourhoods in Taipei. Out in the northern district of **Beitou**, a Japanese-era legacy of hot-spring bathhouses and ryokan-style hotels are built into the forested slopes and are Taipei's best antidote to the urban heat. Further north still, **Yangmingshan National Park** is a belt of volcanic craters, hot springs, and hiking trails above the city — particularly beautiful in March when the cherry and calla-lily blossoms are out.
