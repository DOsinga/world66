---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Tajik food is honest, filling, and built around bread, rice, and meat. It shares much with the broader Central Asian tradition but has its own character — more use of fresh herbs, more dairy, and some Persian influences.

**Non** (flatbread) is central to every meal. It is baked in a tandoor oven and eaten with everything. Tajiks treat bread with near-sacred respect: it is never placed upside down, and a dropped piece should be picked up and put somewhere high rather than discarded. If someone offers you bread, accept it.

**Plov** (or osh/palov) is the national dish — rice cooked with lamb, carrots, and onion in a large wok, rich with oil. Every family and every region has a slightly different recipe. Eaten communally from a shared plate at celebrations, it is the dish of weddings, Navruz, and any occasion that calls for generosity.

**Qurutob** is arguably the most distinctively Tajik dish: flatbread soaked in a broth of kurt (dried sour cheese balls), topped with fresh herbs and onions. Simple and delicious. You find it mostly in Dushanbe and the west.

**Shurbo** is a simple meat and vegetable soup eaten everywhere. **Laghman** — noodles in broth — is also common. **Shashlyk** (grilled mutton skewers) is sold at street stalls and restaurants, often with raw onion and flatbread. **Samsa** (baked pastries stuffed with meat and onion) are a reliable snack from any bazaar.

Tea — always green, sometimes black — is constant and free. It comes before, during, and after meals, and is offered to any visitor. Saying no is not an option.

Summer brings extraordinary fruit: melons, grapes, apricots, figs, pomegranates. The bazaars in Dushanbe and Khujand are worth visiting for the produce alone.

In cities you will find Russian-influenced dishes alongside Tajik food — borsch, pelmeni, cutlets. The larger hotels in Dushanbe have European menus, but you will eat better and more cheaply at a local chaikhana (teahouse).
