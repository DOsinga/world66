title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Getting around Tajikistan takes patience. Roads are poor in the mountains, distances are longer than they look on a map, and weather can close passes at any time of year. That said, there is a functioning network of shared transport and the country is well-traveled by independent visitors.

## Shared taxis

The backbone of intercity travel. Shared taxis (usually Soviet-era Ladas or newer Toyota Camrys) depart from central points in each town when full — typically four passengers. You can pay for extra seats if you want to leave sooner or have more space. Fares are cheap by Western standards. Dushanbe to Khujand costs around $10–15 per seat for a 6–8 hour journey via the tunnel.

## Marshrutkas

Shared minibuses (marshrutkas) cover urban routes and shorter intercity runs. They are cheaper than shared taxis and slower. Useful within cities and for shorter hops.

## Pamir Highway

For the Pamir Highway, most travelers hire a 4WD vehicle with driver in Dushanbe, or arrange to join a shared jeep. The journey from Dushanbe to Khorog takes a minimum of 12–14 hours on the M41, longer with stops. Coming back the same way is one option; crossing into Kyrgyzstan at the Kyzyl-Art pass is another. Some travelers cycle the entire route — it is a bucket-list ride, but physically demanding.

## Domestic flights

Tajik Air and Somon Air run flights from Dushanbe to Khujand and Dushanbe to Khorog. The Khorog flight is famously spectacular — and famously unreliable. Don't plan tight connections around it.

## Within Dushanbe

[Dushanbe](/asia/tajikistan/dushanbe) is a walkable city for the centre. Taxis are cheap and plentiful; agree on a price before getting in or use the Yandex Go app which works here. City buses and minibuses cover the outer suburbs.

## Train

A single train route runs from Dushanbe south to Kurgan-Tyube and onward to Uzbekistan (via Termez), with a connection eventually reaching Khujand — though it involves a transit through Uzbekistan, which requires an Uzbek visa or negotiating at the border. In practice, almost nobody uses trains for domestic travel.
