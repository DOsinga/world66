title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**The Pamir Highway** is the reason most independent travelers come to Tajikistan. The M41 highway runs from [Dushanbe](/asia/tajikistan/dushanbe) to Osh in Kyrgyzstan, crossing some of the highest and most remote terrain in the world. The full route takes at least a week to do properly, with side trips into the Wakhan Corridor along the Afghan border among the most memorable. See the [Pamir Highway](/asia/tajikistan/pamir_highway) page for more.

**The Fann Mountains** near [Penjikent](/asia/tajikistan/penjikent) offer excellent trekking through a landscape of turquoise lakes, glaciers, and traditional villages. The Seven Lakes (Haft Kul) valley is deservedly popular. Penjikent itself has one of the best-preserved Sogdian archaeological sites in Central Asia, with wall paintings from the 5th century still visible.

**Iskanderkul Lake**, about 90 km north of Dushanbe in the Fann foothills, is an accessible and beautiful destination — a glacial lake at 2,200 meters said to be named after Alexander the Great. Day trips from Dushanbe are easy; staying overnight is better.

**Khujand** in the north is Tajikistan's second city and has a more relaxed, Uzbek-influenced character than Dushanbe. Its bazaar and old fortress are worth half a day. The city is separated from the rest of Tajikistan by mountains and is much easier to reach from Tashkent than from the capital.

**Hissar Fortress**, just 30 km west of Dushanbe, is the ruined 18th-century seat of a Bukharan regional governor. The site includes two well-preserved madrasas and an old caravanserai. It makes an easy half-day excursion from the capital.

**Nurek Dam**, 70 km from Dushanbe on the Vakhsh River, was the tallest dam in the world when completed in 1980 (300 m). The reservoir behind it is striking. It is not a major tourist site but worth combining with a drive toward Penjikent.
