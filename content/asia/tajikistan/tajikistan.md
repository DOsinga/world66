Tajikistan is the smallest of the Central Asian republics, and the most mountainous. Over 90 percent of the country is covered by mountains, most of it above 3,000 meters. The Pamirs — the so-called Roof of the World — dominate the east, sharing borders with China and Afghanistan. It is wild, spectacular, and unlike anywhere else in the region.

The country only opened meaningfully to independent travelers in the early 2000s, after a devastating civil war in the 1990s left deep economic scars. The infrastructure is still rough in places, but that is part of the appeal. Guesthouses in remote villages, shared taxis on improbable roads, Soviet-era buildings alongside ancient Silk Road ruins — Tajikistan rewards travelers who come prepared.

[Dushanbe](/asia/tajikistan/dushanbe), the capital, is a calm and relatively pleasant city that serves mainly as a base. The real draw is elsewhere: the Pamir Highway to [Khorog and the Wakhan Corridor](/asia/tajikistan/pamir_highway), the Fann Mountains outside [Penjikent](/asia/tajikistan/penjikent), the Sogdian ruins, and the sheer scale of empty highland landscape. Tajikistan is primarily a destination for trekkers, cyclists, and road-trippers with time and a tolerance for adventure.

The people are warm and hospitality is taken seriously. Expect to be invited for tea, then a meal, then to stay the night. The country is predominantly Tajik and Uzbek, with a small Russian-speaking minority that shrank after independence. Tajik is the official language, a close relative of Persian; Russian still gets you surprisingly far in cities.
