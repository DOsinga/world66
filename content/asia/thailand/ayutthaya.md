For over four centuries, Ayutthaya was the capital of Siam and one of the largest cities in the world. Founded in 1350 on an island at the confluence of three rivers, it grew into a cosmopolitan trading hub that hosted merchants from China, Japan, India, Persia, and Europe. In 1767, Burmese armies burned it to the ground.

What remains is extraordinary. The ruins of temples, palaces, and monasteries spread across an island you can cover in a day by bicycle. Brick prangs rise from flat plains, tree roots wrap around stone Buddha heads, and crumbling chedis line the riverbanks. The UNESCO World Heritage Site covers the historical core, but interesting ruins extend well beyond the official park boundaries.

Ayutthaya sits just 80 kilometres north of Bangkok, making it one of the easiest and most rewarding day trips in Thailand. But spending a night here is worth it. The ruins are at their best in the early morning light, before the tour buses arrive, and the night market along the river is a fine place to end the day.
