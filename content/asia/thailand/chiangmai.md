Chiang Mai is the cultural capital of northern Thailand — a 700-year-old walled city in the foothills of the Himalayas, founded in 1296 as the centre of the Lanna kingdom and still, despite rapid modern growth, feeling like a completely different country from the hot coastal south. Nearly 300 Buddhist temples (*wats*) are packed inside the old city walls, the mountain ring is full of hill-tribe villages and trekking routes, and the cooler climate and slower pace have made it the favourite Thai city of long-term travellers and digital nomads.

The **Old City** — a perfect 1.5 km square bounded by a moat and crumbling 14th-century brick walls — is the heart of the experience. Inside, the major temples cluster: **Wat Phra Singh** (the most revered), **Wat Chedi Luang** (with its massive ruined 15th-century stupa), **Wat Chiang Man** (the oldest in the city, built by the founding king), and a couple of hundred others. Most are free to enter; dress respectfully.

Outside the walls, **Doi Suthep** — the 1,676m forested mountain that rises immediately west of the city — holds **Wat Phra That Doi Suthep**, a gilded hilltop temple founded in 1383 and reached by either a 306-step nāga-flanked stairway or a cable car. The view down over Chiang Mai from the terrace is the defining image of the city.

Chiang Mai's night markets are a draw in themselves. The **Sunday Walking Street** on Ratchadamnoen Road closes to traffic and fills with craft stalls from every hill-tribe in the region. The **Saturday Walking Street** on Wualai Road and the nightly **Night Bazaar** on Chang Klan Road serve the same purpose. The nearby **Warorot Market** (Talad Kad Luang) is the everyday wholesale market and the place to eat like a local.

Food is northern Thai — **khao soi** (curry noodle soup with crispy noodles on top), **sai ua** (herby northern sausage), **nam prik ong** (pork-tomato chilli dip), and plenty of green curries and fried fish. Street stalls are everywhere; **Khao Soi Khun Yai** inside the old city and **Cowboy Lady** by the North Gate are two beloved cheap stops.

Chiang Mai is the base for **trekking** in the mountains — one- to three-day trips to Lisu, Karen, Lahu, and Akha villages, with elephant camps, waterfalls, and bamboo rafting. Be selective about elephant experiences — **Elephant Nature Park** is the main reputable rescue sanctuary.

Cool season (November–February) is the best time to visit, with pleasant days (25°C) and cool nights. Hot season (March–May) is unpleasantly smoky thanks to agricultural burning in the highlands. The rainy season (June–October) is green and quiet.
