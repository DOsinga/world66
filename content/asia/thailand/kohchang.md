Thailand's second-largest island sits in the Gulf of Thailand near the Cambodian border, about 300 km southeast of Bangkok. Unlike Phuket or Koh Samui, Koh Chang still has a rugged, jungled interior that takes up most of the island — steep mountains, waterfalls, and thick tropical forest. The Mu Ko Chang National Marine Park protects the island and dozens of smaller ones in the surrounding archipelago.

The west coast has the beaches and most of the development. White Sand Beach in the north is the busiest, with restaurants, bars, and dive shops lining the road. Further south, Klong Prao and Kai Bae are quieter, and Lonely Beach — despite the name — has become the backpacker hub. The east coast remains largely untouched, with fishing villages and mangroves.

Koh Chang is a good base for exploring the wider archipelago. [Koh Kood](/asia/thailand/kohchang/koh_kood) and [Koh Mak](/asia/thailand/kohchang/koh_mak) are smaller, quieter islands reachable by speedboat, each worth a day trip or a few nights on their own. The diving and snorkeling around the outer islands is decent, with good visibility from November through May.

The island has changed a lot since its backpacker days. The main road around the west coast is now fully paved, there are ATMs and 7-Elevens in the beach towns, and the old bamboo-hut scene has mostly given way to concrete bungalows and small resorts. But the interior is still wild, and if you rent a scooter and head to the east coast, you will find a much quieter island.
