Koh Lanta is the Thai island that people discover after they have had enough of the full-moon-party circuit. Part of an archipelago of 52 islands in the Andaman Sea off Krabi province, it is Ko Lanta Yai — the big island — that draws visitors: a long, hilly strip fringed by a dozen west-coast beaches, each one quieter than the last as you head south.

The island has a different feel from most Thai beach destinations. The interior is thickly forested, the southern tip is a national park, and the local population is predominantly Muslim, with mosques far outnumbering temples. Old Town on the east coast is a charming wooden-stilted fishing village built over the water, originally settled by Chinese traders and sea gypsies. It is one of the most atmospheric spots on the island.

The beaches run from busy Klong Dao in the north, with its family resorts and wide shallow water, down to the near-deserted coves around Mu Ko Lanta National Park in the south. In between you will find Long Beach (Phra Ae), Klong Khong with its backpacker bars, and Kantiang Bay — arguably the prettiest beach on the island, backed by jungle hills. Snorkelling and diving are excellent, particularly around the offshore islands of Ko Rok and Ko Haa.

The monsoon season runs from June to October, when many resorts close and ferries become irregular. The best time to visit is November to March, when the seas are calm and the skies are clear. Getting around requires a motorbike or hired car — there is no real public transport, and the west-coast road is the island's only artery.
