The Phi Phi islands are the kind of place that looks too good to be real — sheer limestone cliffs rising from turquoise water, white sand beaches backed by palms, and coral reefs just offshore. Located in the Andaman Sea between [Phuket](/asia/thailand/phuket) and [Krabi](/asia/thailand/krabi), the archipelago consists of two main islands: Phi Phi Don, where all the accommodation and nightlife is, and Phi Phi Leh, an uninhabited national park island famous as the filming location for *The Beach*.

Phi Phi Don is tiny enough to walk end to end in under an hour. The narrow isthmus connecting the island's two limestone hills concentrates the village, with its dive shops, restaurants, bars, and guesthouses packed tightly together. The atmosphere is backpacker-friendly and loud after dark. Away from the village, quieter beaches like Long Beach and Laem Tong offer a calmer side of island life.

The local population is a mix of Thai Muslims — descendants of the Urak Lawoi sea gypsies who have lived on these islands for generations — Thai Buddhists, and workers drawn from the mainland by tourism. The islands were devastated by the 2004 tsunami and have been substantially rebuilt, though the scars are still visible in places.

Snorkelling and diving are the main draws. The waters around Phi Phi are warm, clear, and full of marine life. Day trips to Bamboo Island (Ko Pai), about thirty minutes north of Phi Phi Don, are popular — it is one of the most pristine and unspoiled islands in the area.
