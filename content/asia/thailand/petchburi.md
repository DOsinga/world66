Phetchaburi is one of Thailand's oldest cities, with roots stretching back to the Dvaravati kingdom over a thousand years ago. Unlike the beach towns further south, this is a place where Thai history sits in plain sight -- hilltop palaces, centuries-old cave temples, and monastery murals that rank among the finest in the country.

The city sprawls at the base of Khao Wang, a hill crowned by the summer palace of King Rama IV. Below it, the old town is a tangle of narrow streets lined with traditional wooden shophouses, many of them selling the sweets Phetchaburi is famous for. Khanom mo kaeng, a rich custard made with palm sugar, is practically the city's identity.

Most travelers pass through on the way to Hua Hin or Cha-am, but Phetchaburi rewards a stop of a day or two. The temples are outstanding -- Wat Mahathat Worawihan and Wat Kamphaeng Laeng alone justify the detour -- and the pace is unhurried in a way the southern beach towns have long since lost. Kaeng Krachan National Park, Thailand's largest, is just an hour west.
