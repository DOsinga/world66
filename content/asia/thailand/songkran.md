Thailand's New Year celebration runs from 13 to 15 April and turns the entire country into a water fight. What began as a gentle ritual of pouring scented water over elders' hands has evolved into three days of citywide drenching with water guns, buckets, and garden hoses. The timing is deliberate -- mid-April is the hottest part of the Thai year, and getting soaked is genuinely welcome.

The biggest celebrations happen in Bangkok (particularly along Khao San Road and Silom Road), Chiang Mai (where it runs a full six days and the old city moat becomes a communal water source), and Pattaya. Chiang Mai is widely considered the most intense. In Bangkok, Silom Road gets closed to traffic and packed with revellers.

Beyond the water fights, Songkran is fundamentally a family holiday. Thais return home, visit temples, offer food to monks, and pour water over Buddha images. Many temples parade their Buddha statues through the streets for public washing. It is also traditional to build sand pagodas in temple grounds. If you want to experience the spiritual side, go early in the morning before the street festivities start.

Practical advice: waterproof your phone and wallet in a ziplock bag, wear clothes you do not mind getting soaked in, and avoid riding a motorbike during the festival -- water gets thrown at moving traffic and the roads become slippery.
