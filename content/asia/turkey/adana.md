Adana is Turkey's fourth-largest city, sitting on the Seyhan River in the fertile Cukurova plain near the Mediterranean coast. It's primarily an industrial and agricultural city — cotton is king here — and most travelers pass through rather than stop. But it has more to offer than its reputation suggests.

The old bridge, the Taskopru, dates from Roman times and still carries foot traffic across the Seyhan. The Sabanci Central Mosque, completed in 1998, is one of the largest mosques in the Middle East. The Archaeological Museum has a strong collection from sites in the region.

Adana's real claim to fame is its food. Adana Kebab — the spicy ground lamb kebab served on a flat sword-like skewer — originated here and is taken very seriously by locals. The kebab restaurants along Ataturk Bulvari are the places to eat it. Salgam (a fermented turnip and black carrot juice, sharp and purple) is the local drink, often accompanied by fiery Adana kebab.

The city is hot and humid in summer — not the most comfortable time to visit. Spring and autumn are more pleasant.
