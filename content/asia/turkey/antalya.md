Antalya is the biggest city on the Turkish Mediterranean coast and the gateway to the so-called **Turquoise Coast** — the long stretch of beach resorts, Roman and Lycian ruins, and limestone mountains that runs from Fethiye in the west to Alanya in the east. It is also the fifth-largest city in Turkey, with a metropolitan population of over 2.5 million, and the arrival point for the vast majority of package holidays in southern Turkey. The modern city is sprawling, sun-bleached and unlovely from the highway, but its compact historical core — **Kaleiçi**, the walled Ottoman old town — is one of the most atmospheric quarters in Turkey and reason enough to visit.

**Kaleiçi** sits on a low bluff above the harbour, ringed by partly-surviving Roman and Seljuk walls. Its narrow lanes of restored Ottoman wooden houses are now full of boutique hotels and cafés, but manage (just) to avoid feeling entirely like a theme park. At the land entrance is **Hadrian's Gate**, a triple-arched triumphal arch built in 130 CE to mark the Roman Emperor's visit, still the main way into the old town. Nearby stands the fluted **Yivli Minare** (the Grooved Minaret), a 13th-century brick minaret that has become the symbol of the city. At the sea end, the old harbour has been converted into a marina, with boats offering short cruises and fish restaurants spilling onto the quayside. Above it all, the clifftop **Karaalioğlu Park** has the city's finest view — sheer limestone cliffs dropping straight into the Mediterranean, with the snow-capped Taurus Mountains rising behind.

The **Antalya Museum**, a short walk west of the old town, is one of the finest archaeological museums in Turkey — particularly strong on the Roman statuary recovered from nearby **Perge**, which rivals anything in Istanbul. Beyond the city, the coast and the hills hold a remarkable density of ancient sites: **Perge**, **Aspendos** (with its Roman theatre, still in use), **Termessos** (a mountain fortress city the size of Athens that even Alexander the Great failed to take), **Side** and **Phaselis** are all within day-trip range. Further west lie the Lycian coast and the classic turquoise beaches. The city itself has **Konyaaltı Beach** at its western edge and **Lara Beach** in the east, both lined with resort hotels. Come in May, June or September; July and August are genuinely hot (over 35°C) and the beaches are packed.
