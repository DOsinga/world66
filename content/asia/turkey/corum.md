Çorum sits on the edge of a fertile plain in north-central Turkey, roughly 240 km east of Ankara, where the Black Sea region meets Central Anatolia. It was a historic trading town on the routes between the Anatolian plateau and the coast, and its bazaars once sold hand-spun textiles, copper utensils, and leather goods. Today it's a quiet provincial city best known as the gateway to some of Turkey's most important Hittite sites — and, famously, for its roasted chickpeas.

The real reason to come here is the archaeology. Within a short drive of the city are three of the most significant Hittite sites in the country. **Hattusa** (Boğazkale), about 85 km south, was the capital of the Hittite Empire and is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site — the massive walls, temples, and the three great gates (Lion, Royal, and Sphinx) are extraordinary. Two kilometres from Hattusa is **Yazılıkaya**, an open-air rock sanctuary carved with reliefs of Hittite gods and kings dating to the 13th century BC. And **Alacahöyük**, 36 km from Boğazkale, has a Hittite gateway flanked by sphinxes and a museum of Bronze Age finds.

Back in the city, the 13th-century Ulu Mosque built by the Seljuks and the 19th-century clock tower are the main sights. The Museum of Çorum has a good collection of finds from the surrounding excavations. Allow a full day if you're visiting both Hattusa and Yazılıkaya — they're easily combined and worth the effort.
