Diyarbakır is defined by its walls — a nearly continuous 6km circuit of black basalt built by the Romans and extended by successive rulers over two millennia. Walk the top of them for views over the old city, the Tigris valley, and the flat steppe stretching toward the Syrian border. The walls were designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2015, together with the gardens (hevsel bahçeleri) that fill the flood plain below.

Inside the walls, the city is dense with mosques, hans, and old stone houses. It's the de facto capital of Turkey's Kurdish southeast, and the atmosphere is distinct from western Turkey — the food is different (meatballs stuffed with pistachios, stuffed liver), the politics are more charged, and you'll hear Kurdish as much as Turkish. Diyarbakır rewards two full days, more if you want to use it as a base for the surrounding region.

For practical orientation, head to the tourism office in the Kültür Sarayı building near the old city centre — they have good maps showing the mosques, hans, and key sites within the walls.
