Doğubayazıt is a small, dusty town in far eastern Turkey, a few kilometres from the Iranian border, and the main base for visiting İshak Paşa Palace and climbing Mount Ararat. It sits on a high plateau at around 1650 metres, with the great white cone of Ararat (5137m) dominating the skyline to the north. The mountain is supposed to be the resting place of Noah's Ark, which has attracted archaeologists, conspiracy theorists, and curious travellers in roughly equal numbers for generations.

The town itself is rough around the edges — infrastructure is basic, and the security situation in this part of Turkey has historically been complicated — but the friendliness is genuine. You'll encounter Iranians here on holiday, who cross the border to enjoy things not available at home. The atmosphere is distinctly frontier.

İshak Paşa Palace, about 6km outside town, is one of the most dramatic buildings in Turkey: a half-ruined Ottoman-Persian complex perched on a cliff above a valley, built in the late eighteenth century by a local Kurdish chieftain. The setting alone is worth the trip. Go in the late afternoon when the light on the stone is best.

To climb Ararat you need a permit and a licensed guide, and the season runs roughly June to September. The normal route takes two days up and one down from the base camp at Eli (2200m). Acclimatisation is important; most people spend a night in Doğubayazıt first.
