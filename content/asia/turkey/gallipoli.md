Gallipoli — *Gelibolu* in Turkish — is the long peninsula on the European side of the Dardanelles strait, and the site of one of the most famous and costly military failures of the First World War. Between April 1915 and January 1916, Allied forces (British, French, Australian, New Zealand, Irish, Indian and Newfoundland troops) landed here in an attempt to force the strait, take Constantinople, and knock the Ottoman Empire out of the war. After eight and a half months of brutal trench warfare, they withdrew without having advanced more than a few kilometres. The campaign cost over half a million casualties on both sides. For Australia and New Zealand, whose young nations first went to war here as separate ANZAC forces, the battle became a founding national myth; for Turkey, the defence of Gallipoli launched the military career of Mustafa Kemal (later **Atatürk**) and became the first step towards the modern Turkish Republic.

The battlefields are now preserved as the **Gallipoli Peninsula Historical National Park**, a tranquil, green stretch of pine woods, olive groves and low hills above clear blue water — a far cry from the landscape of 1915. The main sites on the Aegean side are the **ANZAC Cove** landing beach, the **Lone Pine** and **Chunuk Bair** memorials, the **57th Regiment** Turkish memorial (commemorating the unit that was told to "die, not advance" by Mustafa Kemal), and the vast **Turkish Monument** at the southern tip. On the Dardanelles side, **Helles** holds the British memorial and the French cemetery. Every year on **25 April — Anzac Day** — thousands of Australians and New Zealanders gather at ANZAC Cove for a dawn service; on **18 March** Turks commemorate their own naval victory against the allied fleet with ceremonies at Çanakkale.

Most visitors base themselves in **Çanakkale** on the Asian side of the strait and take a day tour or drive across on the car ferry. Plan at least a full day to see the main sites. Whatever your politics, the peninsula is a deeply moving place.
