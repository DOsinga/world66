Istanbul is famously spread across two continents, and crossing the Bosphorus from the European shore to the Asian side is more than a ferry ride — it is a genuine shift in character. The slogan Istanbul used when bidding for the 2000 Olympics was *"Meet where the continents meet,"* and the difference between the two halves of the city runs deeper than geography.

Board a ferry at Eminönü or Karaköy and twenty minutes later you step off at Kadıköy, the main district of the Asian side. The neighbourhood has long drawn writers, artists, and university graduates, giving it a more bohemian, bookish atmosphere than most of Istanbul. The streets feel calmer, the coffee shops more literary, the political graffiti more abundant.

Start your visit by staying close to the waterfront. A few minutes' walk from the ferry terminal brings you into Kadıköy Çarşısı, the market quarter — a tangle of pedestrian lanes lined with old buildings, churches, fishmongers, greengrocers, bakeries, and open-fronted pubs serving beer and mussels at prices that feel generous by Istanbul standards. The arcade Akmar Pasajı has been a haunt of the city's metal music fans for decades, its basement crammed with second-hand record and book shops.

Moving east from the harbour towards Bağdat Caddesi, the neighbourhood shifts upmarket. This long boulevard is one of Istanbul's prime see-and-be-seen strips, lined with boutiques, cafes, and restaurants. It makes for an easy afternoon walk, especially if you continue down to the waterfront suburb of Moda, a quieter, tree-shaded quarter popular with locals on weekends.
