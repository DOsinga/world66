**Pamukkale** — literally "Cotton Castle" in Turkish — is one of the most distinctive natural sites in the world: a 2,700-metre-long white limestone escarpment cascading down a hillside in south-western Turkey, with hot mineral-rich spring water flowing over a series of gleaming travertine terraces and shallow pools. From a distance the whole thing looks exactly like a frozen waterfall or, as the name suggests, a huge fluffy cotton castle. It has been a tourist destination for at least 2,000 years — the Romans built a spa city on top of it — and is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

On the ridge immediately above the terraces sit the ruins of **Hierapolis**, the Graeco-Roman spa city founded in the 2nd century BCE. The site is enormous and surprisingly well preserved: a 12,000-seat Roman theatre carved into the hillside (one of the finest in Turkey), a complete colonnaded main street, several necropolises with thousands of tombs, a temple of Apollo, and a small but excellent **Hierapolis Archaeological Museum** housed in the restored Roman baths. The **Cleopatra's Pool** (also called the Antique Pool) is a thermal pool inside the site where you can swim among genuine fallen Roman columns — the columns were knocked down by a medieval earthquake. Bathing costs extra but is worth it for the bizarre experience of swimming 38°C mineral water with ancient marble under your feet.

The travertines themselves must be walked **barefoot** — this is a strict rule to protect the fragile calcium carbonate surface — and you should stay inside the marked paths; much of the once-accessible terrace system has been cordoned off in recent years to allow regeneration. In dry months some of the upper terraces are also dry, which can be disappointing for first-time visitors; go in spring after the winter rains for the best effect. Most travellers visit Pamukkale as a day trip from **Denizli** (20 km away, the nearest large town) or as an overnight from **Antalya**, **Selçuk** or **Fethiye**. The nearby village of Pamukkale itself has grown up entirely to serve visitors and has dozens of cheap guesthouses.
