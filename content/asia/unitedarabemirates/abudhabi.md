Abu Dhabi is the capital of the United Arab Emirates and the seat of its federal government. It sits on a T-shaped island jutting into the Persian Gulf, connected to the mainland by three bridges. First settled in the 18th century by the Bani Yas tribe, who found fresh water there — Abu Dhabi translates roughly as "land of the gazelle" — the city has transformed beyond recognition since oil was discovered in 1958.

Today it is a global city of gleaming towers, sprawling boulevards, and ambitious cultural institutions. The **Corniche**, a manicured 8km waterfront promenade, runs along the western edge of the island and is the social heart of the city. At the other end of the cultural spectrum, the **Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque** is one of the largest mosques in the world and genuinely one of the most beautiful buildings on the Arabian Peninsula.

Saadiyat Island, just offshore, has become the city's cultural district. The **Louvre Abu Dhabi** opened in 2017 under a Jean Nouvel-designed dome that scatters patterns of light across the galleries below. The Guggenheim Abu Dhabi is under construction nearby. The **Qasr Al Hosn** fort — the oldest stone building in the city — anchors the heritage district on the main island.

Abu Dhabi is distinctly different from Dubai: less frantic, more planned, more traditional. The pace is slower, the streets less crowded, and the mix of cultures — with a large South Asian and Arab expatriate population alongside Emirati nationals — makes it an interesting place to spend a few days.
