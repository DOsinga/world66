---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Uzbek food is hearty, meat-forward, and genuinely good. The national obsession is plov — a rice dish cooked with lamb, carrots, onion, garlic, and cumin in a huge cast-iron kazan over open fire. Every region has its own version: Tashkent plov is rich and dark from the fat, Samarkand plov is lighter and often served with chickpeas and raisins, Khorezm plov (from around Khiva) is quite different again. Plov is typically eaten communally from a shared dish in the morning or at midday — it's a social event as much as a meal. Finding a good plov house early in the morning is one of the rituals of Uzbek travel.

Samsa are baked pastries filled with minced lamb and onion, fresh from a clay tandyr oven. The crust is flaky, the inside hot and juicy. They're sold at every bazaar and bakery and are one of the better street foods in Central Asia.

Lagman is a pulled noodle dish served in a meat and vegetable broth, reflecting the Dungan (Chinese Muslim) influence in Central Asian cooking. It shows up everywhere and is a reliable lunch option. Shashlik — skewered lamb or beef grilled over charcoal — is the standard outdoor food at festivals and markets.

Non, the round bread baked in a tandyr oven and stamped with an intricate pattern from a special wooden stamp (non-qolib), appears at every meal. Uzbeks treat bread with genuine respect; don't throw it away or turn it upside down on the table.

Tea culture is central to social life. Green tea is drunk throughout the day, always from a bowl (piala) rather than a cup, always without milk. Black tea is common in the east. Chaikhanas — traditional teahouses, usually shaded and often near a fountain — are where men spend hours talking and eating. Sitting in one is a good way to watch Uzbek life at its most relaxed.

The bazaars deserve special attention: the covered markets in Samarkand, Bukhara, and the Chorsu bazaar in Tashkent offer piles of dried fruits, nuts, spices, and breads that are as enjoyable to browse as to buy. Uzbek melons and grapes in late summer are extraordinary.
