## The Registan, Samarkand

The three madrassas that form the Registan are the most recognisable image of Central Asia — and they live up to it. Standing in the central square at dusk, surrounded by enormous tiled facades in turquoise and gold, is one of those rare travel moments that exceeds expectation. The complex was the ceremonial heart of Timur's empire in the 14th century. [Samarkand](/asia/uzbekistan/samarkand) has other remarkable monuments too: the Gur-e-Amir mausoleum where Timur is buried, the Shah-i-Zinda necropolis of tombs stretching along a narrow alley, and the observatory ruins of the astronomer-king Ulugh Beg.

## Bukhara's old city

[Bukhara](/asia/uzbekistan/bukhara) is described by UNESCO as the most complete medieval city in Central Asia, and wandering its lanes gives you a stronger sense of the living past than almost anywhere in the Silk Road world. The Poi-Kalyan complex — with its 12th-century minaret that Genghis Khan apparently spared out of admiration — anchors the old centre. The Ark fortress, the Kalon Mosque, and dozens of restored trading domes and caravanserais fill in the picture. Bukhara is also the best place to buy Uzbek crafts, from silk robes to suzani embroidery.

## Khiva's walled city

[Khiva](/asia/uzbekistan/khiva) feels like a film set — because the entire inner city (Itchan-Kala) has been so thoroughly restored it can tip into unreality. But it's also genuinely remarkable: a complete medieval Central Asian city contained within mud-brick walls, filled with mosques, minarets, and madrassas. It's quieter and more intimate than Samarkand or Bukhara, and the sunsets over the walls are worth the long journey from Tashkent.

## The Savitsky Museum, Nukus

One of the great surprises of Central Asian travel. Igor Savitsky, a Russian artist who came to the Karakalpak Republic in the 1950s to document nomadic culture, ended up secretly collecting thousands of works by Russian avant-garde artists who had been suppressed under Stalin. The result, housed in [Nukus](/asia/uzbekistan/nukus), is one of the most important collections of 20th-century Russian art in the world — and virtually unknown outside specialist circles. The journey to Nukus is long, but the museum alone justifies it.

## Moynaq and the Aral Sea

The former fishing port of [Moynaq](/asia/uzbekistan/moynaq) sits on a dry desert plain that was the southern shore of the Aral Sea until the Soviet irrigation schemes of the 1960s and 1970s diverted the rivers feeding it. Today, rusted ships lie scattered on the sand where the harbour used to be, a monument to one of the world's worst environmental disasters. It is a sobering and unforgettable place.

## The Desert Fortresses (Qalas)

Scattered across the Kyzyl Kum desert near Urgench are the ruins of dozens of ancient mud-brick fortresses — khorezm fortresses that date back as far as the 5th century BC. [Toprak Qala and Ayaz Qala](/asia/uzbekistan/theqalas) are the most accessible, rising dramatically from the flat desert. Getting here requires hiring a car from Urgench or Khiva, but the combination of desert landscape, ancient ruins, and near-total absence of other tourists makes for one of the most memorable days in Uzbekistan.

## Navruz in spring

The spring new year festival, celebrated on 21 March, transforms towns and cities across the country. Street celebrations, communal cooking of sumalak (a slow-cooked wheat dish that simmers overnight), traditional games on horseback, and music performances happen in every mahalla (neighbourhood). Timing a visit around Navruz gives you Uzbekistan at its most festive.
