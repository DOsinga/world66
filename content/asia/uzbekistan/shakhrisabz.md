Shakhrisabz is Tamerlane's hometown — the place where Amir Timur was born in 1336 and where he intended to be buried. For a ruler who built one of history's great empires and left monuments all over Central Asia, it's fitting that his birthplace is a modest Uzbek town where locals still outnumber tourists by a comfortable margin. That's part of what makes it worth visiting.

The town's centerpiece is the **Ak-Saray Palace**, Timur's summer residence. What stands today are two enormous gateway towers, the remains of what was once a colossal structure — inscriptions boasted "if you doubt our power, look at our buildings." The ruins are striking precisely because of their scale; you can sense the ambition even in the rubble. The nearby **Dorus-Saodat complex** contains the Crypt of Timur, though Timur himself was eventually buried in Samarkand's Gur-e-Amir — the crypt here holds his son and grandson.

Don't come expecting the dense, polished grandeur of Samarkand or the intact medieval streetscape of Khiva. Shakhrisabz is quieter, rougher around the edges, and more genuinely lived-in. The **Kok-Gumbaz mosque** and the adjacent bazaar area give you a feel for the town as it actually functions, not as a heritage attraction.

Getting here from Samarkand takes about two hours by shared taxi or bus, crossing the Takhta-Karacha Pass — a mountain road with real scenery, a relief after the flat stretches of the Uzbek steppe. It makes for a full day trip, though the pace is relaxed enough that you won't feel rushed.
