Hanoi has been the capital of Vietnam for a thousand years, and it wears that history more visibly than any other city in Southeast Asia. Narrow medieval streets tangle with French colonial boulevards, Soviet-era apartment blocks rise beside pagodas older than most of Europe's cathedrals, and the whole thing runs on a quiet combination of small-shop commerce, five million scooters, and extraordinarily good food.

The **Old Quarter** north of Hoan Kiem Lake is the city's beating heart — 36 guild streets, each traditionally named after the craft sold there (Silk Street, Silver Street, Paper Street), most still selling something related. It is best explored on foot, ideally in the early morning when the tai chi crowds are on the lake and the pho vendors are out. South of the lake lies the **French Quarter**, with the Opera House, tree-lined boulevards, and a handful of grand colonial hotels.

West of the centre are the set-piece sights. The **Temple of Literature**, founded in 1070 as Vietnam's first university, is a beautifully preserved Confucian complex. The **Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum** on Ba Dinh Square holds the embalmed body of Uncle Ho; nearby are the One Pillar Pagoda and Ho's surprisingly modest former house on stilts. The **Vietnam Museum of Ethnology** further west is the best introduction to the country's 54 ethnic groups.

Hanoi's food deserves a paragraph of its own. This is the birthplace of **pho**, and any Hanoian will tell you the southern version is an impostor. **Bun cha** — grilled pork patties in a light fish-sauce broth with rice noodles — is the city's other signature dish; it's what Anthony Bourdain and Barack Obama ate together on their 2016 visit. **Egg coffee** (ca phe trung) is a Hanoi invention: a hot cappuccino-like drink topped with a whipped egg-yolk and condensed-milk foam, absurdly good. Most of the best food is on the street.

Hanoi is the natural base for trips to **[Halong Bay](/asia/vietnam/halongbay)**, **[Ninh Binh](/asia/vietnam/ninh_binh)** (the inland karst country), and **[Sapa](/asia/vietnam/sapa)** in the northern mountains. Expect it to be hot and sticky in summer, surprisingly cold and grey in winter — the best months are October–November and March–April.
