Vietnam is one of the most rewarding countries in Southeast Asia for independent travel. It has an obvious tourist trail — Hanoi, Halong Bay, Hue, Hoi An, Ho Chi Minh City — and the trail exists for good reason. The destinations are genuinely remarkable. But the country also rewards going further: into the northern mountains, the central highlands, or the quieter parts of the coast.

## Hanoi

The capital is one of the great cities of Southeast Asia. The Old Quarter's tightly packed streets, each historically dedicated to a single trade, still feel like a different era. The Temple of Literature, Hoan Kiem Lake, and the Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum complex are all within easy reach of the center. Hanoi's street food — bun cha, pho, banh cuon, cha ca — is arguably the best in the country. See [Hanoi](/asia/vietnam/hanoi).

## Halong Bay

Several thousand limestone islands rising from the Gulf of Tonkin. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1994. The standard way to see it is a two- or three-night junk boat cruise, which lets you get away from the crowded central area into quieter waters. The scenery is genuinely spectacular. Quality varies enormously between operators — book a reputable boat rather than the cheapest option. See [Halong Bay](/asia/vietnam/halongbay).

## Hue

The former imperial capital on the Perfume River. The citadel, the royal tombs along the river, and the Thien Mu pagoda are the main draws. Hue has a quieter, more melancholy atmosphere than either Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City. The city's cuisine is distinct — spicier, more elaborate, historically influenced by imperial court cooking. See [Hue](/asia/vietnam/hue).

## Hoi An

A beautifully preserved trading port from the 15th to 19th centuries, with a mix of Vietnamese, Chinese, Japanese, and European architectural influences. The pedestrianized ancient town fills with lanterns in the evening. Day trips to My Son (Cham ruins) and the beaches at An Bang are easy from here. It gets crowded but handles it better than most. See [Hoi An](/asia/vietnam/hoian).

## Ho Chi Minh City (Saigon)

The country's commercial engine. Chaotic, dynamic, and inexhaustible. The War Remnants Museum is essential for understanding the American war from the Vietnamese side — unflinching and not easy viewing. The Reunification Palace, Notre Dame Cathedral, and the Ben Thanh Market area fill a day. The city's food scene is exceptional. Day trips to the Cu Chi Tunnels and the Mekong Delta are worth the effort. See [Ho Chi Minh City](/asia/vietnam/hochiminhcity).

## Sapa and the Northern Highlands

The town of Sapa is a base for trekking through rice terrace valleys and villages of Hmong, Dao, and Tay ethnic minorities. The scenery is outstanding. The tourist infrastructure has grown considerably; the weekend market at Bac Ha (further east) is less developed and more authentic. Fansipan, Vietnam's highest peak at 3,147m, can be trekked or accessed by cable car. See [Sapa](/asia/vietnam/sapa).

## Hoi An to Hue by Road

The Hai Van Pass connecting Da Nang to Hue is one of the most dramatic coastal drives in the country. The road winds along a ridge with views down to the sea on both sides. Coming from Da Nang, the lagoon and peninsula at Lang Co just before the pass are worth a stop.

## Phong Nha-Ke Bang

A vast cave system in central Vietnam (Quang Binh province), including Son Doong — the largest known cave in the world. Phong Nha cave can be visited independently by boat; Paradise Cave is accessible without a guide. Son Doong requires a licensed tour. The national park has grown into a proper destination. See [Phong Nha](/asia/vietnam/phong_nha__ke_ban).
