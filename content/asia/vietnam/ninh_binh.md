Ninh Binh is where Vietnam goes vertical. The province sits 90 km south of Hanoi and is carpeted with limestone karst towers — the same dramatic geology as Ha Long Bay, except here the peaks rise from rice paddies and slow rivers instead of ocean. The effect is extraordinary: boats drift between cliffs, egrets stalk through flooded fields, and Buddhist temples cling improbably to cliff faces above it all.

The centrepiece is Tam Coc, where a short rowing boat trip takes you through three tunnel caves carved by the Ngo Dong River. It's genuinely beautiful, though heavily touristed — go early morning if you can. A few kilometres away, Trang An is larger, wilder, and a UNESCO World Heritage site; its boat circuit takes two to three hours and feels considerably less like a theme park.

Hoa Lu, the fortified capital of Vietnam's first independent dynasty in the 10th century, is now a peaceful complex of temples in a valley ringed by mountains. It doesn't have the grandeur of Angkor, but the setting is lovely and the history resonates.

Cuc Phuong National Park, about an hour's drive northwest, is Vietnam's oldest national park and the easiest place in the country to see endangered primates up close — the primate rescue centre there rehabilitates lorises, gibbons, and langurs. Phat Diem Cathedral, near the coast, is a remarkable hybrid of Vietnamese and European ecclesiastical architecture built in the 1890s entirely from local stone.

Ninh Binh City itself is unremarkable, but it's a perfectly functional base. Most visitors doing a Ha Long Bay alternative base themselves here for a night or two and find it far quieter and cheaper than the coast.
