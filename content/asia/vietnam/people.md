---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Vietnam has a population of around 98 million, making it one of the most densely populated countries in Southeast Asia. The Kinh (Viet) people make up roughly 86% of the population and dominate the lowland coastal regions, the Red River Delta in the north, and the Mekong Delta in the south.

The remaining 14% belongs to 53 officially recognized ethnic minority groups, many of them concentrated in the northern highlands and the Central Highlands. The Tay, Thai, Muong, Hmong, Dao, Nung, and Khmer are among the larger minority groups. In the north, the market towns around Sapa and Bac Ha bring together multiple ethnic groups in their traditional dress — a genuinely striking sight. The Central Highlands' Bahnar, Ede, and Jarai peoples have distinct cultures centered on longhouses, communal life, and gong music.

Vietnamese culture values family, community, and a respect for elders that shapes daily interactions in tangible ways. Ancestor veneration is widespread across religious affiliations. The concept of "face" — maintaining dignity and avoiding public embarrassment — influences social behavior. Directness about personal matters is not always the norm; travelers sometimes find that "yes" can mean many things.

The Vietnamese are pragmatic and historically resilient, having endured centuries of foreign occupation and devastating wars within living memory. The war with the United States ended in 1975 — a fact that shapes the country's politics and its relationship with foreigners, though Vietnamese people themselves tend to be hospitable and forward-looking rather than dwelling on it.

The north and south have distinct cultural personalities, a product of history and geography. Hanoians are often described as more formal and traditional; Ho Chi Minh City (still called Saigon by many residents) has a faster, more commercial energy. The central coast has its own identity, shaped by centuries as the seat of the Nguyen lords and imperial Hue.

The country's urban-rural divide is significant. Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City are modern, noisy, and increasingly affluent; rural areas, particularly in the northwest and central highlands, remain poor and less connected to the tourist economy.
