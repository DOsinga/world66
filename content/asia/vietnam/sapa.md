Sapa is a hill station in the far north-west of Vietnam, set at 1,600 metres in a spectacular valley surrounded by the Hoang Lien Son mountains — the highest range in Indochina, culminating in **Fansipan** (3,143 m), Vietnam's tallest peak. The French built it as a mountain retreat in the 1920s, and the remnants of their stone villas — crumbling, moss-covered, atmospheric — still dot the hillside above the town. Today Sapa is Vietnam's main trekking base and a window onto the hill-tribe cultures of the northern highlands.

The draw is the landscape and the people. The terraced rice paddies that cascade down the valleys around Sapa are among the most photographed in Southeast Asia, especially in September and October when the rice turns gold before harvest. The valleys are home to several ethnic minority groups — the **Hmong** (Black Hmong and Flower Hmong), the **Dao** (Red Dao), and the **Tay** — who still wear traditional dress, farm the terraces by hand, and sell embroidery and indigo-dyed textiles in the town market. Trekking from Sapa through the villages of **Cat Cat**, **Lao Chai**, **Ta Van**, and **Ta Phin** is the standard one- or two-day excursion, and hiring a local guide from one of the minority communities is both the most ethical and the most rewarding way to do it.

The town itself has changed rapidly — what was a quiet backpacker stop a decade ago is now a busy resort town of hotels, karaoke bars, and tour operators. The construction is relentless and not always attractive. But the mountains don't care: step five minutes out of town on any trail and you are in some of the most dramatic highland scenery in Vietnam. The **Fansipan summit** can now be reached by cable car (opened 2016), which has democratised the view but annoyed purists; the two- or three-day trek up the old route is still available and still worth doing.

Come in September–November for the harvest and clear skies, or March–May for spring flowers. December–February is cold and foggy — Sapa can drop below freezing at night, and the cloud often sits in the valley for days, though the ethereal mist has its own beauty. Sapa is reached by overnight train from Hanoi to Lao Cai (8 hours), then an hour by bus or taxi up the mountain.
