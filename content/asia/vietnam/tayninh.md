Tay Ninh is a small provincial capital about 100 kilometres northwest of [Ho Chi Minh City](/asia/vietnam/hochiminhcity), best known as the seat of the Cao Dai religion — one of the most distinctive and unusual faiths in the world. Cao Dai blends elements of Buddhism, Christianity, Islam, Taoism, and European humanism into a single syncretic religion with over a million followers in Vietnam.

The Great Cao Dai Temple, built between 1933 and 1955, functions as the religion's Vatican — a vast, vividly coloured building with twin towers, a dragon-wrapped columns, and an enormous all-seeing eye above the entrance. The noon ceremony is the most popular with visitors: worshippers in white, blue, and yellow robes file into the nave while chanting fills the hall. You watch from the gallery above.

A visit to Tay Ninh is usually combined with the [Cu Chi](/asia/vietnam/cuchi) tunnels, which are on the way back to Saigon. Most visitors come on a day trip from Ho Chi Minh City — the drive takes about an hour and a half.
