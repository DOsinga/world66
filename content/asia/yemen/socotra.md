Socotra is one of the most alien landscapes on Earth. This remote archipelago in the Indian Ocean, closer to the Horn of Africa than to the Yemeni mainland it technically belongs to, has been isolated long enough to evolve a flora found nowhere else — most famously the dragon blood tree, with its mushroom-shaped canopy, and the bizarre bottle-shaped desert rose.

A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2008, Socotra has over 300 endemic plant species and a stark, otherworldly beauty that mixes white sand beaches, limestone plateaus, and deep canyons. The main island is about the size of Rhode Island. Hadibo, the only real town, is a dusty collection of shops and guesthouses on the north coast.

Getting here is the main challenge. Flights from mainland Yemen have been disrupted by the ongoing conflict, and access routes change. When flights do operate, they typically connect through Aden or Mukalla. There is no real tourism infrastructure — visitors camp, travel by 4x4, and rely on local guides. That remoteness is both the difficulty and the point: Socotra feels like arriving somewhere evolution forgot to standardise.
