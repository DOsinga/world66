Thola sits on a commanding hilltop some 54 km northwest of Sana'a, at around 2,400 metres above sea level. It is one of the older fortified towns of the Yemeni highlands — the kind of place where the defensive logic of the site is immediately obvious: a mountain perch ringed by cliffs, with an ancient castle that reputedly was never taken by force.

The town's name traces back to the pre-Islamic Himyarite era, and physical evidence of that past is still present in the form of cave systems, grain reservoirs, and rock-cut water cisterns carved into the mountain. The castle dominates the skyline. Below it, the old market quarter and a cluster of mosques — most notably Al-Motahir Bin Imam Sharafidin, dating to around 980 Hejra — give the town its lived-in character.

Thola is a half-day or day trip from Sana'a. There is no significant tourist infrastructure here, which is part of the appeal for travellers who want to see a highland Yemeni town going about its own business.
