---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Samoan food is built on a small set of staples: taro, breadfruit, coconut cream, fresh fish, and pork. It's hearty, filling, and rarely fussy.

**Palusami** is the dish most worth seeking out. Young taro leaves are filled with coconut cream, sometimes with onion or corned beef, wrapped in breadfruit leaves and cooked in an umu (earth oven). The result is rich and creamy, with a slight earthiness from the taro. It's made for communal occasions and not always easy to find in restaurants, but markets and church gatherings are good places to encounter it.

The **umu** — a traditional stone-lined earth oven — produces the basis for most ceremonial meals. Food is cooked slowly on heated rocks. Sunday to'ona'i (lunch after church) is often prepared this way, and the spread can be considerable.

Fresh fish is excellent and widely available. Oka (marinated raw fish in coconut cream, similar to ceviche) is a local staple and a good introduction to the cuisine.

Pago Pago has a mix of local eateries and American fast food — the latter has become dominant in everyday eating, reflecting the territory's US ties and the affordability of imported processed food. Prices in local restaurants are generally moderate; a sit-down meal won't break the bank.

If you're invited to a family meal or a fa'alavelave (a community gathering for a life event), accept. These are occasions where food, conversation, and tradition come together properly.
