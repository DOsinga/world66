---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

The penguins. Nothing quite prepares you for it. Gentoos, chinstraps, and Adelies live at enormous scale — colonies of hundreds of thousands, all noise and motion and fishy smell — and they have no instinctive fear of humans. You're required to keep five metres away. The penguins don't know that. They'll approach on their own terms, study you with one tilted eye, and waddle off. Watching them porpoise through the water, or squabble over pebbles, or feed a chick that's nearly their own size, is hypnotic for hours.

The icebergs are the other thing. You expect them to be impressive and they still exceed expectations. Icebergs the size of city blocks drift through glassy bays. The colour of old glacial ice — a deep, compressed blue that reads almost luminous — is unlike anything you'll see elsewhere. On overcast days the light diffuses across the water and everything glows. The zodiac rides through iceberg fields, the engines cut low so you can hear the creaking and the occasional crack of calving ice, are among the best hours of the trip.

Wildlife beyond penguins is extraordinary too. Weddell seals haul out on ice floes looking preternaturally calm. Leopard seals, huge and prehistoric-looking, watch the zodiacs with cold interest. In late season especially, humpback whales feed in the channels — surfacing close enough to the inflatable boats that you can see the barnacles on their flukes and smell the oily blast of their breath.

Deception Island, a flooded volcanic caldera still geologically active, lets you walk on black sand beaches with steam rising through the ground while icebergs float offshore. Port Lockroy, a restored 1940s British base on Wiencke Island, functions as a museum and post office where you can send a postcard stamped from Antarctica — it will actually arrive. The South Georgia extension, available on longer itineraries, adds king penguin colonies numbering in the hundreds of thousands and the grave of Ernest Shackleton.

And then there's the sky. Twenty-four hours of light in midsummer, the sun spiralling low on the horizon without setting. Pink and gold at midnight over black water and white mountains. No light pollution, ever. Antarctica at 2am looks like the rest of the world at golden hour, and it doesn't stop.
