---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

Wildlife watching is the main event, and it will exceed your expectations. Penguins waddle up to inspect you with zero fear — IAATO rules say keep five meters away, but nobody told the penguins. You'll sit on a rock and watch gentoos, chinstraps, and Adelies go about their chaotic, noisy lives at close range. Leopard seals lounge on ice floes looking menacing. Weddell seals look like they're smiling. Humpback whales surface next to your zodiac close enough to smell their breath, which is exactly as bad as you'd imagine.

Zodiac cruising among the icebergs is extraordinary. Your expedition team drives the inflatables slowly through bays filled with sculpted ice in shades of blue you didn't know existed. Some icebergs are the size of apartment buildings. You'll hear them crack and groan, and occasionally watch a piece calve off and crash into the water. The light in Antarctica — especially on overcast days when the clouds diffuse everything — makes the ice glow from within.

Most trips include at least one continental landing where you actually set foot on Antarctica itself. Standing on the seventh continent is a quiet thrill. Some itineraries visit Deception Island, a flooded volcanic caldera where you can walk on black sand beaches with steam rising from the ground. Others stop at Port Lockroy, a restored British base that now operates as a museum and post office — you can send postcards stamped from Antarctica.

Many expedition ships offer optional activities at additional cost. Sea kayaking among the icebergs is peaceful and surreal. Camping overnight on the ice — no tent, just a bivouac bag on compacted snow — is cold but unforgettable. And then there's the polar plunge: jumping into the Southern Ocean in your swimsuit. The water is around minus two Celsius. You will scream. You will scramble out immediately. You will be very glad you did it.

Photography deserves special mention. The light in Antarctica is unlike anywhere else — soft, diffused, and constantly shifting. Reflections off the water double every scene. Bring more memory cards than you think you'll need, keep spare batteries warm inside your jacket (cold kills them fast), and shoot in raw format. Some expedition ships carry professional photographers who give daily workshops and critique sessions. Even if you're shooting on a phone, the landscapes here are so dramatic that almost everything you point at comes out looking improbable.
