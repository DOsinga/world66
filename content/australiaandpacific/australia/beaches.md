---
title: "Beaches"
type: section
---

Australia has around 10,000 beaches — more than any other country — and some of them are genuinely extraordinary. The culture of the beach is deep here: surf lifesaving clubs, "slip slop slap" sun safety, and the social egalitarianism of a public strip of sand have shaped Australian identity as much as anything else.

Always swim between the red-and-yellow flags on patrolled beaches. Rips (fast-moving underwater currents) can pull even strong swimmers out to sea quickly. If caught in a rip, don't fight it — swim parallel to the shore until clear, or float and signal for help. Surf lifesavers are volunteers and take their job seriously.

**Whitehaven Beach** in the Whitsunday Islands (Queensland) is consistently rated among the world's best — pure white silica sand, shallow turquoise water, and no development whatsoever. Access is by boat or seaplane from Airlie Beach or Hamilton Island.

**Bondi Beach** (Sydney) is the most famous Australian beach and earns the reputation. The arc of sand, the promenade, the Icebergs pool at the southern end, and the costal walk south to Coogee are all excellent. Expect crowds in summer.

**Cable Beach** near Broome (Western Australia) runs 22 km along the Indian Ocean, with red pindan cliffs at its back and camel trains at sunset. Remote, but worth the journey.

**Bells Beach** (Victoria) near Torquay is Australia's surfing shrine — the Rip Curl Pro, one of the longest-running professional surfing events in the world, is held here. The waves are powerful and the water is cold.

**The Whitsundays** have dozens of beaches on uninhabited islands. Catamaran charters and sailing trips operate out of Airlie Beach and allow you to anchor off beaches you'll have entirely to yourself.

**Cape Le Grand National Park** (Western Australia) near Esperance has beaches — Lucky Bay in particular — that rank with the best in the country: white sand, clear cold water, and kangaroos that wander down to the shore.

In northern Queensland and the Northern Territory, beaches are often beautiful but not swimmable. Box jellyfish (stingers) are deadly in shallow coastal waters from October to May; saltwater crocodiles inhabit estuaries and some beaches year-round. Stinger nets protect designated swimming areas at some north Queensland beaches, but read the signs carefully before entering the water.
