Bondi Beach is Australia’s most iconic stretch of sand — a crescent of golden beach roughly a kilometre long, seven kilometres east of central [Sydney](/australiaandpacific/australia/newsouthwales/sydney). The name is believed to come from an Aboriginal word meaning the sound of breaking waves, and that sound is the constant backdrop here: Bondi faces the open Pacific and gets proper surf. It is the kind of place that manages to be both a working neighbourhood and a destination, with locals jogging the coastal path at dawn and visitors filling the cafes along Campbell Parade by mid-morning.

The beach itself is broad, especially at the southern end, and patrolled by professional lifeguards year-round. Swim between the red and yellow flags — the northern end and centre are generally safest for swimmers, while the southern end is given over to board riders. A shark net sits about 150 metres offshore, and there has not been a shark fatality here since 1937.

At the southern end, the Bondi Icebergs swimming club occupies one of the most photographed pools in Australia, cut into the rocks right above the surf. The club has been running since the 1920s and is open to visitors for a lap swim with a view. Above it, the Icebergs Dining Room is one of Sydney’s best-located restaurants. At the northern end you will find the Children’s Wading Pool and the Wally Weekes Pool, tucked around the headland.

The Bondi Pavilion, opened in 1929 in a Mediterranean Georgian Revival style, anchors the beachfront. Originally built as a grand changing pavilion, it now serves as a community cultural centre with a gallery, theatre, and cafe. The two surf lifesaving clubs — Bondi (founded 1906, claiming to be Australia’s oldest) and North Bondi (founded 1907) — still stand at either end of the beach in their handsome interwar clubhouses.

The famous Bondi to Coogee coastal walk starts at the southern end of the beach and follows the cliffs for six kilometres past Tamarama, Bronte, and Clovelly to Coogee. It takes about two hours at a comfortable pace and is one of the best free walks in Sydney. Along the way you will pass Aboriginal rock carvings near McKenzies Beach and the old Waverley Cemetery perched on the clifftop.

Bondi hosts several annual events worth timing a visit around, including the Sculpture by the Sea outdoor exhibition (October–November), the Festival of the Winds kite festival, the Cole Classic ocean swim, Flickerfest short film festival, and the finish line of the City to Surf fun run from Sydney’s CBD. Christmas and New Year celebrations draw huge crowds, especially from overseas visitors.

Getting here is straightforward: buses 380 and 333 run frequently from Bondi Junction station (which connects to the Sydney Trains network) and from the city centre. The trip from Circular Quay takes about 40 minutes.
