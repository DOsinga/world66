South Australia is a state of sharp contrasts: Mediterranean wine country, arid outback, a long and largely empty coastline, and [Adelaide](/australiaandpacific/australia/southaustralia/adelaide) — a planned colonial city that has become Australia's festival and food capital.

Adelaide is the starting point for most visits. It's a compact, walkable city with good restaurants, excellent wine bars, and the Adelaide Central Market (one of Australia's best). The **Adelaide Festival** (annual, March) and the **Adelaide Fringe** (the world's second-largest fringe festival after Edinburgh) make March the liveliest time to visit. The city sits at the foot of the Adelaide Hills — a green, cool escape with charming small towns, wineries, and wildlife parks including Cleland, where you can hand-feed kangaroos.

The **Barossa Valley**, an hour north of Adelaide, is one of Australia's most famous wine regions. The Shiraz here — grown from vines brought to the valley by German settlers in the 1840s — is among the best in the country. Cellar doors are open most days; the valley is easily explored by car.

**McLaren Vale**, half an hour south, is a smaller, more intimate wine region with ocean views. The **Fleurieu Peninsula** beyond it has the Victor Harbor beach resort and Kangaroo Island ferry access.

**Kangaroo Island** — two hours south of Adelaide including the 45-minute ferry from Cape Jervis — is one of the best places in Australia to see wildlife in the wild. Sea lions haul out on the beaches of Seal Bay; koalas sleep in the roadside gums; Cape Barren geese wander the farmland. The island suffered severe fire damage in the 2019-20 season but has largely recovered.

The **Flinders Ranges**, five hours north of Adelaide, offer outback scenery without the extreme remoteness of the Northern Territory. Wilpena Pound — a natural amphitheatre of quartzite ridges — is the centrepiece, with excellent bushwalking and Aboriginal rock art sites.

Beyond the Flinders, the landscape becomes serious outback. **Coober Pedy**, 850 km north of Adelaide, is the world's opal mining capital and a genuinely bizarre town — a significant portion of the population lives underground to escape the heat. The underground churches and dugout homes are worth seeing.

The **Coorong**, a 200-km lagoon system south of Adelaide, is a critical waterbird habitat and the setting for Colin Thiele's *Storm Boy*. Pelicans and migratory waders congregate here in large numbers.
