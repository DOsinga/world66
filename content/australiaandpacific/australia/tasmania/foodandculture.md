Despite its small size, Tasmania punches well above its weight for food and culture. The island produces some of Australia's finest ingredients: cheeses and dairy from the northwest and King Island, apples from the Huon Valley, berries everywhere, and superb seafood — scallops, mussels, abalone, crayfish, and fresh fish. To wash it all down, vineyards in the Tamar Valley, East Coast, and southeast produce excellent cool-climate wines, and two of Australia's premier breweries are here: Boag's in Launceston and Cascade in Hobart (Australia's oldest operating brewery).

Hobart has some of the best seafood restaurants in the country, particularly around the waterfront at Sullivan's Cove. North Hobart and Sandy Bay are also worth exploring. In Launceston the centre is compact enough to cover on foot. Do not be afraid to try restaurants in small towns throughout the state — many serve local produce bought straight from the farmer or fisherman that day.

For culture, Hobart is the centre. The Tasmanian Symphony Orchestra is nationally renowned for its interpretations of baroque and classical works. The Tasmanian Museum and Art Gallery has fine colonial art and natural history displays. The galleries along Salamanca Place showcase local artists, and the Saturday morning Salamanca Market is not to be missed.
