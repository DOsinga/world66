Tasmania is shaped like an arrowhead pointing south, separated from the Australian mainland by Bass Strait (about 240 kilometres wide). It covers 68,000 square kilometres and is Australia's most mountainous state, with no truly flat terrain — though its highest point is only about 1,600 metres.

The western half is wild and wet, dominated by ancient mountain ranges, temperate rainforests, and rivers. The Franklin River, still undammed along its entire length, is one of the great wilderness rivers. The eastern half is drier and lower, with eucalypt forests and farmland.

Tasmania's climate is cooler and wetter than the mainland. Winter temperatures can drop below freezing inland, while summers are mild — typically 20–30°C. Weather comes from the west, off the Southern Ocean, bringing steady rain to the west coast and leaving the east comparatively dry.

The flora has more in common with New Zealand and Patagonia than with the rest of Australia — a legacy of the ancient supercontinent Gondwana. Temperate rainforests of Huon pine, myrtle beech, and sassafras cover the western ranges, while the drier east is dominated by eucalypts. Tasmania is also home to unique fauna including the Tasmanian devil, the platypus, and the wombat.
