Bunbury is Western Australia's second city and the gateway to the South West — the wine country, surf coast and wilderness that stretches from here to Cape Leeuwin. Most travellers pass through on their way to [Margaret River](/australiaandpacific/australia/westernaustralia/margaretriver) or [Busselton](/australiaandpacific/australia/westernaustralia/bussleton), but the city rewards a stop of its own.

The most distinctive thing about Bunbury is its dolphins. Up to 90 bottlenose dolphins have made Koombana Bay their home, and they regularly swim up to the beach to interact with people wading in the shallows. The Dolphin Discovery Centre on the foreshore is the hub for this — you can wade in and hope for an encounter, or join a guided cruise out into the bay.

The old city centre has reinvented itself around food. Victoria Street, locally called the Cappuccino Strip, is lined with cafes and restaurants that fill up on weekend mornings. The Marlston waterfront development, carved out of industrial land on the northern edge of the CBD, adds a modern stretch of eateries and bars overlooking the Leschenault Estuary.

The landscape around Bunbury has some geological quirks worth knowing. Ancient lava flows left distinctive basalt formations along the city beach, and the world's last native Tuart forest — a woodland of massive, gnarled trees found nowhere else — lies just south of the city. The same flows created Marlston Hill, now a residential ridge with views across the estuary and Koombana Bay.

Bunbury sits about 175 km south of Perth — roughly two hours by road — and makes a practical base for exploring the South West before committing to the wineries further down.
