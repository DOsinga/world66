Baker Island is a tiny, flat coral atoll sitting almost exactly on the equator, roughly 2,600 km southwest of Honolulu. It is an unorganized, unincorporated territory of the United States — and it is completely off-limits to the general public.

The island measures about 1.6 km long and 1.1 km wide, with no lagoon, no fresh water, and no permanent residents. Its highest point barely reaches 8 meters above sea level. The landscape is scrub and sparse grasses, bleached by a relentless equatorial sun. It is not a destination in any conventional sense.

What Baker Island does have is wildlife. The island sits within the Pacific Remote Islands Marine National Monument and is managed as a national wildlife refuge by the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service. More than a million seabirds breed here, including lesser frigatebirds, masked boobies, sooty terns, and brown noddies. Green and hawksbill turtles nest on the beaches. The surrounding reef supports gray reef sharks, spinner dolphins, and Hawaiian monk seals. For researchers, it is a significant and largely undisturbed ecosystem.

The island was first sighted by American whalers in the early 19th century and formally claimed by the United States in 1857 under the Guano Act, which authorized American companies to mine bird droppings for fertilizer. In 1935, a small group of young Native Hawaiian men — part of the U.S. Colonization Program — were brought to Baker Island to establish a permanent American presence and counter British territorial claims. They built shelters, a lighthouse, and a small settlement. In 1942, Japanese aircraft bombed and strafed the island, killing two of the colonists. The survivors were evacuated and never returned. The ruins of their settlement, along with a small cemetery, remain on the west coast.

Today, entry to Baker Island is by special-use permit only, restricted to scientists, researchers, and educators with specific purposes. There are no facilities, no airstrip, and no regular supply service. If you are not arriving by research vessel with official authorization, Baker Island is not a place you can go.
