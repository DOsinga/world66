Fifteen islands scattered across an area of ocean the size of India, with a total population of around 17,000 people. That ratio — vast sea, tiny population — tells you most of what you need to know about the Cook Islands. These are not undiscovered islands dressed up as paradise. They are the real thing.

The Cook Islands sit in the heart of the South Pacific, about halfway between New Zealand and Hawaii. They are self-governing in free association with New Zealand, which means Cook Islanders hold New Zealand citizenship — a fact that contributes to the country's curious demography: more Cook Islanders live in New Zealand and Australia than in the islands themselves.

Tourism is concentrated almost entirely on [Rarotonga](/australiaandpacific/cookislands/rarotonga), the main island and home to the capital Avarua. It handles about 140,000 visitors a year and has the airports, restaurants, and infrastructure that goes with it. The real revelation, though, is [Aitutaki](/australiaandpacific/cookislands/aitutaki), whose lagoon — fifteen small motus ringing a vast turquoise expanse — is routinely called one of the most beautiful in the world. The outer islands — Atiu, Mangaia, Mauke, Mitiaro — see almost no tourists and reward those who make the effort with a quieter, older version of Polynesian life.

There are no high-rise hotels. Sundays are taken seriously. The pace is slow and mostly unapologetic about it.
