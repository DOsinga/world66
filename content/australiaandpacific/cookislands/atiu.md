Atiu is the third largest Cook Island and one of the most interesting for travellers who want more than beaches. The island is surrounded by *makatea* — raised coral limestone — a jagged, densely forested ring that gives Atiu its dramatic character and makes most of its coastline inaccessible. Inside the makatea sits a fertile plateau where five villages cluster together.

The main draw for visitors is the island's cave system. Anatakitaki Cave is home to the *kopeka* (Atiu swiftlet), a small bird found nowhere else in the world that navigates in the dark by echolocation. Tours through the cave are run by local guides and are the highlight of most visits. Other caves served as burial sites in pre-Christian times.

Atiu has a tiny but committed birdwatching community — the island's isolation has preserved several endemic species, and the *ura* (Atiu fruit dove), *kura* (Atiu starling), and the kopeka are all targets. Local guide Roger Malcolm is well-known in birding circles.

Accommodation is limited to a handful of small guesthouses. There are no restaurants; your hosts cook. This is not a hardship — it is part of the point. Get there on Air Rarotonga (about an hour).
