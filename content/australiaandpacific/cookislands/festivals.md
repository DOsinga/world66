---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

**Te Maeva Nui** (late July to August 4) is the country's national celebration, marking Constitution Day on August 4th. The week-long festival includes traditional dance performances, choral competitions, float parades, art exhibitions, and large communal feasts. Every island sends performers to Rarotonga, and the competitive dancing is serious business — schools and villages prepare for months. It is the best time to experience Cook Islands culture at full intensity, and the atmosphere is genuinely celebratory. Flights and accommodation fill up weeks in advance; book early.

**Te Mire Ura — Dancer of the Year** (April or May) is an annual dance competition held at the National Auditorium on Rarotonga. Performers in elaborate traditional costumes compete for a title that carries real prestige. For anyone interested in Polynesian dance, it is an extraordinary display of skill and preparation.

**Manureva Aquafest** is held on Aitutaki, typically in October, and centres on the lagoon. Events include outrigger canoe racing, kitesurfing, stand-up paddleboarding, and swimming competitions, with traditional food and craft stalls at the associated night market. A good combination of sport and culture.

**Te Mire Tiare Flower Festival** (November–December) fills Rarotonga with floral decorations — buildings, vehicles, and homes are decorated following a theme, and various flower-related events take place. Lighter in scale than Te Maeva Nui but pleasant if you happen to be visiting.

The standard Pacific holiday calendar applies: Christmas and Easter see influxes of New Zealand and Australian visitors, prices go up, and the islands feel livelier than usual.
