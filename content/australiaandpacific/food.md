---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Food across this region varies enormously — from the sophisticated multicultural restaurant scenes of Sydney and Melbourne to the seafood and root vegetable cooking of remote Pacific islands, where tradition and geography dictate what ends up on the table.

**Australia** has developed one of the most interesting food cultures in the world over the past few decades. The foundation is British-influenced, but successive waves of migration — Greek, Italian, Lebanese, Vietnamese, Chinese, Indian — have transformed Australian cooking into something genuinely distinctive. Sydney and Melbourne have world-class restaurant scenes, strong café cultures, and an obsession with coffee (the flat white originated here and the standards are high). Bush tucker — native ingredients like wattleseed, quandong, lemon myrtle, and kangaroo — has moved from novelty to mainstream in upscale restaurants. Seafood is excellent along the coasts: Sydney rock oysters, Moreton Bay bugs, barramundi, and crayfish from Western Australia are all worth seeking out.

**New Zealand** produces exceptional ingredients — Marlborough wine, Bluff oysters, king salmon from the South Island's rivers, Canterbury lamb — and the food scene has matured significantly. Auckland's restaurant landscape is varied and cosmopolitan. Smaller towns along the South Island offer farm-to-table cooking with local produce at its best. The Maori hangi — meat and vegetables slow-cooked in an earth oven — is worth trying at a cultural event; it's a communal meal as much as a cooking method.

**Fiji** blends indigenous Fijian cooking with significant Indian influence, a legacy of indentured labourers brought to work sugar plantations in the 19th century. Kokoda — raw fish marinated in citrus and coconut cream — is the standout local dish, a ceviche that matches the climate. Indian-Fijian food, particularly the curries and roti, is often better than what you'd find in many parts of India itself.

**French Polynesia** brings French technique to Pacific ingredients, with results that can be genuinely excellent in Papeete's better restaurants. Poisson cru — raw fish in lime juice and coconut milk — is the local staple and appears at every meal. Breadfruit, taro, and plantains fill out the traditional diet. Prices are high; French Polynesia is an expensive destination and food is no exception.

**Across the Pacific islands**, the common thread is the earth oven — called a lovo in Fiji, an umu in Samoa and Tonga, a himaa in Tahiti. Meat, fish, and root vegetables wrapped in leaves and slow-cooked over hot stones produce something tender and smoky that's difficult to replicate. If you're invited to a feast, go. Coconut features in almost everything — as cream, as cooking medium, as drink. Fresh fish, often eaten raw or barely cooked, is the primary protein across most island nations.

The Pacific is not a fine dining destination — in most places, eating is simple and communal. What it offers instead is food that is tightly connected to place, season, and community in ways that can be hard to find elsewhere.
