**Typee** and **Omoo** by Herman Melville. Melville jumped ship in the Marquesas in 1842 and spent time among the Taipi people — *Typee* is his novelised account. *Omoo* continues the story in Tahiti. More adventure yarn than ethnography, but beautifully written and still the best literary introduction to 19th-century Polynesia.

**The Moon and Sixpence** by W. Somerset Maugham. Loosely based on Paul Gauguin, the story of a stockbroker who abandons London for Tahiti to paint. Maugham visited Tahiti himself and captures the heat and languor of the place better than any travel writer of his era.

**Noa Noa** by Paul Gauguin. Gauguin's own account of his first years in Tahiti — part memoir, part fantasy. Read it alongside his paintings for context, and approach it critically; he was not always an honest or sympathetic narrator. His grave is in Atuona on Hiva Oa in the Marquesas.

**The Pillars of the Earth of the Pacific** — the Marquesas have attracted writers for over a century. Robert Louis Stevenson visited on his Pacific voyages and wrote about the islands in *In the South Seas* (1896), which remains a vivid account of island-hopping in the late 19th century.

**Tahiti and French Polynesia** by Jan Prince. A practical guide that has gone through multiple editions. Useful for the Society Islands and the outer archipelagos.

**Hidden Tahiti and French Polynesia** by Robert Kay. More detailed on lesser-visited islands than most guides.
