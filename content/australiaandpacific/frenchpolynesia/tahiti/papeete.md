Papeete is the capital of **French Polynesia**, a low-rise waterfront city of about 130,000 people on the north-west coast of **Tahiti**. It is not a beautiful city — the harbour is industrial, the centre is dominated by concrete offices and a cruise-ship terminal, and for many travellers it is mostly a transit point on the way to Bora Bora, Moorea, or the Marquesas. That said, nearly every visitor to French Polynesia passes through here at some point, and a day or two is enough to see what there is of it.

The **Marché de Papeete**, a two-storey covered market in the centre of town, is the most characterful spot — ground floor for fish, flowers, tropical fruit and vanilla, upper floor for pareos, woven hats, *monoi* coconut oil, and the famous **Tahitian black pearls**. Along the harbour, **Place Vai'ete** comes to life every evening when the *roulottes* (food trucks) set up to serve cheap grilled fish, steak-frites, and chow mein to locals and tourists alike. For culture, the **Museum of Tahiti and Its Islands** (a short drive west of the centre in Punaauia) has the best collection of Polynesian artefacts in the country, and is worth a half-day. The **Cathedral of Notre-Dame** on Rue Général de Gaulle is the city's one pretty colonial building.

Beyond Papeete, Tahiti itself has good hiking in the mountainous interior (the **Fautaua Valley** waterfall is reachable on a half-day walk), black-sand volcanic beaches on the east coast, and the smaller **Tahiti Iti** peninsula in the south-east where the surfing break of **Teahupo'o** is one of the most famous in the world. Most visitors, however, use Papeete as a jumping-off point for the outer islands; ferries to **Moorea** leave several times a day and take 30 minutes, and the inter-island airport handles flights to Bora Bora, Rangiroa, and the Marquesas.
