**Liberation Day (July 21)** is the most important public holiday on Guam. It marks the day in 1944 when US forces landed at Apra Harbor and began liberating the island from nearly three years of Japanese occupation. The celebration is large: a parade through Hagatna, village fiestas, food stalls, and performances. For many Chamorros, it carries complex emotions — relief, pride in survival, and an ongoing reckoning with what occupation and liberation both meant. It's worth being here for if you're traveling in July.

**Village fiestas** are the heartbeat of Chamorro social life. Each of Guam's 19 villages celebrates a feast day honoring its patron saint, typically with an outdoor mass followed by an enormous communal meal. Tables fill with red rice, kelaguen, barbecued meats, and desserts. Visitors are generally welcome — Chamorros are famously hospitable at fiestas, and showing up respectfully is usually invitation enough. The fiestas are spread throughout the year; ask locally which village is celebrating when.

**Guam History and CHamoru Heritage Day** falls on the first Monday of March and celebrates Chamorro culture specifically, not just history. The largest event is the Humatak Festival held in [Umatac](/australiaandpacific/guam/umatac) on the south coast — the bay where Magellan landed in 1521 — with a re-enactment of the landing, traditional outrigger canoe demonstrations, and cultural performances. It's a good-natured event that takes its history seriously.

**Micronesia Island Fair**, held periodically at the Festival Market near Hagatna, brings together cultures from across Micronesia — traditional crafts, music, dance, and food from Guam, Palau, the Federated States, and the Marianas. Dates vary; check with the Guam Visitors Bureau.
