*[Australia and Pacific](/australiaandpacific) > [Kiribati](/australiaandpacific/kiribati) > Tarawa*

Tarawa is the capital atoll of [Kiribati](/australiaandpacific/kiribati) and the country's political, economic, and social center. South Tarawa, a narrow ribbon of islets connected by causeways, is home to roughly half of Kiribati's entire population, making it one of the most densely populated places in the Pacific. The contrast between the turquoise lagoon and the crowded, often chaotic settlements along the road is striking and honest: this is a place of real beauty and real pressure, where rising seas and a growing population compete for a sliver of coral land barely three meters above the waterline.

Tarawa is best known internationally for the brutal Battle of Tarawa in November 1943, one of the bloodiest engagements in the Pacific War. Over three days of fighting on the tiny islet of Betio, more than a thousand US Marines and nearly five thousand Japanese defenders were killed. The battle shocked the American public and changed how amphibious assaults were planned for the rest of the war. Today Betio still holds rusting gun emplacements, bunkers, and the occasional piece of wreckage along its shores, making it a sobering and fascinating place to walk through.

Beyond the war history, Tarawa offers a window into contemporary Pacific life that few travelers ever see. The markets sell fresh fish and imported rice, maneabas (traditional meeting houses) host community gatherings, and the lagoon side of the atoll is good for swimming once you find a clean stretch. There is a small diving scene in North Tarawa, where the reef is healthier and the crowds thin out. Getting here means flying through Fiji or the Marshall Islands on limited schedules, so plan well ahead and bring patience.
