Bikini Atoll is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most consequential places in the Pacific. Between 1946 and 1958, the United States detonated 23 nuclear weapons here, including Castle Bravo in 1954 — the largest US nuclear test ever, a thousand times more powerful than the Hiroshima bomb. The fallout from Castle Bravo drifted over inhabited atolls to the east, contaminating the people of Rongelap and Utrik.

The Bikinians were relocated in 1946 with promises of a safe return. They have never been able to go back. Radiation levels on the main island remain too high for permanent habitation, and the coconuts and fish are still contaminated. The community, now dispersed across several atolls and parts of the United States, has been fighting for compensation and justice ever since.

What the tests left behind in the lagoon is what draws the visitors who do make it here. The warships sunk during Operation Crossroads in 1946 — including the aircraft carrier USS Saratoga and the Japanese battleship Nagato — now rest in the lagoon at depths between 40 and 55 metres, encrusted with coral and draped in marine life. The visibility is exceptional. For wreck divers, this is one of the great sites on earth.

Access is difficult. There is no regular transport and no facilities on the atoll. Visits are organized exclusively through live-aboard dive boats, and permits must be arranged in advance through the Bikini Atoll local government. The cost is substantial and the logistics complex. For those who make the effort, the combination of history, marine life, and sheer remoteness makes it unforgettable.
