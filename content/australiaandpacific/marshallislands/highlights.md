title: "Highlights"

---

**Diving Bikini Atoll.** Bikini is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most extraordinary dive destinations on earth. The atoll's lagoon holds a graveyard of warships — the USS Saratoga, the Nagato, the Arkansas — sunk during the 1946 Operation Crossroads nuclear tests. Coral has colonized them over the decades, and the visibility in the lagoon is stunning. Getting here requires a live-aboard boat and serious planning, but serious divers consider it a once-in-a-lifetime experience. The nuclear history is impossible to separate from the diving, and you should not try to — the ships sit where they were deliberately sunk as test targets, and the people who lived on this atoll have never been able to come home.

**The lagoon at Arno Atoll.** Just southeast of Majuro, Arno is one of the easiest outer atolls to reach and one of the most beautiful. Its lagoon — enormous, shallow in places, clear — is ideal for snorkeling, kayaking, and simply being on the water. The pace of life here is slower and more traditional than in the capital. Day trips from Majuro are possible; staying overnight on the atoll gives a much better sense of the place.

**Kwajalein's wreck dives.** [Kwajalein](/australiaandpacific/marshallislands/kwajaleinatoll)'s lagoon holds the remains of over 100 Japanese aircraft and numerous ships from the Battle of Kwajalein in early 1944. It is a different experience from Bikini — closer, more accessible, less dramatic above water — but underwater it is exceptional. Access is through Ebeye, the Marshallese community adjacent to the U.S. military base.

**Majuro's Alele Museum.** Small but genuinely interesting, the Alele Museum in [Majuro](/australiaandpacific/marshallislands/majuro) holds traditional navigational stick charts (rebbelib and mattang), canoe models, and exhibits on Marshallese history including the nuclear testing era. The navigational charts — made from curved sticks and shells to map ocean swells — represent one of the most sophisticated non-written navigation systems ever developed.

**The outer island experience.** The Marshalls have 29 inhabited atolls, and most receive very few visitors. Taking a field trip ship to a remote atoll — [Jaluit](/australiaandpacific/marshallislands/jaluitatoll), [Wotje](/australiaandpacific/marshallislands/wotjeatoll), or [Maloelap](/australiaandpacific/marshallislands/maloelapatoll) — takes patience and flexibility, but the reward is a Pacific that feels genuinely untouched. WWII ruins dot many of these atolls; Japanese fortifications still stand at Jaluit.
