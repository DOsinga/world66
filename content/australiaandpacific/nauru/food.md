---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Eating in Nauru requires low expectations and flexibility. Almost all food is imported from Australia, arriving by ship or air at irregular intervals — sometimes as infrequently as every six to eight weeks. Between deliveries, fresh produce is scarce or unavailable, and shop shelves can look bare.

The main hotels — primarily the Menen Hotel — have restaurants that offer the most reliable meals. Expect Western food alongside Chinese dishes; Chinese-run restaurants are common on the island. Seafood appears on menus and is worth ordering when available.

Traditional Nauruan diet centred on fish, coconut, and pandanus fruit. That diet has largely been displaced by imported processed food, a shift directly linked to the island's severe public health crisis. Fresh fruit and vegetables are hard to find and expensive.

There are a handful of small shops and takeaway places around the island, but don't rely on them for every meal. Stock up on snacks and long-life supplies when you see them, in case the next delivery hasn't come in.
