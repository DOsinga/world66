---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**Anibare Bay** is Nauru's finest beach — a long arc of white sand on the eastern coast with clear water and good snorkelling on the reef. It is calm and largely deserted. The underwater visibility is excellent.

**Buada Lagoon** sits in the interior of the island, surrounded by tropical vegetation. It is the only freshwater body on Nauru and feels remarkably lush given the devastated landscape around it. Local children swim here. Walking paths connect it to the coast.

**Command Ridge** is the island's highest point — around 71 metres above sea level, which passes for dramatic on a flat coral island. During the Japanese occupation in World War II it served as an observation post. Bunkers and gun emplacements are still visible. The views across the island are striking, particularly of the phosphate wasteland and the coast beyond.

**The Phosphate Moonscape** is not pretty, but it is one of the most honest landscapes on Earth. The interior of Nauru — roughly 80 percent of the island's land area — was strip-mined over the course of a century. What remains is a field of jagged limestone pinnacles called pinnacles or "topside," with abandoned mining equipment rusting among them. The Nauru Phosphate Corporation offers tours. It is sobering and worth seeing.

**WWII Relics** are scattered around the island. Nauru was occupied by Japan from 1942 to 1945. Coastal gun placements, concrete bunkers, and the rusted hulks of Japanese equipment remain at several sites, particularly near Anibare Bay. The Nauruan wartime experience — including the deportation of hundreds of islanders to Truk — is a significant part of local history.

**The Ring Road Circuit** takes under an hour by car or a few hours by bicycle. It passes through every district, skirts the reef, and gives a complete picture of the island. There is no better way to orient yourself.
