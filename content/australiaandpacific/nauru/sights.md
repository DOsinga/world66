---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Nauru's sights are not the kind found in guidebooks that list opening hours and admission prices. Most require nothing more than showing up. The island is small enough to see everything in two or three days.

The **phosphate mining area** in the interior is the defining sight. Nothing quite prepares you for the scale of it: a vast field of jagged limestone pillars — the coral skeleton left behind after the phosphate was extracted — stretching across most of the island's interior. The Nauru Phosphate Corporation offers guided tours that put the history and economics into context. It is one of the most direct illustrations of resource extraction anywhere in the Pacific.

**Command Ridge**, at 71 metres the island's highest point, holds the best-preserved WWII relics. Japanese gun emplacements and observation bunkers survive in reasonable condition. The views over the island are excellent. The Japanese occupied Nauru from August 1942, deported over 1,200 Nauruans to Truk (only around 700 returned), and used the island as a strategic base until Allied bombing and naval blockade made the position untenable. The physical traces of that occupation are still easy to find.

**Anibare Bay**, on the eastern coast, is both a sight and an activity. The bay is beautiful — long, calm, and nearly empty — with a reef that drops into clear water. Snorkelling here on a calm day is straightforward and rewarding.

**Buada Lagoon** is a freshwater lake in the island's interior, surrounded by lush vegetation that contrasts sharply with the mined-out wasteland nearby. It's a pleasant walk and a popular spot for local families.

The **Nauruan Parliament House** and the main government district near Yaren are modest but worth noting as evidence of a functioning state administration in a very small country.
