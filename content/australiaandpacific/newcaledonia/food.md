title: "Food"
type: section
---

New Caledonian food reflects the territory's layered history: French technique meets Kanak tradition, with significant Asian influence from Vietnamese and Indonesian communities, and the Pacific's extraordinary seafood.

**Bougna** is the essential Kanak dish. Root vegetables — yam, taro, sweet potato — are combined with chicken, fish, or shellfish, wrapped in banana leaves, and cooked over hot stones in the ground. It takes hours to prepare and is typically made for ceremonies or communal celebrations. You're unlikely to find it on a menu; you're more likely to be invited to share it. Accept if you can.

**Seafood** is the territory's great strength. The lagoon produces blue prawns (sweeter and firmer than most), spiny lobster (langouste), coconut crab, and a huge variety of fish. In Noumea, several restaurants serve grilled langouste with French sauces; further afield, simpler preparations — fish grilled over charcoal, eaten with your feet in the sand — are often better.

**French bistro cooking** is taken seriously in Noumea. You'll find steak-frites, cassoulet, charcuterie, and decent wine lists. The quality is genuinely good. Lunch is often better value than dinner.

**Vietnamese and Indonesian food** is available in Noumea and adds variety — a legacy of indentured laborers brought to the territory in the early 20th century.

**Poe** is the local dessert: a pudding made from banana or pumpkin, served with coconut cream. Simple and good.

**Drinking:** Beer (locally Numbo and Manta) is the everyday drink. French wine is expensive but available. Kava, though not the Pacific's main kava culture, is drunk in some Kanak communities.

For restaurant recommendations in the capital, see [Noumea](/australiaandpacific/newcaledonia/noumea).
