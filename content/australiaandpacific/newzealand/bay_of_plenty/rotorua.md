Rotorua smells like rotten eggs and it doesn't apologize for it. Hydrogen sulphide from the volcanic vents beneath the town gives the air a sharp sulphurous edge, and once you're used to it you start to appreciate that this place is genuinely different — a city built on a geothermal field where steam rises from roadside drains and mud pools bubble in public parks.

The geothermal activity here is dramatic. Te Puia, on the southern edge of the city, holds the Pohutu Geyser — one of the largest active geysers in the Southern Hemisphere, erupting up to 20 times a day to heights of 30 metres. Whakarewarewa Thermal Village, adjacent to Te Puia, has been continuously inhabited by Māori for centuries; people still cook in the boiling pools and the village is one of the most honest encounters with living Māori culture you'll find anywhere in the country.

Rotorua has been a tourist destination since the 1880s, when visitors first came to see the then-spectacular Pink and White Terraces (destroyed by the 1886 Tarawera eruption). That long tradition of welcoming visitors means the infrastructure is good, the experiences are well-organized, and the Māori cultural performances are polished — sometimes a little too polished, but still worth your time if you approach them with the right expectations.

The Polynesian Spa on the lakefront is one of the better reasons to linger. Fed by two distinct mineral springs, it has a range of outdoor pools at different temperatures with views over Lake Rotorua.
