Christchurch is the largest city on New Zealand's South Island and the country's most English-feeling place — the founding settlers in the 1850s explicitly set out to build a model Anglican city on the Canterbury Plains, and for 150 years they largely succeeded. The River Avon (its name imported from England along with the willow trees that line its banks) winds through the centre, the grand neo-Gothic cathedral dominated Cathedral Square, and the old colleges and stone public buildings gave the city the unmistakable atmosphere of a 19th-century English university town transplanted to the South Pacific. Then, on 22 February 2011, a 6.3-magnitude earthquake hit directly beneath the central city, killed 185 people, and destroyed or damaged more than 80 percent of the buildings in the CBD.

Rebuilding has been slow, difficult, and not without controversy, but the Christchurch you will visit now is one of the most interesting cities in Australasia precisely because of what it has been through. The central city is a patchwork of old stone survivors, gleaming new architecture, empty lots, temporary installations, street art, and ambitious projects like the **Cardboard Cathedral** (a transitional church by Japanese architect Shigeru Ban, built from cardboard tubes and originally intended to last only 50 years), the **185 Empty Chairs** memorial — 185 white chairs of different styles arranged on the site of a destroyed office building, one for each of the victims — and the **Riverside Market**, a new covered food hall along the Avon that has become the social heart of the rebuild. The old **ChristChurch Cathedral** remains partly in ruins, with a lengthy restoration finally underway.

Around the city centre, several of the best things have survived or come back. The **Canterbury Museum** holds a strong collection on Antarctic exploration (Christchurch was the historic departure point for expeditions south, and the **International Antarctic Centre** near the airport is an excellent family attraction that simulates the Antarctic experience). The **Christchurch Botanic Gardens** and adjoining Hagley Park cover 164 hectares in the city centre and are among the finest public gardens in the Southern Hemisphere. The **Arts Centre**, the beautiful Gothic Revival stone complex that was once the University of Canterbury, has been painstakingly restored after the quake and is now full of small galleries, craft shops, cafés, and the **Rutherford's Den** exhibit where physicist Ernest Rutherford once studied. Ride the restored heritage tram through the central city.

Christchurch is also the main base for exploring the South Island. It is a ninety-minute drive to **Akaroa** on the **Banks Peninsula** (a French-founded village with dolphin-swimming tours), two hours to the **Canterbury Plains high country**, three hours west to the West Coast via the spectacular **TranzAlpine train** through Arthur's Pass, and five hours south-west to Queenstown. Rent a campervan, spend a day in the city, and head out across one of the most dramatic landscapes on Earth.
