The **Coromandel Peninsula** juts out into the Hauraki Gulf east of Auckland, close enough to see from the city on a clear day yet unmistakably its own place. Jagged volcanic hills covered in native bush run down the spine of the peninsula, dropping to a coastline of sandy beaches, sheltered coves, and rocky headlands. It's 90 minutes from New Zealand's largest city, but it feels considerably further away.

The peninsula made its money from kauri timber and gold in the 19th century. Small towns like [Coromandel Town](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/coromandel) still have the colonial timber architecture to show for it. The forests that weren't logged still hold stands of giant kauri — some over a thousand years old — and the Coromandel Forest Park protects much of the interior.

The two coasts have different characters. The western shore faces the Hauraki Gulf with sheltered harbours and gentle beaches. The eastern coast catches the Pacific swell, offering long white-sand surf beaches and quieter bays. [Whitianga](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/whitianga) is the main town on the east coast; [Hahei](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/hahei) and its dramatic Cathedral Cove are a short drive further south.

Hot Water Beach is the Coromandel's most famous quirk: at low tide, visitors dig into the sand with rented spades and warm thermal water rises to fill the hole. It works best within two hours either side of low tide, and it's genuinely good fun despite the crowds.
