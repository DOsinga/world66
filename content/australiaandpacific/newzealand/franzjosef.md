Franz Josef is a small glacier town on the West Coast of New Zealand's South Island, named for the glacier that flows down from the Southern Alps to within a few kilometres of the rainforest. The Franz Josef Glacier is one of the few glaciers in the world that descends to such a low altitude — it reaches roughly 300 metres above sea level — creating a startling juxtaposition of ice and temperate rainforest. The glacier has retreated significantly in recent decades, but it remains an extraordinary sight.

The town itself is little more than a strip of hostels, cafes, and activity operators, but it is the best base for exploring the glacier and the surrounding Westland Tai Poutini National Park. Guided heli-hike trips — fly by helicopter to the glacier's upper reaches and walk on the ice with crampons — are the most popular experience. The lower glacier valley is accessible on foot via a well-marked track, though the terminal face has retreated to the point where the ice itself is now some distance from the main viewpoint. The surrounding forests and Waiho River valley are worth exploring even without glacier access.

Fox Glacier, a similar and equally impressive glacier, is 25 km to the south and makes a natural pairing. Between them, they draw a steady flow of visitors despite the remoteness of the West Coast.
