Kapiti Island is a long, craggy ridge of land rising out of the Tasman Sea about five kilometres off the Kapiti Coast, an hour's drive north of [Wellington](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/wellington). For the last century it has been a strictly protected nature reserve — predators have been eradicated and the island is now one of the best places in New Zealand to see birds that have almost vanished from the mainland: kokako, takahe, stitchbird, saddleback, and plenty of noisy, curious kaka.

Visitor numbers are limited and a permit from the Department of Conservation is required before you can land. Two licensed operators run boats across from Paraparaumu Beach each morning; you choose between the main Rangatira landing, with its walking tracks climbing to the 521-metre summit of Tuteremoana, and the northern end at Waiorua Bay, where a Maori-owned lodge offers day visits and overnight stays focused on guided birdwatching.

The crossing is short but often rough. Bring layers, waterproof boots, and a packed lunch — there are no shops on the island, and the rules about what you can carry ashore (no food in plastic bags, no seeds, no soil on your boots) are strict and strictly enforced.
