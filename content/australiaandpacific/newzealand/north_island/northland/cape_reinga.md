Cape Reinga is the northernmost accessible point of the North Island, and one of the most dramatic places in New Zealand. Standing at the lighthouse, you can watch the Pacific Ocean and the Tasman Sea collide — a visible white line of churning water stretching out toward the horizon where the two bodies of water meet.

The contrast is striking. The Tasman Sea, to the west, is the wild side: rough, temperamental, with heavy surf crashing against the coastline at Te Werahi Beach below. Turn to the east and the Pacific is comparatively calm, its waves rolling in with less force. The difference in colour and mood between the two seas is obvious even from the clifftop.

For Māori, this place carries deep spiritual significance. Cape Reinga — Te Rerenga Wairua in te reo Māori, meaning "the leaping-off place of spirits" — is where the souls of the dead depart the earth, sliding down the roots of an ancient pohutukawa tree and journeying north toward Hawaiki. The gnarled tree still clings to the cliffs below the lighthouse.

Getting here requires commitment. The cape is about 110 kilometres from Kaitaia, mostly on sealed road. Many visitors come on day tours from the Bay of Islands or Paihia, which often include a drive along Ninety Mile Beach. The lighthouse at the tip is a short walk from the car park.
