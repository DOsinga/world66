The Whanganui region of New Zealand's North Island takes its name from the Whanganui River, which winds 290 km from the slopes of Mount Tongariro to the Tasman Sea. The river is one of the few in New Zealand navigable for most of its length, and for a century before roads arrived, it was the only route into the interior — a kind of antipodean Amazon for travellers and missionaries.

The spelling of the name — Whanganui or Wanganui — has been contested for decades, and the debate is a live issue rather than an academic one. The Māori cultural revival centred in this region has pushed for the original form, and both spellings remain in official use.

The city of [Wanganui](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/wanganui) at the river's mouth is the region's main centre. The river upstream, through the Whanganui National Park, can be explored by canoe or jet-boat — one of New Zealand's Great Walks follows the river for five days through dense bush and past remote marae.
