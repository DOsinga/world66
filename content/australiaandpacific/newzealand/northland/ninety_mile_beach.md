Ninety Mile Beach -- or Te Oneroa a Tohe, its Maori name -- stretches roughly 90 kilometres along Northland's west coast. It is officially gazetted as a road, though rental car companies will not insure vehicles driven on it. There is a reason for that: this is wild, open Tasman Sea coastline, and the conditions change with every tide.

The best access point is at Waipapakauri. Head north out of [Kaitaia](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/kaitaia), through Awanui, and follow the signs. The road ends at a car park where you should stop, get out, and check the conditions before driving onto the sand. Aim to arrive about three hours after high tide, but always assess the surface -- even with the right timing, currents, wind, and the moon can change things dramatically between tides.

At the northern end, [Te Paki Stream](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/northland/te_paki_stream) serves as the main on-ramp and off-ramp for vehicles and tour buses. The giant sand dunes on the northern side of the stream are popular for sandboarding.

The beach is a serious fishing destination. At the end of January the annual Ninety Mile Beach Fishing Contest draws anglers from across the country. Snapper, kahawai, shark, and flounder can all be caught here depending on the season, by surf casting or drag net. Tuatua, a shellfish, can be gathered in the shallows.

Every tree along this coast grows with a lean to the east -- a testament to the relentless westerlies blowing off the Tasman. When the sky is clear, the sunsets are extraordinary: a huge orange fireball dropping straight into the ocean.
