Milford Sound is technically a fiord, not a sound — carved by glaciers rather than rivers — but the Welsh explorer who named it after Milford Haven in Wales did not know the difference in 1823, and the name stuck. Whatever you call it, nothing quite prepares you for the place: towering granite walls rise over a thousand metres from dark water, waterfalls cascade from every hanging valley, and the weather shifts from rain to blazing sunshine in minutes.

This is the most photographed spot in [New Zealand](/australiaandpacific/newzealand), and justifiably so. Two tectonic plates — the Pacific and the Australasian — meet here, which goes some way to explaining the dramatic landscape.

A boat cruise is essential. The tours take you the length of the fiord, passing beneath waterfalls that range from gentle trickles to roaring torrents after rain. Guides discuss the geology and Polynesian history, and you will likely see fur seals, dolphins, and, in deeper water, penguins. Near the northern end, an underwater observatory lets you watch the marine life through submerged windows — black coral, anemones, and fish thrive in the dark freshwater layer that sits atop the seawater.

Most visitors drive in from [Te Anau](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/otago/te_anau), about two hours away on one of the most scenic roads in the country. There are several stop-off points along the way that are worth your time if the weather is fine. Fill up with petrol before you leave — there are no fuel stations at the sound.
