title: "Highlights"

---

New Zealand is a small country with an extraordinary variety of landscapes. Most visitors see only a fraction of it — the main tourist trail covers Auckland, Rotorua, Queenstown, Milford Sound, and Christchurch, which are all worth visiting, but the country rewards those who venture further.

**North Island**

[Auckland](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/auckland) is the starting point for most visitors. The city itself has good food, lively neighbourhoods, and easy access to Waiheke Island (wine, beaches, 35 minutes by ferry) and Rangitoto Island (volcanic cone, lava caves, day trip). The [Bay of Islands](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/bayofislands) in Northland is where European settlement began — small towns, historic sites, sailing, and dolphin swimming.

[Rotorua](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/rotorua) smells of sulphur and leans hard into Maori tourism, some of it better than others. The geothermal activity is genuinely impressive — boiling mud pools, geysers, and steaming hillsides. Te Puia and Whakarewarewa are the main cultural sites. The surrounding region has excellent mountain biking.

The Tongariro Alpine Crossing is one of the most popular day walks in the country: 19.4km across an active volcanic plateau past emerald crater lakes. Start early, check the weather, and arrange transport both ways. [Taupo](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/taupo) makes a good base.

[Wellington](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/wellington) is compact, walkable, and punches above its weight culturally. Te Papa, the national museum, is genuinely excellent and free. Cuba Street is the place to eat and drink.

**South Island**

The [Marlborough](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/marlborough) wine region at the top of the South Island is worth a day or two. The Marlborough Sounds, accessible by boat or kayak, are strikingly beautiful.

[Nelson](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/nelson) and the Abel Tasman coast offer beaches, walking, and kayaking in a more relaxed setting than the main tourist towns. The Abel Tasman Coast Track is the country's most popular multi-day walk.

[Christchurch](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/christchurch) is still rebuilding after the 2010–11 earthquakes but has developed an interesting culture around temporary spaces, container markets, and unconventional architecture. The Canterbury Plains stretch west toward the Southern Alps, with Arthur's Pass as the main route through.

[Queenstown](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/queenstown) is the adventure capital — bungy jumping was invented here, and the region offers skiing, skydiving, jet boating, and mountain biking. It's expensive and heavily marketed, but the setting on Lake Wakatipu beneath the Remarkables range is hard to argue with. [Wanaka](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/wanaka) is similar but quieter and increasingly worth choosing over Queenstown.

[Milford Sound](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/milfordsound) (technically a fiord, not a sound) is the most visited natural attraction in New Zealand. The drive in through Fiordland is spectacular. It gets around 200 days of rain a year, which fills the waterfalls and creates the famous misty atmosphere. Day cruises run year-round; overnight cruises are worth it if you can afford the extra time.

The Franz Josef and Fox Glaciers on the [West Coast](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/west_coast) have retreated significantly due to climate change and heli-hike access is now the main way to see them up close. The West Coast is wild, wet, and relatively untouristed — the pancake rocks at Punakaiki are worth a stop on the coastal drive.
