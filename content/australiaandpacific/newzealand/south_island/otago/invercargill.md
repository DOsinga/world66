Invercargill sits at the bottom of the [South Island](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/south_island), closer to Antarctica than to Auckland. It is the southernmost city in New Zealand and one of the southernmost in the world — a fact locals are quietly proud of. The city was laid out on a grand Scottish grid in the 1850s, and it still has the unhurried, slightly eccentric feel of a provincial town that has always done things its own way.

The centre is compact and walkable, with a handful of good museums and some fine Victorian and Edwardian streetscapes. Anderson Park and its art gallery, and the Southland Museum and Art Gallery — home to the country's largest population of tuatara — are the two main draws. The surrounding Southland plains are flat and fertile, constantly lashed by weather rolling in off Foveaux Strait.

Invercargill is a practical base for exploring the far south: [Fiordland](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/south_island/southland/fiordland) is within reach to the northwest, and the ferry to Stewart Island — one of New Zealand's best places to see kiwi in the wild — departs from Bluff, 27 km south.
