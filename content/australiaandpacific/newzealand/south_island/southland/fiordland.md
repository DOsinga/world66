**Fiordland** occupies the southwest corner of the [South Island](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/south_island), a vast wilderness of glacier-carved fiords, ancient rainforest, and peaks that catch cloud and rain for most of the year. Almost all of it falls within Fiordland National Park — at 1.2 million hectares, one of the largest national parks in the world and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. There are valleys here that have never been walked, and aerial surveys regularly find new waterfalls no one has named.

The main draw is [Milford Sound](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/milfordsound), the fiord that tourists reach by bus or small aircraft from Queenstown. Mitre Peak rises 1,692 metres straight from the water, waterfalls drop hundreds of metres off hanging valleys, and dolphins, seals, and occasionally penguins move through the dark green water. It rains around 200 days a year here — the waterfalls are most spectacular in wet weather, and the rain keeps the sandflies active, so come prepared.

Doubtful Sound, deeper and harder to reach, sees a fraction of the visitors and rewards the effort: it is three times the length of Milford, completely roadless, and genuinely remote. Access requires a boat across Lake Manapouri and a bus over Wilmot Pass.

The great walks — the Milford Track and the Kepler Track — are among the most famous hiking routes in New Zealand. Both require advance booking months ahead, especially in summer (December to February). The Routeburn Track, shared with [Otago](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/south_island/otago), offers similar scenery with slightly less competition for hut places.

Fiordland's isolation is also its appeal: this is genuinely wild country, and the experience of arriving by boat into a fiord surrounded by unbroken forest and vertical rock is one the region offers nowhere else.
