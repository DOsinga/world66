Tutukaka is a small harbour village on the northeast coast of New Zealand's North Island, about 30 minutes from [Whangarei](/australiaandpacific/newzealand/whangarei). It exists almost entirely as the launching point for trips to the Poor Knights Islands, 15 km offshore — a marine reserve that Jacques Cousteau rated among his ten favourite dive sites in the world.

The Poor Knights are a strict nature reserve: no one is allowed to set foot on the islands without scientific permits. But the surrounding waters are open, and they are extraordinary. The volcanic rock is riddled with caves, arches, and tunnels; the warm East Auckland Current brings subtropical species unusually far south. Visibility regularly exceeds 20 metres. You will not find tropical coral here — instead, expect kelp forests, enormous schools of fish, moray eels, scorpionfish, stingrays, and occasional bronze whaler sharks. The underwater landscape itself is dramatic: sheer walls dropping into blue water, swim-through caves, and vast archways.

Several dive operators run trips from the Tutukaka marina, with options for both certified divers and snorkellers. Kayaking trips to the island cliffs are also available. Back on shore, Tutukaka itself is tiny — a marina, a couple of places to eat, and a few B&Bs. Most visitors base themselves in Whangarei or make a day trip from the coast road.
