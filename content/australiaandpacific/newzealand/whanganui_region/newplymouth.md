New Plymouth sits on the North Island's west coast in the shadow of Mount Taranaki, a near-perfect volcanic cone that dominates everything here — the landscape, the weather, the local identity. Abel Tasman called it "the noblest hill I've ever seen" when he sailed past in the 17th century, and it is hard to argue with him.

The mountain, also known as Mount Egmont, has served as a stand-in for Japan's Mount Fuji on film. Its Maori name means "Gliding Peak," and legend has it that Taranaki was banished to the west coast after a love affair with Pihanga, the wife of the volcano Tongariro.

New Plymouth itself is a pleasant coastal city with excellent surfing. The "Surf Highway" runs south along the western base of the mountain, and since the drive from town takes only thirty minutes, this is one of the few places in the world where you can ski and surf in the same day. The Coastal Walkway, an eleven-kilometre path along the foreshore, is one of the best urban walks in New Zealand, passing Len Lye's kinetic Wind Wand sculpture and a series of parks and beaches.

The Govett-Brewster Art Gallery, home to the Len Lye Centre, is genuinely world-class and reason enough to stop. The city also makes a good base for tramping on the mountain itself — the Pouakai Circuit is a superb two-day walk through alpine tarns and goblin forest.
