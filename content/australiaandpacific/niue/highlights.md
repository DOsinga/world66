---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Niue's appeal is almost entirely natural. The coast is the main attraction — a succession of limestone formations, tidal pools, sea caves, and chasms that reward exploration on foot or by snorkel.

**Matapa Chasm** is the island's most popular swimming and snorkelling spot: a narrow slot in the reef that fills with crystal-clear ocean water. At high tide it's calmer; at low tide you can walk along the edges. Locals have swum here for generations.

**Talava Arches** is reached by a forest track on the northern coast. The main arch spans around 35 metres across the reef flat and is best seen at low tide. Allow about 45 minutes each way from the road.

**Avaiki Cave** is on the west coast near Alofi and has strong cultural significance — it is said to be where the first canoe of settlers arrived. A short walk down through the coastal forest leads to a large sea cave with a tidal pool inside.

**Humpback whales** arrive between July and October to breed and nurse their young in Niue's sheltered waters. Because the deep ocean is close to shore, you can sometimes see them from clifftop viewpoints. Niue Blue is one of the few licensed whale-watching operators; their boat trips run July through September.

**Diving and snorkelling** are the island's main draw for many visitors. Visibility regularly exceeds 60 metres and often approaches 100. The underwater terrain includes caves, drop-offs, and coral walls. Sea snakes are harmless and common. Several dive operators in Alofi hire equipment and run guided dives.

**Hikulagi Sculpture Park** is a pleasant diversion near the coast — an outdoor collection of sculptures made from natural and recycled materials, set among the trees. Free to visit.
