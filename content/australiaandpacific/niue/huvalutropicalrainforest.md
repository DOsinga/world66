The Huvalu Forest Conservation Area covers roughly 54 square kilometres of the island's southeastern corner — the largest remaining area of primary forest in Niue. It was established in 1992 and protects tropical rainforest, coastal forest, and the jagged limestone formations of the east coast.

The main visitor attraction is the Togo Chasm Sea Track, a walk of about 45 minutes each way through the forest that ends at a dramatic chasm on the eastern coast. The transition from dense jungle to the sharp pinnacle-like limestone formations near the sea is striking. Take your time — it's worth it.

A 13-kilometre bike trail loops through the Huvalu forest along the Vinivini Bush Road, taking around an hour to cycle. The Laufoli Umu Pit, a short walk from the road, is a historical site with cultural significance.

Ebony Rainforest Tours runs guided walks and cultural tours through the conservation area. This is a good option if you want context for what you're seeing — the guide explains both the ecology and the traditional relationship between Niueans and their forest.

The terrain is largely flat and there are no dangerous animals or plants, making it accessible for most visitors.
