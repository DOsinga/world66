---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Eating well on Norfolk Island is not difficult, which surprises some visitors who expect a remote Pacific island to have limited options. There are around 20 restaurants, cafes, clubs and takeaway outlets — a reasonable number for a population of 2,200 — and the quality of fresh ingredients is genuinely high.

The volcanic soil is fertile and most restaurants source produce locally. Beef and pork are raised on the island, and the surrounding waters yield yellowfin tuna, kingfish, sweet lip and other reef and pelagic species. Seafood landed that morning appears on lunch menus by afternoon. Local artisan producers make Norfolk honey, goat cheese, cow cheeses and island sea salt.

Traditional Norfolk Islander food draws on the Pitcairn culinary heritage — a blend of 18th-century English cooking and Tahitian ingredients. Pilhi (a banana pudding) and mudda (green banana fritters) appear at community gatherings and the occasional restaurant menu. They're not easy to find on a regular dinner menu, but asking locals will point you in the right direction.

Most of the restaurants cluster around Burnt Pine or along the Kingston road. The Homestead Restaurant is well regarded for its wood-fired Argentinian grill and farm-to-plate approach, sourcing meat and produce from local farms. Hilli Restaurant has been a fixture of the island dining scene for years and receives consistently good reviews. Café LaPérouse brings French cafe influence to the island, with pastries and savoury dishes. Bailey's is a reliable option for a substantial meal in a relaxed setting.

Hours vary and seasonal closures happen. Ring ahead or check at the Visitor Information Centre before making firm plans for dinner, especially outside peak season.
