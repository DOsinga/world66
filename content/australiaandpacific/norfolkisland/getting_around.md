---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

There is no public transport on Norfolk Island. A single taxi service operates on the island but is impractical for anything beyond airport transfers. The vast majority of visitors hire a car, and this is the right call — the island has over 120 kilometres of road, and the best things to see are spread across it.

Car hire operators offer sedans, hatchbacks and small SUVs. You don't need a 4WD. A valid Australian or New Zealand licence is sufficient; international licences are also accepted. You must be at least 17 years old to drive. Traffic moves on the left, and the maximum speed limit is 50 km/h, dropping to 40 km/h in some areas. Expect livestock on the road, particularly cows and feral chickens — they have right of way, and this is not a joke. A few unsealed roads become impassable after heavy rain; watch for signage and use common sense.

Many accommodation packages include a hire car, which usually makes them better value. There are three petrol stations, all in Burnt Pine, open seven days a week.

Bicycles and e-bikes are available for hire through the Visitor Information Centre. The terrain is hilly in places, so the e-bikes are popular. For a guided experience with local commentary, several operators run half-day and full-day tours covering history, nature and scenic highlights. These are worth considering for at least one day of your trip.

Driving Norfolk Island takes getting used to. The roads are narrow, the local custom is to wave at every passing vehicle (a raised finger, a nod or a full wave all work), and nobody is in a hurry. Lean into it.
