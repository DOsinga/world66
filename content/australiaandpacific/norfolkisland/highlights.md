---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Norfolk Island is one of Australia's least-visited territories and one of its most rewarding. It sits 1,500 kilometres due east of Brisbane, a two-and-a-half-hour flight away, but feels much further removed from the mainland in every way that matters.

The island's strongest selling point is the layering of history. Kingston, the original colonial settlement, is home to a remarkably intact collection of Georgian convict-era buildings — Quality Row, the cemetery, the old gaol and the mill ruins — all set against a backdrop of crashing surf. The Kingston and Arthur's Vale Historic Area is listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Australian Convict Sites inscription. Walking it in the late afternoon, when the light is soft and the tourists have thinned out, is genuinely moving.

Emily Bay is the island's most sheltered beach, tucked inside a reef that turns the water glassy and warm. It was named Australia's third-best beach in recent rankings. The snorkelling here is easy and rewarding, with turtles and reef fish common. Nearby Slaughter Bay, despite the name, is another good swimming spot.

Norfolk Island National Park covers around a third of the island's interior and northern coast. The native Norfolk Island pines, which can reach 60 metres, are everywhere — they were what Cook noticed from the sea in 1774, and they're still the island's defining visual feature. The park has short walking tracks through remnant subtropical forest and dramatic coastal cliffs.

Captain Cook Monument stands on the headland where Cook's lookout once gave him his first clear view of the island. The views over the western cliffs and out to Philip Island are as good as anything on Norfolk. Philip Island itself, visible offshore to the south, is a nature reserve and looks almost comically volcanic — a bare, eroded cone rising from the blue.

Away from the main sites, Norfolk's best quality is simply its pace. Cows wander across roads. Everyone waves at passing cars. Burnt Pine, the main shopping strip, has a handful of cafes and a supermarket that keeps island hours. Most visitors find this slowness either relaxing or maddening. If you're drawn to it, Norfolk will reward you.
