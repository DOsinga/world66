---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Norfolk Island has a permanent population of around 2,200, though the number swells considerably during peak tourist season. What makes it unusual is the deep cultural split between two distinct communities who have learned to share a small rock in the Pacific.

About 40 percent of residents are descendants of the Bounty mutineers and their Tahitian companions, who settled Pitcairn Island after the 1789 mutiny and were resettled here by the British Crown in 1856 when Pitcairn became overcrowded. The surnames tell the story: flip through the phone book and you'll find rows of Christians, Quintals, Nobbs, Buffetts, Adamses and McCoys — all traceable back to the nine mutineers who made it to Pitcairn. Because so many people share the same surnames, the island's phone directory has long used nicknames to distinguish entries. Amongst the Christians alone you'll find Bodge, Loppy, Smudge, Toofie and Tatie.

The other residents are Australians, New Zealanders and a smattering of others who came for work or lifestyle and stayed. Relations between the two groups are generally cordial, though the question of local self-governance — Norfolk Island had its own elected assembly until Canberra abolished it in 2015 — remains a source of frustration for many islanders of Pitcairn descent.

The local language, Norf'k, is a remarkable linguistic survival: a creole descended from 18th-century English and Tahitian, carried here from Pitcairn. It was formally recognised as a co-official language alongside English and declared endangered by UNESCO in 2008. Fluent speakers are now mostly older islanders, though efforts to teach it in schools and use it in signage have given it some renewed visibility. You'll hear it in conversation, see it on road signs, and notice it in the warmth with which locals greet each other.

Bounty Day on 8 June is the island's most important cultural event, marking the anniversary of the Pitcairners' arrival in 1856. The Kingston foreshore fills with islanders in period dress re-enacting the landing from HMS Morayshire. It's worth timing your visit around it if you can.
