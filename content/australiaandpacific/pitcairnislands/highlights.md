---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Pitcairn's appeal is the place itself as much as any individual sight. There are no resorts, no tourist infrastructure in the conventional sense, and nowhere else on Earth quite like it. What you actually do here is explore a small island with the people who live on it.

**Bounty Bay and the wreck**

The bay where the Bounty mutineers burned their ship in 1790 is still where all activity centres. The rusted anchor and a few fittings salvaged from the wreck are in the museum. The wreck itself lies a few metres below the surface just offshore and is diveable — one of the more historically charged dives available anywhere.

**Christian's Cave**

A natural ledge high above Bounty Bay, reached by a steep climb from the Eco Trail. Fletcher Christian is said to have used it as a lookout to watch for approaching ships. The view down to the bay and out to sea is striking. Do not attempt it after rain — the path becomes dangerously slippery.

**Pitcairn Island Museum**

In Adamstown, the museum holds the Bounty Bible, a chronometer from the ship, and various artefacts from both the mutiny era and the earlier Polynesian settlement. Entry is around US$5. Small, but worth an hour of your time.

**St Paul's Pool**

A sea-carved tidal pool on the far side of the island with clear water and good snorkelling when conditions allow. The walk to get there is part of the appeal.

**Henderson Island**

About 200 kilometres northeast, Henderson is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — an almost untouched raised coral island with four bird species found nowhere else. Access is by occasional longboat expedition from Pitcairn, subject to weather and community schedules. If one is organised during your stay, go.

**The community itself**

Islanders offer craft-making sessions, guided quad tours, fishing trips, and whale watching (June to October, when humpbacks pass through). Much of what makes Pitcairn interesting happens through conversation with the people who live here. The population is tiny enough that you will meet most of them.
