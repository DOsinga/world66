Pitcairn is the most remote inhabited island on Earth. It sits in the South Pacific roughly halfway between New Zealand and Peru, 2,170 kilometres southeast of Tahiti. The nearest airport is in Mangareva, French Polynesia — a 32-hour voyage by sea. There are no flights. There is no harbour deep enough for large ships. You arrive by longboat through the surf at Bounty Bay.

About 35 to 50 people live here, depending on the year. Almost all are descendants of the nine Bounty mutineers and their Tahitian companions who settled the island in 1790 after Fletcher Christian led the most famous mutiny in maritime history. Many share surnames — Christian, Young, Warren, Brown — that have been on the island for eight generations.

The island is steep, forested, and spectacularly isolated. The roads are dirt. The vehicles are quad bikes. There is one settlement, Adamstown, and a small museum where you can see the Bounty Bible and other relics of the ship. The community is Seventh-day Adventist, which means Saturday is the Sabbath and the island goes quiet. There is no alcohol sold on the island.

Visiting requires serious planning. You must book a spot on the MV Silver Supporter, the government's supply vessel, arrange accommodation in advance, and carry medical insurance that covers evacuation. Stays run 4, 11, or 18 days depending on the ship schedule. It is not cheap and it is not easy, but for those who make the effort, Pitcairn offers something genuinely unlike anywhere else: a tiny, functioning community living out one of history's strangest stories.
