## Visas

Most nationalities — including citizens of the US, EU, Australia, New Zealand, and the UK — can enter the Solomon Islands without a visa for stays up to 90 days. You'll need a valid passport, proof of onward travel, and sufficient funds. Check your country's requirements before departure as rules can change.

## Currency

The local currency is the Solomon Islands Dollar (SBD). As of 2025, roughly SBD8-9 exchanges to USD1. ATMs are available in Honiara and a few larger towns, but unreliable or absent on outer islands. Bring plenty of cash if you're heading anywhere beyond Honiara or the main provincial centres. Credit cards are accepted at some hotels and dive operators but not widely.

## Language

English is the official language and used in government and education. In practice, Solomon Islands Pijin (a Melanesian creole) is the lingua franca across the islands. A handful of words of Pijin go a long way: "Halo" (hello), "Tanggio tumas" (thank you very much), "Wanem nao nem blong iu?" (what's your name?).

## Communications

Mobile coverage (Solomon Telekom and bmobile) reaches most populated islands but is patchy in remote areas. Data is slow and expensive by international standards. Wi-Fi is available at most Honiara hotels. Bring a local SIM if you need connectivity on the road.

## Opening Hours

Government offices and most businesses run Monday to Friday, 8am to 4:30pm. Markets operate early — get to Honiara Central Market by 8am for the best produce. Many businesses close for part of Friday afternoon and are closed Saturday afternoon and Sunday.

## Electricity

240V, 50Hz. Australian-style plug sockets (Type I). Bring an adapter if coming from the US or Europe.

## Time Zone

GMT+11. The Solomon Islands do not observe daylight saving time.

## Cultural Etiquette

The concept of *kastom* — customary law and tradition — is central to life outside Honiara. Always ask permission before entering a village, photographing people, or using land. Dress modestly when visiting communities: cover shoulders and knees. Remove shoes when entering homes. Meals often begin with prayer; wait for the host's signal before eating.

The *wantok* system — a web of mutual obligation based on shared language and kinship — underpins social life. Expect hospitality, but also some expectation of reciprocity. Small gifts or contributions to community funds are appreciated.
