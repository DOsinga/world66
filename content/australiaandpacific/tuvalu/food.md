---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Tuvaluan food is built around two things: fish and the coconut palm. Fresh tuna, parrotfish, and octopus are caught daily and prepared in a variety of ways — grilled, raw with lime juice, or cooked in coconut cream. The other pillar of the traditional diet is pulaka, a root vegetable related to taro that grows in pits dug into the freshwater lens beneath the soil. Pulaka is laborious to cultivate but deeply important — it keeps through droughts and has sustained communities here for centuries.

*Fekei* is the dish most associated with Tuvaluan cooking: grated pulaka mixed with coconut cream and steamed inside pulaka leaves. It is dense, slightly sweet, and filling. *Palusami* — taro leaves cooked in coconut cream — is another staple that appears at community feasts and family meals. Both are things you are more likely to be offered as a guest than to find in a restaurant.

Dining options for visitors are limited, particularly outside Funafuti. On the main island there are a handful of small guesthouses that serve meals — usually a combination of local fish, imported rice, and whatever vegetables are available. Do not expect menus or set hours. Eat when the food is ready, ask what is available, and be flexible. The cooking is often excellent in a simple, fresh way.

There are no dedicated restaurants in the conventional sense. The local market in Funafuti sells fresh fish and coconuts, and is worth visiting in the morning. Imported goods — tinned food, rice, flour, soft drinks — arrive on supply ships from Fiji and are available in the small shops along the main road on Fongafale. Prices reflect the logistics of getting goods here.

If you are invited to eat with a family or at a church gathering, go. Communal meals are where Tuvaluan food is at its best.
