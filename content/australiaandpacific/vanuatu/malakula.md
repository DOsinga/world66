Malakula is the second largest island in Vanuatu and one of the most culturally rich places in the entire Pacific. Over thirty distinct languages are spoken here, a density of linguistic diversity that reflects centuries of isolated communities separated by the island's rugged interior of dense jungle and steep mountains. For travellers willing to make the effort to get here, Malakula offers an experience of traditional Melanesian culture that has largely disappeared elsewhere in the archipelago.

The island is best known for the Small Nambas and Big Nambas peoples, whose traditional ceremonies, dances, and customs are still practised in villages across the island. Witnessing a Rom dance or a grade-taking ceremony is a genuine privilege, though visits should always be arranged respectfully through local guides. The surrounding waters are excellent for snorkelling and diving, with healthy coral reefs and warm, clear water. The main settlement of Lakatoro on the northeast coast is small and basic, but there are a handful of simple guesthouses scattered around the island, most run by local families who can also arrange cultural tours and jungle walks.
