title: "People and Culture"
type: section
---

Samoa has a population of around 220,000, predominantly Polynesian. The great majority live on Upolu; Savai'i is larger in area but sparsely populated. A substantial Samoan diaspora lives in New Zealand, Australia, and the United States, and remittances are an important part of the economy.

**Fa'a Samoa**

Samoan society is organised around fa'a Samoa — the Samoan way — a system of values and obligations that places family (aiga) above the individual. The matai (chief) system means that extended families are headed by titled chiefs who make decisions, hold land, and represent the family in village councils (fono). Titles are not inherited automatically but bestowed by family consensus.

Central values include alofa (love and generosity), fa'aaloalo (respect for elders and chiefs), and tautua (service). Fa'alavelave — ceremonial exchanges of gifts and money at births, funerals, weddings, and title bestowals — are a serious social obligation and can involve significant sums. Visitors will notice that many Samoans prioritise these family duties above work or other commitments.

**Religion**

Christianity arrived in the 1830s and is deeply embedded in daily life. Around 98% of Samoans identify as Christian, spanning a range of denominations. Church attendance is near-universal, and the sound of choir singing on Sunday morning is one of the more beautiful things about being in Samoa. On Sunday evenings, many villages observe a period of sa (sacred quiet) at dusk — families gather for prayers and you should sit quietly for the 10–15 minutes it lasts.

**Tattoo**

The traditional pe'a — a dense geometric tattoo covering the body from waist to knee — is one of the most complex and significant tattoos in the world. For men, receiving it marks a transition to full adult status. The female equivalent, the malu, covers the thighs. Both are done by specialist tattooist families using traditional tools. If you see a man with the pe'a, it represents years of pain and deep cultural commitment.

**Etiquette**

Dress modestly away from the beach. Remove shoes before entering a fale. Ask before photographing people or villages. If invited to share a meal or drink kava, accept graciously — hospitality is taken seriously and refusing can give offence.
