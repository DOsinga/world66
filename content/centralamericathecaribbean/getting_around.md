---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Getting around Central America and the Caribbean takes some flexibility. The region spans a vast mainland stretch from [Mexico](/centralamericathecaribbean/mexico) down to [Panama](/centralamericathecaribbean/panama), plus hundreds of islands scattered across the Caribbean Sea. No single transport mode covers it all, so you'll likely combine buses, flights, ferries, and the occasional white-knuckle shuttle ride.

On the Central American mainland, buses are king. [Mexico](/centralamericathecaribbean/mexico) has one of the best long-distance bus networks in the Americas — first-class lines like ADO run comfortable, air-conditioned coaches between major cities on schedule and at reasonable prices. Further south through [Guatemala](/centralamericathecaribbean/guatemala), [Honduras](/centralamericathecaribbean/honduras), [El Salvador](/centralamericathecaribbean/elsalvador), and [Nicaragua](/centralamericathecaribbean/nicaragua), you'll encounter the famous chicken buses: retired US school buses painted in wild colors, packed to the roof, and remarkably cheap. They go everywhere, but comfort and safety standards are minimal. Tourist shuttles — shared minivans running fixed routes between traveler hubs — are a popular middle ground in Guatemala and [Costa Rica](/centralamericathecaribbean/costarica). They cost more but save hours of transfers.

Driving yourself is doable but demands patience. Road conditions range from excellent toll highways in Mexico to potholed mountain tracks in rural Honduras. Border crossings by car involve paperwork and sometimes unofficial "fees." [Costa Rica](/centralamericathecaribbean/costarica) and [Panama](/centralamericathecaribbean/panama) have the best road infrastructure in the isthmus. Domestic flights are worth considering for longer distances — particularly in Mexico, where budget carriers like Volaris connect cities cheaply, and in Panama, where short flights reach the San Blas Islands and the Darien.

In the Caribbean, inter-island travel is mostly by air. Regional carriers like Caribbean Airlines, LIAT's successors, and various small operators connect the major islands, though schedules can be unreliable and fares add up fast. Book early for better prices. Ferries run between some island pairs — notably between St. Kitts and Nevis, Antigua and Barbuda, and among the US and British Virgin Islands — but there's no grand island-hopping ferry network. For the smaller islands, you may need to charter a boat or catch a ride with a local operator.

Within individual islands and cities, options vary enormously. [Cuba](/centralamericathecaribbean/cuba) has its own logic of classic car taxis, horse carts, and crowded state buses. [Jamaica](/centralamericathecaribbean/jamaica) runs on route taxis and minibuses. In most Caribbean destinations, renting a car gives you the most freedom, though you'll need to get used to driving on the left in several former British colonies. Taxis are widely available but agree on the fare before you get in — meters are rare outside Mexico City and San Jose.
