Visa requirements across Central America and the Caribbean vary widely, so check before you go. The CA-4 agreement between [Guatemala](/centralamericathecaribbean/guatemala), [Honduras](/centralamericathecaribbean/honduras), [El Salvador](/centralamericathecaribbean/elsalvador), and [Nicaragua](/centralamericathecaribbean/nicaragua) lets most visitors move freely between those four countries on a single 90-day entry. [Mexico](/centralamericathecaribbean/mexico), [Costa Rica](/centralamericathecaribbean/costarica), [Panama](/centralamericathecaribbean/panama), and [Belize](/centralamericathecaribbean/belize) each have their own entry rules but generally grant tourists 30 to 90 days on arrival. [Cuba](/centralamericathecaribbean/cuba) requires a tourist card (tarjeta de turista), which you typically buy through your airline or a Cuban embassy before departure. Most Caribbean islands admit tourists for short stays without much hassle, though US citizens should note that some islands are overseas territories of France or the Netherlands with EU entry rules.

Money matters differ from country to country. The US dollar is legal tender in [Panama](/centralamericathecaribbean/panama), [El Salvador](/centralamericathecaribbean/elsalvador), and several Caribbean islands, and it's widely accepted alongside local currency in [Belize](/centralamericathecaribbean/belize), [Honduras](/centralamericathecaribbean/honduras), and tourist areas of [Costa Rica](/centralamericathecaribbean/costarica). The Mexican peso is strong and stable, and you'll get better value paying in pesos than in dollars in [Mexico](/centralamericathecaribbean/mexico). ATMs are available in cities and larger towns throughout the region, though they can be scarce in rural areas. Carry some US cash as backup — small bills are easier to change. Credit cards work in hotels and upscale restaurants, but street food, local buses, and small-town shops are cash-only territory.

Spanish is the dominant language on the mainland, from Mexico through Panama (except English-speaking [Belize](/centralamericathecaribbean/belize)). In the Caribbean, the linguistic map gets colorful: English in [Jamaica](/centralamericathecaribbean/jamaica), [Trinidad and Tobago](/centralamericathecaribbean/trinidadandtobago), [Barbados](/centralamericathecaribbean/barbados), and the former British colonies; French or French Creole in [Haiti](/centralamericathecaribbean/haiti), [Guadeloupe](/centralamericathecaribbean/guadeloupe), and [Martinique](/centralamericathecaribbean/martinique); Dutch and Papiamento in [Aruba](/centralamericathecaribbean/aruba) and the ABC islands. On the mainland, even basic Spanish goes a long way. In tourist zones you'll get by with English, but venture off the beaten path and a phrasebook becomes essential.

Safety varies enormously across the region, and blanket statements are useless. [Costa Rica](/centralamericathecaribbean/costarica) and [Panama](/centralamericathecaribbean/panama) are generally safe for travelers. Parts of [Honduras](/centralamericathecaribbean/honduras) and [El Salvador](/centralamericathecaribbean/elsalvador) have high crime rates, but tourist areas are usually fine with basic precautions. [Mexico](/centralamericathecaribbean/mexico) is a vast country — the Yucatan and Oaxaca are very different from Sinaloa. In the Caribbean, petty theft is the main concern; use common sense with valuables and avoid unlit areas at night. Everywhere in the region, take local advice seriously — hotel staff and fellow travelers usually know which neighborhoods and routes to avoid.

Cell coverage and wifi have improved dramatically across the region. You'll find reliable connectivity in cities and tourist towns throughout Central America and the Caribbean. Buying a local SIM card is cheap and straightforward in most countries — look for Claro, Tigo, or Movistar on the mainland. In more remote areas and smaller islands, don't count on a signal. Many cafes, hostels, and restaurants offer free wifi, though speeds can be slow. [Cuba](/centralamericathecaribbean/cuba) has expanded its connectivity significantly in recent years, but internet access remains slower and more expensive there than anywhere else in the region.

Electricity is mostly 110V/60Hz throughout Central America and the Caribbean, same as North America, so US and Canadian visitors won't need adapters. European and Australian travelers will need a plug adapter and possibly a voltage converter for some devices. Power outages happen, especially in rural areas and during storms. A small flashlight and a portable battery pack are worth their weight.
