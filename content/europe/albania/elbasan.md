Elbasan is a mid-sized city in central Albania, sitting in the broad Shkumbin river valley between Tirana and the mountains that climb toward North Macedonia. It is an industrial town — a massive Soviet-built steel plant dominates the outskirts and left a mark on the city's character — but at its heart is a remarkably intact Ottoman walled city that most visitors driving through never bother to stop for. They should.

The old town is enclosed by 15th-century castle walls, built by the Ottomans on the site of the ancient Roman garrison town of Scampis. Inside those walls you find a tight grid of streets, old churches and mosques standing side by side, and a bazaar quarter that still functions as a real market. The scale is human and unhurried. It takes an hour or two to walk properly, and it feels a long way from the Tirana tourist trail.

The city's history layers fast. The Romans were here first, on the Via Egnatia — the road that linked Rome to Constantinople. The Ottomans rebuilt it as a military stronghold in 1466. The communists tried to industrialise it into an Albanian Sheffield. Today it is a city of around 115,000 that has absorbed all of that and is quietly getting on with itself.

Elbasan is most naturally a stop on the route between [Tirana](/europe/albania/tirana) and [Korçë](/europe/albania/korce) or [Pogradec](/europe/albania/pogradec). The castle and the old town can be seen in two to three hours; with lunch, it makes a comfortable half-day stop.
