---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Andorra's most rewarding sights are its Romanesque churches — a remarkable concentration of 11th and 12th-century architecture scattered across the mountain valleys.

**Casa de la Vall** in Andorra la Vella served as the parliament from 1702 until 2011. Built in 1580, this solid stone manor house looks much the same as it always has. Free guided tours (in English, Catalan, and French) take about 30 minutes and cover the council chamber, the kitchen, and the general history of the principality. Worth a visit even if architecture isn't your thing.

**Sant Joan de Caselles** in Canillo is arguably the finest Romanesque church in the country. The bell tower stands apart from the main nave, 11th-century frescoes survive inside, and the setting — above the valley with mountains on three sides — is hard to beat.

**Santa Coloma** near Andorra la Vella is the oldest church in Andorra, pre-Romanesque in parts, with a distinctive circular bell tower from the 12th century. The interior has original painted decoration.

**Sanctuary of Meritxell**: The original 12th-century chapel burned down in 1972; the current building was designed by Ricardo Bofill in 1976 and is an interesting modernist contrast to the old chapel ruins preserved beside it. This is the spiritual heart of the country — the Virgin of Meritxell is Andorra's patron saint.

**Caldea** in Escaldes-Engordany is Europe's largest mountain spa complex, fed by natural thermal springs at 68°C. The futuristic glass tower is a landmark visible from across the valley. A three-hour session costs around €36; booking ahead is wise in winter.

**Lake Engolasters** sits at 1,616 metres east of the capital. A short drive and easy walk bring you to a glacial lake with the Church of Sant Miquel d'Engolasters on its shore — another fine Romanesque bell tower. Good picnic spot.
