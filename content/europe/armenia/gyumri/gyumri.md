Armenia's second city has a personality quite different from [Yerevan](/europe/armenia/yerevan). Where the capital is polished and outward-looking, Gyumri is rougher around the edges, proud of its craft traditions, and still marked by the catastrophic 1988 earthquake that killed tens of thousands and levelled much of the city. That history is impossible to miss — rebuilt blocks stand alongside Soviet-era ruins, and some families were still living in temporary metal shelters well into the 2000s.

Yet Gyumri is not a sad place. Its old quarter, centred on the black-and-red tuff stone architecture of Vardanantz Square (known locally as Peace Square), is genuinely lovely, and the city has long had a reputation as Armenia's creative and comedic heartland. Gyumretsis — Gyumri natives — are famous across the country for their wit and for stubbornly maintaining their own dialect.

The churches are worth your time. Seven were built here in the 19th century, a civic achievement the old song "Eyed the Wits" simultaneously mocks and celebrates: the townspeople had no bread, the joke goes, but they built seven temples. The most impressive is the Surb Amenaprkitch (Holy Saviour), a grand basilica modelled on the Ani cathedral that anchors the main square. Saint Yotverk, smaller and older, was the only church in all of Armenia to stay open throughout the Soviet era.

Getting here is straightforward — minibuses run regularly from Yerevan (about 2.5 hours), and the train is a slow but scenic alternative. Gyumri also sits close to the Georgian border, making it a natural stop for travellers heading north to [Tbilisi](/europe/georgia/tbilisi).
