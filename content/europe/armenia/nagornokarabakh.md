Nagorno-Karabakh — known to Armenians as Artsakh — is a mountainous region that was the subject of conflict between Armenia and Azerbaijan for over three decades. After a ceasefire in 1994 ended the first war, ethnic Armenians controlled the territory and maintained a self-declared republic for nearly thirty years.

A second war in autumn 2020 resulted in significant Azerbaijani territorial gains. Then in September 2023, following a nine-month blockade of the Armenian population, Azerbaijan launched a rapid military offensive and recaptured the enclave entirely. Within two weeks, more than 100,000 ethnic Armenians — virtually the entire population — had fled to Armenia. The exodus included around 288 civilian deaths, among them 218 killed in an explosion at a fuel depot during the flight.

Nagorno-Karabakh is now fully under Azerbaijani administration. The ethnic Armenian population is gone. Travel to the region for tourists is not possible from the Armenian side; the Lachin corridor, previously the only road connection between Armenia and the enclave, is closed. Access from Azerbaijan may be possible in future but the situation remains sensitive.

The displacement has had a significant effect on Armenia itself. Many of the refugees have settled in Yerevan and other Armenian cities, where the government and diaspora organisations are working to integrate a population that arrived with almost nothing.
