Armenia's second city sits on a high plateau in the northwest of the country, 1,500 metres above sea level. Gyumri has been known by many names — Kumayri in antiquity, Alexandrapol under the Russian Empire, Leninakan during the Soviet era — and each period left its mark. The old centre is built from black and orange tufa stone, giving it a character quite different from Yerevan's pink volcanic rock.

The city was devastated by the 1988 Spitak earthquake, which killed tens of thousands and levelled much of the housing stock. The scars are still visible in places, but Gyumri has rebuilt with determination. The restored historic quarter around Vartanants Square is handsome and walkable, lined with 19th-century merchant houses and the city's famous churches — seven of them, earning Gyumri its old nickname "The Town of Seven Churches."

Gyumri has a reputation as Armenia's most culturally independent city, with a strong tradition in crafts, theatre, and sardonic humour. The people here are proud of their distinct identity and dialect. Winters are long and cold on this exposed plateau, but summers are pleasant and a welcome escape from Yerevan's heat. If you have a couple of days, the city rewards wandering — the old streets, the churches, and the lively central market give a picture of Armenian life well away from the capital.
