Linz is Austria's third-largest city, sitting on the Danube about halfway between Vienna and Salzburg. For a long time it carried an industrial reputation — steel mills on the southern edge, a name most outsiders associated with Hitler's childhood home. That reputation has largely given way to something more interesting: Linz has quietly become one of Austria's best cities for contemporary art and design.

The Hauptplatz is one of the largest medieval squares in Austria, ringed by Baroque townhouses and anchored by the Trinity Column. The Old Town quarter stretches back from here through narrow streets lined with patrician houses, some sitting on Roman foundations — the town was a Roman garrison called Lentia. Worth taking your time through.

The Pöstlingberg hill rises sharply northwest of the centre, and the tram that climbs it claims to be the steepest adhesion railway in the world. The views across the Danube valley from the top are good, especially in clear weather. A pilgrimage church sits at the summit.

Linz was European Capital of Culture in 2009, and the infrastructure built for that year — particularly the Ars Electronica Center on the north bank — has stuck around. The city now has a genuine cluster of good museums within easy walking distance of each other.
