title: "People"

---

Austria is a German-speaking country of about nine million people, but don't make the mistake of calling Austrians German — that won't go well. Regional identity runs deep. A Tyrolean mountain farmer and a Viennese intellectual might share a passport, but they speak noticeably different dialects, eat different food, vote differently, and will both tell you their part of the country is the real Austria. The western provinces — Tyrol, Vorarlberg, Salzburg — are Alpine, conservative, and Catholic. [Vienna](/europe/austria/vienna) is cosmopolitan, left-leaning, and culturally its own universe.

Austrians are formally polite in a way that can surprise visitors from more casual countries. "Grüß Gott" is the standard greeting everywhere outside Vienna (where "Servus" creeps in among friends). Titles matter — Herr Doktor, Frau Magister — and people use them unironically in daily life. Service in restaurants and shops can feel brisk by American standards, but it's not rudeness; it's efficiency. Once you get past the formality, Austrians are warm and generous, especially if you show genuine interest in their region, their food, or their mountains.

The café and the outdoors are the two great Austrian social institutions. In the cities, friendships are maintained over long coffee house sessions and wine evenings. In the countryside, weekends revolve around hiking, skiing, and mountain hut lunches. Austria has one of the highest rates of sports club membership in Europe, and the mountains aren't just scenery — they're a daily part of life for much of the population.
