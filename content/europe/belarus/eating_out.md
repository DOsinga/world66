title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Belarusian food is honest, filling, and potato-heavy. The country's culinary identity was shaped by centuries of peasant farming in a cold climate — expect root vegetables, pork, sour cream, and rye bread. The cooking improves noticeably when you eat at places that take it seriously rather than the canteen-style establishments that serve reheated versions of everything.

The national dish is **draniki** — potato pancakes, fried crisp, and served with sour cream. They're ubiquitous and vary considerably in quality. A good plate of draniki at a decent restaurant is genuinely delicious. **Machanka** is a slow-cooked pork stew, rich and unctuous, traditionally served with blini for dipping. **Khaladnik** is a cold beet soup made with kefir and cucumber — pink, tangy, and refreshing in summer. **Zrazy** (stuffed meat rolls) appear on most traditional menus.

Restaurants in Minsk range from Soviet-era stolovayas (canteens) to modern places serving updated versions of Belarusian classics. The canteens are cheap and efficient; the food is predictable but filling. Mid-range restaurants with traditional interiors and proper cooking exist in good numbers in Minsk and in the larger cities.

Outside the capital, options are more limited. In smaller towns, a local cafe or stolovaya may be your only choice. Standards can be inconsistent — stick to dishes you can see being made or that arrive hot.

Kefir — a fermented sour milk drink — is sold everywhere and worth trying. Kvass, a mildly fermented rye bread drink, is popular in summer. Local beer is inexpensive and decent. Vodka is, of course, available everywhere.
