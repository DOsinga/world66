Brussels wears two hats. Politically, it is the de facto capital of the European Union — home to the European Commission, the Parliament, the Council, and a vast functional neighbourhood of glass-and-steel around Schuman that lives off EU business. Culturally, it is the bilingual capital of a country that is itself oddly constructed — every sign in French and Flemish, every official bureaucracy duplicated, a layer of dry self-aware humour about the whole arrangement.

This second Brussels is what most visitors come for. The **Grand-Place** is one of the great squares in Europe, a UNESCO World Heritage Site of gilded guild houses and a Gothic town hall. Behind it winds a warren of medieval streets with **Manneken Pis** at the dead-end of one of them. Uphill, the **Royal District** holds the museums (Magritte, the Royal Fine Arts, the Musical Instruments Museum) and the formal 18th-century city that the Habsburgs built. Downhill, the antiques district of **Sablon** and the scruffy, wonderful working-class **Marolles** are where Brussels is most itself.

Brussels is also the world capital of **Art Nouveau**. Victor Horta's houses in Saint-Gilles and Ixelles, four of which are UNESCO-listed, are the set pieces, but the style is all over the city if you know what to look for — around Square Ambiorix, Avenue Louise, Place Brugmann. The **Atomium** further out is the great modernist monument, a 102-metre stainless steel rendering of an iron crystal left over from the 1958 World's Fair.

The food is very good. Brussels rivals Paris for density of serious restaurants, and is the world capital of two things — **beer** and **chocolate** — and a strong claimant for **fries** and **waffles**. Eating here is cheaper and less formal than Paris, and the culture of drawing out drinks and snacks into a whole evening is part of the local rhythm.

A warning: Brussels has a reputation for street crime higher than most Western European capitals. The main stations (Midi/Zuid and Nord) and the arc of streets between them are genuinely sketchy after dark. Stick to the historic centre, Ixelles, Saint-Gilles, and the EU district at night, and watch your bag on the metro.

Two nights is the minimum; three or four is better, especially if a festival is on. Brussels is also well placed as a base for [Bruges](/europe/belgium/bruges), [Ghent](/europe/belgium/gent), and [Antwerp](/europe/belgium/antwerp), all within an hour by train.
