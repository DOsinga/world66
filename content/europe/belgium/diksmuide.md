Diksmuide is a small Flemish market town in the flat polder landscape of West Flanders, about 20 km inland from the North Sea coast. It would be unremarkable were it not for what happened here between 1914 and 1918: the front line ran straight through this area for four years, and Diksmuide was almost entirely destroyed. The town was rebuilt after the war, but the real draws are the preserved WWI sites on its outskirts — among the most affecting on the Western Front.

The two main sites are the Trench of Death (Dodengang), a remarkably intact stretch of Belgian frontline trenches along the IJzer river, and the IJzer Tower (Ijzertoren), a 84-metre peace monument erected on the site of a wartime cemetery. Together they tell a specific and sobering story: the Belgian army held this narrow strip of land for the entire war, the river behind them, the German lines a few metres ahead.

The town itself is quiet, with a pleasant market square and a handful of cafes. It is best visited as a half-day or full-day excursion from [Bruges](/europe/belgium/bruges) or the coast, though the sites justify the trip on their own.
