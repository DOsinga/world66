Genk is the most multicultural city in Flanders, a distinction that came about through hard industrial necessity rather than cosmopolitan planning. When the Belgian coal mines of the Kempen region were at full production in the mid-20th century, labor was recruited from southern Italy and later from Turkey; those communities stayed, built mosques and churches alongside each other, and their children and grandchildren now give the city a character unlike any other in the country. Walking through certain neighborhoods, the languages shift block by block, and the food — Anatolian bakeries, Italian delicatessens, Congolese restaurants — reflects a layered immigration history that spans decades.

The industrial past is most visible at C-Mine, the transformed Waterschei colliery complex in the center of the city. The headframes and machine halls have been converted into a cultural center with a cinema, design school, exhibition spaces, and a rooftop viewing point that looks out over the flat Kempen landscape. The transformation is well done — enough industrial texture preserved to give the place weight, enough new programming to make it a living space rather than a memorial. There is a deliberate pride in the mining heritage here, a refusal to airbrush the labor history of the place in favor of something easier to market.

Just outside the city, the Bokrijk Open Air Museum is one of Belgium's largest and most visited attractions — a reconstructed ensemble of Flemish village buildings gathered from across the country, spanning centuries of rural and small-town life. It is thorough, intelligently arranged, and worth considerably more than a quick walk-through. The Kattevennen recreational forest offers cycling and walking for those who want to feel the flat, pine-scented Kempen landscape underfoot. Genk rewards visitors who approach it without expectations calibrated to historic Flemish cities: its interest is in the present — in what a post-industrial, post-monoculture European city actually looks and feels like.
