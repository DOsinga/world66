Oudenaarde sits on the Scheldt River in the rolling hills of the Flemish Ardennes, and if you follow professional cycling at all, you already know the name. This is the spiritual home of the Tour of Flanders — the Ronde van Vlaanderen — and the town's cycling museum, the Centrum Ronde van Vlaanderen, occupies the ground floor of the spectacular late-Gothic town hall on the Markt. The surrounding hills, with their short sharp climbs and narrow farm roads, are the battlefield where the race is decided each spring.

The town hall itself is the finest building in town, a sixteenth-century masterpiece of Brabantine Gothic with an ornate belfry that is part of the UNESCO-listed Belfries of Belgium. Inside, the MOU Museum displays Oudenaarde's other claim to fame: tapestries. From the fifteenth to the eighteenth century, the town was one of the great tapestry-weaving centers of Europe, and the collection here includes some remarkable verdure tapestries — richly detailed scenes of forests and wildlife.

Oudenaarde is a pleasant, unhurried town that most visitors pass through quickly, but the combination of Gothic architecture, tapestry heritage, and cycling culture gives it more character than its modest size suggests. The surrounding countryside is excellent for cycling, with well-marked routes over the same cobbled climbs — the Koppenberg, the Kwaremont — that the professionals tackle each April.
