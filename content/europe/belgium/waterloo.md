Waterloo is famous for a single day: 18 June 1815, when Napoleon's army met the Duke of Wellington's forces on the rolling farmland south of this small [Belgian](/europe/belgium) town. The battle ended Napoleon's rule and reshaped Europe. Today Waterloo is a prosperous commuter suburb of [Brussels](/europe/belgium/brussels), but the battlefield remains largely unchanged.

The **Butte du Lion**, a 40-metre artificial mound topped with a cast-iron lion, marks the spot where the Prince of Orange was wounded and offers a panoramic view of the entire battlefield. At its base, the Memorial 1815 museum uses immersive displays to walk visitors through the events of the day. The Panorama of the Battle of Waterloo, a vast circular painting from 1912, gives a vivid sense of the chaos. Several farms that served as strongpoints during the battle — Hougoumont, La Haie Sainte, La Belle Alliance — can be visited on a walking circuit.

The battlefield is about 5 km south of the town of Waterloo itself. The Wellington Museum in the town centre occupies the inn where Wellington had his headquarters.
