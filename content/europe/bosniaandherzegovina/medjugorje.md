Medjugorje is a small village in the hills between [Mostar](/europe/bosniaandherzegovina/mostar) and the Croatian border, and one of the most-visited Catholic pilgrimage sites in the world. Since June 1981, when six local teenagers reported seeing visions of the Virgin Mary on a nearby hillside, over forty million pilgrims have come. The Vatican has not officially recognised the apparitions as authentic, but in 2019 Pope Francis authorised official pilgrimages.

The village itself is small. The **Church of St. James**, at the centre, was built long before the apparitions and is now the focal point — masses run several times a day in different languages, and the evening prayer service draws large crowds. **Apparition Hill** (Podbrdo), where the original visions were reported, is a rough climb up a rocky track; **Cross Mountain** (Križevac), a tougher two-hour hike, is marked by a 14-metre concrete cross and by fifteen bronze stations of the cross along the path.

Most pilgrims come as part of organised tours, flying into **Split** or **Dubrovnik** in Croatia and travelling overland by bus — about two hours from either. Hotels and guest houses line the approach roads; outside of the peak summer months, accommodation is easy and cheap.

Medjugorje is not for casual tourism. If you are not here to pray or walk the hills, an hour is enough. But the atmosphere — the multilingual crowds, the vendors selling rosaries in fifteen languages, the devotion — is hard to find anywhere else in Europe.
