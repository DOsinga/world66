Tuzla is a city built on salt. Its name comes from the Turkish word *tuz* (salt), and its salt springs have drawn settlers continuously for over 6,000 years, making it one of the oldest continuously inhabited places in Europe. Today it is the third-largest city in Bosnia and Herzegovina, after Sarajevo and Banja Luka, and the seat of the Tuzla Canton.

The city's defining attraction is Pannonica, a complex of man-made salt lakes right in the centre of town -- the only salt lakes in Europe. Built on land that subsided due to centuries of underground salt extraction, the lakes turned a geological problem into a genuine draw. In summer, the beaches around Pannonica fill with swimmers and sunbathers, giving the landlocked city an unexpectedly coastal atmosphere.

Tuzla's Old Town retains several Ottoman-era mosques, including the sixteenth-century Turalibeg Mosque with its stone minaret. The open-air museum at Solni Trg (Salt Square) tells the story of the salt industry that shaped the city from neolithic times through the Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian periods. It is a sobering city too: on 25 May 1995, during the Bosnian War, a mortar attack on the town centre killed 72 young people in the single deadliest incident of the conflict. A memorial marks the spot.

What sets Tuzla apart in Bosnian politics is its civic tradition. In the 1990 elections it was the only municipality in Bosnia where non-nationalist parties won control, and that independent streak still colours the city's character today.
