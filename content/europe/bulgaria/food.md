Bulgarian food is Balkan in character — generous, seasonal, and built around a few very good ingredients: sheep's cheese, fresh vegetables, pork, lamb, and dairy that is genuinely excellent. The country has a long tradition of fermented dairy, and the strain of bacteria used to make yoghurt worldwide (*Lactobacillus bulgaricus*) is named for Bulgaria. The yoghurt here is thicker and more sour than what you'll find in a supermarket at home.

**Dishes to order**

*Shopska salad* is the national salad: tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, onion, and a generous grating of white sirene cheese. Simple and excellent when the tomatoes are good.

*Banitsa* is the national pastry — filo dough stuffed with eggs and sirene cheese, baked until golden. Available everywhere in the morning. A good banitsa with a glass of cold ayran (diluted yoghurt drink) is a fine Bulgarian breakfast.

*Tarator* is a cold cucumber and yoghurt soup with walnuts, garlic, and dill. Exactly what you want on a hot Bulgarian summer day.

*Kavarma* and *gyuvetch* are clay-pot dishes — meat and vegetables slow-cooked in a ceramic pot. Kavarma is typically pork or chicken; gyuvetch can include lamb, peppers, tomatoes, and aubergine. Both are standard tavern food and rarely disappointing.

*Meshana skara* — mixed grill — is ubiquitous: kebabcheta (minced meat rolls), kyufteta (meatballs), and pork chops. Better than it sounds at good places.

*Rakia* is the national spirit — fruit brandy, usually made from grapes or plums. Homemade rakia, offered as a gesture of hospitality, can be exceptional or extremely rough. Either way, refusing is slightly rude.

**Where to eat**

Mehanas (traditional taverns) are the best places to eat Bulgarian food — rustic interiors, long menus, low prices. In [Sofia](/europe/bulgaria/sofia), the center has good restaurants of all types; look for mehanas in the backstreets of the old center. [Plovdiv](/europe/bulgaria/plovdiv) has a particularly good eating scene concentrated in the old town. On the Black Sea coast, seafood is worth ordering — fresh fish, mussels, and Black Sea turbot.

Prices remain low by European standards. A full meal with wine in a mid-range restaurant costs €15–25 per person.
