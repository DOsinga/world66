Koprivshtitsa is one of the most beautifully preserved towns in Bulgaria — a village of painted National Revival houses set in a wooded valley in the Sredna Gora mountains, about 100 km east of Sofia. It's on the tourist trail, but lightly enough that the atmosphere remains genuine.

The town's significance goes beyond its architecture. On 20 April 1876, Koprivshtitsa was the starting point of the April Uprising against Ottoman rule — a rebellion that was brutally suppressed but ultimately led, after Russian intervention, to Bulgarian independence in 1878. The house of Todor Kableshkov, from whose window the first shot of the uprising was fired, is now a museum.

Six of the old merchant houses are now house-museums, each showing a different aspect of 19th-century Bulgarian life — the houses of Oslekov, Kableshkov, Lyutov, Debelyanov, Benkovski, and Kableshkov. A combined ticket covers all six. The town's main square, the bridge over the Topolnitsa river, and the cobbled lanes between the houses are worth as much time as the museums themselves.

The International Folk Festival, held every five years in August, brings folk groups from across Bulgaria to perform on the hillsides outside town. It's one of the most important events in Bulgarian traditional culture.

Koprivshtitsa is 2 hours from Sofia by a combination of bus and local train; the train station is 12 km from the village.
