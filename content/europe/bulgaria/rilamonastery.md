Rila Monastery is the largest and most visited monastery in Bulgaria, and one of the most important cultural sites in the country. It sits in a deep valley in the Rila Mountains, about 120 km south of [Sofia](/europe/bulgaria/sofia), surrounded by forest and peaks rising to over 2,000 metres.

The monastery was founded in the 10th century by St. Ivan Rilski (Ivan of Rila), a hermit who retreated into these mountains and attracted followers who built the first monastic community here. Ivan Rilski is the patron saint of Bulgaria. The monastery was destroyed and rebuilt several times over the centuries; most of what stands today dates from the 1830s National Revival rebuilding, following a fire.

The exterior of the main church is covered in frescoes — bands of narrative scenes from the Bible and saints' lives, rendered in vivid colours under the striped arcade of the courtyard. The style is unmistakably Bulgarian: a fusion of Byzantine traditions with local folk elements. Inside, the iconostasis is magnificent — a wall of carved and gilded wood nearly 10 metres wide, with icons in silver and gold frames. Photography inside the church is not permitted.

The monastery museum holds a remarkable collection of icons, illuminated manuscripts, and ecclesiastical objects. The prize exhibit is the Raphael's Cross — a carved walnut cross completed in 1802 by a monk named Raphael, who spent 12 years carving 140 biblical scenes with 1,500 figures into a surface the size of a sheet of paper. He went blind in the process.

Rila Monastery is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is still an active monastic community. Basic accommodation is available within the monastery walls for pilgrims; the surrounding village has guesthouses and restaurants. Most visitors come as a day trip from Sofia, which is straightforward by car (about 2 hours) or organized tour.
