The Channel Islands sit closer to the coast of Normandy than to England — just 14 miles from France at their nearest point — yet they are British Crown dependencies with their own parliaments, legal systems, and tax rules. They are not part of the United Kingdom and not part of the EU, which gives them an unusual autonomy that shapes everything from prices to alcohol policy.

There are five main islands: [Jersey](/europe/channelislands/jersey) is the largest and most populous, with around 110,000 people. [Guernsey](/europe/channelislands/guernsey) is quieter and perhaps more characteristically Norman. [Alderney](/europe/channelislands/alderney) is small, windswept and unhurried. [Sark](/europe/channelislands/sark) has no cars and until recently had no democratically elected government — it was feudal in a literal sense. [Herm](/europe/channelislands/herm) is tiny enough to walk around in an afternoon.

The islands share a mild Atlantic climate, a complicated history of Norman and German occupation, and a local identity that is neither quite English nor French. Seafood is excellent. The pace of life is slow. The coastline — particularly the tidal flats, granite cliffs, and hidden beaches — is genuinely beautiful. Prices are broadly comparable to the UK, though alcohol and tobacco are notably cheaper.
