Sark is three miles long and one and a half miles wide, and there are no cars. Tractors are used for farming and to haul luggage from the harbour; otherwise everyone gets around by bicycle or horse-drawn carriage. Mobile signal is poor. The pace drops noticeably from the moment you step off the ferry from Guernsey.

Until 2008, Sark operated under a feudal system of governance — the last in the western world. It now has a democratically elected Chief Pleas (parliament), though many traditions and land laws remain distinctive. The island is divided into Great Sark and Little Sark, connected by a narrow ridge called La Coupée with a sheer 300-foot drop on either side.

The island has no light pollution, which makes it one of the world's designated Dark Sky Islands — on a clear night the Milky Way is visible with the naked eye. There are around 500 permanent residents. Day trips from Guernsey take 50 minutes by fast ferry; you can also stay in one of the small hotels or self-catering cottages on the island.
