Croatian food splits sharply along geographic lines. The coast and islands are Mediterranean: seafood, olive oil, garlic, and grilled vegetables. Inland Croatia is Central European: stews, smoked meats, paprika, and dumplings. Both are worth eating.

## Coastal and Dalmatian

Fresh fish is the staple. Grilled whole fish (dourada, bass, dentex) is simple and excellent when done well. Crni rižot — black risotto made with cuttlefish ink — is one of the finest things you can eat in Dalmatia. Buzara is shellfish (usually mussels or langoustines) cooked in white wine, garlic, and parsley; it sounds simple and is perfect.

Peka is a method of slow-cooking meat or octopus under a heavy iron dome buried in embers. It takes hours to prepare and is usually ordered in advance at local restaurants. Worth the planning.

Oysters from Ston on the Pelješac peninsula are among the best in the Adriatic. You can eat them directly from producers cheaply; the drive to Ston itself is spectacular.

Pag cheese (paški sir) is a hard sheep's milk cheese with a distinctive sharp flavour, influenced by the herb-covered limestone karst the sheep graze on. It appears on every cheese board in Croatia and rightly so.

## Istrian

Istria has a distinct culinary identity, heavily influenced by Italy. Truffles (both black and white) are found in the Motovun forest and appear in pasta, risotto, and eggs from October onwards. Istrian olive oil is excellent. The local pasta — fuži and pljukanci — is served with truffle sauces or wild boar ragù.

Malvazija Istarska is the local white wine: light, slightly aromatic, and very drinkable. Teran is the Istrian red — earthy and tannic.

## Inland and Slavonian

Zagreb and the interior have their own traditions. Štrukli — dough filled with cottage cheese, boiled or baked — is a Zagreb speciality worth seeking out. Purica s mlincima (roast turkey with flatbread soaked in the cooking juices) is the classic Sunday lunch. Kulen is a spiced paprika sausage from Slavonia that appears on charcuterie boards across the country.

## Wine

Croatia produces serious wine from native grape varieties that are largely unknown outside the country. Plavac Mali from Dalmatia is the flagship red — full-bodied, high alcohol, good with grilled fish. Dingač from the Pelješac peninsula is the premium appellation. For whites, Pošip and Grk from Korčula island are excellent, as is Malvazija from Istria. Graševina is the workhorse white of inland Croatia.

## Eating Out

Restaurant menus in tourist areas are often in multiple languages and priced accordingly. For better value, look for konobas — family-run taverns, often informal, where locals eat. Lunch (ručak) is the main meal of the day; restaurants offer a lunch menu (dnevni menu) with soup, main, and sometimes dessert for €8–12. Dinner prices run higher. Tipping is not compulsory but 10% is standard for good service.
