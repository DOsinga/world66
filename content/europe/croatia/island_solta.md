Šolta is a quiet Dalmatian island just a short ferry ride from Split, yet it sees a fraction of the crowds that descend on Hvar or Brač. That relative obscurity is precisely the point: this is where Dalmatians themselves go for summer, and the pace of life reflects it.

The island stretches 19 km from west-northwest to southeast and reaches about 5 km at its widest. It is the 13th largest Croatian island, midsize by Dalmatian standards but intimate in character. The interior is covered in olive groves and stone villages that have changed little over centuries. Summer brings reliably hot weather — air temperatures regularly exceed 30°C in July — and sea temperatures that hover around 22°C, ideal for swimming.

The main villages are Grohote (the administrative centre), Maslinica on the western tip, and Rogač, which is where the ferry from Split docks. Nečujam, on the north coast, has a sheltered bay that fills with boats in high summer. Each village has its own rhythm, and wandering between them on foot or by bicycle is one of the island's pleasures.

Šolta has deep roots. Its stone holds traces of every era: prehistoric hill-forts, Roman villae rusticae, early Christian basilicas, and a Baroque castle. The filmmakers of the Yugoslav television epic "Velo Misto" used Šolta's streets, towers, and courtyards to evoke old Split. More recently, the entire film "The Fall of Italy" was shot here. A local tradition persists of judging the best-kept apartment, garden, and village each summer — so if you see a diploma on the wall of your rental, the owner has earned it.
