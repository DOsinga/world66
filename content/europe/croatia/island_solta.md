Šolta is a small Dalmatian island just a short ferry ride from [Split](/europe/croatia/split), yet far quieter than its more famous neighbours. At 19 km long and under 5 km wide, it has avoided the mass tourism that has overwhelmed parts of the Croatian coast, and its small fishing villages, olive groves, and clear-water bays retain a genuinely unhurried character.

The island's main harbour is Rogač, where ferries arrive from Split. Most visitors quickly move on to the village of Stomorska on the eastern tip, the bay of Nečujam (the largest bay on the island, popular for swimming and boating), or Maslinica in the west, a pretty village with a small marina and a seventeenth-century castle. The interior village of Grohote is the administrative centre and has a parish church with ancient sarcophagi in front of it — evidence of the island's long Roman and early Christian history.

Šolta is known locally for lavender honey and olive oil. Summer temperatures regularly exceed 30°C and sea temperatures reach 22°C, making it excellent swimming territory from June through September.
