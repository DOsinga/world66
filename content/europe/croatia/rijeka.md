Rijeka is Croatia's third city and its main port, set at the head of the Kvarner Gulf where the Adriatic cuts deepest into the continent. It is a working city rather than a tourist showcase, which gives it a character quite different from the polished coastal towns further south. The Austro-Hungarian architecture, the industrial waterfront, and the gritty energy of the Korzo — the main pedestrian street — recall Trieste more than Dubrovnik.

The city has changed hands repeatedly: Habsburg, Hungarian, Italian, Yugoslav, and finally Croatian. This layered history shows in the architecture and in the city's open, multicultural atmosphere. Rijeka was European Capital of Culture in 2020, which brought investment in the waterfront and cultural spaces, though it remains refreshingly ungentrified.

The hilltop Trsat Castle and the pilgrimage church of Our Lady of Trsat are worth the climb for the views over the gulf. The old town, compressed between the hillside and the harbour, has a covered market, a few churches, and narrow lanes that reward wandering. The waterfront itself is in transition — old warehouses and cranes alongside new cultural spaces.

Rijeka is also the gateway to the Kvarner islands — Cres, Lošinj, Krk, and Rab — all reachable by ferry or bridge from the surrounding area.
