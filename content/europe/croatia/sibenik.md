Sibenik is the oldest native Croatian city on the Adriatic, founded by Croats rather than Greeks or Romans. It sits at the mouth of the Krka River where it meets the sea, a compact medieval town of steep limestone streets, fortresses on the surrounding hills, and one genuinely remarkable cathedral.

That cathedral, the Cathedral of St James, is the reason most visitors come and it does not disappoint. Built entirely of stone and marble over 150 years from 1402 to 1555, it blends Venetian Gothic with Tuscan Renaissance in a way that feels surprisingly harmonious. The dome is constructed without any supporting wooden framework, and a frieze of 71 carved portrait heads rings the exterior, each supposedly a real townsperson from the 15th century. UNESCO listed it in 2000, and it remains one of the most impressive churches on the Croatian coast.

Beyond the cathedral, Sibenik rewards a day or two of wandering. Four medieval fortresses overlook the town, with Barone Fortress recently restored into a smart viewpoint and event space. The old town itself is quieter and less polished than [Dubrovnik](/europe/croatia/dubrovnik) or [Split](/europe/croatia/split), which is part of its appeal. Restaurants along the waterfront serve excellent Dalmatian seafood, and the pace is noticeably slower than at the bigger resort towns.

Sibenik also makes an ideal base for visiting [Krka National Park](/europe/croatia/krka_national_park), whose famous waterfalls are just a short bus ride inland. The Kornati Islands, a dramatic archipelago of bare karst islets, are accessible by boat trip from the harbour. If you are looking for a Dalmatian town with real character and fewer crowds, Sibenik is a strong choice.
