**Fiction and Literature**

*The Good Soldier Švejk* by Jaroslav Hašek — The great Czech comic novel, following a bumbling soldier through the chaos of World War I. Irreverent, shaggy, and still funny.

*The Trial* and *The Castle* by Franz Kafka — Kafka was born in Prague and wrote in German. Both novels carry the atmosphere of Habsburg-era Prague in every page, even though neither explicitly names the city.

*The Unbearable Lightness of Being* by Milan Kundera — Set against the 1968 Prague Spring and its aftermath. Kundera is a divisive figure in Czech literary circles, but this is the novel that put Czech literature on the international map.

*I Served the King of England* by Bohumil Hrabal — Lighter than Kundera, warmer, and arguably the better writer. Hrabal's novel traces a waiter's life through the upheavals of the 20th century. His *Closely Watched Trains* (also filmed) is equally good.

**History and Non-Fiction**

*Prague Winter* by Madeleine Albright — A memoir exploring Albright's Czech-Jewish family history and the occupation, the war, and the communist takeover. Personal and historically grounded.

*...I Never Saw Another Butterfly...* — A collection of drawings and poems by children held at the Terezín concentration camp between 1942 and 1944. Devastating and essential.

*The Coasts of Bohemia* by Derek Sayer — A cultural history of Czechia from the early modern period to 1989. Dense but rewarding for anyone who wants more than the surface.

**Travel and Practical**

The *Rough Guide to the Czech Republic* and *Lonely Planet Czech Republic* are both solid for practical planning. Rick Steves covers Prague well in his Central Europe guide.
