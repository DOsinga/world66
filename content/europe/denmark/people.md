---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Denmark has a population of around 5.9 million. The vast majority are ethnic Danes, with significant minority communities from Turkey, the Middle East, and South Asia, particularly in Copenhagen and the larger Jutland cities. The Faroe Islands and Greenland are self-governing territories within the Kingdom of Denmark, with their own distinct cultures and languages.

Danes have a reputation for being reserved with strangers but warm once you know them. The stereotype holds some truth — don't expect enthusiastic small talk on public transport — but Danes are generally helpful and almost all speak excellent English. The concept of *hygge* (roughly: cosy togetherness, the particular comfort of being with people you like in relaxed surroundings) is genuinely embedded in the culture, not just a marketing idea. Evenings with candles, good food, and unhurried conversation are taken seriously.

Danish society is famously egalitarian. There is a strong cultural norm against displaying status or wealth — the Jante Law (*Janteloven*), a set of unwritten social rules that caution against thinking yourself better than others, is real and observed even if not always consciously. This can read as modesty, even to the point of self-deprecation.

The official religion is Lutheran Christianity (the Church of Denmark), though regular attendance is low and Denmark is a broadly secular society. The official language is Danish; Faroese and Greenlandic are also official in their territories. A small German-speaking minority lives near the German border. Life expectancy is around 81 years. Denmark regularly tops global happiness and quality-of-life rankings.
