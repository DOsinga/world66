Vadehavet is the Danish Wadden Sea — the northernmost stretch of the vast tidal flat system that runs along the coasts of the Netherlands, Germany, and Denmark. The entire Wadden Sea is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and the Danish section was added in 2014. It is one of the most important wetlands in Europe, a critical stopover for millions of migratory birds on the East Atlantic Flyway.

The landscape is stark and hypnotic: endless mudflats exposed at low tide, salt marshes, sandbanks, and shallow channels that shift with every storm. Twice a day the sea retreats to reveal a seabed you can walk across — guided mudflat walks (vadehavsvandringer) are the signature experience here, especially from the mainland town of Ribe or from the islands.

Two inhabited islands sit within the reserve. **Fanø**, reached by ferry from Esbjerg (12 minutes), has wide sandy beaches, traditional thatched villages, and an annual kite festival that draws enthusiasts from across Europe. **Rømø**, connected to the mainland by a causeway, has the widest beach in northern Europe — so wide that you can drive and park on the sand. From Rømø a ferry crosses to the German island of Sylt.

The area is part of the larger [Frisian Islands](/europe/denmark/vadehavet/frisian_islands_in) interregion, a single ecological entity spanning three countries. Birdwatching, seal-spotting boat trips, and oyster-gathering tours are all popular from spring through autumn.
