---
title: "Food and Drink"
type: section
---

Estonian cooking is rooted in the seasons and the land: rye bread, pork, potatoes, dairy, fish, and foraged ingredients. It's not flashy food, but it's honest and — in good hands — excellent. The restaurant scene in Tallinn has improved dramatically in the past decade, and there are now serious farm-to-table restaurants, a craft beer culture, and a genuine interest in reviving and reinventing traditional flavours.

**Things to eat**

*Rye bread* (*leib*) is the foundation of Estonian food. Dense, slightly sour, and served with almost every meal. Don't leave without eating it.

*Verivorst* (blood sausage) is Estonia's national dish — served with sauerkraut and lingonberry jam, usually at Christmas but available year-round in good restaurants.

*Mulgipuder* — mashed potatoes with pearl barley and pork fat — is simple comfort food done well.

*Rosolje* is a vivid beetroot and herring salad, creamy and sweet-sour, a staple at celebrations.

*Kiluvõileib* — an open sandwich topped with spiced Baltic sprats and a slice of boiled egg — is everywhere in Tallinn and makes a quick cheap lunch.

*Kama* is a toasted flour mixture (rye, barley, oat, peas) stirred into kefir or yoghurt. It's an acquired taste that Estonians eat enthusiastically and most visitors approach with caution. Worth trying once.

**Fish**

The Baltic herring (*räim*) is the national fish. Look for smoked, marinated, and pan-fried versions. In Tallinn's Kalamaja neighbourhood and at the central market, fishmongers sell excellent smoked fish.

**Drink**

The local beer scene has grown considerably. *A. Le Coq* and *Saku* are the main lagers; increasingly, craft breweries are making interesting ales and IPAs. *Vana Tallinn* is a sweet herby liqueur — tooth-achingly sweet on its own, better in coffee. Kefir and various fermented dairy drinks are popular nonalcoholic options.

**Where to eat**

In Tallinn, the Old Town has plenty of tourist-trap restaurants with medieval theme nights; avoid these and head to Kalamaja, Telliskivi, or Kadriorg for better food at lower prices. Tartu has a surprisingly good restaurant scene for a city its size.
