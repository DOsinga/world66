---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Estonia has a population of around 1.35 million, making it one of the smallest countries in the European Union. Ethnic Estonians make up about 69% of the population; Russians account for roughly 25%, concentrated mainly in Tallinn and the eastern industrial city of Narva, where Russian-speakers form a large majority. There are smaller Ukrainian, Belarusian, and Finnish communities.

Estonians have a reputation for being reserved at first — don't expect small talk from strangers. But once you get past the initial coolness, they're warm and genuinely hospitable. The stereotype of Nordic stoicism is real, but so is the dry humour once you've earned it.

Religion is not a dominant force in public life. Estonia is one of the least religious countries in Europe. Where it does exist, Evangelical Lutheranism and Estonian Orthodox are the main denominations.

The Russian-speaking minority is a sensitive political topic. Many Russian-speakers have lived in Estonia for generations, yet the question of citizenship, language rights, and integration remains contested. Relations have been complicated further by Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, which prompted Estonia — already spending heavily on NATO commitments — to accelerate its defence posture.

Estonians take their singing seriously. The Song Festival (*Laulupidu*), held every five years in Tallinn, draws tens of thousands of participants and hundreds of thousands of spectators. It played a central role in the peaceful independence movement of the late 1980s, known as the Singing Revolution.
