Tallinn is the capital of Estonia and one of the best-preserved medieval cities in Northern Europe. Since the collapse of the Soviet Union it has transformed from a provincial town in a vast empire into the busy, wired, forward-looking capital of a small Baltic country — and it has done so without losing the cobbled streets, red roofs, and stone towers that made it famous in the first place.

The heart of it all is the **Old Town**, a UNESCO World Heritage site that is compact enough to cover on foot in an afternoon. Start on **Raekoja plats**, the Town Hall Square, then climb **Pikk jalg** up to **Toompea Hill**, where the Russian Orthodox **Alexander Nevsky Cathedral** faces the Estonian parliament and the viewing platforms look out over a sea of medieval rooftops. The city once had 66 defensive towers — 19 still stand, and a few of the oldest (Nunna, Sauna and Kuldjala) can be visited in summer.

Step outside the walls and the contrasts start showing. The glass towers of the business district rise a few hundred metres from Kiek in de Kök. The creative district of **Telliskivi**, a cluster of old railway workshops turned into studios, restaurants and design shops, is a short walk north of the station. To the east, the wooded park and baroque palace of **Kadriorg** house one of the best art museums in the country, and a little further along the coast, the **Estonian Open Air Museum** preserves a hundred farms and windmills from around the country.

Estonia is one of the most digital countries on earth and Tallinn is where you feel it most: free wifi everywhere, contactless everything, e-residency card readers in coffee shops. Eat well — Estonian cooking has shed its Soviet reputation and modern restaurants lean into Baltic seafood, game, rye bread and wild forest flavours — and plan your visit for summer if you can, when the nights barely get dark and the Old Town cafes spill out onto the squares.
