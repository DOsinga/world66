The Faroes' food culture is more interesting than you might expect from eighteen North Atlantic islands. The traditional cuisine is built on fish, lamb, and preservation — and the revival of traditional techniques by a new generation of Faroese chefs has made the islands a genuine destination for serious eaters.

**Ræst** is the defining flavour of Faroese food: meat or fish preserved by wind-fermentation rather than salt or smoke. Lamb (ræst kjøt) and skerpikjøt (wind-dried mutton) are left in wooden drying sheds for months, developing a pungent, complex flavour that locals love and newcomers find challenging. Ræst fish, usually cod, is similarly prepared. Restaurant Ræst in [Tórshavn](/europe/faroeislands/torshavn) serves a modern interpretation that is a good introduction.

**Lamb** is the other great Faroese ingredient — the name Faroe means "sheep islands," and the animals graze freely on hillsides. It is mild, tender, and good. Restaurant Áarstova in Tórshavn is the place to eat it simply and well.

**Salmon** has become a major export and a point of national pride. Farmed in cold, clean Atlantic water, Faroese salmon is considered among the best in the world and turns up in top sushi restaurants internationally.

**KOKS** is the Faroe Islands' Michelin-starred restaurant, serving a tasting menu based entirely on local ingredients interpreted through a Nordic fine dining lens. It moves locations occasionally — previously at Leynavatn lake on [Streymoy](/europe/faroeislands/streymoy). Worth booking well ahead if this sort of thing interests you.

For everyday eating in Tórshavn, the café and restaurant scene is small but decent. The fish and chips at the harbour, the open sandwiches in the cafés, and the lamb burgers are all solid and unpretentious.
