Suðuroy is the southernmost of the eighteen Faroe Islands, a dramatic slab of an island two hours by ferry from Tórshavn across open North Atlantic. It is the Faroes at their most rugged and least touristed: vertical basalt sea cliffs rising more than 400 metres straight out of the ocean on the western side, quiet fishing villages tucked into narrow fjords on the eastern side, and a population of around 4,600 spread across a dozen small communities. Almost no foreign visitors come here, and the islanders like it that way.

The two largest towns are **Tvøroyri** (on the east coast fjord of Trongisvágsfjørður, where the ferry from Tórshavn docks at Krambatangi) and **Vágur**, further south. Both are functional working towns of a couple of thousand people with harbours, schools, and a handful of cafés. The more interesting villages are the smaller ones scattered around the coast: **Fámjin**, on the west coast, which holds the original Faroese flag (hand-sewn in Copenhagen in 1919 by a student from the village) in its tiny wooden church; **Sumba**, the southernmost village, with its view out onto Beinisvørð, the second-highest sea cliff in the Faroes at 469 metres; **Sandvík**, the northernmost, reachable only through an old road tunnel, and the place where the Viking chief Sigmundur Brestisson — who first brought Christianity to the Faroes around 1000 AD — was killed after swimming ashore from nearby Skúvoy; and **Hvalba**, the only place in the Faroe Islands where coal is still mined.

The point of coming to Suðuroy is the landscape and the silence. The **Beinisvørð** cliff walk, starting from Sumba, is one of the great sea-cliff walks in northern Europe. The **Eggjarnar** viewpoint above Vágur looks straight down several hundred metres to the Atlantic. **Akraberg**, the southern tip of the island, has a 1909 lighthouse, vertical cliffs, and nesting puffins in summer. There are good walks between almost all the villages, a few small museums in 19th-century turf-roofed houses, and the sort of absolute quiet that comes from being on a small island far from anywhere.

The car ferry **Smyril** runs between Tórshavn and Tvøroyri two or three times a day in summer; the crossing takes two hours and can be rough. Bring a warm jacket regardless of the season, and don't plan any itinerary too tightly — the weather dictates what you can do on the Faroes, and Suðuroy is the most exposed of the islands.
