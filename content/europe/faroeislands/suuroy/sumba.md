Sumba is the southernmost village in the Faroe Islands, perched on the southwestern tip of Suðuroy with the North Atlantic crashing against the cliffs below. About 250 people live here, and the setting is spectacular even by Faroese standards.

The village's great landmark is Beinisvørð, a 469-metre sea cliff that drops vertically into the ocean — the second highest in the Faroe Islands. Seen from the sea it is a sheer wall; from the village side it is a steep green mountainside. You can drive to the viewpoint at Hesturin or walk to the top for views along the wild west coast of Suðuroy. Two kilometres east of Sumba, at Akraberg, is the southernmost point of the entire archipelago: a lighthouse built in 1909, vertical cliffs, and colonies of seabirds.

Sumba was home to one of the Faroes' most celebrated poets, Poul F. Joensen, who wrote repeatedly about Beinisvørð and the dramatic landscape that surrounds the village.
