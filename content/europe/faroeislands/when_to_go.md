---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

**Summer (June to August)** is the prime season, and for good reason. Days are very long — around the solstice, darkness barely falls at all. The weather is as stable as it gets (which is not saying much — the Faroes are notoriously changeable). Puffins are on Mykines from late April to August; the first week of August sees [Ólavsøka](/europe/faroeislands/festivals), the national holiday, which draws Faroese from all over the islands to [Tórshavn](/europe/faroeislands/torshavn) for rowing races, concerts, and chain dancing in the streets. The G! music festival on [Eysturoy](/europe/faroeislands/eysturoy) takes place in mid-July.

The downside of summer: the Faroes have become a popular destination, and popular spots like Gásadalur and Mykines can feel crowded on fine days. Accommodation should be booked well in advance.

**Spring and autumn** offer quieter visits. May and September can be beautiful — the light is extraordinary, the green hills are at their most vivid in May, and you are unlikely to share a mountain trail with many others. Bird migration peaks in spring and autumn. Weather is more unpredictable, and some tourist services run reduced schedules.

**Winter (November to February)** is for the committed. Days can be as short as five hours, storms are frequent, and ferry services to smaller islands may be disrupted for days at a time. But the Faroes are hauntingly beautiful in winter light, and a clear evening can bring a dramatic aurora over the harbours. If you can handle the dark and the cold, you will have the islands essentially to yourself.

The average summer temperature is around 13°C; winter averages around 3°C. It is never extremely hot or extremely cold — the Gulf Stream keeps the climate milder than the latitude suggests.
