The Åland Islands are an archipelago of over 6,000 islands and skerries in the Baltic Sea, halfway between Finland and Sweden. Politically Finnish but culturally and linguistically Swedish, the islands have a degree of autonomy unique in Europe: their own parliament, their own flag, their own postal stamps, and Swedish as the sole official language. They are also permanently demilitarised under international treaty — a status that has held since 1856.

The main island, Fasta Åland, holds most of the population and the capital Mariehamn — a charming wooden town of wide avenues and a genuine maritime atmosphere. But the real draw is the archipelago itself: clear Baltic water, pink granite shores, traditional fishing villages, and a pace of life that feels genuinely unhurried. Cyclists are well catered for, and the inter-island ferries — free for pedestrians — give access to the outer islands without a car.

The islands are best reached by ferry from Turku or Stockholm; the crossing from either side makes an overnight stopover or a scenic day on the water. A small airport at Mariehamn serves domestic routes. The best time to visit is summer, when days are long and the archipelago fills with sailors and kayakers, though spring and autumn have their own quiet appeal.
