---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Finland punches well above its weight on the festival front. Summer is the main season — the long days and mild nights create an atmosphere that pulls Finns out of doors with unusual intensity after months of cold and dark.

## Midsummer (Juhannus) — Late June

The most important festival in the Finnish calendar, typically celebrated on the weekend closest to June 21. Finns head to their summer cottages by the lakes, light bonfires, and stay up through the brief near-dark of the midsummer night. Cities empty out noticeably. If you're in Finland at Midsummer, expect many restaurants and shops to close — it's a genuinely observed holiday, not just a date on the calendar.

## Vappu — May 1

May Day is Finland's other great street celebration. Cities fill with students in white graduation caps drinking sparkling wine, eating doughnuts, and singing. Helsinki's Senate Square and Esplanadi park are the centres of the action. Vappu Eve (April 30) can be rowdier than the day itself.

## Pori Jazz — July

Held annually in [Pori](/europe/finland/pori) since 1966, this is one of the oldest and most respected jazz festivals in Europe. It runs for about nine days in mid-July and pulls major international artists. The main stage is in Kirjurinluoto park by the river. Around 100 concerts spread across multiple venues. Draws around 150,000 visitors.

## Savonlinna Opera Festival — July–August

Perhaps Finland's most famous cultural event. Opera performances are staged inside the medieval Olavinlinna Castle in [Savonlinna](/europe/finland/savonlinna), right on the lake. The acoustics and setting are remarkable — this is worth building a trip around if opera is your thing. Runs from early July to early August. Book well in advance.

## Ruisrock — Early July

Finland's oldest rock festival (founded 1970), held on the island of Ruissalo in [Turku](/europe/finland/turku). A well-organised event with a strong lineup of international and Finnish acts across rock, pop, and electronic genres. The island setting on the Baltic makes it feel distinctly Finnish.

## Flow Festival — August, Helsinki

A more recent addition to the calendar but now one of the most prominent in Northern Europe. Held in the old Suvilahti power plant area in Helsinki over three days in August, it combines international music headliners with Nordic food and design. The industrial setting and mix of genres give it a strong identity.

## Midnight Sun Film Festival — June, Sodankylä

Every June, the tiny Lapland town of Sodankylä hosts one of Finland's most unusual cultural events: five days of films shown around the clock, including through the white night. Founded in 1986 by the Kaurismäki brothers, it retains a cult following and screens classics alongside new films.

## Independence Day — December 6

Not a festival in the festive sense, but Finland's Independence Day is marked with quiet dignity. The President's Independence Day Reception (broadcast live) is watched by enormous numbers. Cities are lit with candles in windows.
