## Currency and Paying

Finland uses the **Euro (€)**. Cards are accepted almost everywhere — Finland is one of the most cashless societies in Europe. You can go days without needing physical money in cities, though a small amount of cash is useful in very rural areas or small island ferry kiosks.

Tipping is not customary. Service is included in all bills. Rounding up or leaving a euro or two at a restaurant is appreciated but never expected.

## Language

Finnish is famously one of the hardest languages in Europe for an English speaker to learn. Fortunately, English is almost universally spoken — you'll have no trouble in cities, tourist areas, or most of the country. Swedish is an official co-language, and the coastal towns (Turku, Vaasa, Jakobstad) and the Åland Islands have significant Swedish-speaking populations.

A few useful Finnish words: *kiitos* (thank you), *anteeksi* (sorry/excuse me), *hei* (hello), *hei hei* (goodbye).

## Visa

Finland is part of the Schengen Area. Citizens of the EU, EEA, and many other countries (including the US, Canada, Australia, and UK) can enter visa-free for stays up to 90 days. Check the requirements for your nationality before travel.

## Climate and Clothing

Finland has four very distinct seasons. Summers (June–August) are warm and can be genuinely hot, with temperatures in Helsinki reaching 25–30°C on good days. Winters are cold, dark, and snowy — temperatures in Helsinki drop well below freezing, and Lapland regularly sees –20°C or colder. Layering is essential in any shoulder season. Bring mosquito repellent if visiting lakes or forests in summer, especially in the north — the insects can be relentless.

## Health and Safety

Finland is extremely safe. Crime rates are low, tap water is excellent and safe to drink throughout the country. The standard EU emergency number **112** works for all emergencies.

## Sauna

Sauna is not a tourist attraction — it is genuinely central to Finnish life. There are around two million saunas in a country of 5.5 million people. If a Finn invites you to their sauna, accept. In public saunas, nudity is normal in single-sex facilities; mixed saunas usually allow swimwear. The correct etiquette is to sit quietly, sweat, and leave when you feel like it. Jumping in a lake or rolling in snow afterward is recommended.

## Opening Hours

Most shops are open Monday to Saturday, roughly 9:00–18:00 or later. Larger supermarkets often open until 21:00 or 22:00, and Helsinki's S-market chain has some 24-hour branches. Museums typically close on Mondays. Note that public holidays (including Midsummer) see widespread closures — plan food shopping in advance.

## Public Holidays

Key dates: **New Year's Day** (Jan 1), **Epiphany** (Jan 6), **Good Friday and Easter**, **May Day/Vappu** (May 1, a major street party), **Ascension**, **Midsummer's Eve and Day** (late June — the country essentially shuts down), **All Saints' Day** (early November), **Independence Day** (Dec 6), **Christmas** (Dec 24–26).

## Internet and Mobile

Mobile coverage is excellent across most of the country, though you may lose signal in very remote Lapland wilderness. Free Wi-Fi is available in most cafes, libraries, and hotels.
