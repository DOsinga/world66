---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

Finland is a year-round destination but what you're after shapes heavily when you should go.

## Summer (June–August)

The most popular time to visit. Cities come alive, ferries to the archipelago run frequently, hiking trails in the national parks are accessible, and the days are extraordinarily long. North of the Arctic Circle, the sun doesn't set at all from late May to late July — the **Midnight Sun** is one of Finland's strangest and most compelling experiences. Lake and archipelago holidays are at their best in July. Temperatures in the south regularly reach 20–25°C, sometimes higher. The downside: accommodation fills up, especially at Midsummer, and some cottage areas get crowded. Mosquitoes in northern forests can be brutal in July.

## Autumn (September–October)

Underrated. The forests turn gold and rust, particularly in Lapland where the *ruska* (autumn colours) can be spectacular. The first northern lights begin to appear. Fewer tourists and lower prices. Weather in the south is still pleasant in September. Lapland in October can already have snow.

## Winter (December–March)

Finland in winter is genuinely magical if you're prepared for cold. Lapland becomes a snow-globe landscape of frozen lakes, reindeer, dog sleds, ice fishing, and snowmobile safaris. This is the season for the **Northern Lights** (aurora borealis), most reliably seen in Lapland on clear nights from September through March, with the equinox months of September and March often the most active. Helsinki in midwinter is dark — only a few hours of pale light per day — but Christmas markets and the sauna culture make it liveable and atmospheric.

Expect serious cold in Lapland: –20°C is not unusual, and –30°C or below happens. Dress in proper layers; base layer, mid layer, insulated outer layer, and a windproof shell. Rental cold-weather clothing is available at most Lapland resorts.

## Shoulder (May and November)

May is wonderful in the south — long days, spring wildflowers, Vappu celebrations on May 1. Many Lapland operators shut down in November (between the tourist seasons), making it the least useful time to visit the north. In the south, November is grey and quiet.

## In Short

- **Best for Northern Lights**: February–March (clear skies, comfortable cold)
- **Best for Midnight Sun**: June–July (go north)
- **Best for Helsinki city break**: May–September
- **Best for lake holidays**: July–August
- **Best for autumn colours**: Late September in Lapland
