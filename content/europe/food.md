European food is not one thing. The continent spans olive oil and pasta in the south to butter and smoked fish in the north, with beer-braised pork, rye bread, and dumplings filling the middle. What unites it is a shared seriousness about eating well — even a motorway service station in [France](/europe/france) will offer something worth eating.

**Mediterranean south.** [Italy](/europe/italy) and [France](/europe/france) are the two poles of European cuisine, and arguing about which is better is a productive way to spend an evening. Italian cooking is regional to the point of obsessiveness — what they eat in Bologna bears little resemblance to what's on the table in [Sicily](/europe/italy/sicily) or Naples. Fresh pasta in [Emilia-Romagna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emilia-Romagna), pizza in Naples, seafood along every coast, gelato everywhere: it's hard to eat badly in [Italy](/europe/italy) if you avoid restaurants with photographs on the menu. French food leans more formal and more sauce-driven — cassoulet in the southwest, bouillabaisse in Marseille, Alsatian choucroute in the east, the butter-and-cream cooking of Normandy. Both countries have strong cheese traditions that reward serious exploration.

[Spain](/europe/spain) has its own claim to greatness. The north, particularly the Basque Country and San Sebastián, is one of the most concentrated areas of culinary talent in the world — more Michelin stars per capita than almost anywhere. But everyday Spanish eating is just as satisfying: pintxos (Basque bar snacks), jamón ibérico sliced to order, tortilla española, fresh seafood, and the whole culture of tapas as a social meal rather than a starter.

[Greece](/europe/greece) gets underestimated. Good Greek food — grilled fish on the islands, slow-roasted lamb, spanakopita fresh from the oven, proper tzatziki made with strained yogurt — is excellent. The problem is tourist-trap versions that have buried the real thing under feta-covered mediocrity. Ask where locals eat and you'll find it quickly.

**Central Europe.** [Germany](/europe/germany), [Austria](/europe/austria), and [Switzerland](/europe/switzerland) have a more substantial style of cooking — pork, bread dumplings (Knödel), sausages, pickled vegetables, heavy soups. Schnitzel is better in Vienna than almost anywhere. Sausage quality in Germany is genuine, not a cliché. The bread culture across German-speaking Europe is serious: hundreds of varieties, dense and flavorful in ways that make most supermarket bread seem like foam. Beer in [Germany](/europe/germany), [Czechia](/europe/czechrepublic), and [Belgium](/europe/belgium) deserves the reputation it has. [Belgium](/europe/belgium) alone has around 1,500 distinct beers; the Czech Republic produces the world's best lager; Germany's brewing traditions vary enormously by region.

[Hungary](/europe/hungary) and [Poland](/europe/poland) are underrated food destinations. Hungarian cuisine — goulash, chicken paprikash, langos (fried dough from street stalls), the extraordinary pastries of [Budapest](/europe/hungary/budapest) — is deeply satisfying. Polish food is hearty in the best sense: bigos (hunter's stew), pierogi stuffed with every possible filling, żurek (sour rye soup), and an excellent craft beer scene that has emerged in the last decade.

**Northern Europe.** Scandinavian food has been transformed in the last 20 years. New Nordic cuisine — local, seasonal, fermented, foraged — put [Copenhagen](/europe/denmark/copenhagen) on the global culinary map and inspired a generation of chefs across the region. But the traditional foundation is still worth knowing: smoked salmon, pickled herring, open-faced smørrebrød sandwiches, and the general Nordic conviction that dairy and bread are the backbone of civilization. [Finland](/europe/finland) has its own distinctive flavors: game, freshwater fish, wild mushrooms and berries gathered from forests.

The [United Kingdom](/europe/unitedkingdom) has a reputation for bad food that it's been slowly outrunning for 30 years. [London](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/london) is genuinely one of the most exciting restaurant cities in the world, largely because of immigration from South Asia, the Middle East, and beyond. British pub food, at its best, is excellent — a Sunday roast done properly, a proper Cornish pasty, fish and chips by the sea. [Ireland](/europe/ireland) has followed a similar arc: the country's dairy and seafood are world-class, and the restaurant scene in Dublin has caught up with its reputation.

**Eastern Europe and the Balkans.** This is where food gets interesting and cheap at the same time. Georgian cuisine — if you accept Georgia as European — is extraordinary: khachapuri (cheese bread), khinkali (dumplings), walnut sauces, pomegranate, and the rich red wines of the Kakheti region. [Romania](/europe/romania) and [Bulgaria](/europe/bulgaria) have strong grilled meat traditions, excellent cheeses, and markets full of vegetables that still taste like vegetables. Balkan grilled meats — ćevapi, pljeskavica — appear across the former Yugoslavia and are best eaten standing up from a fast-food counter. Simple, satisfying, and very cheap.

**Eating on a budget.** Market halls are the best value in most European cities — fresh ingredients for self-catering, or prepared food stalls where you eat what locals eat. A lunch menu (the set midday meal offered by most restaurants in [France](/europe/france), [Spain](/europe/spain), and [Italy](/europe/italy)) gives you a full meal at prices dramatically lower than the same restaurant in the evening. Street food in eastern Europe — langos in Hungary, zapiekanka in Poland, burek across the Balkans — is filling, good, and costs almost nothing.
