In 1983, the city of Lyon decided to create a specialized department in charge of forming a collection of contemporary art within the Fine Arts Museum, with its own program of exhibitions and responsible for the organization of the "Octobre des Arts" Show, replaced in 1991 by the Biennial Exhibition of Contemporary Art. In 1988, the title of Museum of Contemporary Art was conferred by the Museums of France administrative agency. On December 19, 1995, the Museum of Contemporary Art inaugurated its new building at the Cité Internationale and welcomed 130,000 visitors during the 3rd edition of the two-month long Biennial Exhibition.

Since December 1995, the Lyon Museum of Contemporary Art, created in 1984, has been located in a building by Renzo Piano, in the heart of the Cité Internationale, blending the heritage of the past (the 1930's façade of the Palais de la Foire) and modern design (the new red brick structure).

Renzo Piano's design of this building included the preservation of the 1930's façade (former Palais de la Foire, or Fair Hall) facing the park, with a new structure of brick and glass opening out onto a pedestrian promenade linking the Museum to a vast cinema complex. Though the museum's exterior is voluntarily hybrid, the interior displays perfect unity and the public may pass through freely.

One-of-a-kind in France, the museum's collection is based on works created on site, directly by the artists. It is the largest collection of in situ art ever gathered in one European museum. Representative of international creative trends since the end of the sixties, the collection will be regularly displayed, in alternance with special exhibitions, starting in September 1996. Since its creation, the Museum of Contemporary Art has organized more than 100 exhibitions and published 70 bilingual or trilingual catalogs.

Museum policy determined the choice of a highly flexible interior space capable of adapting to the multiple requirements of artists and to the diverse exhibit settings conceived by curators. This flexibility goes totally unnoticed thanks to a system of removable walls that allow each show to create its own space. An infinite variety of paths and settings gives visitors the impression of a new museum with every exhibition.

The educational mission of the museum is based on the principle of establishing a dialog between the works of art and the visitors. This is achieved by traditional methods (guided tours, workshops) and new approaches such as extramural exhibits. Called "Turnkey" exhibits, they consist of works from the collection and are displayed in schools, companies, hospitals, social centers, neighborhoods, etc. A whole new series of educational activities completes the picture: "Museum Workshops", an initiation to artistic methods conducted directly by artists represented in the museum's collection; "See and Tell" which links contemporary art to the world of storytelling.

Rotating works of video art are regularly shown on the projection wall of the Rhône façade, and will be visible from the covered promenade. The wall also serves as a bulletin board for information concerning the museum. In January 1997, images will be simultaneously broadcast on the museum wall and on video screens in a subway station, 7 days a week.
