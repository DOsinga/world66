Méribel was founded in 1938 by Scottish colonel Peter Lindsay, who built the resort's first lift above the village of Les Allues with the explicit aim of creating a ski resort that remained true to its mountain setting. That architectural discipline has held: all the buildings in Méribel are constructed in the traditional Savoyard style, with wood and stone, making it one of the most visually coherent resorts in France. There are no concrete blocks here.

The skiing is the main draw, and it is exceptional. Méribel sits at the centre of the Trois Vallées — the Three Valleys — which with around 600 km of marked pistes across linked terrain is the largest ski area in the world. From Méribel, Courchevel is accessible in minutes; Val Thorens, the highest resort in the Alps, is reachable in under an hour. The terrain suits every level: long, beautifully groomed blues and reds run through the valley, while the upper slopes above Méribel and Mottaret offer steeper challenges and excellent off-piste options. Reliable natural snowfall combined with 1,200 snow cannons means the season typically runs from Christmas to early May.

Off the slopes, the village has a lively après-ski scene, a good range of restaurants from simple crêperies to Michelin-starred dining, and decent facilities for non-skiers: an Olympic-standard ice rink (built for the 1992 Albertville Games, which used Méribel as its ice-sports venue), a swimming pool, fitness centre, and squash courts. Scenic mountain walks are accessible via the gondolas even in winter.
