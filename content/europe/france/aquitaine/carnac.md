Carnac is home to the largest collection of standing stones in the world — over 3,000 menhirs arranged in long parallel rows stretching across the Breton countryside. The alignments date to around 4500 BC, making them older than Stonehenge by more than a millennium, and nobody knows for certain why they were built. Walking among them is eerie and compelling in equal measure.

The stones are divided into three main groups: Ménec (the largest, with 1,099 menhirs in eleven rows), Kermario (the most dramatic, with some stones reaching 4 metres tall), and Kerlescan (the smallest and least visited — and therefore the most atmospheric). In summer the main sites are fenced off to protect the ground, but guided walks go through them. In winter you can wander freely among the stones.

Beyond the megaliths, Carnac is also a seaside resort on the Quiberon Bay. The Grande Plage is a long, sheltered sand beach popular with families, and the oyster beds at the Trinité-sur-Mer end of the bay produce some of Brittany's finest oysters. The town has good crêperies and a small but well-presented Musée de Préhistoire covering the megalithic culture of southern Brittany.

The best time to visit the stones is early morning or late afternoon, when the light is low and the crowds thin. Carnac is an easy day trip from [Vannes](/europe/france/aquitaine/vannes) or Auray.
