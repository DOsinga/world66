The Ile d'Oleron is the largest French island on the Atlantic coast, connected to the mainland by a toll-free bridge. It is enormously popular with French holidaymakers in summer but still largely unknown to international visitors — which is part of its charm.

The island runs about 30 km north to south and is remarkably varied. The western coast has long sandy beaches backed by dunes, with good surf. The eastern side faces the sheltered waters between the island and the mainland, where oyster beds stretch out at low tide — Oleron oysters are considered among France's finest, and you can buy them straight from the producers at the port of La Cayenne.

The main town, Saint-Pierre-d'Oleron, has a lively market and a striking Romanesque lantern of the dead in the cemetery. The northern tip of the island is dominated by the Chassiron lighthouse, with panoramic views over the Atlantic. Fort Boyard, the famous sea fortress, sits just offshore to the east and is visible from several points along the coast.

Cycling is the best way to get around — the island has an excellent network of bike paths through pine forests and along the shore.
