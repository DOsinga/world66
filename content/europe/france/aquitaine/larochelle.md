La Rochelle is one of the most attractive coastal towns in France — a handsome port city with a seventeenth-century waterfront, arcaded streets, and a rich maritime history. The old harbour, flanked by two medieval towers and the Grosse Horloge gateway, is the heart of the town and one of the most photographed waterfronts on the Atlantic coast.

The centre is compact and largely pedestrianised, with arcaded streets full of independent shops along the Rue du Palais. The Musee du Nouveau Monde, housed in a former ship-owner's mansion, documents La Rochelle's role in the transatlantic trade. The Hotel de Ville is one of the finest Renaissance buildings in France. The modern aquarium near the harbour is excellent and popular with families.

La Rochelle's history is dramatic — it was a Huguenot stronghold besieged by Cardinal Richelieu in 1627-28, an episode that features in Dumas' Three Musketeers. The town was also an important departure point for settlers heading to New France (Canada).

Just south of La Rochelle is the [Ile d'Oleron](/europe/france/aquitaine/iledoleron), France's second-largest island, with long sandy beaches. The Ile de Re to the north is more fashionable and busier in summer.
