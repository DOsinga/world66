Les Eyzies-de-Tayac is the self-proclaimed "capital of prehistory," and the claim is justified. This small village in the Vezere valley of the [Dordogne](/europe/france/aquitaine) sits at the centre of one of the densest concentrations of prehistoric sites in the world. The limestone cliffs that line the valley are riddled with caves and rock shelters that have been occupied by humans for over 250,000 years.

The star attraction is the Grotte de Font-de-Gaume, one of the last caves in France where you can still see original polychrome paintings from around 14,000 BC. The 130-metre cave contains over 250 paintings of bison, mammoths, horses, and human hands — astonishing work created by Magdalenian people using the natural contours of the rock. Visitor numbers are strictly limited; book well in advance.

The National Museum of Prehistory, housed in a dramatic cliff-face setting above the village, provides context for the region's archaeological wealth. The village itself is tiny — a few restaurants, a couple of hotels — but makes an excellent base for exploring the valley by car, on foot, or by canoe on the Vezere River. The surrounding countryside of walnut groves, honey-coloured villages, and river cliffs is beautiful in its own right.
