The Loire Valley is one of the most spectacular regions of France. Along the Loire river stands a string of châteaux built by centuries of kings, queens, and courtiers — a stretch that UNESCO recognises as a World Heritage Site (give or take a pair of nuclear reactors that unfortunately fall inside the boundary).

The highlights are the towns of [Amboise](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/amboise), where Leonardo da Vinci is buried; [Blois](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/blois), with a château where Leonardo also had a hand and the Maison de la Magie devoted to Houdin; and [Tours](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/tours), a lively university city with a beautifully restored medieval quarter.

Some of the finest castles sit a little back from the river. [Chambord](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/chambord) is the giant — 440 rooms, and a fireplace in every one of them. [Cheverny](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/cheverny) is smaller and still lived in. [Usse](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/usse) is said to have inspired Perrault's Sleeping Beauty, and looks it.

Along the river Cher, a tributary, the highlight is [Chenonceau](/europe/france/centre/loirevalley/chenonceau), a château that spans the water on a graceful stone gallery. It has always been owned by women, which (the story goes) explains its charm and the state of repair.

The region is also serious wine country — Chinon, Saumur, Vouvray, Sancerre all come from here. The best way to see all of this is slowly: a few days based in Tours or Amboise, with a car or a bicycle for the back roads between châteaux.
