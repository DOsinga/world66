**Champagne** is a historic province and wine region of north-eastern France, tucked between Paris, Lorraine, and the Belgian and Luxembourg borders. It gives its name to the only wine in the world protected by a geographical indication so strict that no other sparkling wine may legally use the word — and most of what you come here for is connected, in some way, to that fact. The chalky soil, the cool northerly climate, and the blend of **Chardonnay**, **Pinot Noir** and **Pinot Meunier** grapes combine to produce the bottle-fermented sparkling wine that has defined celebration since the late 17th century, when the Benedictine monk **Dom Pérignon** first codified the techniques at his abbey in **[Hautvillers](/europe/france/east/champagne/hautvillers)**.

The two great wine cities are **[Reims](/europe/france/east/champagne/reims)** and **[Épernay](/europe/france/east/champagne/epernay)**, about 25 km apart. **Reims**, the larger, is the historic coronation city of the French kings — every king from Clovis to Charles X was crowned at the city's Gothic **Notre-Dame Cathedral**, one of the most important in France, and still breathtaking despite damage from both World Wars. Under Reims run more than 250 kilometres of chalk cellars (*crayères*) where the great Champagne houses — Veuve Clicquot, Taittinger, Pommery, G.H. Mumm — age their bottles; most offer guided tours and tastings. **Épernay**, smaller and quieter, is built along the famous **Avenue de Champagne** — a single kilometre-long street said to be the richest in the world, with the cellars of Moët & Chandon, Pol Roger, Perrier-Jouët and Mercier all hidden beneath the road surface. The countryside between the two, the **Marne Valley** and the **Montagne de Reims**, is a landscape of chalky vineyards and stone villages that can be explored on day drives or along the signposted *Route Touristique du Champagne*.

Beyond the wine, the region has more. **[Troyes](/europe/france/east/champagne/troyes)** in the south is one of the least-visited and most beautiful medieval towns in France — a UNESCO-worthy candidate whose half-timbered old quarter has somehow escaped both mass tourism and wartime destruction. The forested **Ardennes** in the north cross into Belgium and offer excellent walking country, and the small walled town of **[Provins](/europe/france/east/champagne/provins)**, a former medieval trade-fair city, is a separate UNESCO World Heritage Site and a feasible day trip from Paris.
