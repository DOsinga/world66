Franche-Comté is a region of eastern France, tucked between Burgundy, Alsace, and Switzerland, with a character that borrows from all three neighbours. The landscape is dominated by the Jura plateau — limestone hills, dense forests, rushing rivers, and clear lakes — and the region produces some of France's best cheeses (Comté, Morbier, Mont d'Or) and good Jura wines to match them.

**Besançon**, the regional capital, is the main city — a handsome town on a loop of the Doubs river, with a spectacular Vauban-designed citadel and an elaborate 19th-century astronomical clock in the cathedral. Victor Hugo was born here. **Dole**, the former capital, has a beautiful old town and the house where Pasteur was born.

The rivers are the region's outstanding feature. The **Loue valley** (a tributary of the Doubs) is one of the most scenic river valleys in France, with limestone cliffs, waterfalls, and the village of Ornans — where the painter Gustave Courbet was born and set many of his works. The **Hérisson waterfalls** (cascades du Hérisson) in the Jura are a must: a series of falls through a gorge. Go on a weekday to avoid crowds. The source of the Lison river and the **Cirque de Consolation** (source of the Dessoubre) are both spectacular and little-visited.

The **Royal Salt Factory at Arc-et-Senans** is a UNESCO World Heritage site — an extraordinary 18th-century piece of enlightenment-era industrial architecture designed by Claude-Nicolas Ledoux.

With 6,000 km of navigable rivers, 80 lakes, and hundreds of forest paths, the region is excellent for hiking, cycling, kayaking, canoeing, and river swimming.
