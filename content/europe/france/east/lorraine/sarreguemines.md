Sarreguemines sits where the Sarre and Blies rivers meet, right on the French-German border — the German town of Kleinblittersdorf is a short walk across the bridge. With around 21,000 residents, it's a quietly confident provincial town that knows what it is: the home of one of France's most famous ceramics traditions.

The faience works here date to 1790, when Nicholas-Henri Jacobi founded a manufactory that eventually became one of Europe's largest producers of decorative earthenware. At its peak in the late 19th century the factory employed thousands and shipped its distinctive majolica tiles and tableware across the continent. The factory closed decades ago but the legacy is everywhere — in the tile-covered buildings, in the outstanding ceramics museum housed in the old director's villa, and in the antique shops that stock original pieces.

The town centre is pleasant rather than spectacular: a pedestrian street, a decent market square, a Gothic church. The riverside paths along the Sarre are genuinely nice for a walk. If you have a few hours between Metz and Saarbrücken, or you're interested in industrial history and decorative arts, Sarreguemines rewards a stop.
