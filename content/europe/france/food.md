French cuisine is genuinely as good as its reputation suggests, and the regional variety is remarkable. What you eat in [Alsace](/europe/france/east/alsace) bears almost no resemblance to what you eat in Provence or Brittany. The country has a serious food culture — markets, producers, and mealtimes are treated with real respect.

A few things to know: lunch is the main meal in many regions, and a prix-fixe lunch menu (formule or menu du jour) at a proper restaurant is often excellent value — three courses for €12–18 is not unusual outside Paris. Dinner is taken late, rarely before 7:30pm. Eating at off-hours is difficult outside tourist areas.

**Regional Specialities**

*Alsace* is famous for choucroute (sauerkraut with cured meats and sausages), tarte flambée (a thin crispy flatbread with fromage blanc, onion, and lardons), and Munster cheese. The Germanic influence is unmistakable.

*Burgundy* produces some of the world's most celebrated wines and pairs them with rich dishes: boeuf bourguignon, escargots de Bourgogne, and coq au vin. [Dijon](/europe/france/east/burgundy/dijon)'s mustard is the best in the world.

*Provence and the Riviera* favour olive oil, garlic, tomatoes, and fresh herbs. Bouillabaisse — the fish stew of Marseille — is the signature dish. Salade Niçoise (from [Nice](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/nice)) is a proper composed salad, not the soggy thing you get abroad. Socca, a chickpea flour pancake, is Nice street food at its finest.

*Bretagne* (Brittany) is the land of crêpes and galettes (buckwheat crêpes filled with savoury ingredients), excellent oysters and mussels, and salted butter caramel in everything. The dairy is outstanding.

*Dordogne and Périgord* is truffle and foie gras country — duck confit, magret de canard, and walnut oil on everything. Rich, indulgent, and worth it.

*Loire Valley* produces excellent chèvre (goat's cheese) and the Tarte Tatin was invented here — an upside-down caramelised apple tart that came about by accident when a hotelier put the apples in the pan before the pastry.

**Wine**

France produces more wine than any other country and the quality across the regions is extraordinary. Burgundy, Champagne, Bordeaux, the Rhône Valley, Alsace, and the Loire each have distinct styles. [Champagne](/europe/france/east/champagne) from anywhere other than the Champagne region is just sparkling wine — the French will tell you this firmly.

Ordering house wine (vin de la maison) in a restaurant is not a disgrace; often it's a perfectly decent local wine sold cheaply. In Alsace, drink Riesling and Gewürztraminer with your food.

**Cheese**

France has hundreds of named cheeses. The diversity between a creamy Brie de Meaux, a pungent Époisses, a sharp Comté, and a crumbly Roquefort illustrates the point better than any description. A good cheesemonger at a market is worth visiting even if you don't buy anything.

**Coffee**

A café means an espresso. If you want a large milky coffee, ask for a café crème or café au lait (the latter mainly at breakfast). Ordering a "café latte" will get you a look.
