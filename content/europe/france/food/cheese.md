France produces more varieties of cheese than any other country — estimates range from 400 to over 1,000 depending on how you count. Like wine, many are protected by the Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée (AOC) system, tying production to specific regions and methods. Every corner of the country has its own traditions, and tasting local cheese is one of the simplest pleasures of traveling here.

**Roquefort** is one of the great blue cheeses, made entirely from raw ewe's milk in the Aveyron department. The wheels are aged in the natural limestone caves of Roquefort-sur-Soulzon, where cracks in the rock create the cool, humid conditions the Penicillium roqueforti mould needs. It has been AOC-protected since 1925.

**Camembert de Normandie** is the soft, bloomy-rind cheese the world associates with France. True AOC Camembert comes only from five departments in Normandy and must be made from raw milk, ladled into moulds by hand. The industrial versions sold in supermarkets worldwide bear little resemblance to the real thing.

**Saint-Nectaire** is a semi-soft pressed cheese from the Auvergne, made from the milk of Salers cows that graze volcanic pastures. Look for the green oval label, which marks the fermier (farmhouse) version aged on rye straw. The laitier (dairy) version has a square label and is blander.

**Comté** from the Jura is France's most-produced AOC cheese — a firm, nutty Gruyère-style wheel aged anywhere from four to over eighteen months. Each wheel tastes different depending on the season's pasture and the duration of aging.

**Époisses**, from Burgundy, is washed in Marc de Bourgogne brandy during aging, giving it an orange rind and a pungent smell that belies a surprisingly mellow, creamy interior. It was reportedly Napoleon's favourite cheese.

**Reblochon** from the Savoie is the essential ingredient of tartiflette. This soft washed-rind cheese has a nutty, slightly sweet flavour. The name comes from "reblocher," meaning to milk a cow a second time — farmers would under-report their milk production to avoid taxes, then make cheese from the richer second milking.

The best way to explore is at a fromagerie or market stall, where the vendor will let you taste before buying. Ask what is at its best that day — seasonal and ripeness matter enormously. A good cheese course after dinner typically includes three to five varieties, progressing from mild to strong.
