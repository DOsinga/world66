The GR 20 is widely considered the most demanding of Europe's Grande Randonnée trails, running 180 km from Calenzana in the northwest to Conca in the southeast, straight through Corsica's mountainous spine. Most hikers take about 16 days to complete the full route, though strong walkers have done it in less.

The northern half is the harder section, with steep, rocky terrain, exposed ridgelines, and some sections requiring scrambling with chains and ladders. The southern half is gentler, passing through forests and pastoral landscapes. The highest point, Bocca di Borba, sits at about 2,225 m.

Refuges (staffed mountain huts) are spaced along the route, roughly one per stage, offering bunks and basic meals in season (June to September). Most hikers carry a tent as backup since the refuges fill up. The trail is best attempted between mid-June and mid-October; snow lingers at altitude well into June, and autumn storms can arrive in October.

The GR 20 rewards with extraordinary landscapes: granite needles, glacial lakes, dense pine forests, and views that stretch to both coasts of the island. It is not a trail for beginners, but experienced hikers with good fitness will find it one of the finest multi-day walks in Europe.
