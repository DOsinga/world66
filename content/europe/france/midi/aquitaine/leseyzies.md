Les Eyzies-de-Tayac sits in a dramatic loop of the Vézère river, where limestone cliffs overhang the village and cave entrances dot the valley walls. This small commune calls itself the "Capital of Prehistory" without any hint of false modesty — the density of Palaeolithic sites in this single valley is extraordinary. Cro-Magnon man's remains were discovered here in 1868, and the surrounding area has since yielded more prehistoric art and evidence of early human habitation than almost anywhere else on earth.

The centerpiece is the Musée National de Préhistoire, built into the cliff face above the village, with one of Europe's finest collections of Palaeolithic tools, carvings, and human remains. From Les Eyzies you can reach Font-de-Gaume — one of the last decorated caves in France still open to the public, where bison, mammoths, and horses painted some 17,000 years ago cover the narrow walls. Nearby Combarelles shelters hundreds of engravings. Lascaux itself is a short drive north near Montignac; the original cave is closed, but Lascaux IV is a superb facsimile.

Beyond the caves, the village is a pleasant base for the whole Vézère valley. The river draws canoeists in summer, the surrounding oak forests are hunted for truffles and cèpes in autumn, and Sarlat — the finest medieval town in the Périgord — is just 20 kilometres away.
