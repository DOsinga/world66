Uzès is a small and exceptionally beautiful town in the Gard department, about 25 kilometres north of [Nîmes](/europe/france/midi/languedoc/nimes). It was designated a *Ville d'Art et d'Histoire* in 1962, and its medieval and Renaissance centre has been beautifully restored — a maze of narrow lanes, stone arcades, and small squares that feels like stepping into a Provençal painting.

The town is dominated by the Duché, the residence of the Dukes of Uzès, which has been continuously inhabited since the 11th century. The Tour Fenestrelle, a round Romanesque bell tower unique in France, rises next to the former cathedral. The Place aux Herbes, a lovely arcaded square, hosts a bustling Saturday market with local produce, olives, and lavender.

Uzès sits at the source end of the Pont du Gard aqueduct — the Romans built the famous bridge to carry water from here to Nîmes, and the aqueduct ruins can be traced on walks through the surrounding garrigue. The countryside around the town is classic southern French landscape: limestone hills, vineyards, olive groves, and lavender fields. The Musée du Bonbon Haribo, on the edge of town, is unexpectedly entertaining.
