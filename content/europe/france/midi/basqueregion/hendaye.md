Hendaye is the last town in France before Spain, sitting at the mouth of the Bidasoa river where it empties into the Bay of Biscay. It has one of the finest beaches on the French Basque coast — a long, wide crescent of sand that stretches for over three kilometres, shallow enough for families and sheltered enough that the waves rarely get rough.

The town itself is quiet and residential, a contrast to the livelier resorts of [Biarritz](/europe/france/midi/basqueregion/biarritz) and [Saint-Jean-de-Luz](/europe/france/midi/basqueregion/saintjeandeluz) up the coast. That is part of its appeal. The beachfront promenade is pleasant without being flashy, and the restaurants along it serve good Basque cooking at lower prices than its neighbours.

The old quarter, Hendaye-Ville, sits a couple of kilometres inland near the train station. Between the two, the Domaine d'Abbadia occupies a wild headland with cliff walks, a neo-Gothic castle built for a 19th-century explorer, and views across the estuary to Hondarribia on the Spanish side. You can walk across the border in twenty minutes, or take a small boat across the river.
