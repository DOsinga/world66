The Calanques are the thing people who live in Marseille miss most when they leave — narrow limestone inlets carved into the coast south of the city, with water so clear and so blue it looks implausible. Getting there requires a bus and a walk, but the effort is the point.

Take bus 20 from the Vieux-Port towards Les Goudes. Ride it to the end of the line — about forty-five minutes through the southern suburbs and out along the coast road. From Les Goudes, a small fishing village with a couple of rough restaurants, follow the coastal path southeast towards the calanques proper. The first accessible inlet is Calanque de Callelongue, about fifteen minutes' walk. In high summer it fills up, so keep walking — another twenty minutes brings you to quieter spots where the path dips down to the water and you can jump in from the rocks.

Bring water and food — there are no shops once you leave Les Goudes. The path is rough in places and requires basic footwear. In July and August the coastal paths are sometimes closed due to fire risk; check before you go on the Préfecture de Police website.

Return by the same path and bus. Back in the city by early evening, sun-salty and hungry. The restaurants around the Vieux-Port are well-placed for this — or eat in Les Goudes itself if you want something simpler and cheaper.

For a longer excursion, the calanques further along — En Vau, Port-Miou — require more walking or a boat trip from the Vieux-Port. The boats run April to October from the quay and take around two hours each way.
