Get to the Vieux-Port early. The fish market on the Quai des Belges runs every morning until around noon, when the catch runs out — but the first hour is the best. The fishermen arrive straight from sea, usually before eight, and lay out whatever they pulled in on portable tables along the quay. Rascasse, sea bream, octopus, urchins if the season is right. Locals come to argue prices; tourists come to take photographs of the locals arguing prices. Both are welcome.

From the port, walk up towards Noailles along the Rue de Rome or Cours Belsunce. Noailles is the market quarter — North African, Comorian, and Provençal traders selling everything from preserved lemons to merguez to cheap trainers. The covered market hall at the top of the Rue du Marché des Capucins is the best spot: spices in open sacks, mountains of dried fruit, stalls serving sfenj (Moroccan doughnuts fried to order) or msemen flatbreads with honey. The noise and the smells are part of the experience.

By mid-morning you'll be ready to sit down. Head a few streets east to Cours Julien, the bohemian square with outdoor terraces and secondhand bookshops. Order a coffee and a croissant and let the morning settle. The square fills slowly — this neighbourhood wakes late, and on a weekday you might have a terrace almost to yourself until ten.

The whole thing can be done in three hours at an easy pace, and costs almost nothing unless you buy fish.
