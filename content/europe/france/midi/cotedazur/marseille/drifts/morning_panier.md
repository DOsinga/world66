Le Panier is the oldest part of Marseille, a tangle of narrow streets climbing the hill above the Vieux-Port. The best time to be here is early morning, before the heat comes off the stones and before the tourist groups arrive.

Start at Place de Lenche, the square at the top of the quarter. There are a couple of cafés that open early; take a coffee and look out over the rooftops down to the port. Then walk downhill through the alleys — they are too small for any plan to work, so follow whichever staircase looks interesting and see where it takes you. The streets are lined with coloured shutters, bougainvillea spilling over walls, and occasional pieces of street art, including a large Rone mural visible from several angles.

After twenty minutes of wandering, make your way to the [Vieille Charité](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/vieillecharit). Walk through the gate into the courtyard — one of the finest baroque spaces in the south of France, three tiers of arcaded galleries around a central oval chapel designed by Pierre Puget. Even if you don't go into the museums, the courtyard alone is worth it.

From the Charité, follow the downhill alleys towards the cathedral. The [Cathédrale de la Major](https://www.cathedrale-marseille.fr/) — the enormous striped Romanesque-Byzantine pile at the edge of the Panier — is unmissable. The façade faces the sea and the MuCEM across the boulevard. The MuCEM itself (the Museum of European and Mediterranean Civilisations) is worth a longer visit but easily fills its own afternoon; for this morning drift, just walk along the J4 pier outside it and look back at the city from the water.

By late morning you can loop back up to the port for lunch or a pastis on the quay.
