Start at the [Vallon des Auffes](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/vallondesauffes). This is Marseille's best-kept open secret — a small natural harbour just below the Corniche Kennedy, invisible from the road, reached down a staircase between two apartment blocks. Traditional wooden fishing boats called pointus are moored on the water; the houses around the harbour are painted white and blue; a viaduct arch frames the sky above. Three or four fish restaurants crowd the quay. Arrive around six and take a table outside for the pastis ritual: a glass, a jug of cold water, pour slowly and watch it go cloudy. This is the aperitif of the south.

From the Vallon, walk back up to the Corniche and continue west along the seafront towards the Pharo headland. The [Palais du Pharo](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/palaisdupharo) sits above the entrance to the Vieux-Port on its own promontory. The building is closed to visitors in the evening, but the grounds are open and the terrace is free. Come here for the last twenty minutes of sun — the port below, the Fort Saint-Jean to the east, the Frioul islands on the horizon, Notre-Dame de la Garde on the hill behind you. Marseille looks generous from up here.

Walk back down to the old port for dinner. The quays are lined with restaurants of varying quality; the rule of thumb is to avoid the ones with menus in five languages displayed outside and walk one street back into the streets of the 1st arrondissement — Rue Sainte, Rue Caisserie, and the surrounding streets have better options. Bouillabaisse is the obvious order, though a good one is expensive. A simple plate of grilled fish or a bourride (a creamier Provençal fish stew) is often more satisfying.

After dinner, the bars and café terraces on the quays stay busy until midnight.
