Marseille is a late city. Nothing gets going until ten, most bars stay open until two, and the streets around Cours Julien and La Plaine — the bohemian square known as Place Jean Jaurès — fill up properly around eleven. This is where the city's musicians, artists, and anyone who finds the Vieux-Port too polished eventually ends up.

Start earlier if you want to see a show. [La Criée](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/lacrie), the national theatre on the Vieux-Port, runs ambitious contemporary work most evenings and the building — the city's old fish auction hall, repurposed in the 1980s — is worth seeing regardless of what's on. Nearby, the [Palais des Arts](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/palaisdesarts) sometimes hosts evening openings. Or skip culture entirely and go straight to the bars.

[Cours Julien](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/coursjulien) is the main street. Terraces stay packed until midnight; the bars on and around it — particularly on Rue Crudère and the streets running north from the cours — run later. The music policy varies by venue but tends towards techno, jazz, and whatever is coming out of the local scene on any given night. Follow noise rather than recommendations.

La Plaine — the big market square three streets east — fills differently from the cours: older crowds, louder conversations, card games at outdoor tables, grilled food from the stalls that appear late. There's less intentionality here, which is part of the appeal.

Rue d'Aubagne, running south from Noailles, is where to eat after midnight. The North African and Comorian restaurants stay open late — bowls of harira, méchoui sandwiches, msemen with honey from stalls still frying at one in the morning. Marseille at this hour is not performative; it's just people eating at the end of a long night.
