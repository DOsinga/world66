The MuCEM — the Museum of European and Mediterranean Civilisations — sits at the point where the old port meets the open sea, connected to the 17th-century Fort Saint-Jean by a slender footbridge above the water. The combination of old and new is deliberate and it works: Rudy Ricciotti's lace-concrete cube is one of the best pieces of contemporary architecture in France, and the fort beside it is genuinely ancient.

Start at Fort Saint-Jean itself, which is free to enter. Walk through the fortifications and along the stone ramparts — the views back across the Vieux-Port and up to Notre-Dame de la Garde are as good as any in the city. The gardens inside the fort are quiet and largely overlooked. Take the bridge across to the MuCEM; the walk itself, twenty metres above the sea, is worth the price of admission.

The permanent collection deals with the cultures and histories of the Mediterranean world — trade, religion, agriculture, migration — and is genuinely ambitious. The temporary exhibitions tend to be strong. But the roof terrace, accessible without paying, is where most people end up: open sky, the sea in three directions, the Cathédrale de la Major's striped dome at your shoulder. Buy a coffee at the café here and stay as long as you want.

The cathedral — the [Cathédrale de la Major](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/vieillemajor) — is right next door and worth twenty minutes. The scale of it is vertiginous from inside, a vast Romanesque-Byzantine pile completed in 1893, the largest cathedral built in France in the 19th century. Almost no one is inside at most hours.

From here, walk back along the J4 pier — the broad esplanade between the MuCEM and the Vieux-Port — and into the old port for lunch. The whole route is flat, shaded in parts, and takes about three hours at a comfortable pace.
