The southern districts of Marseille — Montredon, Mazargues, Les Goudes — are where the city gradually gives way to the coast and the calanques, and where the pace drops considerably. The Parc Pastré is one of the best arguments for making the journey down.

Take bus 19 or bus 44 from the city centre to Parc Pastré. The park covers 120 hectares of Mediterranean scrub and pinède on the hillside above the sea — stone pines, wild rosemary, lizards on warm walls. Inside it sits the Château Pastré, a substantial 19th-century bastide built for one of the wealthiest merchant families in Marseille. The building now houses the [Musée de la Faïence](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/musedelafaence), which holds over 1200 pieces of ceramics spanning from the Neolithic to the present — Marseillais faïence particularly, but also regional, French, and European work. The collection is quietly excellent and almost entirely without crowds.

After the museum, walk through the park towards the sea. There are paths down through the pines to the shore at Pointe Rouge, about twenty minutes' walk. The beach at Pointe Rouge is the city's best proper sand beach — shallow water, a sailing club, a couple of restaurants. In summer, local families come here by bus with bags full of food; in autumn or spring, you may have the rocks largely to yourself.

Return to the city in the early evening for an aperitif at the Vieux-Port.
