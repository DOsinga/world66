[Cours Julien](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/coursjulien) is where Marseille's artists, musicians, students, and anyone who finds the Vieux-Port too polished all end up. It's not a single venue but a neighbourhood — a sloping square surrounded by terraces, record shops, a good bookshop (La Mauvaise Réputation), tattoo parlours, and restaurants that change their menu by the week.

Arrive around seven for the evening walk. The streets running off the cours — Rue Crudère, Rue des Trois Mages, Rue d'Aubagne — are covered in murals commissioned from street artists over the past decade. This is one of the densest concentrations of street art in France and none of it is curated; pieces overlap and fade and get replaced. Follow whatever catches your eye.

Eat early and cheaply. The cours and the nearby La Plaine market square have restaurants from Moroccan to Vietnamese to West African to French bistro; most are affordable and aimed at locals rather than tourists. The market square itself — Place Jean Jaurès, known as La Plaine — is a wide esplanade with a Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday market by day and a social scene by night. Tables spill out of bars across the paving stones.

After dinner, check what's on at [La Criée](/europe/france/midi/cotedazur/marseille/lacrie), the national theatre on the Vieux-Port — it's a fifteen-minute walk from the cours. The programme tends towards ambitious contemporary work. If theatre isn't the mood, there are live music venues scattered through the neighbourhood: Espace Julien is the main stage, or follow whatever noise is coming out of whichever bar looks most interesting. Marseille is a late city; nothing much winds down before one.
