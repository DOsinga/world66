La Vieille Charité is one of the finest baroque ensembles in southern France, built between 1671 and 1749 to house the city's poor. The architect Pierre Puget — Marseille's greatest and somewhat underappreciated son — designed the central chapel, an oval domed structure of extraordinary elegance, completely out of scale with the surrounding austere limestone arcades. That contrast is the point: civic duty combined with a touch of grandeur.

Today the complex functions as a cultural centre and museum site in the Panier neighbourhood, Marseille's oldest district above the Vieux-Port. The buildings house the Musée d'Archéologie Méditerranéenne, with strong Egyptian and pre-Columbian collections, and the Musée d'Arts Africains, Océaniens et Amérindiens. The inner courtyard, lined with three tiers of arcaded galleries, is beautifully proportioned and worth visiting in its own right.

The Panier surroundings repay an hour of wandering: steep lanes, street art, a few good small restaurants, and views down to the port.
