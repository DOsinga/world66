Carcassonne is the most complete surviving example of a medieval fortified town in Europe — 52 towers, two concentric rings of ramparts, and 3 kilometres of walls encircling a living Cité on a hill above the Aude river. The Romans were here first (Colonia Julia Carcasso), then the Visigoths, then the Cathars, then the French Crown, and each left layers in the stone. The 19th-century architect Viollet-le-Duc spent decades restoring it; some of his conical slate-roofed towers are considered over-romanticised by purists, but the overall effect is extraordinary.

The Cité is still inhabited — about 100 residents live inside the walls — though it's heavily given over to tourism. In summer the narrow streets are packed, and the cassoulet served in tourist restaurants rivals any in France for price and underperforms many for quality. Come in the morning before the coaches arrive, or in shoulder season, and it's genuinely beautiful. The Château Comtal (the inner castle), the Basilica of Saints Nazarius and Celsus, and the rampart walks are the highlights.

Below the hill, the Bastide Saint-Louis — the lower town on the left bank, planned on a grid by Louis IX in 1247 — is a more lived-in counterpart. The place Carnot is a pleasant square for a coffee, and the food and drink down here is better value than the Cité. The Aude river connects both; the Pont Vieux (old bridge) offers the classic view of the walled city from below.
