Narbonne is one of the oldest Roman towns in France — founded in 118 BC as Narbo Martius, it was once the capital of Gallia Narbonensis and a major Mediterranean port. The sea has long since retreated, but the city retains a quiet confidence that comes from knowing it has been important for two thousand years.

The Canal de la Robine, a branch of the Canal du Midi, cuts through the heart of the old town, lined with plane trees and restaurant terraces. The covered market, Les Halles, is one of the best in France: more than a hundred stalls selling fish from the nearby étangs, local charcuterie, cheeses, and mountains of seasonal produce. It opens every morning until 1pm.

The cathedral is an anomaly — a Gothic choir of extraordinary height with no nave, halted in the 14th century when the city ran out of money and political will. What remains is still striking. The archbishop's palace next door now houses the city's archaeology and art museums, including a remarkable collection of Roman artefacts.

Narbonne sits at a transport crossroads: the A61 and A9 motorways meet here, as do the main rail lines linking the Mediterranean coast to Bordeaux and Spain. The coast is only 15 km away, with the étang de Bages-Sigean and the beaches of Gruissan and Narbonne-Plage within easy reach.
