Menton sits at the very end of the French Riviera, so close to the Italian border that you can walk to [Ventimiglia](/europe/italy/liguria/ventimiglia) in twenty minutes. It is warmer and quieter than [Nice](/europe/france/midi/provence/nice), with a microclimate that allows lemon trees to grow in gardens all over town. The annual Fête du Citron in February, where enormous citrus sculptures parade through the streets, is unlike anything else on the Côte d'Azur.

The old town climbs steeply from the harbour in a cascade of ochre and terracotta facades, topped by the Basilica of Saint-Michel. The Italianate architecture reflects the town's history — Menton was part of Monaco and then Sardinia before joining France in 1860. Jean Cocteau fell in love with the place and decorated the town hall's wedding room with his murals; his dedicated museum sits on the waterfront.

Menton's two long pebble beaches face south and catch the sun all day. The promenade along the Baie de Garavan is one of the most pleasant walks on the Riviera, with views toward Italy and the mountains plunging straight into the sea behind the town.
