Southern France is one of those places that delivers on its promise. The light really is different here — sharper, more golden — and the landscape shifts dramatically within a few hours of driving: limestone gorges, lavender plateaux, vine-covered hills, Mediterranean coves. The word "Midi" simply means south, and this broad swath of France stretches from the Atlantic Basque coast east to the Italian border.

Most people rush through the interior departments — Aveyron, Lot, Tarn, Tarn-et-Garonne — making for the Côte d'Azur or the Pyrénées. That's a mistake. The villages here are genuinely unhurried, the markets sell food people actually eat, and the valleys of the Dordogne and Ardèche are among the most beautiful in Europe.

The coast has its own logic. The Riviera is glamorous and expensive and worth seeing at least once. Languedoc-Roussillon, stretching west from Marseille, has longer, sandier, and far less crowded beaches. The Basque coast around Biarritz is something else entirely — Atlantic surf, pintxos bars, and a distinct identity that feels more Spanish than French.

Getting around is easiest by car. The TGV connects Paris to Marseille, Nice, and Toulouse in three hours or less, but once you're here, the places worth seeing tend to be off the rail lines.
