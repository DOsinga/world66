Arles packs a startling amount of history into a small Provencal town. The Romans built an amphitheatre here that still hosts bullfights and summer concerts. Van Gogh arrived in 1888, painted over 200 canvases in fifteen months, and many of the streets and squares he captured can still be recognised today.

The Roman arena dominates the centre, but there is more to find. The Musee de l’Arles Antique, on the edge of town, holds an exceptional collection of sarcophagi, mosaics, and sculptures. The obelisk on the Place de la Republique and remnants of a temple near the Hotel Pinus hint at how important this city once was.

Beyond the old town, the Camargue stretches south: a vast wetland of flamingos, wild horses, and black bulls. It is superb for cycling, hiking, and horseback riding. The combination of Roman ruins, Van Gogh connections, excellent restaurants, and the Camargue on the doorstep makes Arles one of the most rewarding stops in Provence.
