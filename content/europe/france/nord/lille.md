**Lille** is the fourth-largest city in France, with a metropolitan area of about 1.2 million people stretching across the Belgian border (Lille–Kortrijk–Tournai is effectively a single bi-national conurbation). For most of its history Lille was a Flemish city — it was part of the Spanish Netherlands, then the Austrian Netherlands, before Louis XIV took it for France in 1667 — and that history is still obvious in the architecture, the food, and the language (*ch'timi*, the local dialect, is closer to Walloon than to standard French). It is also the only major French city with a strong red-brick old town that looks, at first glance, more like Bruges or Antwerp than anywhere in France.

Most visitors spend their time in **Vieux-Lille**, the restored old quarter — a compact grid of cobbled lanes, 17th-century Flemish townhouses, stepped gables and small courtyards, centred on the **Grand'Place** (officially *Place du Général de Gaulle*, who was born in Lille in 1890). The square is dominated by the magnificent **Vieille Bourse** — the Old Stock Exchange, built in 1653, a Flemish masterpiece whose interior courtyard is now a gathering place for chess players and second-hand booksellers. Around it, the streets north and west are among the most pleasant in northern France for wandering, eating, and window-shopping. The **Cathédrale Notre-Dame-de-la-Treille** has a startling modern façade of translucent marble completed in 1999. The **Citadelle**, a massive star-shaped fortress designed by Vauban in the 1670s and still occupied by the French army, sits on the edge of the old town.

The city's big surprise for first-time visitors is the **Palais des Beaux-Arts**, which after the Louvre is the largest and finest art museum in France. Its collection spans Rubens, Van Dyck, Goya, Delacroix and Courbet, plus a superb medieval sculpture gallery and two of Raphael's finest drawings. It is exceptional, and exceptionally uncrowded. Beyond the city centre, **Wazemmes** is Lille's multicultural working-class district with one of the best Sunday markets in France.

**Food** is a major reason to come. The **Welsh** (a baked rarebit), **carbonade flamande** (a sweet-sour Flemish beef and beer stew), **moules-frites**, **chicon gratin** (endive wrapped in ham and baked), and the local **maroilles** cheese are the essential dishes. The twice-yearly **Braderie de Lille** — the enormous first-weekend-of-September flea market, the largest in Europe, when the entire city centre becomes a giant street sale — is an unforgettable event if you can time a visit for it. Lille is 1 hour from Paris by TGV, 80 minutes from London by Eurostar, and 35 minutes from Brussels — one of the most useful stopovers in north-western Europe.
