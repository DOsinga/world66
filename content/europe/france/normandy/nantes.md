Nantes sits at the head of the Loire estuary in western France, historically the capital of Brittany (a designation the Bretons still contest and Nantes still claims) and now the sixth-largest city in France. It is one of those cities that has reinvented itself dramatically in a single generation: the old shipyards and industrial docks have been turned into one of the most ambitious and successful urban renewal projects in Europe, anchored by a full-sized mechanical elephant that walks the streets carrying passengers on its back.

That elephant — part of **Les Machines de l'île**, a workshop and public art project housed in the old covered shipbuilding yards on the Île de Nantes — is the thing most people have heard of, and it is genuinely extraordinary: a 12-metre-high articulated mechanical beast made of steel and wood that walks, sprays water from its trunk, and carries up to fifty passengers on a ride around the old docks. The workshop also houses a three-storey mechanical carousel of marine creatures and a growing "Tree of Herons" art installation. It is half Verne, half steampunk, and entirely worth the trip.

Beyond the machines, Nantes has a handsome 18th-century city centre built on the wealth of the Atlantic slave trade — a history that the city now addresses head-on in the **Mémorial de l'abolition de l'esclavage**, a moving underground installation on the riverbank that is one of the most important slavery memorials in Europe. The **Château des Ducs de Bretagne**, a massive moated fortress in the city centre, houses the Nantes history museum and is where the Edict of Nantes — Henri IV's 1598 decree granting religious freedom to French Protestants — was signed. The cathedral of Saints Peter and Paul, a few streets away, holds the tomb of François II, the last Duke of Brittany, carved in white marble.

Nantes has a strong food and drink scene: the local Muscadet wine is dry, mineral, and excellent with the seafood that dominates menus here. Walk the **Passage Pommeraye**, a 19th-century covered shopping arcade of ornate ironwork and carved columns, eat galettes (buckwheat crêpes) and drink cider in one of the many crêperies, and take the tramway out to the Île de Nantes for the elephant. Come in summer, when the **Le Voyage à Nantes** art trail installs temporary artworks along a green line painted on the streets that guides you through the whole city.
