Deauville is the most glamorous seaside resort of Normandy — a place where Parisian wealth has been arriving since the Second Empire to take the sea air, bet on racehorses, and promenade on the famous boardwalk. It has nicknames to match: "the 21st arrondissement of Paris," "city of the horse," "gateway to Calvados."

The grand villas lining the streets behind the beach showcase the Belle Époque and Norman-revival architecture that defines Deauville's character. Even those not open to the public are worth walking past. The Promenade des Planches — the long wooden boardwalk above the beach — is the social spine of the town, lined with restaurants and cafes and dotted with the names of film stars from the annual American Film Festival.

Beyond the beach and the casino, the surrounding countryside offers horse racing, golf, sailing, and thalassotherapy. The twin town of [Trouville-sur-Mer](/europe/france/normandybrittany/trouville) sits just across the Touques river and has a more lived-in, less precious feel.

Deauville is the most glamorous seaside resort in northern France — a favourite weekend escape for well-off Parisians since the Second Empire. The name alone conjures elegance: grand villas in Norman half-timber style, a manicured boardwalk lined with parasols, a racecourse, a casino, and a film festival that draws Hollywood stars every September.

The boardwalk — Les Planches — runs the length of the beach and is where most of the restaurants and bars cluster. Behind it, the town is surprisingly compact: a few streets of boutiques, art galleries, and patisseries. The real pleasure is the architecture. Posh villas from the late nineteenth century line the surrounding streets, many in a theatrical Norman-revival style that is unique to this stretch of coast.

Beyond the beach, the area offers horse riding, sailing, golf, and the Calvados countryside is just inland. Deauville's twin town [Trouville](/europe/france/normandybrittany/trouville), across the river Touques, has a grittier charm and better seafood.
