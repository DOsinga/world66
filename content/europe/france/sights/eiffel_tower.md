Gustave Eiffel's 330-metre iron tower was built for the 1889 World's Fair and was meant to be temporary. Parisians hated it at first -- Guy de Maupassant supposedly ate lunch in the tower's restaurant just so he would not have to look at it. It stayed, of course, and became the most recognizable structure in Europe.

There are three levels open to visitors. The first two are reachable by stairs or lift; the summit requires a lift. On a clear day the view from the top extends 70 kilometres. The first floor has a glass floor section that lets you look straight down, and a small exhibition on the tower's history. The second floor is where most people linger -- it has the best balance of height and detail.

Buy tickets online well in advance, especially for the summit. Evening visits are worthwhile: the tower sparkles with lights for five minutes every hour on the hour after dark.
