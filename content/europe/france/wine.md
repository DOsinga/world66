---
title: "Wine"
type: section
---

No country takes wine more seriously than France, and the range is staggering — from the great Bordeaux châteaux to a simple pichet of house red in a Lyonnaise bouchon. The main wine regions each have their own distinct character, grape varieties, and traditions.

**Bordeaux**

The most internationally famous French wine region, Bordeaux produces reds dominated by Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot, whites from Sauvignon Blanc and Sémillon, and the famous sweet whites of Sauternes. The classification system dates to 1855 and remains a useful (if imperfect) guide. The city of [Bordeaux](/europe/france/aquitaine/bordeaux) has an excellent wine museum, La Cité du Vin, worth visiting even for non-specialists.

**Burgundy**

Smaller in scale but arguably more revered among connoisseurs, Burgundy uses single grape varieties: Pinot Noir for reds, Chardonnay for whites. The best bottles from grands crus such as Chambertin, Montrachet, or Romanée-Conti are among the most expensive wines in the world. [Beaune](/europe/france/east/burgundy/beaune) is the hub of the trade; the famous wine auction at the Hospice de Beaune takes place every November.

**Champagne**

The only true Champagne comes from the [Champagne](/europe/france/east/champagne) region around [Reims](/europe/france/east/champagne/reims) and Épernay. The méthode champenoise — secondary fermentation in the bottle — creates the bubbles. The great houses (Moët, Veuve Clicquot, Krug) are all worth visiting for cellar tours and tastings; smaller récoltants-manipulants (grower Champagnes) are increasingly fashionable and often better value.

**Loire Valley**

The Loire produces outstanding whites from Chenin Blanc (Vouvray, Savennières), crisp Sauvignon Blancs (Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé), and elegant reds from Cabernet Franc (Chinon, Bourgueil). The region also makes excellent sparkling wines. The vineyards run alongside some of the finest châteaux in France — combining a wine tour with castle-visiting is one of the pleasures of the Loire.

**Alsace**

Unlike most of France, Alsace labels its wines by grape variety rather than appellation. Riesling, Gewürztraminer, Pinot Gris, and Muscat are the key varieties. The wines tend to be aromatic and food-friendly. The Route des Vins d'Alsace runs through a string of picturesque villages between Strasbourg and Mulhouse and is one of the most scenic wine routes in the world.

**Rhône Valley**

The northern Rhône (Hermitage, Côte-Rôtie) produces powerful Syrah reds; the southern Rhône (Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Gigondas) makes bigger blends. The Rhône also produces Viognier whites of great character. Tavel, near Avignon, makes one of France's finest rosés.

**Provence**

Provence is the largest rosé-producing region in the world. The dry, pale pink rosés (made primarily from Grenache, Cinsault, and Syrah) are perfect summer wines. The region also produces structured reds from the Bandol appellation, dominated by the Mourvèdre grape.
