Bordeaux is the world's most famous wine region, producing reds built on Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot that have set the benchmark for serious winemaking for centuries. The region divides roughly into the Left Bank (Médoc and Graves, dominated by Cabernet Sauvignon), the Right Bank (Saint-Émilion and Pomerol, where Merlot leads), and the sweet white appellations of Sauternes.

The classification system dates to 1855, and the top châteaux — Latour, Margaux, Mouton Rothschild, Haut-Brion, Pétrus — command prices that put them beyond casual tasting. But Bordeaux also has large stretches of excellent everyday wine from the broader appellations, and many smaller châteaux welcome visitors for tours and tastings at far more reasonable prices.

The city of Bordeaux itself, a short distance from the vineyards, has a superb historic centre classified as a UNESCO site, and the Cité du Vin wine museum is the most comprehensive introduction to wine culture in France. The most straightforward way to visit the wine country is to rent a car from the city and work through the Médoc's famous Route des Châteaux.
