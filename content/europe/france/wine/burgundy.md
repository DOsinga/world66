Burgundy produces some of the most celebrated wines in the world, and the landscape itself is the explanation — a patchwork of meticulously tended vineyards rolling across limestone slopes that the monks mapped by taste a thousand years ago. The concept of terroir was essentially invented here, and visiting the region is the fastest way to understand what the word actually means.

The heart of Burgundy wine is the Côte d'Or, a narrow escarpment running south from Dijon through Nuits-Saint-Georges to Beaune. The northern half (Côte de Nuits) produces the great Pinot Noirs — Gevrey-Chambertin, Vosne-Romanée, Chambolle-Musigny — while the southern half (Côte de Beaune) is where the finest Chardonnays come from, including Meursault and Puligny-Montrachet. South of the Côte d'Or, the Côte Chalonnaise offers excellent value, and the Mâconnais produces approachable whites.

Beaune is the natural base for exploring. The town is compact, walkable, and entirely devoted to wine — from the medieval Hospices de Beaune to the dozens of cellars offering tastings along the ramparts. The Route des Grands Crus threads north through the most famous villages, and most domaines welcome visitors without appointment.
