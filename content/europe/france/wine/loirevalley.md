The Loire Valley is France's third-largest wine region, stretching nearly 300 km along the Loire river from the Atlantic coast inland past [Tours](/europe/france/touraine/tours) and [Orleans](/europe/france/ile_de_france/orleans). The diversity here is remarkable — the region produces everything from bone-dry Muscadets near Nantes to the honeyed sweet wines of Vouvray and Quarts de Chaume.

The most celebrated whites are Pouilly-Fume and Sancerre, both made from Sauvignon Blanc on the eastern end of the valley. Vouvray, from Chenin Blanc grapes, ranges from dry to lusciously sweet depending on the vintage. For reds, Chinon and Bourgueil produce light, aromatic Cabernet Franc wines that are some of the most food-friendly in France.

Many producers welcome visitors without appointment, particularly around Chinon, Saumur, and Vouvray, where you can taste in caves carved into the soft tufa limestone. The landscape itself is part of the appeal — the Loire is a UNESCO World Heritage site, and the vine-covered slopes sit alongside some of France's most famous chateaux.
