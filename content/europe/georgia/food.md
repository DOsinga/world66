Georgian food is one of the great surprises of travel in the Caucasus. It draws on Persian, Turkish, and Russian influences but is unmistakably its own: walnut pastes, fresh herbs, sour plum sauces, pomegranate, and an abundance of cheese appear in combinations that are unlike anything to the west or east.

**Khachapuri** is the national dish — a flatbread filled with melted sulguni cheese, baked in various regional styles. The Adjaran version (*adjaruli khachapuri*) is boat-shaped, filled with cheese and topped with a raw egg and butter stirred in at the table. It is rich, filling, and very good.

**Khinkali** are large soup dumplings, pleated at the top. The traditional filling is spiced meat; vegetarian versions exist. The correct technique is to pick one up by the knot, bite a small hole in the side, slurp the hot broth, then eat the rest. The knot is left on the plate — how many you leave is counted. Eating khinkali without making a mess takes practice.

**Mtsvadi** (shashlik) — skewered grilled meat, usually pork or lamb — is everywhere, especially at outdoor restaurants in summer.

**Badrijani nigvzit** — slices of fried aubergine rolled around a walnut-garlic paste — is one of the best of Georgia's many cold appetisers (*pkhali*).

**Churchkhela** — walnuts strung on a thread and dipped repeatedly in grape must until they form a waxy sausage shape — are sold everywhere and make a good snack for the road.

## Wine

Georgia's claim to be the birthplace of wine is backed up by 8,000-year-old pottery residue. The traditional method — fermenting grape juice with skins and seeds in a buried clay *qvevri* — produces amber-coloured wines with tannins and complexity quite unlike conventional whites. Rkatsiteli (white) and Saperavi (red) are the main grape varieties worth knowing. Both are good.

Cheap wine from a local store will be very cheap and often very drinkable. The craft wine revival has produced some excellent small producers, especially in [Kakheti](/europe/georgia/kakheti). Restaurants in Tbilisi now have serious wine lists.

**Chacha** is the Georgian grape marc spirit, roughly equivalent to grappa. It is strong and often homemade. You will be offered it. Refusing repeatedly is acceptable.

## Eating out

Outside Tbilisi, most restaurants serve straightforward Georgian food: khachapuri, khinkali, salads, grilled meat. This is rarely a disappointment. In [Tbilisi](/europe/georgia/tbilisi) there are good options covering most cuisines.

Eating in a family home or guesthouse (*homestay*) is often the best meal you will have — abundant, generous, and accompanied by wine from the family's own vineyard.
