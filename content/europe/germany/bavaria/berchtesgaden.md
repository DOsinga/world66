**Berchtesgaden** is a market town of about 8,000 people in the far south-eastern corner of **Bavaria**, 150 km south-east of Munich and surrounded by the snow-capped peaks of the **Berchtesgaden Alps**. It sits in one of the most dramatically beautiful valleys in Germany — a cul-de-sac of the Alps almost entirely surrounded by Austrian territory, and protected since 1978 as **Berchtesgaden National Park**, the only Alpine national park in Germany. The landscape is what brings almost everyone here: the jade-green waters of **Königssee**, the 1,874-metre **Watzmann** (Germany's third-highest peak, a colossal double-summited mountain that dominates the valley), and the cable-car-accessed **Jenner** and **Kehlstein** viewpoints.

The single most-visited sight is **Königssee**, a deep, narrow alpine lake ringed by sheer cliffs, reached by electric boat from the village of Schönau at its northern end. The silent electric-only boats (motorised transport has been banned on the lake since 1909) take you to the famous red-onion-domed **St Bartholomä church** on a small peninsula halfway down the lake, and further on to the quiet **Obersee** beyond. The boat captains traditionally stop halfway and play a flugelhorn, whose echo bounces back off the cliffs — it is a cliché, and it is still wonderful. Above the town, the **salt mines** (*Salzbergwerk*) of Berchtesgaden, working since the 16th century, are open for tours on little underground trains. The **Nationalpark-Zentrum "Haus der Berge"** is an excellent free museum on the park's ecology.

For many visitors, Berchtesgaden is also inseparable from its more uncomfortable history. The mountain above the town, **Obersalzberg**, was where **Adolf Hitler** had his mountain retreat, the **Berghof**, and where much of the senior Nazi leadership built holiday homes during the 1930s and 1940s. The **Berghof** itself was destroyed by bombing in 1945 and then completely demolished by the Bavarian government in 1952 to prevent it becoming a shrine, but the mountaintop **Kehlsteinhaus** ("Eagle's Nest"), a stone pavilion built for Hitler's 50th birthday in 1939, survives and is now a restaurant — reached by a dramatic road carved into the mountain and a brass-panelled elevator inside the rock. The excellent **Dokumentation Obersalzberg** museum, opened in 1999, is an unflinching and detailed account of the history of the Nazi era here. Most visitors combine the two faces of Berchtesgaden: a morning at Königssee, an afternoon at the documentation centre, and a return to Munich in the evening.
