Furth lives in the shadow of its bigger neighbour [Nuremberg](/europe/germany/bavaria/nuremberg), just seven kilometres to the east, but has its own distinct character. The two cities have grown together physically — the U-Bahn connects them in minutes — yet Furth feels quieter, more residential, and proudly independent.

The city's main claim to fame is transport history: Germany's first railway, the Adler, ran from Nuremberg to Furth in 1835. The station building from that era is gone, but the railway museum in Nuremberg covers the story well. Furth also has an unusually well-preserved collection of Wilhelmine and Art Nouveau architecture — the Hornschuchpromenade and Konigstrasse are particularly handsome streets, their grand facades a reminder of the city's prosperous industrial past.

Furth had one of Bavaria's largest Jewish communities before the war, and the old Jewish cemetery and several former synagogue sites are worth visiting. Henry Kissinger was born here in 1923, and Ludwig Erhard, architect of Germany's post-war economic miracle, grew up in the city. For visitors staying in Nuremberg, Furth makes an easy half-day trip with a character all its own.
