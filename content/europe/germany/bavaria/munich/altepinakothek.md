One of the oldest and most important art galleries in the world, the Alte Pinakothek holds Munich's collection of Old Masters from the 14th to the 18th century. The building itself, designed by Leo von Klenze and opened in 1836, set the standard for museum architecture across Europe.

The collection is particularly strong in German and Flemish painting. Albrecht Durer's self-portrait from 1500 is the star of the German rooms, while the Rubens collection -- over 70 works -- is one of the largest anywhere. There are also major works by Raphael, Leonardo, Titian, and Rembrandt spread across the upper galleries.

Allow at least two hours. The building suffered heavy damage in World War II and was rebuilt in a simpler style; the patched brickwork on the exterior was left deliberately visible as a reminder. The museum sits in the Kunstareal district alongside the Neue Pinakothek and Pinakothek der Moderne, making it easy to visit all three in a day.
