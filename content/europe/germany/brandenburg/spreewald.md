The Spreewald is a vast network of canals, meadows, and oak forests south of Berlin — a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve that feels like a different world entirely. The Spree river fans out here into around 300 waterways, and for centuries the local Sorbian people navigated them by punt rather than by road. That tradition is still very much alive, and a boat ride through the channels remains the best thing you can do here.

The two main gateway towns are [Lübben](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%BCbben) and [Lübbenau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%BCbbenau), about 13 km apart. Lübbenau is the more touristy of the two, with boat departure points right by the market square. Lübben is quieter and a better base if you want to escape the summer crowds. Between the two towns, the village of Lehde — accessible only by boat or footpath — preserves old Sorbian farmsteads and a way of life that largely vanished elsewhere.

The Spreewald is also a working landscape. The dark, rich soil produces what are arguably Germany's finest cucumbers — Spreewälder Gurken, pickled in dill, are sold everywhere and are genuinely worth bringing home. The Sorbs, a Slavic minority who have lived here for over a thousand years, maintain their own language and traditions, particularly in the villages of the Unterspreewald around Cottbus.

Hiking and cycling are both excellent. The Wald is laced with trails, and a bike lets you explore channels and clearings that the boat tours skip. The 13 km walk or cycle from Lübben to Lübbenau along the Hauptspree is a good introduction — flat, well-marked, and rewarding.
