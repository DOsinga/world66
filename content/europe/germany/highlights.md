---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Germany doesn't have one obvious capital of tourism — it has a dozen cities and regions each with a compelling case for your time. Here are some of the standouts.

**[Berlin](/europe/germany/berlin)** is the obvious starting point. No other European capital has reinvented itself so dramatically in living memory. The Wall came down in 1989 and the city has been building, arguing, and creating ever since. Museum Island, the Reichstag, the memorials, the Stasi Museum, the club scene, the food — Berlin rewards a week or more.

**[Munich](/europe/germany/bavaria/munich)** is the counterpoint: prosperous, orderly, and beautiful, with world-class museums, the Englischer Garten, and some of the best beer halls in the world. It's the gateway to Bavaria and the Alps.

**The Bavarian Alps** — Garmisch-Partenkirchen, Berchtesgaden, the Zugspitze — offer serious mountain scenery and excellent hiking and skiing. Neuschwanstein Castle, Ludwig II's fairy-tale creation near Füssen, is every bit as dramatic as the photographs suggest.

**The Rhine Valley** between Koblenz and Bingen is one of Germany's most scenically concentrated stretches: vine-covered slopes, medieval castles on every promontory, and small wine towns like Bacharach and Rüdesheim. Do it by boat or cycle rather than car.

**[Dresden](/europe/germany/saxony/dresden)** was comprehensively bombed in 1945 and painstakingly rebuilt. The Frauenkirche, the Zwinger palace, and the Semper Opera are all back. It's a handsome city with excellent museums and a good base for the Elbe Sandstone Mountains (Sächsische Schweiz).

**[Hamburg](/europe/germany/hamburg)** is Germany's great port city — confident, international, and underrated by most visitors who fly to Berlin or Munich. The Elbe Philharmonic Hall (Elbphilharmonie) is as remarkable inside as out. The harbour, the Speicherstadt warehouse district, the fish market at 5am on Sunday.

**[Cologne](/europe/germany/northrhinewestphalia/cologne)** has the cathedral and not much else that's medieval — the rest was bombed flat — but it's a lively, likeable Rhineland city with excellent museums, a proper pub culture, and its own distinctive beer (Kölsch, served in small glasses, continuously, until you put your beer mat on top).

**The Romantic Road** is a tourist route through Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg connecting Würzburg to Füssen — medieval walled towns like Rothenburg ob der Tauber and Dinkelsbühl, Baroque monasteries, and Alpine foothills. It can be too packaged, but the individual towns are genuinely beautiful.

**[Weimar](/europe/germany/thuringia/weimar)** is a small city with a disproportionate history: Goethe, Schiller, the Bauhaus school, and then the Weimar Republic that gave Germany's ill-fated first democracy its name. Buchenwald concentration camp is nearby and impossible to ignore.

**The Black Forest** in [Baden-Württemberg](/europe/germany/badenwrttemberg) is best explored slowly: spa towns like Baden-Baden, cuckoo-clock villages, hiking trails through dense spruce forest, and a cuisine (Black Forest cake, cold cuts, Flammkuchen) very different from Bavarian or northern German food.
