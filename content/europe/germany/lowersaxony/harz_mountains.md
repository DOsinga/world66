The Harz Mountains are the highest range in northern Germany, rising abruptly from the North German Plain in a dark mass of spruce forest and granite. The Brocken, at 1,141 metres, is the highest peak and the most visited — on a clear day the views stretch across half of northern Germany. This is the mountain where Goethe set the witches' gathering in Faust, and the place still has a slightly wild, folkloric atmosphere.

The range straddles the former border between East and West Germany, and that history is visible everywhere. The Brocken was a restricted military zone during the Cold War, bristling with Soviet listening stations. Today you can ride the Brockenbahn, a narrow-gauge steam railway, to the summit — one of the most enjoyable train journeys in Germany.

The old mining towns scattered through the mountains are worth exploring in their own right. [Goslar](/europe/germany/lowersaxony/goslar), with its medieval centre and Romanesque imperial palace, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Wernigerode has a fairy-tale hilltop castle and rows of half-timbered houses. Quedlinburg, on the northern edge, is one of the best-preserved medieval towns in Europe.

In winter, the Harz is popular for cross-country skiing and snowshoeing, though the downhill skiing is modest by Alpine standards. In summer, the hiking is excellent — hundreds of kilometres of marked trails wind through forests, past waterfalls, and along former mining paths. The Harzer Hexenstieg, a long-distance trail running east to west across the entire range, takes about five days and is one of Germany's finest walks.
