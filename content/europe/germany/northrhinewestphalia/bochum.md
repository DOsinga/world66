Bochum is a Ruhr city that ran on coal for over a century, then had to figure out what to do when the mines closed. The last shaft went dark in the 1970s, the steel works followed, and Bochum spent the 1980s and 1990s rebuilding itself around universities, culture, and a surprisingly vibrant music and theater scene. It has not become glamorous — this is still a working-class city with a blunt civic pride — but it is livelier and more interesting than its industrial reputation suggests.

The German Mining Museum, the largest of its kind in the world, is the city's standout attraction. You descend into a real underground demonstration mine and come up with a genuine sense of what it meant to work here. Above ground, the Schauspielhaus Bochum is one of Germany's most respected repertory theaters, drawing productions that regularly travel to Berlin and Hamburg.

The Bermuda3Eck, the triangle of streets around Viktoriastrasse and Kortumstrasse, is where students and locals drink, eat, and go out. It is unpretentious and gets noisy on weekends. The Starlight Express musical has been running in a purpose-built arena on the edge of the city since 1988 — a curiosity worth knowing about if you have children or a tolerance for roller-skating spectacle.

Bochum sits at the heart of the Ruhr conurbation. [Essen](/europe/germany/northrhinewestphalia/essen) is 20 minutes west by S-Bahn, [Dortmund](/europe/germany/northrhinewestphalia/dortmund) 20 minutes east. The whole region is easy to explore by train.
