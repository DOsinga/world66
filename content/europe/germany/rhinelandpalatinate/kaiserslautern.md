Kaiserslautern is a mid-sized city in the Palatinate, set on the northern edge of the Pfälzerwald — Germany's largest contiguous forest. It takes its name from the imperial hunting lodge Emperor Frederick Barbarossa built here in the twelfth century, the ruins of which still sit in the city centre. Locals just call it K-Town, a nickname picked up from the unusually large American population: the nearby Ramstein Air Base and the surrounding installations make this the biggest US military community outside the United States.

The city itself is workmanlike rather than picturesque. It was heavily bombed in the Second World War and rebuilt in pragmatic post-war German style, with the original Barbarossa ruin and the gothic Stiftskirche the main reminders of the older town. The football stadium on the Betzenberg, home to 1. FC Kaiserslautern, is a more modern landmark — the club's loyal following gives match days an atmosphere the rest of the week can't quite match.

The real reason to come is the forest. The Pfälzerwald begins on the city's southern edge: a vast, gently hilly landscape of beech and pine, dotted with sandstone cliffs, ruined castles and small wine villages. Trails head out from the city in every direction, and in autumn the local restaurants take advantage of the season with game, mushrooms and the new wine from the nearby Deutsche Weinstraße.

Kaiserslautern is a mid-sized city in the Palatinate, ringed by the dark woods of the Pfälzerwald — Germany's largest contiguous forest. The city takes its name from Emperor Frederick Barbarossa, who built a hunting lodge here in the 12th century; the ruins of his castle still sit in the town centre, sunk below modern street level and hemmed in by the Rathaus.

Two things shape the place today. The first is football: 1. FC Kaiserslautern is one of the storied names of German football, and on match days the red scarves of the Fritz-Walter-Stadion are everywhere. The second is the United States military. Ramstein Air Base and the surrounding installations form the largest American community outside the US, and the Kaiserslautern Military Community has been part of local life since the 1950s — visible in the diners, the gyms, and the bilingual shop signs.

Beyond that, it is a working German town with a pleasant pedestrian centre, a few good museums (the Pfalzgalerie has a respectable collection of 19th and 20th century German painting), and easy access to the Palatinate wine route and the forest. Most travellers come here as a base for hiking the Pfälzerwald or as a stop on the way to the wine villages of the Deutsche Weinstraße to the south.
