The Frisian Islands are an archipelago stretching in a crescent along the coasts of the Netherlands, Germany, and Denmark. Though divided by national borders, they form a single ecological entity — a chain of low, sandy barrier islands sheltering the Wadden Sea, one of the world's most important tidal wetlands and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

In the west, the Dutch [Wadden Islands](/europe/netherlands/waddenislands) are popular holiday destinations. Island hopping by ferry is straightforward, and cycling is the main way to get around — on Vlieland and Schiermonnikoog, cars are banned for visitors. Along the German coast, the [East Frisian Islands](/europe/germany/lowersaxony/east_frisian_islan) belong to the Lower Saxony Wadden Sea National Park, while the [North Frisian Islands](/europe/germany/schleswigholstein/north_frisian_isla) further north fall within the Schleswig-Holstein Wadden Sea National Park. In Denmark, the chain continues through the [Vadehavet](/europe/denmark/vadehavet) Nature Reserve.

The islands share a flat, wind-scoured character — wide beaches on the North Sea side, mudflats and marshes on the Wadden Sea side, dunes in between. Birdwatching, mudflat walking (wadlopen), cycling, and simply sitting on an empty beach in a Strandkorb are the main activities.
