Sylt is the largest of the North Frisian Islands and one of the most celebrated holiday destinations in Germany. A narrow sliver of land stretching 40 kilometres from north to south, it is connected to the mainland only by the Hindenburgdamm railway causeway — cars ride the train across.

What the Hamptons are to New York, Sylt is to Hamburg and Berlin. The island draws a well-heeled crowd, particularly to Kampen on the northern end, where thatched-roof houses hide behind tall hedges and the restaurants charge accordingly. But Sylt is more than its reputation. The western coast is one long, wild beach backed by dramatic dunes, and the raw North Sea wind makes it a paradise for surfers and kite surfers. Westerland, the main town, has a lively boardwalk and the island's best infrastructure. List, at the very northern tip, is Germany's northernmost settlement and home to excellent oyster beds.

The eastern side, sheltered from the open sea, faces the Wadden Sea mudflats — a completely different landscape of tidal pools, salt marshes, and enormous flocks of migratory birds. The contrast between the wild west coast and the calm east is one of the things that makes Sylt special.
