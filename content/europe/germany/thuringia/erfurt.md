Of all Germany's large cities, Erfurt is the one most redolent of prewar Germany. Although it lost a couple of important monuments in bombing raids, it was otherwise little damaged in World War II — and its streets of grand turn-of-the-century shopfronts were saved from postwar developers by the fact that the city ended up in East Germany.

The city's most photographed sight is the **Krämerbrücke**, a medieval bridge lined with half-timbered houses that have been continuously inhabited for centuries — one of only a handful of such bridges still standing anywhere in Europe. At the top of the old town, the **Dom St. Marien** and the **Severikirche** rise side by side from a broad flight of steps, a dramatic ensemble that dominates the skyline. Between them and the Krämerbrücke stretches a network of lanes and squares that reward wandering without a map.

Erfurt was also where Martin Luther studied and was ordained as a monk, and the Augustinian monastery where he lived is now a museum and hotel. The city has worn its history lightly — it feels lived-in and unpretentious, with a busy market square and a university that keeps it young.
