Jena occupies a dramatic position in the valley of the Saale river, where limestone cliffs and wooded hillsides press close on both sides of the water. The city has the feel of a place shaped by ideas as much as by industry, and this is historically accurate: the Friedrich Schiller University, founded in 1558, attracted a concentration of philosophical and literary talent in the 1790s and 1800s that remains almost unparalleled in German cultural history. Schiller himself lived and wrote here, Goethe visited frequently from nearby [Weimar](/europe/germany/thuringia/weimar), Hegel wrote the Phenomenology of Spirit in the city, and Fichte and Schlegel held chairs here simultaneously.

The Carl Zeiss optical works, founded here in 1846, gave the city a second identity as a center of precision optics and scientific instrumentation. The Zeiss Planetarium, built in 1926, was the world's first public planetarium and is still operational — it remains one of the best places in Germany to see a serious astronomical program. The Botanischer Garten, attached to the university, is one of the oldest university botanical gardens in Germany. The Paradies park along the Saale provides the city's main green lung, and the river walks are excellent in spring when the limestone cliffs turn white with the blooms of cherry trees planted on them. The surrounding hills are threaded with hiking trails, and the Jenzig — the steep hill above the eastern bank — provides a view over the valley and city that was extensively painted by Romantic artists.

The student population makes Jena feel younger and more animated than its Thuringian neighbors, and the city center around the Marktplatz has a lively bar and café culture proportionally much larger than you'd expect from a city of its size. The Schiller Memorial and the old university buildings anchor the central pedestrian area. Weimar is thirty kilometers west, making the two cities a natural double visit for anyone following the Thuringian cultural route.
