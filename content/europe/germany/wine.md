title: "Wine"
type: section
---

Germany is one of Europe's great wine countries, though it often gets overlooked in favour of France and Italy. The focus is predominantly on white wine, and the king grape is Riesling — grown along the steep slate slopes of the Mosel, Rheingau, and Nahe, it produces wines of extraordinary finesse, from fiercely dry to opulently sweet.

**Mosel** — The river valley between Trier and Koblenz is Germany's most dramatic wine landscape, with vertiginous slate terraces dropping to the river. The wines are light, aromatic, and full of minerality. [Trier](/europe/germany/rhinelandpalatinate/trier) is the best base for exploring the region; smaller villages like Bernkastel-Kues, Traben-Trarbach, and Piesport are worth the detour.

**Rheingau** — A compact region east of the Rhine near Wiesbaden, producing some of Germany's most structured Rieslings. Johannisberg and Rüdesheim are the main villages.

**Nahe** — Less visited than Mosel or Rheingau, the Nahe produces elegant, complex Rieslings at often better prices.

**Baden** — Germany's warmest wine region, straddling the Upper Rhine near Freiburg. Spätburgunder (Pinot Noir) here is among the country's best red wine.

**Franconia (Franken)** — The wine region around Würzburg, known for dry, earthy whites in distinctive flat-bottomed Bocksbeutel bottles. Worth trying with the local food.

The Ahr valley, north of Koblenz, specialises in Spätburgunder and has produced some impressive reds in recent years.

Cycling between wine villages along the Mosel or Rhine is one of Germany's better slow-travel experiences.
