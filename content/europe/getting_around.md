---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Europe's rail network is one of the best reasons to visit. High-speed trains connect major cities at speeds that make flying feel pointless once you factor in airport hassle — the TGV links [Paris](/europe/france/paris) to Lyon in under two hours, Germany's ICE runs a web across the country, and Spain's AVE whisks you from Madrid to Barcelona in 2.5 hours. Night trains have made a serious comeback, with services like the Nightjet connecting [Austria](/europe/austria), [Germany](/europe/germany), and [Italy](/europe/italy). If you're covering a lot of ground, a Eurail pass can save money, but do the math first — point-to-point tickets booked early are often cheaper for a fixed itinerary.

Budget airlines transformed European travel and they remain the cheapest way to cover long distances. Ryanair, easyJet, and Wizz Air connect hundreds of cities, often for absurdly low fares if you book early and pack light. The catch is always the same: secondary airports far from city centers, brutal baggage fees, and seats designed for people without legs. Check the total cost including bags and transfers before you celebrate that nine-euro fare.

FlixBus has quietly become the backbone of budget overland travel, with bright green coaches running routes that trains don't cover at prices trains can't match. They're comfortable enough for daytime journeys and most have Wi-Fi and power outlets. For longer hauls, the bus is slower but dramatically cheaper — [Prague](/europe/czechrepublic/prague) to [Berlin](/europe/germany/berlin) runs about five hours and costs a fraction of the train.

Driving gives you the most freedom, especially in rural areas where public transport thins out. Western European motorways are excellent, though tolls in [France](/europe/france) and [Italy](/europe/italy) add up fast. Eastern Europe's road quality has improved enormously thanks to EU funding, but secondary roads can still surprise you. The Schengen Agreement means you'll barely notice most border crossings — just drive through. Renting a car is straightforward, though city parking will test your patience and your wallet.

Ferries remain essential for reaching the islands and connecting Scandinavia. Regular services run from [Italy](/europe/italy) and [Spain](/europe/spain) to Mediterranean islands, from [Greece](/europe/greece) to its archipelago, and across the Baltic between [Sweden](/europe/sweden), [Finland](/europe/finland), and the Baltics. The Scandinavian ferries are destinations in themselves — floating hotels with restaurants, saunas, and duty-free shops. In the Mediterranean, ferry networks are extensive in summer but thin out dramatically in winter.
