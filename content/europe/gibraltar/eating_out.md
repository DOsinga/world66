---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Gibraltar's food scene reflects the territory's layered history. British pub fare, Spanish tapas, Moroccan spices, Genoese pasta dishes, and Sephardic Jewish cooking all leave their mark. This is not a destination for haute cuisine, but it's a more interesting place to eat than its small size and British-territory status might suggest.

The local dish to seek out is calentita — a dense, oven-baked chickpea flour cake, similar to Ligurian farinata or Socca from Nice. It's served hot, cut into squares, and eaten as a snack. You'll find it at the Calentita festival in June and in a handful of places that serve traditional food year-round. Panissa is the fried version, similar to chickpea polenta. Both are deeply unglamorous and genuinely delicious.

The main eating areas are Casemates Square (lively, touristy, good range of options), Queensway Quay marina (more upscale, good for seafood), and Main Street (everything from fast food to old-school bistros). Ocean Village marina has a cluster of restaurants and bars popular with locals in the evening.

**House of Sacarello** on Irish Town has been a Gibraltar institution for decades — a bistro-style place serving good coffee, soups, sandwiches, and light meals. Unpretentious and reliable.

**Monique's** at Queensway Quay is the place for Iberian cuisine with a focus on fresh seafood. More expensive than average but consistently rated among the best in Gibraltar.

Fast food chains are present (Gibraltar has a McDonald's and a Pizza Hut), but you can do much better without spending much more. The pubs around Casemates Square generally serve decent British-style food.

Prices are broadly comparable to the UK, with the advantage of no VAT on alcohol — a pint in a pub costs noticeably less than in most English cities.
