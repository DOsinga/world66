Amorgos is the easternmost island of the Cyclades, a long narrow ridge of rock and scrub rising steeply from the Aegean. It is quieter and less developed than the big-name Greek islands, and it has stayed that way partly because it takes a while to get here — ferries from Naxos or Piraeus are not quick.

The island became famous as the setting for Luc Besson's *Le Grand Bleu* (The Big Blue), and the deep blue water that attracted the filmmaker is still the main draw. The monastery of Panagia Hozoviotissa, a white slash of a building clinging to a 300-metre cliff above the sea, is one of the most dramatic sights in the Cyclades. Inside it is tiny — just a few dark, incense-scented rooms — but the setting is unforgettable.

Chora, the main village, is a classic Cycladic hilltop settlement: whitewashed houses, winding lanes, a handful of tavernas where you eat on terraces overlooking the sea. The south coast has the best swimming at Mouros and Agia Anna beaches. Hiking trails connect the villages across the island's spine, and the walking is excellent if you can handle the heat.
