Corfu (Kerkyra) is the northernmost of the Ionian Islands, lying in the Adriatic off the coast of Albania. Its position at the entrance to the Adriatic made it strategically important for centuries: the Venetians held it for four hundred years (1386–1797), leaving a deep imprint on the architecture of Corfu Town, the language, and the food. After Venice came the French and then the British (1814–1864), who built roads, introduced cricket, and planted the esplanade. Greece took possession in 1864.

Corfu Town is one of the finest Venetian-era towns in the Mediterranean, with two fortresses, a French-built arcaded esplanade, and a dense old town of narrow Venetian lanes. Beyond the town, the island is lushly green — unusually so for Greece — with olive groves, cypress trees, and low hills. The west and north coasts have the best beaches. Day trips to the Albanian site of Butrint and the small island of Paxos are popular. The island receives very large numbers of British tourists in summer.
