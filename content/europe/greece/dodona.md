Dodona was the oldest oracle in Greece, older even than Delphi. Tucked into a green valley in Epirus, surrounded by the mountains of northwestern Greece, it was sacred to Zeus, and priests interpreted the god's will by listening to the rustling of a sacred oak tree. Pilgrims came from across the Greek world to ask questions, scratching them onto small lead tablets. Thousands of these tablets have been found, and reading them is surprisingly moving: people asked about lost property, business decisions, whether to marry, whether their children were really theirs.

The site's centerpiece is a large and well-preserved theater from the third century BC, one of the biggest in Greece, seating around 18,000 people. It was heavily restored in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries and is used again today for summer performances. Behind the theater lie the remains of the sanctuary itself, including the foundations of the temple of Zeus and the bouleuterion. The sacred oak is long gone, though a replacement has been planted.

Dodona sits about 22 kilometers southwest of [Ioannina](/europe/greece/ioannina), the capital of Epirus, and is most easily visited by car or taxi from there. Public transport is limited. The setting is beautiful and quiet, a strong contrast to the busy archaeological sites of southern Greece. You are likely to have the place largely to yourself.
