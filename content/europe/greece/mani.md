The Mani peninsula is Greece at its most untamed. Jutting south from the Peloponnese into the Mediterranean, this rugged finger of land spent centuries as a place apart — settled by descendants of Sparta, never fully conquered by the Ottomans, and connected to the rest of Greece by road only in the 1960s. Stone tower houses rise from bare hillsides, Byzantine chapels hide in olive groves, and the beaches are among the best in southern Greece.

The region divides into two distinct halves. Exo Mani (Outer Mani), stretching south from [Kalamata](/europe/greece/kalamata), has the most tourist facilities and the finest beaches. The coastal road winds past [Kardamili](/europe/greece/mani/kardamili) and [Stoupa](/europe/greece/mani/stoupa), both excellent bases with good swimming and a growing number of tavernas. Mesa Mani (Inner Mani), further south, is starker and wilder — an arid landscape of fortified villages, blood-feud tower houses, and a fierce local pride that predates the modern Greek state.

Areopoli is the gateway to Inner Mani and the place where the Greek War of Independence began in 1821. The Maniots celebrate independence day earlier than the rest of Greece — "because we celebrate when we started fighting." Just south of Areopoli, the Diros Caves are a world-class attraction: an underground river system you explore by boat through caverns of stalactites and stalagmites.

[Kalamata](/europe/greece/kalamata) is the usual starting point, with its airport and bus connections. From there, the mountain road into the Mani offers spectacular views as it drops toward the coast. Give yourself at least three or four days — the pace here is slow, and that is very much the point.
