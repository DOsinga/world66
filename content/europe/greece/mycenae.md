Mycenae was the most powerful city in the Aegean world for five centuries — from around 1600 BC until its sudden collapse around 1100 BC. The citadel that survives today, perched on a rocky hill between two mountains in the northeastern Peloponnese, gave its name to an entire civilisation. It is one of the most important archaeological sites in Europe and a genuine thrill to visit, even for travelers who are not especially interested in ancient history.

What makes Mycenae different from other Greek sites is the sheer physicality of it. The walls, built from stones so massive that the Greeks later called them Cyclopean — only a race of giants could have moved them — still stand to considerable height. The Lion Gate at the main entrance, carved around 1250 BC, is the oldest monumental sculpture in Europe still standing in its original position. You can walk through it and into the citadel exactly as people did 3,200 years ago.

The site is most closely associated with King Agamemnon, commander of the Greek forces at Troy in Homer's Iliad. Whether Agamemnon was real is debated, but the royal shaft graves Heinrich Schliemann excavated in 1876 — finding gold masks, weapons, and jewelry of extraordinary richness — proved that a powerful royal dynasty existed here. The most famous find, the gold "Mask of Agamemnon," is now in the National Archaeological Museum in Athens.

The surrounding area is the ancient region of Argolis, and the nearby town of [Nafplio](/europe/greece/nauplia) makes the obvious base. You can combine Mycenae with [Epidaurus](/europe/greece/epidaurus) and [Tiryns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiryns) in a single day trip from there.
