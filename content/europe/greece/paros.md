Paros is one of the Cyclades islands that manages to sit in a sweet spot: popular enough to have good ferry connections and decent tavernas, but not so overrun as [Mykonos](/europe/greece/mykonos) or [Santorini](/europe/greece/santorini). The island has fine beaches, a handful of genuinely interesting villages, and enough character to hold your attention for several days.

The main town, Parikia, is where all ferries dock. The waterfront strip of hotels and ticket offices is not the prettiest introduction, but push past it into the old town and the atmosphere changes entirely — whitewashed alleys, bougainvillea, and the Church of Ekatontapiliani, one of the most important early Christian churches in the Aegean. The adjacent archaeological museum is worth a visit for the fragment of the Parian Chronicle, a marble tablet recording events in Greek history from mythical times to the third century BC.

On the north coast, Naoussa has grown from a fishing village into a lively little resort without entirely losing its charm. A ruined Venetian castle guards the harbour entrance, and the waterfront fills with tables in the evening. Lefkes, in the hilly interior, is the most atmospheric of the mountain villages — a maze of marble-paved lanes that feels genuinely remote despite being twenty minutes from the coast.

The main reason many visitors choose Paros is the beaches and the wind. The island is one of the top windsurfing destinations in Europe, with the Professional Windsurfers Association holding events at Golden Beach (Chrysi Akti) on the southeast coast. If you prefer calmer water, Kolymbithres on the north side has smooth granite boulders that create sheltered coves. Scuba diving and horse riding are also available.

From Paros you can easily hop to neighbouring [Naxos](/europe/greece/naxos) — the ferry takes about thirty minutes — or use the island as a base for exploring the smaller Cyclades: Antiparos is just a ten-minute boat ride away.
