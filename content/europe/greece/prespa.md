*Europe > Greece > Prespa*

The Prespa Lakes sit in the far north-west corner of Greece at 850 metres, where the borders of Greece, Albania and North Macedonia converge. There are two lakes: the larger Macro Prespa (259 km²) is shared by all three countries; the smaller Micro Prespa is almost entirely Greek. Both are protected as a transboundary national park.

This is one of the most important wetlands in the Balkans. The lakes support breeding colonies of Dalmatian and White Pelicans alongside pygmy cormorants and some 260 bird species in total. The surrounding mountains are among the last places in Europe where brown bears, wolves, and chamois share the same landscape. Seventeen fish species live in the lakes, several found nowhere else.

The human history goes back to Byzantine times. Churches, hermitages, monasteries, and rock paintings from the 10th century onwards are scattered among the fifteen villages of the municipality. The island of Agios Achillios in Micro Prespa — reached by a long wooden pontoon bridge — holds the ruins of a 10th-century basilica and hosts the annual Prespia Festival each August. The villages are built in traditional stone; most have only a few dozen inhabitants.

This is not a destination for people who want infrastructure. There is no public transport between villages, though buses connect to Florina (50 km away). A car is essential. Come for the birds, the hiking, the Byzantine ruins, and the genuine remoteness — a corner of Greece that feels nothing like the islands.
