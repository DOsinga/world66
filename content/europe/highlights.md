title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Europe's highlights are so numerous and so well-known that the challenge isn't finding them — it's narrowing the list. Here are the experiences that justify the trip, regardless of where you start.

**The great cities.** [Paris](/europe/france/paris), [Rome](/europe/italy/lazio/rome), [Barcelona](/europe/spain/barcelona), [Amsterdam](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam), [Prague](/europe/czechrepublic/prague), [Vienna](/europe/austria/vienna), [Lisbon](/europe/portugal/lisbon) — each of these cities is a world unto itself. Don't try to see them all in one trip. Pick one or two, stay long enough to walk beyond the monuments, eat where locals eat, and sit in a square with nowhere to be. European cities reward slow travel more than any checklist approach.

**The Mediterranean coast and islands.** [Greece](/europe/greece)'s Aegean islands range from the party scene of Mykonos to forgotten fishing villages where you'll be the only non-Greek in the taverna. Croatia's Dalmatian coast — Dubrovnik, Hvar, Korčula — combines medieval architecture with water so clear it seems impossible. The Amalfi Coast, Cinque Terre, the French Riviera, the Spanish costas — the Mediterranean has endless variations on the theme of warm sea, good food, and long evenings. Go in May or September and you'll have it largely to yourself.

**The Alps.** The mountain range that runs from [France](/europe/france) through [Switzerland](/europe/switzerland), [Austria](/europe/austria), and into [Italy](/europe/italy) and [Slovenia](/europe/slovenia) is one of the most dramatic landscapes in the world. In winter, it's the best skiing in Europe. In summer, hiking trails connect high mountain huts where you can walk for days without carrying a tent. Chamonix, Zermatt, and Innsbruck are the classic bases, but the quieter Austrian and Italian valleys give you the same scenery without the crowds.

**Eastern Europe's old towns.** [Prague](/europe/czechrepublic/prague) survived WWII intact and shows it — a medieval city center that feels almost too perfect. Tallinn in [Estonia](/europe/estonia) has a remarkably preserved old town that most visitors still miss. Kraków in [Poland](/europe/poland) has a market square that ranks among the most beautiful in Europe. Plovdiv in [Bulgaria](/europe/bulgaria), Sibiu in [Romania](/europe/romania), Mostar in Bosnia — the further east you go, the fewer crowds and the lower prices. These cities are the most underrated travel destinations in Europe.

**Iceland.** [Iceland](/europe/iceland) doesn't fit neatly into any European category. It's a volcanic island where you can watch lava fields from the road, swim in geothermal pools beneath the open sky, see the northern lights in winter and the midnight sun in summer, and hike across landscapes that look like another planet. Reykjavik is small and surprisingly lively. The Ring Road allows you to circle the entire country. Expensive, yes — but unlike anywhere else.

**The Camino de Santiago.** The network of pilgrimage routes crossing northern [Spain](/europe/spain) — and increasingly other parts of Europe — has become one of the most popular long-distance walks in the world. The most traveled route, the Camino Francés, runs 780km from the French Pyrenees to Santiago de Compostela. You don't need to be religious to walk it; most people aren't. The rhythm of walking all day, eating and sleeping simply, and sharing the road with fellow pilgrims is what draws people back repeatedly.

**Scandinavia in summer.** The midnight sun is real and genuinely strange — the sun sitting above the horizon at midnight turns your sense of time upside down. Norway's fjords are deeply impressive even when you've seen them in a hundred photographs. The Swedish archipelago east of Stockholm, Denmark's cycling culture, Finland's lakes — northern Europe in June and July is luminous and worth every extra euro.

**The history.** Rome's Colosseum, the Parthenon in Athens, Versailles, Pompeii, Auschwitz, the D-Day beaches of Normandy — Europe's history spans the full range of human achievement and atrocity, and the physical record of it is remarkably well preserved. The continent doesn't let you forget where you are in time.
