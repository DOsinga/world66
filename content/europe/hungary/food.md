Hungarian cooking is hearty, paprika-driven, and built for cold winters. Pork is the dominant meat; sour cream (tejföl) appears in and on almost everything. The cuisine takes some adjusting if you're used to lighter food, but it's deeply satisfying.

**Goulash (gulyás)** is the national dish — a soup of beef, potato, onion, and paprika that bears little resemblance to the thick stew served in Western Europe under the same name. In Hungary it's a broth, eaten with bread. The richer, stewlike version is pörkölt.

**Chicken paprikash (paprikás csirke)** is chicken simmered in a paprika cream sauce, typically served with egg dumplings (nokedli or galuska). One of the most comforting dishes in the repertoire.

**Halászlé** (fisherman's soup) is a fiery red broth made from freshwater fish — carp, catfish, pike — heavily seasoned with hot paprika. The version from Szeged and the one from Baja are rivals; both are excellent and both are aggressively spicy.

**Lángos** is street food: deep-fried dough, hot and chewy, typically topped with garlic oil, sour cream, and grated cheese. Get it at a market or at Lake Balaton — it's a summer institution.

**Töltött káposzta** (stuffed cabbage) is minced pork and rice wrapped in soured cabbage leaves, stewed slowly and served with sour cream. Classic winter food.

For dessert, look for **somlói galuska** (sponge cake with rum, walnuts, and chocolate cream) or **rétes** (strudel), which Hungarians make with many fillings including cherry, poppy seed, and cottage cheese.

## Drink

Hungary has underrated wine. Tokaj in the northeast produces Aszú, a luscious dessert wine made from botrytised grapes — it really was the Wine of Kings. But the country also makes good dry whites in the Tokaj, Eger, Badacsony (Balaton), and Villány regions, and Egri Bikavér (Bull's Blood) is a red worth knowing.

**Pálinka** is the national spirit — fruit brandy, legally required to be made from Hungarian fruit. Plum (szilva) and apricot (barack) are the most common varieties. It ranges from rough to excellent; the best pálinka is as fine as a good Cognac. Refusing a glass when offered in someone's home is a social misstep.

**Unicum** is the Hungarian herbal liqueur — bitter, medicinal, and very much an acquired taste. It's been made by the Zwack family for over 200 years.

Eating out is excellent value by Western European standards. A sit-down lunch with wine in a good Budapest restaurant typically costs 4,000–7,000 HUF per person; outside the capital, even less.
