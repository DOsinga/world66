## By Train

Hungary's rail network (MÁV) fans out from Budapest and reaches most cities and larger towns. Intercity (IC) trains are fast and comfortable; seat reservations are compulsory and cost a few hundred HUF extra. Regional trains are slower and can be crowded but cover a lot of ground cheaply. Tickets can be bought at stations or online at [jegy.mav.hu](https://jegy.mav.hu/). The Hungary Pass (around 18,900 HUF for 30 days) covers MÁV trains, Budapest public transport, and domestic Volánbusz services — good value if you're moving around a lot.

## By Bus

Volánbusz operates an extensive network of regional buses, useful for reaching smaller towns not well served by rail. Buy tickets from the driver or at bus station ticket offices. Buses are often the only way to reach villages.

## In Budapest

[Budapest](/europe/hungary/budapest) has an excellent public transport network (BKK) of metro lines, trams, buses, trolleybuses, and suburban rail (HÉV). The system runs daily from around 4:30am to 11:30pm, with night buses covering key routes overnight. Validate your ticket when you board — inspectors are common and fines are steep.

A single ticket costs 500 HUF (as of 2025). For anyone staying more than a day or two, travelcards (24-hour, 72-hour, 7-day) are better value and don't require per-journey validation after the initial purchase. The BKK app and Google Maps both work well for navigation.

## By Car

Renting a car is the best way to explore the countryside, the Danube Bend, the wine regions, and smaller spa towns at your own pace. Roads are in reasonable condition; motorways require a vignette (electronic toll pass) purchased by registration number. Hungary has a strict zero-tolerance drink-driving law.

## Cycling

Hungary has been expanding its cycling infrastructure, and [Lake Balaton](/europe/hungary/lakebalaton) has a popular cycle path around much of the lake. Budapest is increasingly bike-friendly, with rental schemes and dedicated lanes in the city.
