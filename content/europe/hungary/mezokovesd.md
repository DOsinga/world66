Mezőkövesd is a small town in northeastern Hungary, about 130 km east of Budapest, and the heartland of Matyó folk culture — a tradition of extraordinarily elaborate embroidery recognised by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage.

The Matyó people of Mezőkövesd have embroidered their clothing with dense, colourful floral patterns — roses, tulips, carnations, lilies of the valley — for centuries, and no two motifs are exactly alike. Legend has it that a Matyó girl won back her captured lover from the Turks by embroidering all the flowers of spring and summer onto her apron in the depth of winter. Whether or not the story is true, the craft is real and still practised.

The old district of Hadas preserves traditional Matyó houses, several of which now serve as workshops and activity centres where visitors can try embroidery, weaving, pottery, and furniture painting. The Town Gallery holds a permanent exhibition of Matyó embroidery spanning centuries.

The town suffered repeatedly during the Turkish occupation and later conflicts, but its people rebuilt each time. Today Mezőkövesd is a quiet, pleasant place to stop, especially during the annual Matyó Festival in spring.
