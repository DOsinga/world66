Lake Mývatn sits in northeast Iceland, a shallow volcanic lake ringed by lava fields, pseudocraters, and steaming geothermal vents. The name means "midge lake" in Icelandic, and in summer you'll understand why — but those midges feed one of Europe's richest concentrations of breeding ducks and other waterbirds. The surrounding landscape shifts constantly between otherworldly and serene: the dark lava pillars of Dimmuborgir, the bubbling mud pots of Námaskarð, and the still blue water reflecting whatever the Icelandic sky decides to do that hour.

The area works well as a base for exploring the northeast. The Mývatn Nature Baths offer a quieter, less crowded alternative to the Blue Lagoon, with milky-blue geothermal water and views over the lake. Nearby Krafla volcano and its caldera lake add to the geological drama. You can drive the lake in an hour, but give yourself at least two days to hike, soak, and take in the birdlife.

Most visitors arrive via Route 1, the ring road, making Mývatn a natural stop on any circuit of the island. The village of Reykjahlíð on the lake's northeast shore has guesthouses, a small supermarket, and a fuel station — enough to keep you comfortable, not enough to distract you from what you came here to see.
