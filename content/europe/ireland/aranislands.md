The Aran Islands — *Oileáin Árann* — are three low, grey, windswept islands sitting in the mouth of Galway Bay, thirty kilometres out from the Connemara coast. They are famous for three things: the Iron Age cliff fort of **Dún Aonghasa**, the traditional hand-knitted **Aran sweaters**, and for being one of the last strongholds of the **Irish language**. All three are worth the crossing.

**Inis Mór** (Inishmore), the largest island, takes most of the visitors and has most of the sights — Dún Aonghasa, the "worm-hole" tidal pool at Poll na bPéist, the seven churches of the Seacht dTeampaill. Most people base in the village of [Kilronan](/europe/ireland/aranislands/kilronan) and hire a bike for the day.

**[Inis Meáin](/europe/ireland/aranislands/inismeain)** (Inishmaan), in the middle, is the quietest and most traditional — 150 inhabitants, two pubs, still mostly Irish-speaking. This is where J.M. Synge spent his summers and heard the stories that became *Riders to the Sea* and *The Playboy of the Western World*. Stay a night.

**[Inis Oírr](/europe/ireland/aranislands/inisoirr)** (Inisheer), the smallest, has the rusting hulk of the *Plassey* shipwreck beached on its eastern shore — recognisable from the opening sequence of *Father Ted* — and a 14th-century tower house at the top of the island.

Ferries run year-round from **Rossaveal** in Connemara (40 minutes) and from **Doolin** in County Clare in summer (30 minutes to Inis Oírr). Aer Arann operates flights from Inverin to all three islands in eight minutes.
