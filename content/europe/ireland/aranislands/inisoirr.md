Inis Oirr (Inisheer) is the smallest of the three [Aran Islands](/europe/ireland/aranislands), measuring just over three kilometres on each side, but it packs a remarkable amount of history and beauty into that space. The island sits at the mouth of Galway Bay, with the Cliffs of Moher visible across the water to the east and the Connemara coast to the north.

Irish is the first language here, and the island has a strong Gaeltacht culture. The landscape is classic Aran: bare limestone karst crisscrossed by dry-stone walls, with wildflowers pushing through the cracks in spring and summer. Bronze Age remains were uncovered here in 1885 — pottery urns inverted over cremated bones, along with a small bronze pin.

The main sights are easily walked in a day. Teampall Chaomhain, the medieval church of St. Kevin, is half-buried in sand dunes near the shore. The ruins of Caislean Ui Bhriain (O'Brien's Castle) sit on the highest point of the island with views in every direction. Cill Ghobnait and Tobar Einne are quieter sites with deep local significance. The wreck of the cargo ship Plassey, thrown onto the rocks in 1960, lies rusting on the shore and has become one of the island's most photographed landmarks.

With a population of about 350, Inis Oirr is small enough to feel genuinely remote but large enough to have a couple of pubs, a handful of B&Bs and a welcoming atmosphere.
