Bantry sits at the head of Bantry Bay, one of the finest natural harbours in [Ireland](/europe/ireland), with the wild Beara and Sheep's Head peninsulas reaching out on either side. It is a small West Cork town with an outsized sense of place — colourful shopfronts line the main square, fishing boats bob in the harbour, and the mountains behind shift between green and grey depending on the light.

The town's centrepiece is Bantry House, a stately home still occupied by the descendants of the White family, with Italianate gardens that slope down toward the bay. The house itself is an eclectic collection of tapestries, furniture, and art gathered across Europe over two centuries, and the view from the terrace across the water is worth the admission alone. In the courtyard, a small exhibition recalls the failed French invasion of 1796, when Wolfe Tone's fleet was scattered by storms in the bay.

Bantry has become something of a food town. The weekly Friday market on the square is one of the best in West Cork, with local cheeses, smoked fish, and baked goods. The area around the bay produces excellent mussels and oysters, and you will find them on menus throughout the town. For a region that was once among the poorest in Ireland, the quality of the eating here is remarkable.

The town works well as a base for exploring the two peninsulas. The Beara Peninsula to the northwest is wilder and less visited than the Ring of Kerry, with the Healy Pass offering dramatic mountain driving. The gentler Sheep's Head Peninsula to the southwest has a walking route along its spine with views of both Bantry Bay and Dunmanus Bay. Either makes a fine day out, and you will have far fewer tour buses for company than on the more famous routes to the north.
