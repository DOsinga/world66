Cashel is a small town in County Tipperary, a little more than two hours south-west of Dublin, and is entirely overshadowed — both literally and figuratively — by the Rock that stands above it. The **Rock of Cashel** is a 60-metre outcrop of limestone rising straight out of the surrounding Tipperary plain, topped by one of the most dramatic collections of medieval buildings in Ireland: a round tower, a Romanesque chapel, a Gothic cathedral, and a 15th-century Hall of the Vicars Choral. Seen from the road at dawn or dusk, silhouetted against the sky, it is one of those rare sights that actually lives up to a thousand years of reputation.

The Rock was the seat of the Kings of Munster from the 4th century until 1101, when King Muirchertach Ua Briain granted it to the Church. St Patrick is said to have baptised King Aengus here in 450 — and, according to legend, accidentally drove the tip of his crozier through Aengus's foot during the ceremony, which the king endured in silence thinking it was part of the rite. **Cormac's Chapel**, built between 1127 and 1134 under King-Bishop Cormac Mac Carthaigh, is the first and finest Romanesque church in Ireland — inside, you can still see traces of the original 12th-century frescoes that decorated the walls (a Norman knight, the Magi, symbols of the evangelists). The larger cathedral next to it is 13th-century, built across the transepts and now roofless after being burned in 1495 by Gerald Fitzgerald, Earl of Kildare — who, when asked by Henry VII why he had burned the cathedral, reportedly replied "because I thought the archbishop was inside." The **Round Tower**, 28 metres high and the oldest building on the Rock, is an 11th-century marker of the site's importance.

Below the Rock, the **Brú Ború** cultural centre is a small and well-done interpretive centre with a strong focus on Irish traditional music, dance, and storytelling; on summer evenings there are live performances. **Hore Abbey**, a ruined 13th-century Cistercian monastery, is a short walk from the Rock across an open field and almost always empty — arguably more atmospheric for the solitude than its more famous neighbour.

Cashel itself is a small market town with a couple of good pubs and several decent places to eat. Most visitors come as a day trip from Dublin, Kilkenny, or Cork, but staying a night means you can walk up to the Rock at last light or first light, when the coach parties are gone and the ruins are at their best.
