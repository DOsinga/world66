Kinsale is a small harbour town on the southern coast of Ireland, about 25 kilometres south of [Cork](/europe/ireland/cork). It has been called the gourmet capital of Ireland, and while that sounds like tourism board hyperbole, the town genuinely punches above its weight when it comes to food. A cluster of excellent restaurants lines the narrow streets around the harbour, and the annual Gourmet Festival in October draws crowds from across the country.

The town wraps around a sheltered inlet where fishing boats and sailing yachts bob side by side. The streets climb steeply from the waterfront, lined with brightly painted Georgian and Victorian buildings. At the mouth of the harbour, the star-shaped Charles Fort — one of the best-preserved military fortifications in Ireland — guards the approach from a grassy headland with views across the water to the Old Head of Kinsale.

Kinsale's history runs deeper than its restaurant scene. The Battle of Kinsale in 1601, when Spanish troops allied with Irish chieftains were defeated by English forces, effectively ended Gaelic Ireland as an independent political power. The town later became an important naval base, and the harbour still has the feel of a place that has always watched the sea. Today it is a popular sailing centre and a base for fishing trips, whale watching, and walks along the coastal cliffs. It is small enough to explore on foot in an afternoon, but the kind of place where you end up staying for dinner.
