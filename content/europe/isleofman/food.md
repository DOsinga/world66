---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

The Isle of Man has genuine local food traditions, and the island's small scale means that good ingredients are genuinely local.

**Queenies** (queen scallops) are the island's most celebrated ingredient — small, sweet, and fished from Manx waters. In season, they appear in restaurants everywhere, often simply pan-fried in butter. They are worth seeking out at their best.

**Manx kippers** are smoked herring, produced by the smokeries in Peel on the west coast. They are a proper, full-flavoured kipper — considerably better than most supermarket versions. You can buy them fresh from the smokeries to take home.

**Chips, cheese and gravy** is the island's own comfort food — thick-cut chips topped with shredded Manx Cheddar and a thick gravy, somewhere between a Yorkshire chip butty and a Canadian poutine. It is universally available and consistently satisfying.

Traditional Manx cooking was simple farm and fishing food: potatoes, herring, oatcakes (bonnag), and salt fish. The island also produces Manx Loaghtan lamb — from a heritage breed with distinctive four or six horns — which has a deep, gamey flavour quite different from commercial lamb.

Dining options have improved considerably in recent years. Douglas and Peel both have decent restaurants; the food scene during TT fortnight is chaotic but energetic. Outside summer, options in smaller towns can be limited.
