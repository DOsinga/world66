---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

The Isle of Man rewards exploration beyond Douglas. The island is compact enough to cover most of it in a long weekend, and the heritage transport network — steam railway, electric railway, horse trams — makes getting around half the fun.

**Peel** is the most atmospheric town on the island. St Patrick's Isle, connected to the shore by a causeway, holds the ruins of Peel Castle and the 13th-century Cathedral of St German. The town itself is small and unpretentious, with a working harbour and the island's best fish-and-chips. The Peel smokeries produce Manx kippers, which have been a local staple for generations.

**Castletown** was the island's capital until 1866 and still feels the part. Castle Rushen, a Norman castle with Viking foundations, is one of the best-preserved medieval castles in the British Isles. The town around it is small and quiet, with Georgian streets leading down to a sheltered harbour.

**Laxey** is the departure point for the Snaefell Mountain Railway, the only mountain railway in the British Isles. The Laxey Wheel — known as Lady Isabella — is the largest working waterwheel in the world, built in 1854 to pump water from the lead mines below. Taking the electric tram up to the 621-metre summit of Snaefell is one of the island's great excursions: on a clear day you can see England, Scotland, Ireland, and Wales simultaneously.

**The TT Course** is a public road for most of the year, and driving or cycling sections of the 37.73-mile Mountain Course gives a visceral sense of what the racers contend with — narrow lanes, stone walls inches from the road, blind crests. If you are not here for the TT itself, it is still worth following parts of the route.

**The coastal path** (Raad ny Foillan, meaning Way of the Gull) circles the entire island — 100 miles of coastal walking, passing clifftops, glens, beaches, and fishing villages. You can walk it in sections using the bus network.
