---
title: "People"
type: section
---

The Manx people — people born on the island — have a distinct identity that is neither simply British nor straightforwardly Celtic, though both threads run through it. The island's population of around 85,000 includes a significant proportion of incomers: UK professionals drawn by the tax regime and financial sector, retirees, and a smaller number of European and Irish residents. The native Manx can be a minority in their own capital.

The cultural roots are Norse and Celtic. The Tynwald parliament, the place names (Snaefell, Ramsey, Laxey), and many surnames carry obvious Viking heritage. The Celtic strand comes through in the language, music, and festivals, and in a strong connection to the other Celtic nations — Ireland in particular. The island is officially recognised as one of the six Celtic nations.

Manx culture is not loud about itself. The people tend to be reserved in the way of northern British and Irish communities — friendly once engaged, but not immediately effusive. There is a dry, self-deprecating sense of humour and a pragmatic attitude to the island's unusual status. Conversations about tax haven politics and financial regulation are a sensitive area; most islanders would rather talk about the scenery, the TT, or the queenies.
