Basilicata sits between Puglia and Calabria in the instep of Italy's boot, largely bypassed by mass tourism and all the better for it. The region divides neatly into two provinces — Potenza in the west, Matera in the east — and packs in a surprising range of landscapes: sandy Ionian coast, a short but dramatic stretch of Tyrrhenian shoreline around Maratea, deep river gorges, and the pale limestone heights of the Lucanian Dolomites.

Matera is the undisputed draw. Its ancient cave districts, the Sassi, were carved into a ravine over thousands of years and are among the oldest continuously inhabited settlements in the world. UNESCO listed them in 1993, and in 2019 Matera served as a European Capital of Culture. Despite growing attention, the city still feels authentically itself — a place to wander the tuff-stone lanes, peer into rock-cut churches decorated with Byzantine frescoes, and watch swifts wheel over the gorge at dusk.

Away from Matera, the region rewards slow travel. Maratea's rocky Tyrrhenian coast is one of the most beautiful in southern Italy, where a small historic hilltop town overlooks a scattering of coves and clear water. Melfi preserves a Norman castle where Frederick II held court. Venosa, birthplace of the poet Horace, has a ruined abbey and Roman remains sitting companionably side by side. Along the Ionian coast, the Greek colony of Metaponto left behind a remarkably intact Doric temple and a well-displayed archaeological museum.

The inland villages — Pisticci with its tiers of white houses, Miglionico dominated by a Norman castle, Montescaglioso with its abbey — move at their own pace. Time really does seem to have passed them by, which is either a frustration or the whole point, depending on the traveller.
