The Costa di Maratea is Basilicata's only stretch of coastline — roughly 30 kilometres of dramatic cliffs, hidden coves, and clear Tyrrhenian water wedged between Calabria to the south and Campania to the north. It is one of southern Italy's least-known coastal areas, which is precisely its appeal.

The main town, Maratea, is actually a collection of small settlements strung along the hillside above the sea. The medieval borgo sits high up, clustered around narrow lanes and stone churches, with views down to the water. On the peak of Monte San Biagio above the town stands a 22-metre statue of Christ the Redeemer — smaller than Rio's but striking against the sky, and a good hike or short drive for the panorama.

The beaches are small and rocky by Italian standards, many accessible only by boat or steep paths through the macchia. Cala Jannita, known locally as the Black Beach for its dark sand, is probably the most famous. Others — Cala di Filocaio, Spiaggia d'a Gnola — are quieter and feel genuinely wild. In summer, boat excursions from the harbour at Maratea Porto visit the best coves.

The coast is not a resort strip — there are no high-rise hotels or big beach clubs. Accommodation is mostly small hotels and agriturismi scattered through the hills. The food is excellent: fresh seafood, hand-rolled pasta, and the fiery peperoni cruschi (fried dried peppers) that are Basilicata's signature. Getting here by public transport is possible but slow; a car opens up the winding coastal road properly.
