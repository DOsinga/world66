Potenza is probably not on your dreamlist — and the city itself will not argue the point. The capital of Basilicata sits on a long ridge at over 800 metres, making it the highest regional capital in Italy, a distinction that brings cold winters and an occasionally bleak atmosphere. The city has been destroyed multiple times by earthquakes over the centuries, most recently in 1857 and 1980, and what you find now is mostly postwar concrete and 1970s architecture with very little of historical note surviving.

That said, Potenza is a useful hub. The old centre, strung along Via Pretoria, has a decent atmosphere in the evenings when the passeggiata fills the street, and there are a few churches worth glancing at, including the Cathedral and the church of San Francesco. But the real draw is what surrounds the city: the extraordinary cave city of [Matera](/europe/italy/basilicata/matera) to the east, the Greek temples at Metaponto to the south, and a scattering of genuinely attractive hilltop villages in the Agri valley and the Lucan Apennines.

Potenza has a train station that connects to Naples, Taranto, and Reggio Calabria, making it a practical if unremarkable overnight stop for anyone exploring the deep south of Italy.
