Calabria is the toe of Italy's boot, a mountainous peninsula wedged between the Tyrrhenian and Ionian seas. It is one of Italy's poorest regions and one of its least-visited, which is exactly what makes it rewarding. The beaches are uncrowded, the food is fierce with chili, and the hilltop towns feel like they belong to another century.

Three great massifs form the backbone of the region: the Pollino in the north, the Sila plateau in the centre, and the wild Aspromonte in the south. Between them, the land drops steeply to 800 kilometres of coastline. On the Tyrrhenian side, cliffs and coves; on the Ionian side, long sandy beaches stretching to the horizon.

The history runs deep. This was Magna Graecia, and the ancient Greeks left temples and town plans that still shape the landscape. After them came Romans, Byzantines, Arabs, Normans, and Spaniards, each leaving their mark on the hilltowns that cling to every ridge. The people are proud, hospitable, and famously stubborn. They speak a dialect that other Italians sometimes struggle with, and they make some of the best cured meats and cheeses in the south.

[Tropea](/europe/italy/calabria/tropea) is the most famous destination, perched on cliffs above a sandy beach with views to the Aeolian Islands. [Scilla](/europe/italy/calabria/scilla_1) is a fishing village out of a painting. [Cosenza](/europe/italy/calabria/cosenza) has a lively old town and a castle built by Frederick II. The [Sila](/europe/italy/calabria/la_sila) mountains are a world apart from the coast, all pine forests and lakes. And [Diamante](/europe/italy/calabria/diamante_1), a fishermen's village on a rock, hosts the annual Festival del Peperoncino, which tells you everything about Calabrian priorities.
