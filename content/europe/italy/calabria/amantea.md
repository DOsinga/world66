Amantea ranks high on a list of Calabria's prettiest towns, and it is one of the region's most historically layered. Long before the Greeks and Romans, Bronze and Iron Age settlements flourished around the mouth of the river Savuto near the port.

The Bruzi, Calabria's native warrior tribe, held off Greek colonisation for decades, which explains the lack of Greek architecture here, unlike almost every other Calabrian town. The Romans eventually prevailed and used 300 km of surrounding land as an *ager*, a supply territory for Imperial Rome, building a major port to ship the produce.

After the fall of Rome, Amantea sat on the border between Lombard and Byzantine territories. The Byzantines built a castle on the hill above town, still prominent today, commanding views of the coast in both directions. In 839 the Arabs took the city and established an emirate around it. The name Amantea comes from the Arabic *Al Mantiah*, meaning "stronghold."

Modern Amantea divides into two parts: the historical centre on the hill above, and the newer town below with shops, restaurants, and piazzas lining the main boulevard. It is a friendly, easy place to find your way around.
