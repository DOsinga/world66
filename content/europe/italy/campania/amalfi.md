*Europe > Italy > Campania > Amalfi*

The Amalfi Coast takes the southern side of the Sorrento peninsula and does something extraordinary with it: a corniche road cut into cliffs that plunge straight into the sea, connecting a series of towns that seem to defy gravity. The road itself is an experience — hairpin bends, one-lane tunnels, buses somehow squeezing past each other above a hundred-metre drop.

[Positano](/europe/italy/campania/positano), with its pastel buildings stacked up the cliff face, is the most-photographed town on the coast. [Ravello](/europe/italy/campania/ravello), perched 350 metres above the water, has the finest gardens and the quietest atmosphere. Furore, accessible from the coast road, is the smallest — "the town that never existed," they call it, its walls now covered in murals by artists invited to paint the village.

Amalfi itself was once one of the four great maritime republics of medieval Italy — alongside Genoa, Venice, and Pisa — with a population of 70,000 at its peak. The Normans sacked it in 1131 and an earthquake in 1343 finished what the Normans started. The Duomo, with its striped 13th-century facade, is what remains of the medieval republic's ambitions. Today the town is a comfortable resort that makes a better base than Positano for exploring the coast — more affordable, and with a functioning piazza rather than a beach full of sunbeds.

For less expensive accommodation and a different pace, consider using Sorrento, Maiori, or Salerno as a base and visiting Amalfi as a day trip by bus or ferry.
