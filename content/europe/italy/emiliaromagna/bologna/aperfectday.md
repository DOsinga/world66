---
title: 'A Perfect Day'
type: section
---

Bologna is a beautiful city where you can easily spend a week. It is lesser known than its industrial brother in the west, Milan, and its sexier sister in the south, Florence. But if you ask me, I prefer Bologna. Make sure you wake up early today because it is going to be a fulfilling day -- and don't forget that the city winds down during the early afternoon hours.

For breakfast we are off to a perfect little bar in Via Indipendenza, named *Gugliemo*. They make their own blend of coffee and it is said to be the best in the city. Italians do not have large breakfasts. A cappuccino and a brioche will do. (By the way, Italians do not drink cappuccino after breakfast -- the milk is too heavy, they say -- whereas they have no problem drinking strong coffee till 10 in the evening. If you order a cappuccino after dinner, expect a little snicker.) We will get more food soon anyway.

First stop on our route is immediately the highest point in Bologna: the two towers in Piazza di Porta Ravegnana, **Torre Asinelli** and **Torre Garisenda**. The taller of the two can be climbed. Goethe did it two hundred years ago, so why don't you? From the tower you can see that Bologna was built on the edge of the Pianura Padana (plain of the river Po) right where the Apennines start. From here you can also see the magnificent church of **San Luca** on top of a mountain to the southwest.

From the tower we can see to the southeast a church complex called the **Church of Santo Stefano** (Via Santo Stefano 24, open Mon-Sun 9-12 and 3:30-6, entrance: free). This is our next stop. Make sure to get there before noon. In fact it is not one church but seven churches built right next to each other. It is fascinating to move from one into the other, and a great game is to see if you can find all seven.

Getting hungry? It is not really lunchtime yet, but the Italians have a wonderful thing called the *aperitivo*, starting at around 11-11:30. Walk roughly in a west-northwest direction and you will run into a bar that serves this light drink (you can also simply take something non-alcoholic, such as a popular *Sanbitter*). Sometimes you will be served little pieces of pizza, olives, chips -- enough to still your most urgent hunger.

Off we go, back in the direction of **Piazza Maggiore**, the centre of the city with the Basilica of San Petronio and the statue of Neptune. About the statue: legend has it that a church official complained Neptune's penis was too large and the sculptor should change it. He did so reluctantly, but constructed Neptune's hand in such a way that from a certain angle of the sun, the effect is... suggestive.

The square is magnificent. From here, walk south along Via D'Azeglio or explore the medieval streets around Via Clavature, where food shops and little restaurants crowd together. This is the old market area, and it is still the best place to pick up supplies for a picnic lunch. Alternatively, duck into the **Osteria del Sole** (Vicolo Ranocchi 1d) -- one of the oldest osterie in Bologna, where you bring your own food and they provide the wine and atmosphere.

After lunch, the city quiets down. This is a good time for the **Pinacoteca Nazionale** (Via Belle Arti 56), which has an excellent collection of Emilian painting, or the **Museo Morandi** on Piazza Maggiore. If you prefer fresh air, walk up to **San Luca** along the world's longest portico -- 3.8 km of covered arcades climbing up the hill. The views from the top are spectacular.

As evening comes, the city wakes up again. Head to Via del Pratello for dinner -- the street is full of restaurants and bars, and the atmosphere is lively and local. End the day with a gelato from **Gelateria Ugo** (Via San Felice 24), considered by locals to have the best homemade ice cream in the city.
