---
title: 'Eating Out'
type: section
---

Let me organize this section chronologically, starting with breakfast and ending up in one of the many bars ("osteria" they are called) in Bologna.

**Breakfast.** Breakfast is not an important part of the staple diet of a typical Italian. For him or her, it just consists of a quick cappuccino and a brioche from any of the little bars you can find everywhere. Notice that there are generally two different prices: those standing at the counter pay a lower price than those who sit down and let themselves be served.

**Healthy snack.** If you want to merge with the locals doing their grocery shopping and also buy some cheap fruit, bread, cheese, or meat yourself, go to the daily (excluding Sunday) covered market called *Mercato delle Erbe* just off Via Ugo Bassi, a little west from Piazza Maggiore. The market is held in a covered galleria. Look for the sign "Alimentari, Mercato delle Erbe." The market generally closes around 12:30 and starts very early. It is also open in the afternoon after 3:30pm.

**Pre-lunch and lunch.** Fashionable Italians generally take an *aperitivo* just before lunch. Most bars serve aperitivi in two varieties: alcoholic and non-alcoholic. Just ask for "aperitivo della casa alcolico/analcolico." Aperitivi are generally around 6 euros, but they allow you to eat as much of the little snacks put out on the counter, so it could be enough for a quick, light lunch.

A great traditional place with long wooden tables is **Osteria del Sole** (Vicolo Ranocchi 1d), one of the oldest osterie of Bologna. It encourages you to bring your own lunch, sit down at one of its tables, and order a glass of wine to wash it all down. The osteria is just east of Piazza Maggiore and nearby there are many little markets to buy your own lunch.

**Dinner.** For dinner there are many good options in Bologna, although there is quite some turnover in the restaurant scene. Some reliable picks:

- **Pizzeria Belle Arti**, Via Belle Arti 14. Reasonably priced pizza place that makes heart-shaped pizzas for beautiful female customers. Closed Wednesday.
- **Il Montesino**, Via del Pratello 74b. A moderately priced Sardinian restaurant that puts its customers together at tables -- a hosteria tradition that leads to friendly and unexpected socializing.
- **Self-service S. Lorenzo**, Via S. Lorenzo 4. Not a fancy restaurant but a great, cheap, and convenient place to taste traditional Bolognese dishes just off Via Marconi.
- **La Farfalla**, Via Bertiera 12. A cheap, little restaurant just off Via Indipendenza with some great food.

**After dinner.** Bologna is a university town which guarantees a very lively nightlife. Night life traditionally took place in *osterie*, but today you can always find a lot of people around bars and gelaterias that stay open late.

- **Il Cantinone**, Via del Pratello 56A. A great place to come after dinner with a group of friends. Ask for a glass of *fragolino*, a sweet wine made from the fragolino grape -- since its alcohol content is too low to be sold as wine, fragolino is hard to find.
- **Gelateria Mario**, end of Via D'Azeglio near Porta San Mamolo. A great gelateria that stays open late with great fruit-flavoured ice cream. Try the substantial *frappe Mario*, with chocolate and nuts.
- **Gelateria Ugo**, Via San Felice 24. Considered by the locals to have the best homemade ice cream, especially the creamy and chocolate flavours. Ugo himself or his wife frequently stands in the shop, hung with posters of their latest holiday destination.
