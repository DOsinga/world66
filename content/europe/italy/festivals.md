---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Italy's festivals range from ancient religious processions to horse races that have been running for centuries. The calendar is packed — almost every town has its own sagra (food festival), patron saint's day, and local tradition. These are the highlights worth timing a visit around.

**Carnevale di Venezia (February).** Venice's Carnival is the most famous in Italy and one of the most spectacular in the world. The city fills with people in elaborate period costumes and ornate masks. There are outdoor performances in San Marco Square, candlelit boat parades, and ticketed masked balls. It runs for about ten days before Mardi Gras. Go on a weekday if you can — weekends are overwhelming.

**Easter (March/April).** Holy Week is observed seriously throughout Italy. The Vatican holds major ceremonies, culminating in the Easter Mass in St Peter's Square. In Sicily, processions through the streets on Good Friday are intense, sombre affairs, some lasting all night; Trapani and Enna are particularly atmospheric. In Florence, the Scoppio del Carro — a cart loaded with fireworks detonated in the Piazza del Duomo on Easter Sunday — has been going since the 15th century.

**Maggio Musicale Fiorentino (May–June).** Florence's opera and classical music festival is one of the oldest in Europe. Performances run at the Teatro del Maggio and outdoor venues around the city.

**La Festa dei Ceri, Gubbio (15 May).** One of Italy's stranger events: three large wooden structures (ceri) shaped like towers are raced through the narrow streets of this hilltop Umbrian town by teams of young men. It has been going since the 12th century and the whole town participates.

**Il Palio di Siena (2 July and 16 August).** Siena's famous horse race has been held in the Piazza del Campo since the 17th century. Seventeen neighbourhood districts (contrade) compete, each with its own horse and jockey. The race itself lasts about 90 seconds and is almost comically chaotic. The weeks of pageantry, flag-throwing, and neighbourhood rivalry around it are the real event. Standing room in the piazza is free; grandstand seats sell out far in advance.

**Venice Film Festival (late August–September).** The world's oldest film festival takes place on the Lido, the barrier island east of Venice. It runs for about ten days and is more relaxed and accessible than Cannes — films screen at the Palazzo del Cinema and many screenings are open to the public.

**Ferragosto (15 August).** The national Assumption holiday functions mainly as Italy's peak summer break. Italians empty the cities for beaches and mountains. Urban restaurants and shops close en masse. The beach resorts are at their most crowded and their most festive simultaneously.

**Truffle Festivals (October–November).** The white truffle season is reason enough to visit Piemonte in autumn. The International Alba White Truffle Fair runs every weekend in October and November. San Miniato in Tuscany holds its own truffle fair in November. Both are excellent — more relaxed than the big city festivals and the food is very good.
