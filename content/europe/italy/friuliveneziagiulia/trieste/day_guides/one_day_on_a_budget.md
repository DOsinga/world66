Trieste is a compact, walkable city that offers brilliant value compared to Italy's headline destinations — part Italian, part Viennese, quietly weird, and thoroughly underrated. Almost everything worth seeing clusters between the station, the Canal Grande, Piazza Unità d'Italia, and the San Giusto hill. Walk as much as you can; it's the single biggest budget-saver and the best way to absorb the city.

## Morning

Start at **Trieste Centrale**, which drops you right into the city. From there walk downhill toward the sea. The **Canal Grande** is your first stop — a stretch of water lined with grand Habsburg buildings that tells you immediately you're somewhere unlike the rest of Italy. From here it's a short walk to the **Piazza Unità d'Italia**, one of the largest sea-facing squares in Europe. Arrive early to beat the midday crowds.

Coffee is not optional in Trieste — it's the local religion, and the terminology is its own dialect (*capo* for macchiato, *nero* for espresso). Sit in one of the historic cafés rather than ordering at the bar:

- **Caffè degli Specchi**, Piazza Unità d'Italia — a Viennese-style grand café opened in 1839, right on the square with a view of the sea.
- **Antico Caffè San Marco**, Via Cesare Battisti 18 — a literary institution founded in 1914, full of dark wood and intellectual atmosphere.

## Lunch

Trieste has a food tradition that reflects its Austro-Hungarian heritage: the *buffet* (pronounced boo-FAY) — unpretentious, standing or casual lunch spots serving boiled meats, sandwiches, and local wine. Far cheaper and more authentic than tourist restaurants.

- **Buffet da Pepi**, Via della Cassa di Risparmio 3 (est. 1897) — the most famous example. A sandwich runs around €4; a full *bollito* plate around €10.
- **Buffet da Siora Rosa** — a reliable alternative in the same tradition.

## Afternoon

Walk up to the **San Giusto hill** for elevated views over the city and the bay. The **Castello di San Giusto** (open daily Apr–Oct 9:00–18:00, Nov–Mar 10:00–17:00; €6 full, €4 reduced, €1 for the courtyard only) makes a good paid attraction if you want one — the views alone are worth the castle entry.

If you want to venture out of the centre, choose one:

- **Miramare Castle** — spectacular clifftop castle north of the city, free park, €15 for the interior. Check the castle website for current hours.
- **Risiera di San Sabba** — the only Nazi concentration camp on Italian soil, now a museum and memorial. Free admission; audio guide €3.50. Open Apr–Sep 9:00–19:00, Oct–Mar 9:00–17:00. Closed 25 Dec and 1 Jan.

Don't try to fit in both out-of-centre sites on the same day.

## Evening

Trieste evenings are made for a relaxed *aperitivo* followed by dinner. If lunch at a *buffet* was filling, keep the evening light. If you held back at lunch, this is the moment for something more substantial.

## Practical notes

- **Buses**: single ticket €1.50, daily pass €3.35 (worth it for more than two rides). Buy in advance — on Sundays newspaper kiosks may be closed, so stock up at the station.
- **Train from Venice**: approx. 1h40–2h; from Milan: approx. 4h. Book ahead for the best fares.
- **Budget**: prioritise walking, pick one paid attraction, and lean into cafés and *buffet*-style spots for meals.
