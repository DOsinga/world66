---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Getting around Italy is genuinely pleasurable, especially by train. The country is well covered by rail and the high-speed network has transformed travel between major cities.

**Train.** Trenitalia and the private operator Italo both run high-speed services (Frecciarossa, Frecciargento, Italo) on the main spine: Milan–Bologna–Florence–Rome–Naples. These are fast, comfortable, and often cheaper than flying when booked in advance. Rome to Florence takes about 1h30; Rome to Naples around 1h10; Rome to Milan around 3h. For slower regional travel — the Amalfi coast hinterland, smaller Tuscan towns, the Calabrian interior — regional trains are cheap but slow. Book high-speed tickets well in advance at trenitalia.com or italotreno.it; regional trains can usually be bought at the station or validated in the yellow machines on the platform. Forgetting to validate a regional ticket before boarding will earn you a fine, regardless of whether you have a valid ticket.

**Bus.** For places trains don't reach — most of the hill towns of Tuscany and Umbria, much of rural southern Italy — buses are essential. FlixBus and regional operators (SITA, Marino, Marozzi) cover the main intercity routes. Timetables can be sparse outside peak season, so check carefully.

**Car.** Renting a car makes sense for Tuscany, Umbria, the Amalfi Coast hinterland, Sicily, and Sardinia — anywhere you want flexibility to explore smaller places at your own pace. On the main motorway corridors a car is less useful than the train. Drive on the right; Italians drive fast and tend to tailgate on motorways. Petrol is expensive. Restricted traffic zones (ZTL) in historic city centres are enforced by cameras — hire companies will pass on fines automatically. If you're not confident about ZTL maps, park outside the historic centre and walk or take local transport in.

**Ferry.** Ferries to Sardinia depart from Genoa, Livorno, and Civitavecchia. Ferries to Sicily go from Naples, Reggio Calabria, and Villa San Giovanni. For the Aeolian Islands, depart from Milazzo in Sicily. For Elba, take the ferry from Piombino. Hydrofoils are faster but more expensive; car ferries take longer but are much cheaper and let you bring luggage more easily.

**City transport.** Rome, Milan, Naples, Turin, and several other cities have metro networks. Buses and trams cover the rest. Buy tickets before boarding — from tabacchi shops, machines, or apps — and validate them when you board. Not buying or not validating is frequently checked and fines are steep by the standards of the fare.
