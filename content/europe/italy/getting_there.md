---
title: "Getting There"
type: section
---

Italy is one of the best-connected countries in Europe. Getting there is rarely difficult, though how you arrive depends a lot on where you're coming from and where in Italy you want to end up.

**By air.** The main international gateways are Rome Fiumicino (FCO) and Milan Malpensa (MXP). Both have direct flights from North America, Asia, the Middle East, and across Europe. Venice Marco Polo, Naples, Palermo, Catania, and Bologna also handle significant international traffic. Low-cost carriers such as Ryanair and easyJet connect dozens of smaller Italian airports to cities across Europe. If you're flying into Milan, be aware that Milan Bergamo (BGY) is an hour from the city centre — budget extra time and check which airport your flight uses.

**By train from Europe.** High-speed trains connect Italy to France, Switzerland, Germany, and Austria. Paris to Milan takes about 7 hours by TGV; Vienna to Venice around 6 hours. Booking in advance through Trenitalia, SNCF, or Deutsche Bahn gets you the best fares. For anyone coming overland from France or Central Europe, the train is genuinely the more civilised option — you arrive in the city centre without the airport shuffle.

**By ferry.** Italy has excellent ferry connections across the Adriatic and the Mediterranean. The main routes: Ancona, Bari, and Brindisi to Greece (Igoumenitsa, Patras, Corfu); Civitavecchia and Genoa to Barcelona; Genoa, Livorno, and Civitavecchia to Sardinia; Naples, Reggio Calabria, and Villa San Giovanni to Sicily. Grimaldi Lines, Grandi Navi Veloci, Tirrenia, and Moby operate the major routes. The website Direct Ferries is a useful aggregator for schedules and prices.

**By car.** Entering from France via the Mont Blanc tunnel or the coastal A10 is straightforward. From Switzerland and Austria the Brenner Pass is the main route south. Italian motorways (autostrade) charge tolls — you pay at booths as you go. No vignette is required. If you plan to drive into the historic centres of major cities, be aware that most have restricted traffic zones (ZTL — Zone a Traffico Limitato) with automatic cameras; fines for unknowing foreign visitors are common.

**Entry requirements.** EU and Schengen-area citizens enter freely. US, UK, Canadian, and Australian citizens do not need a visa for stays up to 90 days within any 180-day period. From late 2025, most visa-exempt nationalities will need to register for ETIAS authorisation before arrival — a quick online process, not a full visa, but required. Citizens of countries outside the Schengen visa-free list need to apply for a Schengen Type C visa in advance.
