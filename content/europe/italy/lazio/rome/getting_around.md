**On foot**

Walking is by far the best way to see the historic centre. The centro storico is compact — the Pantheon, Piazza Navona, Campo de' Fiori, and the river are all within 15 minutes of each other on foot. The Vatican is a 30-minute walk from the centre or a short metro ride. Comfortable shoes matter; the cobblestones are relentless.

**By metro**

Rome has three metro lines operated by **ATAC**. Lines **A** (orange) and **B** (blue) intersect at Termini. Line **C** (green), which opened in phases from 2014, runs from Monte Compatri-Pantano in the southeast to San Giovanni (where it connects with Line A) — its extension to the centre is ongoing. The metro is fast but limited: most of the historic centre has no stations, largely because construction keeps hitting ancient ruins. The metro runs roughly 05:30–23:30 (until 01:30 on Fridays and Saturdays).

**By bus and tram**

ATAC buses cover the city extensively and reach areas the metro doesn't. The most useful tourist routes run along the main boulevards (buses 40, 64 connect Termini with the Vatican). **Tram 8** is handy for Trastevere. Frequency is inconsistent; use the ATAC app or Google Maps for live times.

**Tickets**

A single BIT ticket costs **€1.50** and is valid for 100 minutes on all buses, trams, and metro (one metro journey only). Multi-day passes: 24h €7, 48h €12.50, 72h €18, 7-day €24. Buy tickets at tabacchi (tobacconists), newsstands, metro station machines, or the ATAC app. **Validate every ticket** when boarding — inspectors do check, and the on-the-spot fine is €54.

**By taxi**

Taxis are metered and relatively honest. Fixed fares apply to/from both airports. Hail on the street, find a rank (outside Termini, at major piazzas), or use the itTaxi or FREE NOW apps. Ride-hailing apps like Uber operate only with licensed drivers here and are not cheaper than taxis.

**By scooter or e-bike**

Electric scooters (Lime, Bird, Tier) and e-bikes are widely available via app and are useful for covering longer distances along the river or to Testaccio and Pigneto. Riding a scooter in Rome traffic is not for the faint-hearted; the roads and the drivers are both unpredictable.

**Driving**

Don't. Parking is nearly impossible in the centre, the ZTL camera system issues automatic fines to foreign plates, and the traffic is genuinely chaotic. Leave the car at a park-and-ride on the ring road and take public transport in.
