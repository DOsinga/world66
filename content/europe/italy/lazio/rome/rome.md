Rome is the kind of city that makes everywhere else seem a little thin. 2,800 years of continuous habitation have left a density of ruins, churches, palaces, and piazzas that takes weeks to absorb and a lifetime to understand. Ancient temples sit next to Renaissance basilicas that sit next to baroque fountains; the layers pile up until the city itself becomes the attraction.

The ancient centre — the Colosseum, the Roman Forum, the Palatine Hill — is the obvious starting point. These are among the most impressive ruins anywhere, and they deserve a full day. The Vatican is a half-day at minimum: St. Peter's Basilica alone is enormous, and the Vatican Museums ending in the Sistine Chapel will take another three to four hours. The Pantheon, Piazza Navona, and the streets of Trastevere fill out another day easily.

Beyond the monuments, Rome is good for wandering. The historic centre is compact and mostly flat. Getting lost between Campo de' Fiori and Piazza Navona turns up small baroque churches, Renaissance palaces, and courtyard cafes that aren't in the guidebooks. The Trastevere neighbourhood, across the Tiber, is the most atmospheric for evening eating and drinking, though it's become increasingly touristy.

The city is at its best in spring (March–May) and autumn (September–October). July and August are genuinely brutal — 35°C, packed, and with many Romans having fled to the coast. Book the major sites well in advance; the Colosseum and Vatican Museums both benefit from online tickets that skip multi-hour queues. The Borghese Gallery requires advance reservation by design.

Rome is not a cheap city, but it doesn't have to be an expensive one. Stand at the bar for coffee (half the price of sitting down), eat lunch at a tavola calda, and walk everywhere in the centre. The expensive tourist traps cluster immediately around the big sights; walk one street over and prices drop.
