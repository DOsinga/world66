Genoa is Italy's most underrated city. While tourists crowd Florence and Venice, Genoa goes quietly about its business — a working port that also happens to have the largest medieval city centre in Europe, a cluster of Renaissance palaces that can match any in Italy, and a food culture built around pesto, focaccia, and fresh fish that the rest of the world has been trying to replicate ever since.

The city was born as a sea republic. From the 11th to the 18th centuries, Genoese merchants and admirals operated across the known world — from the Black Sea to the Atlantic — and the wealth they brought back is written into the city's stones. The *caruggi*, those impossibly narrow lanes between tower-tall houses, lead to sudden piazzas fronted by grand palaces. The Strade Nuove, lined with the homes of the old banking families, are now a UNESCO World Heritage site.

Christopher Columbus was born here, probably around 1451. Niccolò Paganini learned his violin in these streets. Renzo Piano, who redesigned the waterfront for the 1992 Expo, still considers it home. The Acquario di Genova — Europe's largest aquarium — is the centrepiece of that waterfront and one of Italy's most-visited attractions.

Genoa is not a polished tourist destination. Parts of it are rough. The *caruggi* at night feel genuinely medieval. But that rawness is exactly the point: this is a city that has been living its own life for a thousand years and doesn't particularly need your approval. Come here instead of heading straight down the coast — you'll thank yourself.
