The Italian Riviera runs for about 350 km along the Ligurian coast, from Ventimiglia on the French border to La Spezia in the east. It's a landscape of steep terraced hillsides dropping to the sea, pastel-painted fishing villages, and a coastline that shifts from pebbly coves to wide sandy beaches depending on where you land.

The western stretch — the Riviera dei Fiori and Riviera delle Palme — is sunnier and flatter, with well-developed resort towns like Sanremo and Alassio. The eastern stretch around Portofino, the Tigullio Gulf, and the Cinque Terre is wilder and more dramatic. These aren't the same place; choose your section based on what you want.

Liguria has its own cuisine, and it's worth paying attention to. Pesto originates here — made with local basil, pine nuts, and Parmigiano, not the jarred kind you're used to. Try trofie al pesto, focaccia di Recco (thin flatbread stuffed with stracchino cheese), and the local anchovies from Monterosso. The wines are modest but honest: Vermentino and Pigato from the west, Sciacchetrà from the Cinque Terre.

Getting between towns by train is easy and often more practical than driving — the coast road is scenic but slow, and parking in the villages is a nightmare. Most visitors base themselves in one town and make day trips.
