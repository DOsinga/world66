Lake Iseo, also known as Lake Sebino, is the fourth-largest lake in Lombardy. Formed by the Valcamonica Glacier, it stretches 24 kilometres north to south and up to 5 kilometres wide — though the full width is rarely visible because Monte Isola, the largest inhabited lake island in Europe, sits squarely in the middle. No cars are allowed on Monte Isola, making it an ideal destination for walking or cycling; ferries cross regularly from Iseo and other lakeside towns.

The lake is fed from the north by the River Oglio, flowing down from the Val Camonica. At the southern end lies the Torbiere, a peat bog and nature reserve. Further south is the Franciacorta valley, known for producing some of Italy's best sparkling wine. On the eastern shore, a few kilometres up from the water, the Natural Reserve of the Pyramids of Zone offers a striking landscape of pinnacles up to ten metres high, formed by uneven glacial erosion.

The perimeter road links the main lakeside towns — Iseo, Sarnico, Lovere, Pisogne and Marone — each with a promenade, cafés and a handful of churches worth a look. Iseo town is the main hub: relaxed in pace, with a wide main square dominated by a statue of Garibaldi and a view across to Monte Isola. The lake is popular with Italian families rather than foreign tourists, which keeps the atmosphere local and the prices reasonable.

Water sports are well established on the lake, and fishing is a local obsession — tench is the prized catch. Walking and cycling trails follow both shores. In winter, the Presolano area north of the lake offers skiing.
