Sirmione sits on a narrow finger of land that juts three kilometres into Lake Garda from its southern shore. The old town is almost entirely surrounded by water, approached through the gates of a 13th-century Scaligero castle — and for most of the year it is crowded with visitors who've figured out what a good thing looks like. That's fair. The place is genuinely beautiful.

The two headline attractions are at opposite ends of history. The Rocca Scaligera is a working medieval fortress right in the centre of town, still surrounded by its original moat. Walk out to the far tip of the peninsula and you reach the Grotte di Catullo, the ruins of what was once one of the largest Roman private villas in northern Italy. Neither is a reconstruction — both are the real thing.

Sirmione also has a serious thermal tradition. The sulphurous waters that bubble up from the lake bed have been used for treatment since Roman times, and the modern Terme di Sirmione complex is one of the largest thermal centres in Italy. Even if you're not there for a cure, a dip in the thermal pools with views over the lake is hard to argue with.

Come in May or September if you can. July and August bring gridlock on the access road and queues at every gate. If you're stuck visiting in high summer, arrive early — the castle opens at 8:30 and the ruins are quieter before 10.
