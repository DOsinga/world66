Milan is the biggest city of northern Italy — the country's financial powerhouse and, by a distance, its most stylish. It is less obviously pretty than Florence or Venice, but it rewards anyone who looks past the first impression: Gothic churches, Renaissance frescoes, grand nineteenth-century arcades, and some of the best contemporary architecture in Europe.

The historic centre radiates from Piazza del Duomo, where the pink-marble **Duomo** — one of the largest Gothic cathedrals in the world — faces the glass-and-iron **Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II**. A short walk away is **Santa Maria delle Grazie**, where Leonardo's *Last Supper* is painted on the refectory wall (book well ahead). The **Castello Sforzesco** anchors the northwest of the centre, and the **Pinacoteca di Brera** houses one of Italy's best collections of Renaissance painting.

Beyond the monuments, Milan is a city of neighbourhoods. **Brera** is the old artists' quarter, still lively after dark. The **Navigli** canal district is where Milanese go for aperitivo — the early-evening ritual of a drink and a buffet that stands in for dinner. **Porta Nuova** and **Isola** show Milan's newer face, with Stefano Boeri's Bosco Verticale and the skyscrapers of Gae Aulenti square. For shopping, the streets of the **Quadrilatero della Moda** — Via Montenapoleone, Via della Spiga, Via Sant'Andrea, Via Manzoni — are the centre of Italian luxury fashion.

Milan is also a fine base for day trips. The Carthusian monastery at Pavia, the medieval upper town of Bergamo, and the lakes — Como, Maggiore, Garda — are all within an hour or two by train.
