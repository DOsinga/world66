At first sight, you might think that you ended up in a fairytale and that within ample seconds, several dwarfs and elfs will come out of the white washed little houses with their cone-shaped roofs. When you come to think about it, you know that you’ve arrived in Alberobella.


 The historical centre with its ‘trullis’(as the houses are called) was declared an international monument and at present, one can still visit the little houses set amidst almonds and olive trees and see people making ceramics according to the way it used to be done some 500 years ago.


 Marvellous, unique and typical of the [Bari](/europe/italy/puglia/bari) and [Otranto](/europe/italy/puglia/otranto) province, but why? Some say that the counts of the area forced the colonists to make dry dwellings that could be easily pulled down in case one of the tax inspectors would come over to collect the money from permanent dwellers. Another version is that because one only had to pay for permanent houses, the white stone on top of the roof could be easily removed as to show to the inspector that the house hadn’s been finished yet. The same white pinnacles that might either mark the hand of the builder of the house, have been fallen out of the sky as a sign of the (worshipped) sun or simply look good as the finishing touch on the roof.
 Something completely different, but also impressive in its own way is the city [Lecce](/europe/italy/puglia/lecce) in the same area.
