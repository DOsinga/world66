Most travellers end up in Brindisi because they need a ferry — to Greece, to Albania, or onward to the eastern Mediterranean. The port has been doing this job for over two thousand years: it was the terminus of the Via Appia, and the Roman column marking the end of the road still stands on the waterfront.

The town deserves more than just a wait between connections. The old centre has a handful of interesting churches, including the Romanesque San Giovanni al Sepolcro with its frescoed interior, and the waterfront promenade along the harbour is a pleasant walk. Brindisi also makes a reasonable base for exploring the rest of Puglia's Salento region — [Lecce](/europe/italy/puglia/lecce) is only 40 minutes away by train. Ferry services to Greece run year-round, though departures are less frequent outside summer.
