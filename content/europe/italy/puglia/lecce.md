Lecce is the Florence of the South — a designation it's earned. Located at the heel of Italy's boot, roughly midway between Brindisi and the Adriatic coast, the city had its heyday under Spanish rule in the 17th century when local noblemen, merchants, and religious orders competed to build ever more extravagant palaces and churches. The material was a golden local limestone that proved extraordinarily soft to carve, which led to an architecture of dizzying elaboration: curling volutes, grinning monsters, caryatids, and festoons piled onto every available surface.

Piazza Sant'Oronzo is the heart of the old town — anchored by a Roman column that once marked the end of the Appian Way and the remains of a Roman amphitheatre. From there the Via Umberto leads toward the Portale Napoli, and the tangle of streets between holds some 100 churches and palaces worth a look. The undisputed highlight is the church of Santa Croce, whose façade is the most exuberant expression of the Leccese Baroque style anywhere.

The surrounding city is quietly pleasurable — good pasticcierie, a relaxed piazza culture, and far fewer foreign tourists than similar Italian cities. The local specialty is pasticciotto, a small custard pastry, and virtually every bar serves them.
