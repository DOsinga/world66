---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

Cagliari is a proper city with a functioning life beyond tourism — which makes it more interesting than most island capitals. The old hilltop quarter of Castello is the obvious starting point, but the city rewards wandering into the quarters below it.

**Castello**

The walled hilltop district contains most of the major sights. The Bastione di Saint Remy on Piazza Costituzione is the main gateway — climb to the top for panoramic views over the city and the lagoon. Within the walls, the Cathedral of Santa Maria (Piazza Palazzo) dates from the 13th century and contains two impressive 12th-century Pisan pulpits. The Torre di San Pancrazio and Torre dell'Elefante, both 14th-century Pisan towers, are also worth a look.

**Cittadella dei Musei**

The old citadel at the top of Castello houses several museums in one complex. The Museo Archeologico Nazionale di Sardegna is essential — it holds the finest collection of Nuragic bronzes and artefacts on the island, including the remarkable *bronzetti* figurines that are among the most distinctive works of prehistoric European art. Open Tuesday–Sunday.

**Anfiteatro Romano**

A 2nd-century Roman amphitheatre cut into the hillside below Castello, once capable of seating 10,000 people. The remains are substantial and the setting dramatic. Used for concerts in summer.

**Santuario di Bonaria**

The hilltop sanctuary on the southern edge of the city centre contains a much-venerated wooden statue of the Madonna, which according to legend arrived here by sea after being thrown overboard from a ship in a storm. The view from the terrace over the port and the Poetto lagoon is excellent.

**San Saturnino**

The oldest Christian church on the island, dating from the 5th century, on Via Dante in the Villanova quarter. A simple early Byzantine structure in pink stone that has survived remarkably intact given its age.

**Poetto Beach**

The 8-km-long sandy beach southeast of the city, reachable by bus (line PF from the city centre, about 10 minutes). The water is shallow and calm. In summer the beach fills up with locals and has a good selection of beach bars. The lagoon behind it — the Stagno di Molentargius — is a natural reserve where flamingos can be seen year-round.
