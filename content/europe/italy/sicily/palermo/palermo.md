Palermo is one of the most layered cities in Europe — a place where Norman cathedrals encrust Arab foundations, where Baroque church façades open onto Byzantine mosaics, and where the street food is descended from a thousand years of Arab, Jewish, Spanish, and Sicilian cooking piled on top of each other. It is loud, beautiful, chaotic, and unlike anywhere else in Italy.

The city sits in the Conca d'Oro, a wide plain between the sea and a ring of limestone mountains, with Monte Pellegrino rising dramatically to the north. It was successively Phoenician, Carthaginian, Roman, Byzantine, Arab, Norman, Hohenstaufen, and Spanish — each layer left something behind. The Arab-Norman architecture of the 11th and 12th centuries is the most spectacular: UNESCO-listed buildings that combine Islamic geometry with Byzantine mosaic and Norman stone in ways that have no parallel anywhere in Europe.

The historic centre is dense and walkable, organised around four districts — the Kalsa, Albergheria, Capo, and Vucciria — each with its own market and character. The Ballarò and Capo markets are two of the best street food markets in Italy. The food culture generally — arancine, panelle, sfincione, pasta con le sarde, cassata — is one of the strongest reasons to visit.

Palermo has rough edges. Parts of the centre are in serious disrepair, the traffic is aggressive, and the petty crime risk is higher than in northern Italian cities. But it has been improving steadily and is considerably safer than it was twenty years ago. The city repays the effort of engaging with it seriously.

[Mondello](/europe/italy/sicily/mondello), the resort beach 11km north, is connected by bus and worth an afternoon in summer. [Monreale](/europe/italy/sicily/monreale), with its extraordinary Norman cathedral mosaics, is a twenty-minute drive and one of the essential day trips from the city.
