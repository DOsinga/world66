Marina di Campo sits on the south coast of [Elba](/europe/italy/tuscany/elba) where a broad, flat-bottomed valley meets the sea, giving the town what the rest of the island mostly lacks: a long sandy beach. At nearly two kilometres of fine golden sand, it is the largest beach on Elba, and that fact drives the town's entire economy. This is the family resort side of the island — busier and more commercial than Portoferraio or Marciana Marina, but hard to argue with when the water is clear turquoise and the beach is this wide.

The old fishing village centre clusters around a small harbour at the western end of the bay, where boats still go out at night and the waterfront restaurants serve what they brought back. The modern resort stretches east along the beach, backed by pine woods and a flat grid of streets that fills solidly in July and August. A few kilometres inland, at La Pila, sits the only airport in the Tuscan Archipelago — small, seasonal, but a useful entry point for visitors flying in from northern Europe.

History here runs deeper than the sunbeds suggest. The area has been inhabited since ancient times; Roman-era graves were found near the Castiglione hill above town, and the round watchtower at the harbour dates to the sixteenth century, built by the Pisans to watch for Barbary pirates. The municipality itself is relatively young, founded in 1894, but the village it was carved from — then known simply as Campo — had been a small fishing community for centuries, surrounded by vineyards on the hills and a pine forest that still gives the coast its character.

Come here for the beach, the diving on the offshore reefs, and the easy access to the rest of the island. The airport and the beach together make Marina di Campo the busiest entry point after Portoferraio in summer. Go in May, June, or September and you get the beach to yourself and restaurants that are open but not frantic.
