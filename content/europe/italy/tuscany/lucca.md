Lucca is a compact, walled city on the Tuscan plain between Pisa and Florence — and for many people who know Tuscany well, the pick of the three. Where Pisa has the tower and Florence has the Uffizi, Lucca has something rarer: it is still a real, working, lived-in Italian town rather than a tourist backdrop. The medieval centre is almost entirely surrounded by a thick Renaissance wall, four kilometres long, built in the 16th and 17th centuries against Florentine attack and never actually used. Today the top of the ramparts has been turned into a broad elm-lined promenade — arguably the most pleasant urban park in Italy — where you can walk, jog, or (much better) rent a bicycle and cycle right around the top of the city in about 40 minutes.

Inside the walls, Lucca is a maze of narrow stone streets, Romanesque churches, and handsome 17th-century palazzi. Start at **Piazza San Michele**, built on the site of the old Roman forum and dominated by the extraordinary striped marble façade of **San Michele in Foro** — a 13th-century church whose upper storeys are pure Pisan-Romanesque fantasy, with tier upon tier of mismatched twisted columns and heraldic animals. The city's other great church is the **Duomo di San Martino**, just inside the southern walls, with a wonky asymmetrical façade (the builders ran out of room) and, inside, Nicola Pisano's *Deposition* and the **Volto Santo**, a mysterious dark-wood crucifix that legend says was carved from life by Nicodemus and that has been venerated here since the 8th century.

Lucca's single most photographed spot is **Piazza dell'Anfiteatro**, a perfect oval square built into the footprint of the old Roman amphitheatre. The amphitheatre is long gone, but the shape is preserved in the pastel-coloured houses that ring the square — now filled with cafés and a handful of restaurants. Nearby, the **Torre Guinigi** is the odd medieval tower with a clump of holm oaks growing out of the top. Climb it for the best view of Lucca's terracotta rooftops.

Lucca was the birthplace of Giacomo Puccini, and every summer the **Puccini Festival** at Torre del Lago, a few kilometres away on Lake Massaciuccoli, puts on open-air performances of *La Bohème*, *Tosca*, *Madama Butterfly*, and *Turandot* in the theatre Puccini helped design. In the city itself, his birthplace near Piazza San Michele is open as a small museum. Lucca is also the centre of one of Italy's finest olive-oil regions — the Lucchese oil pressed in the surrounding hills is pale-gold, peppery, and sold in every alimentari in town. Eat tordelli lucchesi (meat-stuffed pasta in ragù) and buccellato (a sweet anise-and-raisin bread), stay at least one night rather than day-tripping from Pisa or Florence, and take a long evening walk on the walls.
