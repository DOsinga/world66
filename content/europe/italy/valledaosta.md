Valle d'Aosta is Italy's smallest and highest region, wedged into the northwest corner of the country between the Mont Blanc massif and the Great St Bernard Pass. The mountains here are not background scenery — they are the point. Mont Blanc, the Matterhorn, Gran Paradiso, and the Monte Rosa massif all crowd the skyline, and the valley floor sits at over a thousand metres before the passes climb much higher.

The region has its own cultural identity, shaped by centuries of Savoy rule and proximity to France. French is co-official with Italian, and the local Valdôtain dialect still survives in the villages. The food and architecture feel Alpine rather than Mediterranean — polenta and fontina cheese feature more than pasta and olive oil, and the stone towers and fortified castles that dot the valley reflect a history of strategic importance on the main routes between Italy and northern Europe.

[Aosta](/europe/italy/valledaosta/aosta) is the regional capital and the place to see the Roman remains — the Porta Praetoria, the amphitheatre, and the Arco di Augusto are among the best-preserved in the Alps. For winter sports, [Courmayeur](/europe/italy/valledaosta/courmayeur) at the foot of Mont Blanc is the most fashionable resort, drawing weekenders from [Milan](/europe/italy/lombardia/milan) and [Turin](/europe/italy/piemonte/turin). [Cervinia](/europe/italy/valledaosta/cervinia) above Breuil offers gentler, higher slopes with a link to Zermatt across the Swiss border.
