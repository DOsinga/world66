Veneto is one of Italy's most rewarding regions, and not just because it contains Venice. The northeast corner of the country packs an extraordinary range into a compact area: lagoon cities, Roman amphitheatres, Renaissance hill towns, alpine ski resorts, and some of the country's finest wine country, all within a few hours of each other.

[Venice](/europe/italy/veneto/venice) gets the headlines, and deserves them — the canal city is genuinely unlike anywhere else on earth. But [Verona](/europe/italy/veneto/verona) has an ancient arena that still hosts opera in summer and a charming historic centre that most visitors barely scratch. [Padua](/europe/italy/veneto/padua) holds the Scrovegni Chapel, home to Giotto's frescoes, and the oldest university in the world still in continuous operation. [Vicenza](/europe/italy/veneto/vicenza) is a living showcase of Palladio's architecture, the man whose designs shaped everything from English country houses to the US Capitol.

Away from the cities, the Dolomites rise sharply in the north around [Cortina d'Ampezzo](/europe/italy/veneto/cortina_dampezzo), offering world-class skiing and hiking. The rolling hills around Conegliano and Valdobbiadene produce Prosecco — and the wine route through those vineyards is one of the most pleasant drives in northern Italy. The shores of Lake Garda in the west, shared with Lombardy, attract families and cyclists.

Veneto rewards slow travel. Even the lesser-known towns — [Asolo](/europe/italy/veneto/asolo), [Treviso](/europe/italy/veneto/treviso), [Chioggia](/europe/italy/veneto/chioggia) — have genuine character and far fewer crowds than Venice. If you're based in the region for more than a few days, use Venice as a day trip rather than a base, and you'll see a completely different side of things.
