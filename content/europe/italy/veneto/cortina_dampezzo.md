Cortina d'Ampezzo sits in a broad alpine valley surrounded by the jagged peaks of the Dolomites, and it looks the part. The town hosted the 1956 Winter Olympics and has been a playground for Italian skiers and European celebrities ever since. The setting is genuinely dramatic — rose-tinted rock towers rise on every side, and the light at sunset turns them shades of pink and orange that photographs never quite capture.

Winter brings serious skiing across several interconnected areas, with Tofana, Faloria, and Cinque Torri offering runs for all levels. The town is part of the massive Dolomiti Superski network, which links dozens of resorts across the range. But Cortina is not just a ski town. In summer the same mountains become a paradise for hiking and via ferrata routes, with trails threading through high passes, across wildflower meadows, and past the remains of World War I fortifications carved into the rock at impossible altitudes.

The town itself is compact and walkable, centred on the Corso Italia pedestrian street lined with cafes, boutiques, and sporting goods shops. It has the polished feel of a resort that caters to well-heeled Italians — prices reflect this — but the mountains are free, and once you are on the trails you are sharing them with marmots rather than fur coats. The food is a mix of Veneto and Tyrolean traditions: polenta, game, dumplings, and strudel, washed down with local wines from the valleys below.
