Venice is the city you have already pictured before you arrive. The fairytale labyrinth of canals, alleys and gilded squares is real; the catch is that it is also crowded, expensive, sometimes shabby, and from June to August faintly malodorous. None of which matters for very long. An hour after stepping off the train at Santa Lucia and walking out into the sestieri, the strangeness of a city built on water and almost entirely closed to cars takes over. Get lost on purpose for at least a day; the alleys go nowhere predictable, every corner opens onto something photographable, and the absence of traffic noise is something you stop noticing only when you leave.

The historic centre is divided into six **sestieri** — districts that sit usefully in the head as a way to navigate. **San Marco** is the postcard one: Piazza San Marco, the Basilica, the Doge's Palace, the most expensive cafes in Italy. Most day-trippers never leave this sestiere. **Castello**, immediately east, is bigger and quieter and home to the Biennale gardens. **Cannaregio**, in the north, holds the world's first Jewish Ghetto and a string of churches that still see actual congregations. South across the Grand Canal lies **Dorsoduro**, the museum district — the [Gallerie dell'Accademia](/europe/italy/veneto/venice/things_to_do/accademia) for Venetian painting, the [Peggy Guggenheim](/europe/italy/veneto/venice/things_to_do/peggy_guggenheim) for modernism — and Campo Santa Margherita, the closest thing Venice has to a normal neighbourhood square. **San Polo** and **Santa Croce** sit between Dorsoduro and the railway station, with the Rialto market and the Frari church anchoring them.

The best way to see the [Grand Canal](/europe/italy/veneto/venice/things_to_do/the_grand_canal) is from a vaporetto No. 1, which stops at every landing along its 3.8km S-curve. A gondola ride is a classic, expensive and short — if you do one, take it through the smaller side canals rather than the Grand Canal itself. Better still, just walk: the city is small enough to cross on foot in 45 minutes, and there is no place in Europe where wandering pays off more.

Beyond the centre, the **lagoon** is its own world. **Murano** has glass workshops you can tour, **Burano** is the photogenic fishing island of brightly painted houses, and **Torcello** holds the oldest building in the lagoon — a 7th-century cathedral with extraordinary mosaics. The **Lido**, the long sandbar east of the city, has actual beaches and the September Film Festival.

Venice is sinking, slowly. Pessimists give it fifty years before flooding becomes intolerable; optimists point to the MOSE flood barrier system finally operational since 2020. Either way, see it now.
