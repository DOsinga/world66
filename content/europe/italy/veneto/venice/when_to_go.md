---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

Venice rewards visitors who avoid the obvious. **Summer** is the worst time to come — heat, mosquitoes, the sticky smell of low water in the side canals, and crowds so thick that the alleys around San Marco become single-file. The exceptions are the **Biennale** (the international art exhibition that runs from May to November in odd years and the architecture biennale in even years) and the **Festa del Redentore** on the third Sunday of July, when the city stages an enormous fireworks show across the Bacino and locals watch from boats.

**October to early November** is the sweet spot — crisp air, manageable crowds, and the start of the autumn light that flatters the city's faded palaces. **Acqua alta**, the seasonal high tides that flood low-lying parts of the city, begin in October; the city lays out elevated walkways and the floods generally last only a few hours.

**Winter** can be foggy, damp and quietly magical, with hardly a tourist in sight — except during the two weeks of **Carnevale** before Lent, when the city explodes back into masked, costumed extravagance. **Spring** is mild and lovely; aperitivo on an outdoor terrace is one of the great Venetian pleasures. The **Vogalonga** rowing regatta in mid-May draws over 400 boats from clubs across the lagoon and beyond — a less commercial alternative to the historical regattas later in the year.
