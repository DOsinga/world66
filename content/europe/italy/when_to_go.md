---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

Italy is a year-round destination, but the experience varies dramatically by season.

**Spring (April–May)** is the best time to visit most of the country. Temperatures are comfortable (15–25°C), flowers are out, and the summer crowds have not yet arrived. Easter brings its own festivals and processions — worth timing a visit around, though it does draw more tourists. Late April and May are as good as Italy gets.

**Summer (June–August)** is peak season. Crowds are heavy at every major city and well-known beach. July and August in Rome, Florence, and Naples are genuinely hot (30–38°C) and uncomfortable for all-day sightseeing. August is complicated: Italians take their holidays then, which means cities can feel hollowed out while coastal resorts are impossibly crowded. Ferragosto on 15 August is a national holiday and many businesses close for the surrounding week. If you visit in summer, arrive early at every major site and plan a midday rest.

**Autumn (September–October)** rivals spring as the best time to visit. The heat drops to something manageable, harvest festivals start, and the truffle season opens in October. Crowds thin noticeably after mid-September. Wine regions — Chianti, Barolo, Soave — are at their most beautiful in October. The sea is still warm enough to swim in September.

**Winter (November–March)** has real merits. Prices drop sharply, the crowds almost entirely disappear, and cities like Rome take on a different character without the tourist crush. Christmas markets appear in the north (Trentino and South Tyrol have Italy's best). Skiing runs from December through March in the Alps and Dolomites. The downside: smaller museums and tourist facilities reduce hours or close, and southern coastal areas can feel very quiet. Carnival in Venice in February is spectacular if you can handle the cold.
