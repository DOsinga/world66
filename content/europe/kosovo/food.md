---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Kosovar food is hearty, meat-heavy, and deeply influenced by Ottoman and Albanian traditions — with Serbian and broader Balkan flavours mixed in. Don't come expecting culinary innovation, but do come expecting to eat well and cheaply.

**Qebapa** (or qebab) are the national street food: small grilled minced-meat sausages, usually a mix of beef and lamb, served with flatbread (samun or pitalka), fresh onion, and ajvar (a roasted pepper relish). Every town has qebap joints; quality varies but the best are excellent, and the price is almost always under €3 for a portion.

**Flija** is Kosovo's most distinctively local dish — layers of batter cooked slowly over an open fire in a covered pan, producing something between a crepe and a pastry. It's time-consuming to make and found mainly at home or at special occasions, but some restaurants serve it. Worth seeking out.

**Burek** is the universal Balkan filo pastry, here filled with meat (me mish), cheese (me djathë), or spinach. Pristina bakeries have excellent burek; it's a common breakfast with yoghurt or ayran (a salty yoghurt drink).

**Tavë** — baked dishes in clay pots, often meat and vegetables — appear on most menus. Lamb is popular; bean dishes (fasule) are a staple. Grilled meats generally dominate restaurant menus.

**Dairy** is taken seriously. Kosovar white cheese (djathë i bardhë) is salty, crumbly, and good. Kaçkavall is a hard yellow cheese. Fresh dairy products — yoghurt, cream, butter — are found at village markets and are worth trying.

**Sweets** lean Ottoman: baklava, tulumba (fried dough in syrup), trilece (a three-milk sponge cake that has become fashionable across the Balkans). Coffee culture is strong — espresso is the default, served tiny and strong.

**Beer** is cheap and local: Peja Birra from Peja is the main domestic lager. Rakia (fruit brandy) is offered everywhere as a greeting drink — refusing is impolite; accepting modestly is wise.

Pristina and Prizren have restaurants beyond the Balkan standard — there is decent Italian, and some newer restaurants in Pristina's Dardania district experiment with contemporary Albanian and Kosovar ingredients. But don't overthink it: the best meals are often the simplest ones.
