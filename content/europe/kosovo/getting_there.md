## By Air

Pristina International Airport Adem Jashari (PRN) is Kosovo's only international airport, 18 km southwest of the capital. It is modern and efficient. Airlines serving Pristina include Wizz Air, Austrian Airlines, SWISS, Turkish Airlines, Lufthansa, Eurowings, and several others. There are direct flights from most major Western European hubs — London, Vienna, Zurich, Frankfurt, Istanbul — as well as seasonal connections. No direct flights operate between Belgrade and Pristina.

From the airport, minibuses (furgons) and taxis run to Pristina city centre. The bus station is 3–4 km from the centre; taxis from the airport to the centre cost around €15–20 and the journey takes 20–30 minutes.

## By Bus

International bus services connect Pristina with Tirana, Skopje, Belgrade, Sarajevo, and various cities in Germany, Switzerland, and Austria (serving the large Kosovar diaspora). These are overnight coaches, generally reliable and cheap. From Tirana the journey takes around 4–5 hours; from Skopje around 1.5–2 hours.

## By Land from Albania

The main crossing is at Morina/Qafe Morines (southwest of Prizren, connecting to Kukës and Tirana). The Vermica crossing in the south is the main one for the Pristina–Tirana route. Crossings are generally quick; Albanian and Kosovo border services are both professional.

## By Land from North Macedonia

The Hani i Elezit (Blace) crossing south of Pristina on the main Skopje–Pristina highway is the busiest and most straightforward. The Jazhince crossing further north is also used. Frequent buses and shared taxis (furgons) connect Skopje and Pristina.

## By Land from Montenegro

The Kulla/Čakor crossing in the north connects with Rožaje in Montenegro. Less used than the Albanian or Macedonian crossings but perfectly functional.

## The Serbia Situation

This requires some care. Serbia does not recognize Kosovo's independence and does not consider the Kosovo–Serbia crossing points to be international border crossings. If you enter Kosovo directly from Serbia and then attempt to enter Serbia again, you may be refused entry or face complications — Serbian border officials can treat the Kosovo entry stamp as evidence of illegal entry into Serbia. To avoid this, enter Kosovo via Albania, North Macedonia, or Montenegro. If you are starting in Serbia, cross to a third country first before entering Kosovo.

The crossing from Kosovo into Serbia at Merdare (on the main Pristina–Niš road) does function in practice for many travelers, but it remains politically sensitive. Check current travel advisories before relying on this route.
