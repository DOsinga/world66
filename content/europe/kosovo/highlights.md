---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Kosovo rewards curiosity. It's easy to underestimate a country this small, but the combination of Ottoman architecture, Serb medieval monasteries, dramatic mountain scenery, and an unusually warm reception from locals makes it more than worth the detour.

**[Pristina](/europe/kosovo/pristina)** is the capital and the obvious starting point. It's a city of contradictions — crumbling concrete blocks alongside polished new cafés, a UNHCR presence alongside a young population that grew up entirely post-independence. The Newborn monument (repainted every year since 2008), the National Library's startling honeycombed facade, and the Bill Clinton statue on Bulevardi Bill Klinton are among the sights. The old bazaar is compact but worth an hour. Pristina's café culture is genuinely good — strong espresso, low prices, and people who want to talk.

**Prizren** is the most beautiful city in Kosovo and one of the more beautiful towns in the Western Balkans. An Ottoman-era centre with cobbled lanes, the Bistrica river running through it, a hilltop fortress above, and a skyline of minarets and domes. The League of Prizren building commemorates the 1878 Albanian national movement. Prizren hosts **Dokufest**, one of Europe's best documentary film festivals, every August — outdoor screenings, packed streets, and an international crowd.

**Peja (Peć)** is the gateway to the Rugova Gorge and the Accursed Mountains (Bjeshkët e Nemuna / Prokletije). The gorge is spectacular even from the road; serious hikers can access via ferrata routes and multi-day trails that cross into Albania or Montenegro. The Patriarchate of Peć, a complex of three medieval Serb Orthodox churches inside a walled monastery at the mouth of the gorge, is one of the most important religious sites in Kosovo and is UNESCO-listed.

**Dečani Monastery** (near Deçan, south of Peja) is arguably the finest medieval church in the Balkans — a 14th-century Royal Church with an extraordinary carved marble facade and frescoes that cover every interior surface. It is guarded by Italian KFOR troops and is still an active Serb Orthodox monastery. Entry is free and visitors are welcome, though modest dress is required.

**Gračanica Monastery**, just outside Pristina, is another UNESCO-listed Serb Orthodox church from the early 14th century — a smaller and more accessible introduction to Kosovo's medieval heritage than Dečani.

The **Sharr Mountains** in the south offer skiing in winter (the Brezovica resort, recently being redeveloped) and hiking in summer. The roads into the mountains pass through villages that see very few foreign visitors.
