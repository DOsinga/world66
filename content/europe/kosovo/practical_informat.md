## Currency

Kosovo uses the euro (€) as its official currency despite not being a member of the eurozone — it adopted it unilaterally after independence. Cash is still king for everyday transactions, especially outside Pristina; carry euros. ATMs are available in all towns. Credit cards are accepted at hotels, larger restaurants, and some shops in Pristina and Prizren, but don't count on them elsewhere.

Kosovo is one of the cheapest countries in Europe. A coffee costs €0.50–1, a restaurant meal €4–8, a bus ride €3–5. Budget travellers can get by on €25–35 per day including accommodation.

## Visas

Citizens of the EU, USA, UK, Canada, Australia, and around 90 other countries can enter Kosovo visa-free for up to 90 days. No visa is required on arrival. Your passport should be valid for at least three months beyond your planned departure.

See the Serbia border situation note in [Getting There](/europe/kosovo/getting_there) if your itinerary involves both Kosovo and Serbia.

## Language

Albanian is the primary official language; Serbian is also official. English is widely spoken among younger people, especially in Pristina and Prizren. German and Italian are also useful, given diaspora connections.

## Electricity

Type C and F plugs (the standard European round two-pin). 230V/50Hz — no adaptor needed for European devices; US devices need a transformer.

## Phone and Internet

The country code is +383. Mobile coverage is good across the main towns and corridors; more patchy in mountain areas. Kosovo has its own .xk internet domain but uses standard international systems. SIM cards are cheap and easy to buy. Wi-Fi is available in most cafés in Pristina and Prizren.

## Health

No vaccinations are required. Healthcare standards are basic; medical facilities outside Pristina are limited. Travel insurance with medical evacuation cover is advisable. Tap water quality varies — bottled water is cheap and widely available.

Landmines and unexploded ordnance remain a hazard in remote mountain areas, particularly in the west near the Albanian and Montenegrin borders. Stick to marked paths and established routes when hiking.

## Safety

Kosovo is generally safe for tourists. [Pristina](/europe/kosovo/pristina) and Prizren are relaxed cities with low levels of street crime. Standard precautions apply: keep valuables out of sight, use authorised taxi services.

The exception is the north — the municipalities of Mitrovica north of the Ibar river, Zvečan, Zubin Potok, and Leposavic — where ethnic tensions between Kosovo Albanians and Serbs have periodically led to violent incidents. The UK, US, and other governments advise against non-essential travel to these areas. Do not ignore this advice; the situation can escalate quickly.

## Recognition

Kosovo declared independence in February 2008. As of 2025, it is recognized by over 100 countries including the US, UK, Germany, France, and most EU members. Serbia, Russia, China, Spain, and several others do not recognize it. This affects practical matters for Kosovar passport holders but not for foreign tourists.
