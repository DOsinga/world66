---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Latvian food is hearty, seasonal, and rooted in what the land and sea provide: rye bread, pork, dairy, potatoes, root vegetables, fish, and berries. It's not glamorous, but it's honest, and the best of it is genuinely good.

**Rye bread** (rupjmaize) is the cornerstone of Latvian eating — dense, slightly sour, made with malt and caraway. It's served with almost everything and exported with national pride. Grey peas with bacon (pelēkie zirņi ar speķi) is the unofficial national dish, a simple winter staple that shows up at Jāņi celebrations.

**Cold beet soup** (aukstā zupa) is a summer essential — vivid pink, made with kefir, beets, cucumber, and hard-boiled eggs, always topped with dill. It's better than it sounds. Meatball soup (frikadeļu zupa) is the comforting everyday counterpart.

**Fish** features heavily on the coast and in Riga: smoked sprats, herring in various preparations, and fresh pike-perch from inland lakes. The Central Market in Riga has an excellent fish pavilion.

**Sklandrausis** is a traditional rye-pastry tart filled with carrot and potato, flavoured with caraway — it holds a EU Traditional Speciality Guaranteed designation. **Jāņu siers** is caraway-seed cheese traditionally made for Midsummer.

Riga's restaurant scene has improved markedly in recent years, with a good selection of places doing modern Latvian cooking alongside the usual European options. Outside the capital, choices narrow quickly, but most towns have at least a couple of decent cafes.
