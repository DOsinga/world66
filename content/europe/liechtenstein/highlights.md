---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**Vaduz Castle**

The iconic seat of the princely family stands on a rocky promontory above the capital and is visible from almost anywhere in the Rhine valley. The castle itself is the prince's private residence and not open to the public, but the walk up from Vaduz takes about 20 minutes and rewards you with excellent views over the valley into Switzerland and Austria. On National Day (August 15), the prince opens the castle grounds to the public and hosts a reception for all comers.

**Liechtenstein National Museum**

A well-curated survey of the country's history, natural history, and art, housed in a restored patrician building in central Vaduz. The permanent collection covers everything from Roman-era findings to the development of the principality. Worth an hour or two.

**Kunstmuseum Liechtenstein**

Vaduz's contemporary art museum is a striking black basalt cube on the edge of the pedestrian zone. The permanent collection features international modern and contemporary art as well as works from the princely collections. The rotating exhibitions are consistently strong for a country this size.

**Stamp Museum (Postmuseum)**

Liechtenstein's stamps have been collector's items since the first series in 1912. This small but engaging museum in Vaduz covers the philatelic history of the principality, with complete collections on display. For non-collectors, it also tells a good deal about Liechtenstein's history and the role stamps have played in the national economy.

**Hofkellerei (Prince's Winery)**

The princely family has made wine on the Rhine valley slopes since 1712. The Hofkellerei in Vaduz produces Pinot Noir and Chardonnay that are perfectly respectable by any standard. Guided tastings are available; the adjacent restaurant Torkel, set among the vineyards, holds a Michelin star.

**Triesenberg**

The mountain village above Vaduz has its own identity — it was settled by the Walser people from the Swiss canton of Valais in the 13th century and still maintains a distinct dialect and cultural tradition. The Walser Museum documents this history. The village itself, with its dark timber chalets and onion-domed church, is worth the drive up.

**Malbun**

At 1,600 metres in the eastern mountains, Malbun is the country's only ski resort. In winter it offers a handful of lifts suited to beginners and intermediates. In summer it becomes a hiking base, with trails ranging from easy valley walks to serious alpine routes. The views back down over the Rhine valley on a clear day are superb.

**The Liechtenstein Trail**

This 75-kilometre marked route connects all eleven communes, passing through vineyards, forest, riverside meadows, and mountain terrain. It is a genuine way to see the whole country rather than just Vaduz. Most people walk it in sections over two to four days.
