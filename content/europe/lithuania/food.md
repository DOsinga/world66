title: "Food"

---

Traditional Lithuanian cooking is hearty, filling, and built around potatoes, pork, dairy, and rye bread — well suited to cold northern winters. It's the kind of food you want after a long day outdoors.

**Cepelinai** (zeppelins) are the national dish: enormous potato dumplings stuffed with minced meat or curd cheese, served with sour cream and bacon bits. They're named after Count von Zeppelin's airships, which they resemble. A single serving is a meal. They're found everywhere, from village canteens to city restaurants.

**Šaltibarščiai** is cold beetroot soup, bright pink, served with boiled egg and a side of hot potatoes. A Lithuanian summer staple, refreshing on a warm day, and visually striking if you haven't seen it before.

**Kibinai** are baked pastries filled with mutton or other meat, a speciality of the Karaite community in Trakai. Worth going out of your way for.

**Kepta duona** (fried bread) is dark rye bread cut into strips, deep-fried, rubbed with garlic, and served with a cheese dip. It appears as a bar snack across the country and is addictive.

**Rye bread** itself deserves mention — Lithuanian dark rye is dense, slightly sour, and excellent. You'll find it everywhere.

**Beer and spirits.** Lithuania has a strong craft beer culture, with many small breweries producing interesting ales. Svyturys and Utenos are the main commercial lagers. Midus (mead) is a traditional drink worth trying. Krupnikas is a spiced honey liqueur that appears at celebrations.

Vilnius in particular has seen a restaurant explosion in the past decade. The 2025 Michelin Guide recognised 37 Lithuanian restaurants, with several in [Vilnius](/europe/lithuania/vilnius) and a handful in [Kaunas](/europe/lithuania/kaunas). You can eat very well without spending much by Western European standards.
