---
title: "Language"
type: section
---

Luxembourg has three official languages: Luxembourgish, French, and German. This isn't a source of tension — most Luxembourgers move between all three without thinking about it, often in the same conversation.

**Luxembourgish** (Lëtzebuergesch) is the national language and the everyday spoken tongue. It's a Moselle-Franconian dialect that became an official language in 1984, and it functions as the primary marker of Luxembourgish identity. It is taught in primary schools and offered in language courses aimed at the large foreign-resident population.

**French** is the language of government, law, parliament, and administration. Official documents, street signs in the capital, and most menus are in French. Many of the country's literary and cultural institutions operate in French.

**German** is used in schools (as a language of instruction in the early years), in much of the press, and widely in religious life. In rural areas near the German border you'll hear it more often.

**English** is widely spoken, especially among younger people and the large international community in Luxembourg City. In practice, most visitors can get by in English without difficulty.

If you're making an effort, a few words of Luxembourgish go a long way. "Moien" (hello), "Merci" (thank you — shared with French), and "Äddi" (goodbye) will earn you goodwill almost anywhere.
