title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Luxembourg is compact enough to visit its main sights in a long weekend, but varied enough to reward a slower trip. The country divides naturally into four regions, each with its own character.

**Luxembourg City**

The capital is the obvious starting point. The UNESCO-listed Old Town sits on a dramatic rocky outcrop above the Alzette and Pétrusse valleys, ringed by the remains of Europe's most impressive fortifications. The Bock Casemates — an extraordinary network of underground tunnels carved into the sandstone — are not to be missed. The Chemin de la Corniche promenade, often called "Europe's most beautiful balcony," winds along the old ramparts with views across the gorge. See [Luxembourg City](/europe/luxembourg/luxembourgcity) for the full picture.

**The Mullerthal (Little Switzerland)**

The northeast of the country is Luxembourg's most dramatic landscape: dense beech forests, sandstone gorges, and weird rock formations that earned the region its Swiss nickname. [Echternach](/europe/luxembourg/echternach), the oldest town in Luxembourg, makes the best base. From here a network of well-marked hiking trails — notably the Mullerthal Trail — winds through the gorges. [Beaufort](/europe/luxembourg/beaufort) has a fine ruined castle and good climbing.

**Vianden and the Our Valley**

[Vianden](/europe/luxembourg/vianden) is arguably Luxembourg's most picturesque small town. The massive Gothic-Romanesque castle above the Our Valley was largely ruined before a careful restoration in the latter 20th century — it is now one of the finest medieval castles in the Benelux. Victor Hugo lived in exile here and you can visit the house where he stayed. A chairlift gives good views over the valley.

**The Ardennes (Éislek)**

The sparsely populated north has a rugged beauty quite different from the softer landscapes of the south. [Clervaux](/europe/luxembourg/clervaux) has a fine castle housing Edward Steichen's photography exhibition "The Family of Man" (a UNESCO Memory of the World collection), as well as a Benedictine abbey. The Ardennes saw some of the heaviest fighting in the Battle of the Bulge (1944–45); museums and cemeteries dot the region.

**The Moselle Valley**

The southeastern border with Germany follows the Moselle river through a gentle landscape of vineyards and wine villages. This is where Luxembourg's white wines are produced — light, crisp Rieslings and Auxerrois, and an increasingly respected Crémant sparkling wine. The towns of Remich, Grevenmacher, and Wasserbillig are pleasant for a day's cycling and wine-tasting along the river. [Wasserbillig](/europe/luxembourg/wasserbillig) sits right on the German border.
