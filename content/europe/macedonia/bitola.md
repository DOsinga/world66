Bitola is North Macedonia's second city, set in the wide Pelagonia valley close to the Greek border. It has a relaxed, unhurried feel that distinguishes it from [Skopje](/europe/macedonia/skopje), and its Ottoman-era central boulevard, Širok Sokak, lined with 19th-century buildings and cafe terraces, gives it more elegance than most Macedonian towns.

On the edge of the city lie the ruins of Heraclea Lyncestis, a Greek city founded by Philip II of Macedon in the 4th century BC. The mosaics here — remarkably well-preserved — are among the finest in the Balkans and largely overlooked by tourists.

Bitola has a notable connection to modern Turkish history: Mustafa Kemal, later known as Atatürk, attended military school here in the early 1900s and reportedly spent some of his formative years in the city. A memorial to him stands near the school.

The Manastir Jewish community, once significant, maintained a synagogue and cemetery here until World War II; almost the entire community was deported and killed in 1943. The Jewish cemetery survives.
