**Currency**

Malta uses the euro (€). ATMs are widespread and credit cards are accepted in most hotels, restaurants, and shops. Cash is still expected in village bars, small cafes, and market stalls.

**Language**

Maltese (Malti) and English are both official languages. English is spoken everywhere — on signs, menus, and in everyday conversation — so language is rarely a barrier.

**Electricity**

230V/50Hz. British-style three-pin rectangular plugs are standard, inherited from British rule. Continental European two-pin adaptors will not work without an adaptor, which most hotels can provide.

**Telephone**

Country code +356. Mobile coverage is good across both Malta and Gozo. EU citizens benefit from roaming-included plans. Local SIM cards are cheap and available at the airport and phone shops.

**Health**

Medical care is generally of a good standard. Malta's main hospital is Mater Dei in Msida. EU citizens with an EHIC card can access public health services. Non-EU visitors should carry travel insurance. Tap water is safe to drink, though many people find it tastes slightly brackish and prefer bottled water.

**Safety**

Malta is one of the safer countries in Europe. Petty theft can occur in Valletta and St Julian's, particularly in summer. Standard urban precautions apply.

**Tipping**

Around 10% in restaurants if service is not already included. Rounding up for taxis and cafes is appreciated but not obligatory.

**Opening hours**

Many smaller shops and services close for a long lunch break (roughly 13:00–16:00), especially outside the main tourist areas. This tradition is less observed in Valletta and Sliema but remains common in smaller towns. Banks are typically open Monday–Friday 08:30–13:30.

**Tourist offices**

The Malta Tourism Authority operates a visitor centre at Arrivals in Malta International Airport. There is also an office at 229 Merchants Street, Valletta.
