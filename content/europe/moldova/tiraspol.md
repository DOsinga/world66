Tiraspol is the capital of Transnistria, the unrecognized breakaway republic that declared independence from Moldova in 1990 and has maintained a frozen existence since. The city sits on the east bank of the Dniester, about 70 km from Chisinau.

Walking the main boulevard — Karl Marx Street — past Lenin statues, Soviet-era murals, and the parliament building guarded by a tank frozen on a plinth, is a genuinely disorienting experience. The currency (Transnistrian roubles) is accepted nowhere outside the republic. The shops are stocked with Russian goods. The infrastructure is Soviet in pace and style. It is not threatening; it is simply different in a way that is hard to describe.

The Kvint cognac factory, which has been distilling since 1897, offers tours and sells bottles at prices far below what you would pay in Chisinau. The Sheriff supermarket chain, owned by the company that effectively runs Transnistria's economy, is a bizarre monument to private enterprise in what presents itself as a socialist state.

From Chisinau, marshrutkas and shared taxis run regularly to Tiraspol (about 1.5 hours). At the checkpoint you receive a paper entry slip with a validity period — keep it and present it when you leave. Day trips are straightforward for most Western passport holders.
