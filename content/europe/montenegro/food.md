---
title: "Food and Drink"
type: section
---

Montenegrin food divides along the same lines as the landscape: the coast eats fish and seafood; the mountains eat meat, cheese, and cornmeal porridge. Both are worth exploring.

On the coast, **grilled fish** is the default order — sea bass, sea bream, and red mullet are all excellent. Ask for the catch of the day and let them grill it simply with olive oil and lemon. **Mussels** from the Bay of Kotor are a local speciality — the bay's water is unusually clean and the mussels are outstanding. Order them steamed in wine or buzara-style (with olive oil, garlic, and breadcrumbs). **Black risotto** made with cuttlefish ink shows up on most coastal menus and is reliably good.

In the mountains, **kačamak** is the comfort food — a thick porridge of cornmeal and potato, mixed with cheese and served with soured cream. It sounds simple and it is, but done well it's deeply satisfying. **Ispod sača** (meat slow-roasted under an iron bell covered in embers) takes hours to prepare and results in falling-apart lamb or veal. **Njeguški pršut** is the Montenegrin prosciutto, made in the village of Njeguši in the mountains above Kotor — saltier and smokier than Italian versions. Pair it with Njeguški sir, the local semi-hard cheese.

**Ćevapi** (small grilled meat sausages) and **burek** (filo pastry filled with meat, cheese, or spinach) are ubiquitous snacks and cheap — burek from a pekara (bakery) is the standard local breakfast.

**Wine**: Montenegro has a small but interesting wine production, centred on the Crmnica region around Lake Skadar. Vranac is the dominant local red variety — dark, tannic, and good with grilled meat. Krstač is the white equivalent, dry and citrusy. Plantaže is the major producer; their bottles are widely available and perfectly decent. Rakija (fruit brandy) is everywhere and offered freely by hosts — refusing is considered impolite but accepting repeated glasses is an endurance sport.

Restaurant prices on the coast in peak season are European; in the interior and out of season, food and drink are quite cheap.
