## By Car

A car is the most practical way to explore Montenegro, particularly if you want to reach Durmitor, Lake Skadar, or smaller coastal villages. The main coastal road and the highway connecting the coast to Podgorica are well-maintained. Mountain and rural roads can be narrow, unmarked, and steep — take them slowly and allow more time than your map suggests. Rental cars are available at Tivat and Podgorica airports and in most main towns. In peak summer (July–August) expect to pay €40–70 per day; rates drop significantly in shoulder season. Minimum rental age is typically 21.

Speed limits: 50km/h in towns, 80km/h on main roads, 100km/h on expressways. Police use radar routinely.

## By Bus

Buses are the backbone of inter-city transport and go most places you'd want to visit. The coastal route connects Herceg Novi, Kotor, Tivat, Budva, Petrovac, Bar, and Ulcinj; there are also regular services to Podgorica and into the north. Fares are cheap. Check schedules at Busticket4.me or at bus stations directly. Note: roadworks between Kotor and Budva have been ongoing — allow extra time for that stretch.

## By Train

Montenegro's rail network is limited but scenic. The main line runs from Bar on the coast north through Podgorica, Kolašin, and Bijelo Polje into Serbia. There is also a branch from Podgorica to Nikšić. Trains are slow, cheap, and often delayed, but the Bar–Podgorica–Kolašin stretch is worth taking for the scenery alone. Timetables and tickets at zpcg.me.

## Taxis and Rideshare

Taxis are metered in theory but negotiate or confirm the fare before getting in, especially at tourist spots. YANDEX and local apps operate in Podgorica. On the coast in summer, taxis can be expensive; many locals use shared minivans (kombis) for short hops.

## On Foot

Kotor's old town, Budva's old town, and Perast are all compact and best explored on foot. The Bay of Kotor drive is manageable with a car but not by foot — distances along the bay are longer than they look on a map.
