Montenegro — meaning "Black Mountain" in Venetian Italian — packs an extraordinary amount of landscape into a country smaller than Connecticut. In a four-hour drive you can move from a medieval walled harbour town on the Adriatic, past a lake that straddles the Albanian border, and up into a high mountain plateau with glacial lakes and one of Europe's deepest river canyons.

The country declared independence from Serbia in 2006 after a close referendum, and has since positioned itself as an EU candidate and a destination for upmarket tourism. The coastline, particularly around the Bay of Kotor and Budva, fills up fast in July and August — this is genuinely one of the most crowded parts of the Adriatic in peak season. Come in May, June, or September and you'll get the same landscape with a fraction of the people.

The interior is something else entirely. [Durmitor](/europe/montenegro/dormitor) National Park in the north has rafting, hiking, and some of the most dramatic scenery in the Balkans. [Lake Skadar](/europe/montenegro/skadarlake), partly shared with Albania, draws birdwatchers and kayakers. Between the coast and the mountains, [Cetinje](/europe/montenegro/cetinje), the old royal capital, sits quietly as a reminder that this was once a fiercely independent principality.

The country uses the euro despite not being in the EU. English is widely understood in tourist areas. People are generally direct and hospitable — the Montenegrin reputation for slowness (locals joke about it themselves) can translate into genuinely relaxed service once a connection is established.
