Podgorica is Montenegro's capital and largest city, with about 200,000 people — roughly a third of the country's population in one place. It's the administrative and economic centre, and despite its modest size, it has a genuine city feel: universities, embassies, a reasonable café culture, and some decent restaurants.

The city was heavily bombed in World War II and was rebuilt in a fairly utilitarian Yugoslav fashion. As a tourist destination it's not compelling — most travellers pass through rather than stay. But if you have a day, the old Turkish quarter of Stara Varoš (around the clock tower and the old mosque) gives some texture. The Morača Canyon on the edge of town is worth a detour — a short drive north, the Morača river cuts a dramatic gorge through the limestone, and the 13th-century Morača Monastery sits inside it.

The city sits in a valley and gets very hot in summer — temperatures of 40°C or above in July are not unusual, making Podgorica regularly one of the hottest cities in Europe during heatwaves. The coast is only an hour away if you need relief.

Podgorica Airport (TGD) handles most year-round international flights into Montenegro and is 15–20 minutes from the city centre by taxi. The main bus and train stations are central. Podgorica is a useful transit hub — buses and trains to Kotor, Budva, Bar, Ulcinj, the north, and Serbia all originate here.

Scadar Lake National Park is about 30km south of Podgorica, easily reached by car or taxi from the city.
