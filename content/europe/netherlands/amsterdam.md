Amsterdam is one of the most walkable cities in Europe, and the canal ring at its centre is genuinely as beautiful as advertised. Seventeen canals, 1,200 bridges, and rows of narrow gabled houses built during the 17th-century Golden Age when this was the wealthiest city on earth — it adds up to a place that rewards slow wandering more than any itinerary can capture.

The city is compact. From Central Station to Museumplein is about 3km on foot, passing through the heart of the old town. Get a day pass for trams if your legs give out, but resist the urge to take taxis — they are expensive and slow compared to a bicycle or tram. If you are staying more than two days, rent a bike: Amsterdam makes more sense on two wheels than on foot.

The museums are world-class without apology. The Rijksmuseum has the Night Watch and rooms of Dutch masters. The Van Gogh Museum is the single best place in the world to understand what Van Gogh was doing and why it matters. The Anne Frank House is something else entirely — not a museum in the conventional sense but a confrontation with history in the actual rooms where it happened.

Beyond the museums, Amsterdam is a city for cafes, brown bars and neighbourhood streets. The Jordaan, just west of the main canals, is the best neighbourhood for aimless walking. De Pijp, south of the Rijksmuseum, is where the city eats and drinks on an ordinary Tuesday. Leidseplein and Rembrandtplein are where it goes out on a Friday night.

Amsterdam (pop. 900,000) sits at the confluence of the Amstel river and the IJ, originally on the Zuiderzee and now bordering the freshwater IJsselmeer after centuries of land reclamation. The canal ring — Herengracht, Keizersgracht and Prinsengracht — forms a horseshoe around the historic centre, open at the north end where Central Station faces the water.
