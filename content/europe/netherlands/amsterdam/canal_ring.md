Amsterdam's centre divides between two distinct halves that are only a few minutes' walk apart but feel completely different.

The Canal Ring (Grachtengordel) is the UNESCO World Heritage site that gives the city its defining image. Three concentric semicircular canals — Herengracht, Keizersgracht, and Prinsengracht — were dug between 1613 and 1663 during Amsterdam's Golden Age, when the city was the wealthiest in the world. The canal houses all follow a narrow-frontage, deep-plan pattern dictated by the property tax (assessed by facade width), but each has its own gable type — step, neck, bell, or spout. Walking along [Keizersgracht](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/keizersgracht) and looking up at the rooflines is one of the best ways to understand how the city was built. The canal ring is best seen from the water: canal bikes are available and slow; boat tours give the architectural sequence from the right angle. The specific moment is at dusk in late spring or early autumn, when the trees are leafed out and the house lights are beginning to show in the water.

To the east, De Wallen — the Red Light District — is Amsterdam's oldest neighbourhood and retains a medieval street pattern quite unlike the orderly grids of the later city. The name comes from the wallen (embankments) of the original medieval settlement; the district predates the canal ring by centuries. The [Oude Kerk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/oude_kerk), founded around 1213, is the oldest building in the city, sitting incongruously at the centre of the red-light area with its Gothic nave and medieval choir stalls intact. Two streets away, [Our Lord in the Attic](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/our_lord_in_the_attic) hides a complete clandestine Catholic church in the attic of a 17th-century canal house. The sex work district is real and functioning: the window brothels along [Oudezijds Voorburgwal](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/oudezijds_voorburgwal) have operated since the 15th century. The area is chaotic on weekend evenings; considerably more interesting on a quiet weekday morning when the medieval architecture is visible. [Zeedijk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/zeedijk) — one of Amsterdam's oldest streets — marks the edge of the district and is now the centre of the city's Chinese community, with the Fo Guang Shan Buddhist temple at number 106. Nieuwmarkt square, anchored by the 15th-century Waag building, hosts an organic food market on Saturdays.
