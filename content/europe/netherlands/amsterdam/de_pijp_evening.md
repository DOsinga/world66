Get to the [Albert Cuypmarkt](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/albert_cuypmarkt) an hour before it closes — around four or five in the afternoon. The market is at its least crowded and most generous at this hour: the herring stall will press the last fish on you at a discount; the stroopwafel vendor is still going; the cheese seller has samples out. Nearly 300 stalls running almost a kilometre down Albert Cuypstraat through the heart of [De Pijp](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/de_pijp). Pick up something for later if you're cooking; graze if you're not.

When the stalls start closing, walk south to [Sarphatipark](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sarphatipark) — a small oval park at the centre of De Pijp, ringed by trees, with a fountain and benches. Bring something from the market and sit here for half an hour as the neighbourhood shifts from market day to evening. The park is genuinely used by the people who live around it; on warm evenings it fills with a density of languages that reflects how mixed this neighbourhood remains.

Dinner: the streets around and south of the market — Gerard Doustraat, Van Woustraat, Ceintuurbaan — have a density of good, affordable restaurants that serve the neighbourhood rather than tourists. Surinamese roti, Indonesian rice table, natural wine bars with small plates, North African. There is no singular recommendation; walk until something looks right. The places with handwritten menus and no English translations are usually the best.

Drinks after: De Pijp has bars that run late without being loud. The ones around Gerard Doustraat and the surrounding cross-streets are the most useful.
