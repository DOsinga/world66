De Pijp is the youngest of Amsterdam's classic neighbourhoods and the most urban in feel. Where the Jordaan was built for craftsmen and the canal ring for merchants, De Pijp was built for everyone else — quickly, cheaply, and densely, in the 1870s and 1880s when Amsterdam's population was expanding faster than the old city could absorb. The result is a grid of narrow streets lined with four-storey brick tenements, their facades barely wider than two windows, their ground floors long since converted into shops, cafes, and market stalls. Today it is one of the most interesting places to walk in the city.

Start at the [Heineken Experience](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/heineken_experience) on Stadhouderskade 78. The large red-brick complex was founded as a brewery in 1867 by Gerard Adriaan Heineken, who bought the failing De Hooiberg brewery on this site and rebuilt it on an industrial scale. For over a century the building produced beer for export across Europe; you could smell the hops from the tram. Production moved to a larger facility outside the city in 1988, and the brewery has been a museum ever since. You don't need to go in — the building is worth a look from the outside, its brewery scale conspicuous among the surrounding residential streets.

Cross Stadhouderskade and head south a short distance to [Marie Heinekenplein](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/marie_heinekenplein), a small square named for Gerard Adriaan's wife. The cafes here fill every terrace chair the moment the sun appears; on a warm afternoon it is difficult to pass through without stopping. This square marks the threshold: behind you is Stadhouderskade, the wide ring canal that separates De Pijp from the older city. In front of you the streets narrow and the neighbourhood begins.

Walk south along Ferdinand Bolstraat — the main north-south spine of De Pijp, named for the painter Ferdinand Bol, a pupil of Rembrandt. The streets crossing it to the left and right are named for other Dutch painters: Frans Hals, Jan Steen, Jacob van Ruisdael. The naming was not affectionate — it was simply the convention for a neighbourhood that was being built so fast that no one had time to think of better names. What the builders produced was functional housing for workers, and what time has made of it is a neighbourhood with genuine character.

[Albert Cuypmarkt](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/albert_cuypmarkt) runs the full 300-metre length of Albert Cuypstraat, from Ferdinand Bolstraat in the west to Ceintuurbaan in the east, six days a week. It is the largest daytime market in the Netherlands, and one of the few places in Amsterdam where the city's full diversity is visible all at once: Dutch families buying cheese and fish, Surinamese and Antillean vendors selling spices and tropical produce, Turkish stalls piled with fabric and clothing. Enter at Ferdinand Bolstraat and walk the full length to Ceintuurbaan. Near the western entrance, look for the bronze statue of André Hazes, the folk singer who was born in De Pijp in 1951. A comedian named Johnny Kraaykamp heard him singing at this market in 1959 — Hazes was eight years old — and arranged his first television appearance. Hazes became one of the most beloved popular musicians in Dutch history; when he died in 2004, 48,000 people came to Amsterdam Arena to say goodbye.

Buy a stroopwafel from one of the stalls that makes them fresh, pressed between two iron plates until the caramel centre softens. Walk the full length, looking back west from the far end: the market stretches away to a vanishing point, and on a busy day it is barely possible to see where you came from.

At Ceintuurbaan, turn south and walk down to [Sarphatipark](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sarphatipark), a small Victorian park a few minutes from the market's eastern end. It was designed in 1885 and named for Samuel Sarphati, a Jewish physician who transformed Amsterdam in the mid-19th century through a refuse collection service, a factory producing affordable bread for the poor, and grand building projects including the Amstel Hotel. Sarphati died in 1866 and never saw De Pijp built; the neighbourhood grew up around his legacy. During the German occupation, the park was renamed Bollandpark in 1942 because Sarphati was Jewish. The old name came back in 1945. The monument at the park's centre is easy to find.

From the park, walk west through the quiet backstreets to [Ruysdaelkade](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/ruysdaelkade), the canal that runs along De Pijp's southern edge. After the noise of the market, the canal is noticeably still. This is the older, calmer face of the neighbourhood — the canalside houses are well maintained and the water reflects their facades. Jacob van Ruisdael, for whom the canal is named, painted Dutch landscapes in the 17th century: flat horizons, wide skies, water reflecting light. The view along the canal has something of that quality. Stand here and consider what kind of city builds a neighbourhood this dense and names a canal after a landscape painter.

The walk is approximately 3.1 kilometres and takes about an hour at a comfortable pace, longer if you walk the full length of the market or stop at one of the cafes on Marie Heinekenplein.
