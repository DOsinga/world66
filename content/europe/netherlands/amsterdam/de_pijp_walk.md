De Pijp was built in a hurry and the hurry shows — which is exactly the point. Between 1870 and 1895, a municipal grid plan turned a stretch of Amsterdam polderland into a dense residential neighbourhood almost overnight, filling it with speculative tenements built fast and cheap for the people the city needed but could not properly house. The walk that follows covers the same streets as any tour of De Pijp, but it adds up to something bigger: a compressed argument about what buildings are for, staged through three architecture movements in fifty years, in a neighbourhood that in 1941 produced the only mass popular strike against the persecution of Jews in occupied Europe.

Start at the [Heineken Experience](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/heineken_experience) on Stadhouderskade 78, and stand on the pavement before going past it. Isaac Gosschalk designed it in 1867 for Gerard Adriaan Heineken, who bought the failing De Hooiberg brewery on this site and rebuilt it on an industrial scale. The facade along Stadhouderskade is broad and symmetrical, with a projecting section whose tower-like profile and clock recall a civic building rather than a factory. The step gables above the Ferdinand Bolstraat entrance reach back deliberately to the Golden Age of the 17th century. This building is substantially larger than anything on the residential streets behind it, its walls solid enough to protect the cellars from daylight. This is what the difference between a client with money and ambition and a speculative developer on a tight margin looks like, before you have seen a single tenement.

Head south on [Ferdinand Bolstraat](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/ferdinand_bolstraat_tenements), the main spine of De Pijp. The buildings lining its length were put up between roughly 1870 and 1890 by developers working a method the Dutch called *revolutiebouw* — revolution-building, named not for political upheaval but for the technical revolution of building speculatively for the open market rather than on commission. Plots are typically 5–6 metres wide. Four storeys of brick rise straight from the pavement. Applied ornament runs across the surface — pilasters, cornices, decorative panels — none of it individually designed, all of it bought from trade catalogues by the linear metre. Look at how the cornice lines align across adjacent buildings, then don't quite align on the next one: each plot was a separate contract. On 21 September 1876, numbers 35 and 37 collapsed during construction, pulling number 33 with them. Two workers died. A newspaper investigation found single-thickness walls, undersized beams, foundations sunk into sand. The disaster prompted Amsterdam to establish its first Building Inspection Service.

At [Ferdinand Bolstraat 177](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/hazes_birthplace), an ordinary tenement block — four storeys, narrow brick, the characteristic De Pijp facade — is where André Hazes was born on 30 June 1951. His father was a violent man who rarely worked. There was no money. He began singing at the Albert Cuypmarkt at the age of eight, for coins, to buy his mother a birthday present.

Continue south and turn east on to [Albert Cuypstraat](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/albert_cuypmarkt). The market fills its full 300-metre length: cheese, raw herring, stroopwafels made fresh to order, fabric, spices, and produce from Suriname, Turkey, and Morocco. Near the western entrance, a bronze statue of André Hazes stands at the stall where he sang. On Liberation Day 1959, the comedian Johnny Kraaykamp Sr. heard the eight-year-old here and brought him on national television that same evening. When Hazes died in 2004, at 53, 48,000 people came to Amsterdam Arena for his public farewell. Eight million watched live. While you are walking through the market, look up: the *revolutiebouw* tenements above the shopfronts are still intact, their catalogue pilasters stacked up into the sky exactly as they were in 1885. The market intensified what De Pijp already was; it did not change it.

Walk south from the market and then east on the backstreets toward Van Woustraat. At [Van Woustraat 149](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/koco_ijssalon), there is a memorial plaque on the wall. The building was once an ice cream parlour called Koco, run by Ernst Cahn and Alfred Kohn — German-Jewish refugees who had come to Amsterdam to escape the Nazis and opened a business here. After the German occupation of the Netherlands in May 1940, the parlour was repeatedly attacked by Dutch Nazi sympathisers. The regulars rigged an industrial ammonia canister at the entrance.

On 19 February 1941, German police raided Koco. The ammonia trap discharged. Cahn was arrested and tortured for the name of the person who had built the device. He never gave it up. On 3 March 1941, he was shot — the first person executed in the Netherlands under Nazi occupation. He was 33.

The crackdown that followed triggered the February Strike. On 25 and 26 February 1941, 300,000 Amsterdam workers stopped work and took to the streets: the dockworkers, the tram drivers, the factory workers. It was the only mass popular strike against the persecution of Jews in occupied Europe. The German authorities suppressed it within two days. The plaque marks where it started.

Walk west through the back streets to [Sarphatipark](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sarphatipark). The park was laid out in 1885 and named for Samuel Sarphati (1813–1866), a Jewish physician who transformed Amsterdam through public health initiatives, a factory that produced affordable bread for the poor, and major building projects including the Amstel Hotel. He died before De Pijp was built; the neighbourhood grew up around his legacy. In 1942, the German occupation renamed the park Bollandpark — because Sarphati was Jewish. The name was restored in 1945. Ernst Cahn was shot in March 1941; by 1942, the man who gave his name to the neighbourhood's park had been erased from the street signs. Look at the buildings facing the park as you approach: the same *revolutiebouw* formula as Ferdinand Bolstraat, but the facades are slightly wider, the cornices heavier. A park-facing plot commanded higher rents. The difference between wanting to signal respectability and being willing to pay a fraction more to do so is legible in the depth of the stonework.

From Sarphatipark, walk east along Ceintuurbaan, the broad boulevard marking the boundary between Oude Pijp and Nieuwe Pijp. At number 251–255, pause in front of the [Huis met de Kabouters](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/huis_met_de_kabouters) — the House with the Gnomes. Built in 1884, also during the *revolutiebouw* years, also in brick — and then the resemblance to everything else on the street ends. Architect Boerma reached simultaneously into the Gothic Revival for lancet windows and gargoyles, into the Dutch Renaissance for *kruiskozijn* windows, and into the Swiss chalet for elaborate carved bay windows with deep overhanging eaves. Small gnome figures on the bay windows and gable peaks appear to throw a ball between them — reportedly a visual pun on the client's surname, Van Ballegooijen, meaning roughly "to throw a ball." The catalogue tenements are borrowing authority from a broadly understood classical vocabulary. The Huis met de Kabouters is spending money on individualism. Same moment, same street, completely different decision.

Continue south on Tolstraat, then west and south to [De Dageraad](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/de_dageraad), the housing complex on Pieter Lodewijk Takstraat and Burgemeester Tellegenstraat. Built between 1919 and 1923 for the socialist housing cooperative De Dageraad (The Dawn), designed by Michel de Klerk and Piet Kramer. Stand at the corner and look along both sides of the street.

There are no catalogue pilasters, no standardised cornices, no two-window plots repeating the same formula. Instead: brickwork laid in three colours, shifting in pattern from section to section. Windows at irregular heights. Rooflines that swell into curved forms, break into towers, dip into concave recesses. Corner balconies that project in curves. The chimney stacks — purely functional objects — are given the profiles of ship funnels riding an undulating wave. De Klerk and Kramer did not decorate the building; they shaped it.

This is the opposite argument from *revolutiebouw*. Where the tenement developers applied ornament as a budget supplement to a cheap structure, De Dageraad integrates decoration and structure so thoroughly that you cannot separate them. The Amsterdam School rejected the split between building and ornament on the grounds that working-class residents had as much right to surroundings designed with intention as anyone in the city. The complex contains 294 dwellings. De Klerk died in 1923, the year it was completed, aged 39.

Walk west from De Dageraad to [Ruysdaelkade 75](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/mondrian_house), on the canal along De Pijp's western edge. After the density and ambition of everything you have seen, the canal is notably still. Piet Mondrian lived at this address from 1908 to 1911, while he was 36 and painting conventional Dutch landscapes — windmills, flat horizons, grey water. In 1911, he attended an exhibition at the Stedelijk Museum where he saw Cubist works by Picasso and Braque for the first time. He left for Paris that year, dropped an "a" from his name, and spent the next three decades moving toward the red-yellow-blue grid paintings that made him famous. He abandoned Dutch landscape painting while living on a canal named for the great Dutch landscape painter Jacob van Ruisdael, in a neighbourhood named for other Dutch painters, in a city full of them.

There is no plaque on the building.

The walk is approximately 4 kilometres and takes about 2.5 hours at a comfortable pace, longer if you walk slowly through the market or visit Museum de Dageraad at Burgemeester Tellegenstraat 128.
