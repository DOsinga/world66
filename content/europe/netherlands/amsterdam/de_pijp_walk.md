De Pijp is where Amsterdam dropped its ambitions for a while, then picked them back up. The neighbourhood was built fast and cheap in the 1870s to house workers flooding into the city during industrialisation — four-storey brick blocks, no ornament, long narrow streets that gave the place its name. That is what you are walking into: a district that started as an afterthought and became one of the most distinctive parts of the city.

Start at the **Heineken Experience** on Stadhouderskade, the red-brick brewery that Gerard Adriaan Heineken opened in 1867 on the canal that would mark De Pijp's northern boundary. The building was an anchor for what came next — the streets behind it were filled with housing precisely because a brewery of this scale needed people within walking distance. Look at the facade before going in, or instead of it: the gabled industrial front is a National Monument, and the brewing closed in 1988. The tourist attraction inside is optional. The building itself is not.

Turn south down Gerard Doustraat. This is De Pijp's most lived-in street — restaurants, cafes, a hardware shop, a barber — and at **number 67, third floor**, you are standing below where André Hazes was born in 1951. Hazes grew up here, one of six children. In 1957, when he was eight, he was busking on the Albert Cuypmarkt to earn money for a birthday present for his mother when Johnny Kraaykamp sr. heard him sing and told him he had something. He went on to become the voice of the working-class Netherlands — his music inseparable from the streets he came from. The Eddy Bar, his favourite, is further along the same street.

Continue east to the **Albert Cuypmarkt**, which runs along Albert Cuypstraat between Ferdinand Bolstraat and Van Woustraat. On a market day — any day but Sunday — the street narrows to a corridor of 260 stalls: raw herring, stroopwafels, wheels of cheese, bolts of fabric, flowers, and every manner of cheap household goods. The market has operated since 1904 and is named for the 17th-century Dutch painter Aelbert Cuyp, whose name was given to the street before anyone thought about what would happen to it. Halfway along, near the Eerste van der Helststraat junction, a bronze statue of Hazes stands on the pavement: lifesize, microphone in hand, unveiled in 2005.

Walk the full length of the market east to **Albert Cuypstraat 272**. The building is now a shop, but the top floor still has the large windows that Piet Mondrian used as a studio from 1906 to 1908. He was not yet the Mondrian — he was a Dutch painter working in a representational style, doing canals and dunes and church spires. He lived above the market, moved to Ruysdaelkade 75 two years later, and left Amsterdam for Paris in 1912. By the time he returned for a visit in 1914, the abstraction had begun. What remained constant was the grid: the long, narrow streets of De Pijp below.

Double back west on Albert Cuypstraat to the southern end of **Sarphatipark**, the neighbourhood's oval green space. The park is small enough to read from one end: a central fountain, tall trees, benches arranged around the perimeter. On a summer afternoon it fills with people from the surrounding streets — not a tourist destination, a neighbourhood amenity. It was built as exactly that, a breathing space in the middle of streets that otherwise offer none.

From Sarphatipark head east on Tweede Jan van der Heijdenstraat to Van Woustraat, then along Ceintuurbaan towards the east end of the neighbourhood. The **Huis met de Kabouters** at Ceintuurbaan 251–255 breaks up the otherwise uniform streetscape with deliberate force. The building dates from 1884 — before the Amsterdam School, before social housing reform — and architect A.C. Boerma filled the 27-metre facade with ornamental brickwork, stone friezes, and two gnome figures on the roofline, each 2.5 metres tall, posed as though passing a ball between them. Local tradition holds that the ball changes hands at midnight, though sceptics insist it only happens on 29 February. The building has been a rijksmonument since 1984. The gnomes are genuinely large.

From Ceintuurbaan follow the route south and west through the Diamantbuurt to **De Dageraad** at Burgemeester Tellegenstraat and Pieter Lodewijk Takstraat. The corner here — arrive from the east and stop before you reach the junction — is the right place to see it. Michel de Klerk and Piet Kramer designed the housing complex between 1919 and 1923 for the socialist association De Dageraad, 'The Dawn.' Three hundred and fifty dwellings in multi-coloured brick, the facades rising and tilting in waves — the boundary between red and yellow brickwork running not in a straight line but in an undulating curve that moves along the block like a tide mark. The roofline at the corner lifts and pivots. The argument behind the building was explicit: the working class deserved not just housing but beautiful housing, built with as much attention as anything in the canal ring. Whether the argument won is a matter of how you look at what they built.

The walk is approximately 3.1 km and takes about an hour at a comfortable pace, longer if you stop at the market.
