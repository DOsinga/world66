Delft and The Hague sit side by side south of Amsterdam — 45 minutes by train — and combine well into a full day out.

**Delft** is compact and beautifully preserved, with the canals and gabled houses that tourists hope for but rarely find in central Amsterdam. Johannes Vermeer was born and died here, and the city's light and geometry pervade his work. The Royal Delft factory (De Porceleyne Fles) still produces the blue-and-white ceramics that have made the city famous since the 17th century. The market square, the Nieuwe Kerk, and the old Prinsenhof museum are all within easy walking distance of the station.

**The Hague** (Den Haag) is the seat of the Dutch government and home to the International Court of Justice — a more formal city than Amsterdam, but with one of the best small art museums in the world. The Mauritshuis holds Vermeer's *Girl with a Pearl Earring* and Rembrandt's *The Anatomy Lesson of Dr. Nicolaes Tulp* in a 17th-century mansion on the edge of the old court pond. Madurodam, a miniature park where the whole of the Netherlands is reproduced at 1:25 scale, is popular with children. The Peace Palace, home to the World Court, can be visited on guided tours.

Both cities are about 15 minutes apart by train, making a combined visit straightforward.
