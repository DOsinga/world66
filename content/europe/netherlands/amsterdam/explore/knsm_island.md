KNSM Island takes its name from the Koninklijke Nederlandse Stoomboot-Maatschappij — the Royal Dutch Steamship Company — whose ships departed from these quays for destinations across the Dutch colonial world. The company and the shipping trade are long gone; in the 1990s the city commissioned a series of large residential blocks on the island as part of the wider Eastern Docklands regeneration, and the result is one of Amsterdam's most architecturally striking residential neighbourhoods.

The two defining buildings are the Piraeus block by Hans Kollhoff, at the western tip, and the Emerald Empire by Wiel Arets, at the eastern end. Piraeus is the larger and more imposing — a broad, dark-brick fortress of apartments that steps down toward the water in a series of terraces. Emerald Empire, clad in glass and aluminium, is colder and more angular. Neither is modest. Together they bracket the island's KNSM-laan and Sumatrakade, the main residential spine, in a way that makes the neighbourhood feel deliberately apart from the rest of the city.

The Lloyd Hotel, on Lloydkade at the island's edge, is the most interesting building and the best reason to visit. Built in 1921 as a luxury transit hotel for emigrants departing for South America, it was repurposed as a prison in the 1930s, served as a deportation holding centre during the German occupation, and reopened in 2004 as a design hotel with a deliberately unusual brief: rooms are rated one to five stars within the same building, at correspondingly varied prices. The single-star rooms are small and share bathrooms; the five-star rooms are large and idiosyncratic. The restaurant is open to non-guests and serves a crowd drawn more from the neighbourhood than from tourism.

The waterfront at both ends of the island gives good views across the IJ and toward the wider Eastern Docklands. The island is best reached by bike — it is about 20 minutes from the city centre — and the circuit of all four Eastern Docklands islands (KNSM, Java, Borneo, Sporenburg) makes for a full morning's exploration of some of the most considered urban housing built anywhere in Europe in the 1990s.
