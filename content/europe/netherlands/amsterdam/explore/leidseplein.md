Leidseplein is the city's primary entertainment square — a wide, irregular space where trams converge, street performers compete for space, and a permanent cluster of bars, cinemas, theatres, and restaurants surrounds the action. The square itself is not beautiful, but the neighbourhood around it is worth knowing: the streets immediately south and west of the square are considerably quieter and contain some of Amsterdam's better cultural institutions.

Paradiso, in a converted 19th-century church on Weteringschans, is one of Europe's best mid-size music venues. The programming spans rock, electronic, and jazz; the main hall, in the original nave, has exceptional acoustics and atmosphere for a standing venue. Tickets sell out quickly for anything significant, but the venue often has late-night club nights with walk-in entry after midnight. De Balie, just off the square, runs an intelligent programme of debates, film screenings, and public talks — it has a decent cafe downstairs that serves as a meeting point for the city's more engaged cultural crowd.

The American Hotel on the square — a Dutch Art Nouveau building from 1902 — is worth a look inside; the Café Americain within it is a genuine Art Nouveau interior, one of the best in the city, with stained glass, ironwork, and reading tables that have been there since the hotel opened. The food is secondary; the room is the point.

The [Leidsekade](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/explore/leidseplein/leidsekade) canal running along the south side of the square offers an easy escape from the noise, with houseboats moored along the bank and canal-side cafes in a row. South of the square, the smaller streets around Cornelis Schuytstraat and the Vondelpark edge shift into the quieter, more residential Old South. The transition is worth noting: five minutes' walk separates the noise of the square from one of the city's most pleasant residential neighbourhoods.
