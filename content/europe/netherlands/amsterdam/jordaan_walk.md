The Jordaan is the most carefully preserved of Amsterdam's 17th-century working-class neighbourhoods, and the one most worth walking slowly. It was built outside the main canal ring to house the craftsmen and labourers who built the Golden Age city but could not afford to live inside it. What remains is a dense grid of narrow streets and small canals, lined with four-storey brick houses that have barely changed in 300 years — and underneath that beauty, a neighbourhood with a habit of fighting back.

Start at the [Westerkerk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/things_to_do/westerkerk) on Westermarkt. Hendrick de Keyser designed it in 1620 and it is his masterpiece: the tallest church tower in Amsterdam at 87 metres, and the clearest example in the city of what historians call Amsterdam Renaissance. The structure is Gothic — a tall stone tower built in the medieval tradition — but covered in Renaissance ornament: pilasters, cornices, and classical mouldings stacked up the shaft. At the very top, the Imperial Crown of Austria, which Maximilian I granted to Amsterdam in 1489 as thanks for commercial support. Inside the churchyard, somewhere, is Rembrandt van Rijn: buried in a rented pauper's grave in 1669, the exact location lost. He had died with nothing, at 63, a few streets south.

At the corner of Westermarkt and Prinsengracht, three interlocking pink granite triangles are set into the paving: the [Homomonument](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/homomonument), unveiled in 1987 — the first monument in the world dedicated to LGBTQ+ people persecuted by the Nazis. On 4 May, the square fills with people who lay flowers here as part of the city's war remembrance.

Cross the Prinsengracht heading south and walk west along Rozengracht. [Number 184](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/rozengracht_184) is currently a tattoo studio, which is not what you would expect from the address where Rembrandt spent the last eleven years of his life. He moved here in 1658, after bankruptcy forced the sale of his grand house on the Jodenbreestraat — the house that is now the Rembrandt House Museum. His partner Hendrickje Stoffels died here in 1663. His son Titus died in 1668. A year later, Rembrandt died at this address too. An inventory of his estate listed 50 items, including 26 unfinished paintings. Look east toward the Westerkerk tower. That is where he was buried.

Walk back east, cross Prinsengracht, and head into the Jordaan. Take Bloemstraat west to reach [Bloemgracht](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/bloemgracht), the Flower Canal. It is often called the most beautiful of the Jordaan's smaller canals — quieter than the main ring, with houseboats and small bridges and none of the tourist traffic. The houses lining it are from the 1630s and 1640s, and they demonstrate what architectural historians call the great Jordaan gable debate in a single glance. The **stepped gable** (*trapgevel*) — the earliest and most common type, fashionable from the late 16th century through about 1660 — ascends in right-angle steps, each face a separate vertical surface. Where a stepped gable is practical and direct, the **neck gable** (*halsgevel*) that replaced it from about 1640 onward has a narrow rectangular neck between two curved flanking scrolls: it draws on Italian Baroque precedents and announces cultural pretension as well as commercial success. Walk along the canal and watch the two types alternate. Both were expensive, relative to what most Jordaan residents could afford.

At [Bloemgracht 87, 89 and 91](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/de_drie_hendricken), three matching stepped-gable houses from 1642 stand in a row: the De Drie Hendricken, the Three Henrys, named for their builder Hendrick Roelofsz. Each has a carved gable stone — a townsman, a farmer, a sailor, the three pillars of the Dutch economy. What the stones don't say: this canal once had 14 sugar refineries, and Willem Blaeu, whose atlases charted the coasts of Africa, Asia and the Americas for 17th-century navigators, had his printing workshop somewhere along here from 1635. The maps that defined the known world were drawn a few doors down from where you are standing.

Head north on Tweede Leliedwarsstraat and turn east on Egelantiersgracht. This is one of the quieter cross-canals, and where the Jordaan's *hofjes* — small enclosed courtyard almshouses — are most concentrated. At [Sint Andrieshofje](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sint_andrieshofje), number 107, a gate in the street wall leads through a covered passage into a small courtyard: whitewashed walls, a garden, complete quiet. Wealthy 17th-century merchants built these as charitable endowments for the city's poor — mostly elderly widowed women, who received a room in exchange for good conduct and church attendance. The architectural trick is the silence of the plan: from the street, you have no idea this courtyard exists. Inside, the scale drops dramatically from the four-storey street buildings to the one-storey courtyard dwellings. It is an architecture of withdrawal.

Continue north through the cross-streets — Tweede and Derde Egelantiersdwarsstraat, Anjeliersstraat — into the denser Jordaan interior. Turn east on Karthuizersstraat to reach [Karthuizerhof](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/karthuizerhof). City architect Daniël Stalpaert built this hofje in 1650 — the same Stalpaert who designed the Royal Palace on Dam Square. Where Sint Andrieshofje is intimate and almost accidental-looking, Karthuizerhof is civic architecture: 91 dwellings arranged around a large formal courtyard, the entrance flanked by classical pilasters, the proportions recalling a cloister. Compare the two: one is a merchant's private charity, the other a city project. Same building type, same social purpose, completely different ambitions. What the guides mention less often: the site was a Carthusian monastery until Protestant mobs demolished it in 1566, then a plague cemetery from 1602. Stalpaert built the 91 dwellings directly on the graves. The garden is immaculate.

On Eerste Leliedwarsstraat, in a quiet stretch of cross-street typical of the Jordaan interior, look for the [Bevrijdingslinde](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/bevrijdingslinde): a single linden tree planted on 5 May 1945 to mark the liberation of Amsterdam. It is easy to walk past without noticing.

Head north on Lindengracht, the wide street that runs the length of the northern Jordaan. In 1886, a police officer cut a rope here during an eel-pulling contest — a street entertainment the authorities had banned as cruel, which the neighbourhood considered a harmless tradition. Three days of fighting followed, residents against police, killing 26 people. The Palingoproer, the Eel Riots. In 1934, the same street came to the barricades again: the government had cut unemployment benefits during the Depression, Jordaan residents tore up cobblestones, dismantled bridges to seal off the neighbourhood, and held the streets for several days. On 5 July, a 26-year-old metalworker was shot through the forehead on this street. At least five people died. There is no plaque for either event. Continue east to Noordermarkt, where a modest bronze sculpture in the corner does commemorate the 1934 riots — easy to miss on a Saturday morning when the farmers' market fills the square.

The [Noorderkerk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/things_to_do/noorderkerk) faces the square. Hendrick de Keyser designed it in 1620, the same year he began the Westerkerk. He died in 1621; his son Pieter completed it in 1623. Stand back and compare: where the Westerkerk is a basilica — tall central nave, lower side aisles, vertical structure, a tower — the Noorderkerk is a perfectly symmetrical Greek cross. Four equal arms project from a central octagonal volume. There is no tower. There is no hierarchy of nave and aisle. Every seat is the same distance from every wall. The plan is a theological statement: the word, preached from a central pulpit, matters more than the direction of prayer. The Jordaan's labourers got a more architecturally radical building than the prosperous burghers of Westermarkt.

Walk north on Prinsengracht to reach [Brouwersgracht](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/brouwersgracht_warehouses), the canal at the northern edge of the canal ring. Stand at the water and look west. The broad-fronted warehouses (*pakhuizen*) on the north quay have **spout gables** (*tuitgevels*): plain triangular tops with a hook at the peak for the loading rope. No classical ornament. Wide because warehouse floors were wide, plain because warehouses didn't need to advertise social standing. Beside and between them, merchant houses with neck gables announce something different — pilasters, carved sandstone coping, the cultural currency of the late 17th century. In two kilometres you have moved from de Keyser's Gothic-Renaissance hybrid at the Westerkerk, through the gable catalogue of Bloemgracht, through the two scales of the hofje, through 400 years of the neighbourhood's ordinary brick vernacular, and ended at the waterfront where working buildings have always been most honest about what they are.

The walk is approximately 3 kilometres and takes about 2 hours at a comfortable pace, longer if you climb the Westerkerk tower or spend time in the Sint Andrieshofje and Karthuizerhof courtyards.
