The Jordaan was built for people who couldn't afford to live anywhere better, and for four centuries it has not let anyone forget it. This walk runs south through its tightest streets, pausing at the places where the neighbourhood's twin instincts — charity and fury — left the most visible marks on the stones.

Start at the **Noorderkerk**, the squat brown church anchoring the northern end of the Jordaan. Hendrick de Keyser designed it between 1620 and 1623 for a working-class congregation that felt the Westerkerk, to the south, was too far and too grand. De Keyser died before it was finished; his son Pieter completed it. The church is built on a Greek cross plan — four equal arms meeting at a central tower — a practical shape that put every pew close to the pulpit in an era when hearing the sermon mattered more than ceremony. On 24 February 1941, the Dutch Communist Party held a public meeting in the square outside. The German occupation had been rounding up Jewish men from the surrounding streets for days. The meeting resolved to call a general strike. Within twenty-four hours, Amsterdam had stopped: trams, ferries, factories, docks. The February Strike spread to most of Noord-Holland before being broken violently within two days. A low stone monument in the square marks where the decision was made.

Walk west along Westerstraat and turn south onto **Lindengracht**. The wide street feels too broad for the rest of the Jordaan — it should be a canal. The city filled it in during the 19th century, one of six Jordaan canals buried under asphalt when the neighbourhood was judged too crowded and too unsanitary to continue as it was. What the filling could not bury was the memory of the Palingoproer — the Eel Riot. On 25 July 1886, police tried to stop the game of palingtrekken on the bridge at the southern end of the street: a sport in which a live, soap-coated eel was strung on a rope over the water and contestants in a moving boat attempted to grab it. The crowd refused to disperse. Three days of fighting followed, the army was called in, and twenty-six people were killed. The official reason was a sporting ban; the real reason was that the Jordaan was a neighbourhood on the edge of starvation, and the police represented a government that had just cut the poor relief. A plaque on the bridge at Zaterdagse Brug records the dead.

Continue south on Tweede Lindendwarsstraat and turn right onto Egelantiersstraat. Partway along, a door to the right of a restaurant leads into the **Claes Claeszhofje** — the name is on the door in small letters, and the door is never locked. Step through and the street noise stops. The hofje is a network of three interconnected courtyards, the oldest section founded in 1616 when a cloth merchant named Claes Claesz. Anslo left his land and houses to shelter elderly people for free. He had spent his life trading linen; he spent his death trying to square his accounts with God. The complex sat empty for twenty years in the mid-20th century before Stichting Diogenes restored it in the 1960s. It now houses artists and students. The irregular geometry — narrow passages opening into unexpected open spaces, doorways at odd angles — reflects three separate hofjes merged into one at different times by different donors, each with slightly different ideas of what charity should look like.

Return to Egelantiersstraat and turn right, then right again onto Egelantiersgracht. The canal runs west; follow it to number 107, where an unpromising door in the street wall opens into the **Sint Andrieshofje**. Inside the door, a corridor lined entirely in blue Delft tiles leads to a courtyard of whitewashed houses around an original 17th-century pump. This is the oldest surviving hofje in Amsterdam after the Begijnhof, founded in 1617 from the bequest of a cattle merchant for poor Catholic widows. There were 36 small dwellings for 66 women, each admitted on condition of good conduct and regular church attendance — a room in exchange for a life lived to specification. The tiled passage is genuinely extraordinary: the same cobalt-and-white tiles used in the Delftware that Dutch merchants were selling across the known world, applied here to a corridor that almost no one knows exists. The courtyard is open on weekday mornings.

Come back along the **Egelantiersgracht** toward the Prinsengracht. This is the canal the Jordaan page usually shows in photographs: narrow houseboats moored nose to tail, 17th-century gabled houses leaning slightly over the water, geraniums on windowsills that belong to actual people. Look at the gable stones above some of the older doorways — carved tablets showing the original owner's trade or family mark, a system introduced in the 16th century when the city required residents to identify their houses by stone sign rather than by hanging wooden boards that blocked the narrow streets. A pig marked a butcher; a loaf of bread, a baker. Some are still readable.

Turn south at the Prinsengracht and then west onto **Bloemgracht** — Flower Canal, named for the dyers who used the pigments grown in gardens along its banks. The canal is widely considered the most beautiful in the Jordaan: three rows of gabled houses reflected in still water, with almost no tourist foot traffic. Rembrandt moved here in his final decade, after bankruptcy forced him out of his grand house on Jodenbreestraat to a modest address on the Rozengracht — a canal now filled in and running just south of here. He continued to work and continued to find patrons, but he died in 1669 in a rented room and was buried in an unmarked pauper's grave in the Westerkerk. The studio where he worked during these years was on this canal.

At the Prinsengracht, turn south and cross to Westermarkt, where the **Homomonument** occupies the canal-side pavement in front of the Westerkerk. Three pink granite triangles, each with ten-metre sides, form one larger triangle of thirty-six metres. Opened in 1987, it was the first monument in the world dedicated to gay men and lesbians persecuted by the Nazis — the pink triangle forced onto concentration camp prisoners reclaimed as a symbol of resistance. Designer Karin Daan aligned the triangles with purpose: one points toward Dam Square and the national war memorial, one toward the headquarters of the COC, the oldest continuously operating gay rights organisation in the world (founded in Amsterdam in 1946), and one toward the Westerkerk.

The **Westerkerk** is where the walk ends and where several of its threads converge. Designed by Hendrick de Keyser — who also built the Noorderkerk — and consecrated in 1631, it is the tallest church in Amsterdam. Rembrandt is buried here in an unmarked grave somewhere in the nave; the exact location was never recorded. Anne Frank wrote in her diary that she could hear the carillon from the hiding place on the Prinsengracht, 300 metres away; it played every quarter hour and told her what time it was in a world she could no longer see. The tower is open to climb in summer, and the view from the top — over the canal ring, the Jordaan's roofline, the city spreading flat in every direction — shows you exactly the shape of the place you have just walked through.

The walk is approximately 1.8 km and takes about 1.5 to 2 hours at a comfortable pace, longer if you enter the hofje courtyards or climb the Westerkerk tower.
