Oud-West (Old West) lies immediately west of Vondelpark and south of the Jordaan — close enough to both to feel their influence but distinct enough to have its own character. It is one of Amsterdam's most pleasant residential neighbourhoods for daily life: less polished than the canal ring, less touristy than the Jordaan, with a high density of independent shops, good food, and streets where people actually live.

[Kinkerstraat](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/kinkerstraat) and Overtoom are the main commercial arteries. The Ten Katemarkt on Saturdays is the neighbourhood market — smaller and more local than the Albert Cuypmarkt but consistently good for fresh produce, cheese, and street food. The streets perpendicular to Kinkerstraat — Bellamystraat, Da Costakade, De Clerqstraat — have seen more restaurants and bars open in the last decade than almost anywhere else in the city.

The neighbourhood was built in the late 19th and early 20th century and has a more architecturally consistent character than some of the more randomly developed inner city districts — the housing blocks are solid, the street widths generous, and the canal crossings frequent. It lacks the architectural drama of the canal ring but compensates with a sense of lived-in normalcy that is harder to find in Amsterdam's more photographed areas.

The northern edge of the area runs into [Westerpark](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/westerpark_park), a large park built around the former Westergasfabriek — a Victorian gasworks complex whose ornate brick buildings have been converted into restaurants, bars, a cinema, and event spaces. Brouwerij 't IJ has an outpost here; Mossel & Gin is one of the better seafood restaurants in the city; the outdoor terraces between the old factory buildings are among the most pleasant places to drink in warm weather. The park is also used for major events: Rollende Keukens (a festival of mobile kitchens in May) and outdoor screenings in summer.

[Haarlemmerdijk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/haarlemmerdijk) and Haarlemmerstraat, running east from the park toward Centraal Station, are among the city's best streets for independent food shops — artisan cheese, Japanese kitchen supplies, wine merchants, and delicatessens within a few blocks. The residential streets north of the Haarlemmer canals are quieter than areas closer to the centre; the architecture here is Amsterdam School — expressive brick housing blocks from the 1920s with decorated gables and sculptural detail quite unlike the sober canal-ring style. [Museum Het Schip](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/museum_het_schip) on the Spaarndammerdijk is the best place to understand the movement.
