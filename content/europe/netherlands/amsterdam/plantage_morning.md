Start at the [Waterlooplein Market](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/waterlooplein_market). Amsterdam's main flea market has been on this square in some form since 1886 — it began as the trading heart of the Jewish quarter, one of the largest in Europe, and carrying that history lightly in its chaotic present. Second-hand clothes, vintage hardware, records, bicycle parts, and a fluctuating supply of things it's hard to name. Best on a weekday morning before the tourist traffic arrives. The square is also framed by the Mozes en Aäronkerk and the Stopera — Amsterdam's combined opera house and city hall, an infamous 1980s compromise that locals have never quite forgiven or forgotten.

From Waterlooplein, walk north through the Jewish Quarter. The Portuguese Synagogue (the Esnoga, 1675) is one of the most beautiful buildings in Amsterdam and still in use. The Joods Historisch Museum opposite tells the story of the community that built it.

Then walk east to the [Hortus Botanicus](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/hortus_botanicus), one of the oldest botanical gardens in the world (founded 1638) and one of the most pleasant gardens in the city. The three-climate greenhouse — tropics, subtropics, and desert in three connected houses — is extraordinary on a cold morning. The 300-year-old Eastern Cape cycad in the middle greenhouse is the oldest potted plant in the world.

Finish at [Brouwerij 't IJ](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/brouwerij_t_ij), the craft brewery in the municipal bathhouse next to the De Gooyer windmill. Open from 2pm. Get a Columbus, sit on the terrace in front of the windmill, and call it a day.
