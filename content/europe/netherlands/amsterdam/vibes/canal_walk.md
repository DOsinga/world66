The [Canal Ring](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/canal_ring) is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the most coherent pieces of urban planning ever executed — three concentric semicircular canals dug between 1613 and 1663 during Amsterdam's Golden Age, lined with narrow gabled houses built for the merchant elite. Walking it is the best thing to do in Amsterdam.

Start at Brouwersgracht, at the northern end of the Jordaan. This is the widest and prettiest canal in the city, and on a clear morning the light on the water is extraordinary. Walk east along it until you hit Herengracht, then turn south. Herengracht is the grandest of the three main canals — the Lord's Canal, where the wealthiest merchants built the biggest houses. The gable types change every few houses: step gables, bell gables, neck gables, spout gables. Look up at the rooflines.

At the Golden Bend, between Leidsestraat and Vijzelstraat, the houses get wider and more imposing — these were the double-fronted palaces of the 17th-century ultra-rich. Continue south and loop back up Keizersgracht or Prinsengracht.

Stop at the [Begijnhof](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/begijnhof), reached through an unmarked door off the Spui — a medieval courtyard hidden in the city centre, ringed by almshouses and a chapel, where the noise of Amsterdam simply stops.

Finish in the [Nine Streets](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/negen_straatjes), the grid of small streets between the canals, where independent boutiques, bookshops, and cafés are packed into a few blocks. Have lunch here before the afternoon rush.
