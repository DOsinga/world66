Ede is a quiet Dutch town of around 120,000 people on the western edge of the **Veluwe**, the largest area of woodland and heath in the Netherlands. Most visitors do not come for the town itself — Ede is pleasant, tidy, and unexceptional, as Dutch provincial towns often are — but for what lies just to the east. A few kilometres from the town centre begins the **Hoge Veluwe National Park**, 5,500 hectares of pine forest, heather moor, drift sand and small lakes, with red deer, wild boar, moufflon, and a kind of landscape you don't really expect to find in the Netherlands.

At the heart of the park is the **Kröller-Müller Museum**, one of the most remarkable art collections in the world for its size — nearly 300 works by Vincent van Gogh, an outstanding holding of Dutch, French and German modernism, and a 25-hectare sculpture garden with pieces by Rodin, Moore, Dubuffet, Oldenburg and Serra hidden among the pines. The museum is the legacy of Helene Kröller-Müller, a German industrialist's wife who spent three decades buying art and eventually gave it, along with the estate around it, to the Dutch state in 1935. Nearby is the **Sint Hubertus Hunting Lodge**, designed by the Amsterdam-school architect H.P. Berlage, and the **Museonder**, the world's first underground museum. Across the park you can pick up one of the free **white bicycles** left at the gates and ride for hours without seeing a car.

The town of Ede itself has a small historic centre, an old church and a local history museum in the former Ede-Centrum railway station; nearby villages like **Otterlo**, at the main gate of the park, and **Bennekom**, with its genteel villas and Hoekelum Castle, are worth a short detour if you are staying longer.
