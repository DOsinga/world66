Dutch food has a modest reputation — honest, filling, a bit plain — and to some extent that reputation is fair. Traditional Dutch cooking is peasant food: potatoes, cabbage, root vegetables, and whatever came from the sea or the farm. But the Netherlands has been transformed over the past 30 years into a genuinely exciting place to eat, and the best Dutch restaurants now rank among Europe's finest.

The national comfort food is **stamppot** — mashed potato combined with vegetables like kale (boerenkool), endive, or sauerkraut, served with a rookworst (smoked sausage). It is winter food, and it is very good. **Erwtensoep** (split pea soup, also called snert) is thick, slow-cooked, and deeply satisfying on a cold day. Look for it at snack bars and markets from October onwards.

Street food is where the Netherlands truly excels. **Haring** (raw herring) is eaten whole with raw onion and pickle, tilted back by the tail into your mouth; you can get it from fish stalls all over the country for a few euros. **Bitterballen** — deep-fried croquette balls filled with a meaty ragout — are the definitive Dutch bar snack, eaten with mustard. **Stroopwafels** (thin waffle sandwiches filled with caramel syrup) are a Gouda invention and one of the great things to eat with a cup of coffee. And Dutch **frites** (chips) are excellent — thick, crisp, served in a paper cone with a terrifying quantity of mayonnaise.

The great overlooked Dutch culinary contribution is **Indonesian food**. Three centuries of colonial history mean that the Netherlands has an extraordinary range of Indonesian restaurants, and the rijsttafel (rice table) — a spread of a dozen or more small dishes — remains one of the best meals you can have here. The Indonesian population also brought **sambal**, **satay**, and **nasi goreng** into Dutch everyday cooking. [Amsterdam](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam) and Rotterdam have the best Indonesian restaurants.

**Cheese** is serious business. Gouda, Edam, and Leiden (cumin-flavoured) are all Dutch originals, and the artisanal versions found at cheese markets — [Gouda](/europe/netherlands/gouda), [Alkmaar](/europe/netherlands/alkmaar), and [Edam](/europe/netherlands/edam) — are far superior to the sealed supermarket versions. Buy from specialist kaasboeren (cheese shops) and taste before you buy.

Dutch gin, **jenever**, is the ancestor of London gin — sweeter, softer, more herbal. Oude (old-style) jenever is the richer, heavier variety; jonge is lighter. Drink it neat, chilled, from a tulip glass — never with tonic. Most traditional brown cafés (bruine kroegen) serve it.
