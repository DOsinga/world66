Rotterdam has inspired some significant writing, particularly about the war, the rebuilding, and the Dutch experience of modernity.

**The Assault** by Harry Mulisch — The Netherlands under occupation and the long aftermath of wartime violence. Mulisch was one of the two or three greatest Dutch novelists of the 20th century, and this is his most accessible and most devastating work. Essential reading before visiting any Dutch city.

**Rotterdam: City of the Future** by Tracy Metz and Maartje van den Heuvel — A richly illustrated account of how Rotterdam rebuilt itself after 1945 and became the most architecturally adventurous city in the Netherlands. For anyone who wants to understand why the city looks the way it does.

**The Discovery of Heaven** by Harry Mulisch — His most ambitious novel, set partly in Rotterdam and Amsterdam, following two friends across the sweep of 20th-century European history. Long and rewarding.

**The Evenings** by Gerard Reve — A short novel capturing post-war Dutch claustrophobia with black humour and precise observation. Reve's flat-lit suburban Netherlands resonates with Rotterdam's rebuilt anonymity.

**The Embarrassment of Riches** by Simon Schama — The definitive cultural history of the Dutch Golden Age: the trade networks, the Calvinist conscience, the domestic interiors, and the imperial ambitions. Rotterdam's identity as a port city starts here.
