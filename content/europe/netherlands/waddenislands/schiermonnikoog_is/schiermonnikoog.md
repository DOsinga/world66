Schiermonnikoog is the smallest inhabited island in the Dutch Wadden chain and arguably the most peaceful. Cars are banned for visitors — you get around by bicycle or on foot, and the silence is striking after the ferry from Lauwersoog docks at the small harbour.

The island is about 16 kilometres long and 4 wide, most of it given over to a national park. The beaches on the north side are enormous, stretching hundreds of metres at low tide, backed by dunes that shift with each winter storm. The tidal flats on the south side, part of the UNESCO-listed Wadden Sea, are a staging ground for vast numbers of migratory birds — spoonbills, oystercatchers, bar-tailed godwits — and you can walk across them at low tide with a guide, sinking knee-deep in mud and spotting seals in the distance.

The single village, also called Schiermonnikoog, has a few streets of brick houses, a couple of cafes, a small supermarket, and not much else. That is the point. People come here to cycle through the dunes, lie on empty beaches, and do very little. The island's name means "island of the grey monks" — Cistercian monks from the nearby Frisian mainland settled here in the Middle Ages, and something of that contemplative spirit survives.

The ferry from Lauwersoog takes 45 minutes and runs several times a day. In summer, book ahead — the island is popular with Dutch families, and accommodation is limited.
