**Wageningen** is a small university town of about 40,000 people on the north bank of the **Rhine** river in the Dutch province of Gelderland, about 85 km south-east of Amsterdam and 30 km west of Arnhem. It sits at the southern edge of the **Veluwe**, the large forested heath area that contains the Hoge Veluwe National Park, and is surrounded by agricultural polders and river flood plains. The town has two distinct identities — as Europe's leading agricultural research centre and as the place where the Second World War in Europe effectively ended.

**Wageningen University & Research** (Wageningen UR) is consistently ranked as the top agricultural university in the world, and the whole town is shaped by it. Nearly a quarter of the population are students, many of them international; the wooded campus on the western edge of town has an atmosphere closer to a research park than a traditional European university quarter. The spillover effect on the town is noticeable: an unusually diverse population, good ethnic food, a busy Saturday market, and more bicycles per capita than almost anywhere in the Netherlands (which is saying something).

On **5 May 1945**, the German surrender of all forces in the Netherlands was signed at **Hotel de Wereld** in the town centre by General Blaskowitz and the Canadian General Charles Foulkes, effectively ending the war in the Dutch territory. The hotel still stands, with a plaque marking the event, and every year on 5 May Wageningen hosts the national **Bevrijdingsdag** (Liberation Day) celebrations and the Bevrijdingsfestival — one of the largest free music festivals in the country. The **Airborne Museum** at nearby Oosterbeek, commemorating the disastrous Operation Market Garden of September 1944 and the Battle of Arnhem, is a moving half-day trip.

Beyond the war memorials and the university campus, the town has a modest but pleasant medieval centre around the 15th-century **Grote Kerk** and the old moat; the riverside **Rhine** walks and the nearby **Rijnstrangen** wetlands are popular with cyclists.
