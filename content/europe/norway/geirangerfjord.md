Geirangerfjord is Norway's most famous fjord and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The narrow, 15-kilometre-long inlet is flanked by near-vertical cliffs up to 1,400 metres high, with waterfalls cascading directly into the deep blue water below. If you have seen a photograph of a Norwegian fjord, there is a good chance it was this one.

The most celebrated waterfalls are the Seven Sisters (Dei Sju Systrene), which drop in parallel ribbons from the south wall, and the Suitor (Friaren) across the fjord — legend has it he was rejected by all seven sisters and took to drink, and you can make out a bottle-shaped formation between the falls. The Bridal Veil (Brudesløret) completes the trio.

The village of Geiranger sits at the head of the fjord and serves as the base for most visitors. It is tiny and can feel overwhelmed by cruise ships in high summer. Arriving by road is more atmospheric: the Golden Route from Åndalsnes crosses high mountain passes with switchbacks and stunning views, while the Eagle Road (Ørnesvingen) descends to Geiranger with a famous viewpoint partway down. The car ferry from [Ålesund](/europe/norway/alesund) through the outer fjords is equally spectacular and considerably cheaper than the tourist boats.
