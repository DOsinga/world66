The **Lofoten Islands** are a hooked chain of islands jutting into the Norwegian Sea, 200 km north of the Arctic Circle. The landscape is absurd: granite peaks rising straight out of deep blue water, red and yellow fishermen's cabins (*rorbuer*) perched on stilts at the water's edge, white beaches that wouldn't look out of place in the Caribbean except for the latitude. The cod that built the islands still dries on wooden racks each winter, as it has since the Viking age.

The main settlements are strung along the archipelago's one main road, the E10. [Svolvær](/europe/norway/lofotenislands/svolvr_the_capi), on Austvågøya, is the administrative centre and the usual arrival point. [Henningsvær](/europe/norway/lofotenislands/henningsvr), a little further south, is a cluster of islands linked by bridges — the football pitch on the rocks is one of the most photographed sports grounds on earth. [Reine](/europe/norway/lofotenislands/moskenes) at the southern end is the Instagram Lofoten, with the tiny hamlet of Å at the literal end of the road.

The best way to see it all is to drive from north to south over two or three days, stopping to hike, to kayak through the fjords, or to stay in a converted rorbu for a night. Major summer activities: the Midnight Sun (visible late May to mid-July), sea-eagle and orca-spotting boat trips, the Viking reconstruction at **Lofotr** on Vestvågøya. In winter, the Northern Lights are regularly visible and there are a few small ski operations. The ferry trip down from Bodø to Moskenes and on to the mainland is itself one of the most beautiful in Europe.

Weather changes rapidly — pack layers even in July, and don't be surprised by snow on the peaks in June.
