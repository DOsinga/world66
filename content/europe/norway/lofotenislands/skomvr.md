Skomvaer is the outermost island in the Rost archipelago, the very last scrap of land at the southwestern tip of the [Lofoten Islands](/europe/norway/lofotenislands) before the open Norwegian Sea. A proud lighthouse built in 1887 stands on the island, marking the edge of the coast for ships heading north. The island is tiny, lush, and windswept, surrounded by some of the richest seabird colonies in Norway.

The artist Theodor Kittelsen lived at Skomvaer for almost two years in the late 1880s, and his time here profoundly shaped his art. His drawings and writings from this period capture the isolation, the storms, and the eerie beauty of life on the outermost edge of the archipelago. Kittelsen is best known for his illustrations of Norwegian trolls and folklore, and you can see how Skomvaer's wild landscape fed that imagination.

Skomvaer is not easy to reach — you need to arrange a boat from [Rost](/europe/norway/lofotenislands/rst), which itself is a ferry ride from the mainland. The lighthouse was automated in 2005 and the island has no permanent residents. But for those willing to make the trip, this is one of the most atmospheric spots in all of Lofoten, a place where Norway simply ends and the Atlantic begins.
