Vardø sits on a windswept island at the far northeastern tip of Norway, 40 kilometres from the Russian border and further east than the whole of Finland. It is the easternmost town in the country, further east than St Petersburg or Istanbul, and it has a claim — along with Tromsø — to being the oldest town in northern Norway. The star-shaped fortress of Vardøhus, built in 1737, still flies the flag over the harbour and is the world's northernmost surviving bastion fort.

The town's most arresting sight is the Steilneset Memorial, a long, gaunt structure on the rocks at the edge of town designed by Peter Zumthor with an installation by Louise Bourgeois. It commemorates the ninety-one people — mostly women — burned for witchcraft here in the seventeenth century during some of the most savage witch trials in Europe. It is strange, raw, and worth the trip to Finnmark on its own.

Beyond that, Vardø is a Barents Sea fishing town. The island, Hornøya, just offshore, is one of the best seabird colonies in northern Europe — puffins, razorbills, kittiwakes, and guillemots in ridiculous numbers between April and August. In winter, the town sits under the polar night and the northern lights; in summer, the sun never sets. You can reach it by the Hurtigruten coastal ship, by the small airport, or by the long and spectacular drive along the E75 across the treeless Varanger Peninsula.
