Europe has around 750 million people and no single dominant culture — which is the whole point. Within a few hours' drive you can cross half a dozen countries, each with its own language, cuisine, architecture, and collective sense of who they are and why they're different from their neighbors.

The continent has around 200 distinct languages, though the practical picture for travelers is simpler. Romance languages (French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian) dominate the south and west. Germanic languages (German, Dutch, English, the Scandinavian languages) cover the north. Slavic languages (Russian, Polish, Czech, Slovak, Ukrainian, Bulgarian, and others) spread across the east. Smaller language families — Finnic, Baltic, Albanian, Greek, Basque — survive in their own corners. Basque, spoken by around a million people in northern [Spain](/europe/spain) and southern [France](/europe/france), is a complete linguistic isolate with no known relatives anywhere in the world.

The north-south divide shapes how people interact. Scandinavians, the Dutch, and the Germans tend toward privacy, punctuality, and reserve with strangers — not unfriendly, but not immediately warm either. Mediterranean cultures are more outwardly expressive, more likely to engage strangers, less concerned with schedules. Eastern European hospitality, once you've made contact, tends to be generous and direct. These are generalizations that collapse on contact with actual people, but they're useful as a starting orientation.

Religion remains a presence across the continent, even in increasingly secular societies. Catholic churches anchor town squares from Galicia to Poland; Orthodox traditions are deeply embedded in [Greece](/europe/greece), [Romania](/europe/romania), [Bulgaria](/europe/bulgaria), [Serbia](/europe/serbia), and [Russia](/europe/russia); Protestant cultures shaped the north. Muslim communities are significant in [Bosnia](/europe/bosnia), Albania, and Kosovo, and are a growing part of most western European cities. The coexistence — not always easy — of these traditions is part of what makes European culture so layered.

Migration has reshaped European cities dramatically since the mid-20th century. [London](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/london), [Paris](/europe/france/paris), [Berlin](/europe/germany/berlin), and [Amsterdam](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam) are genuinely multicultural in ways that show up in the food, the music, and the street life. This is the Europe that actually exists now, and it's more interesting than the monoculture the postcards still sometimes suggest.
