Poland has a rich literary tradition and has been the subject of some essential historical and fiction writing. These are worth reading before or during your trip.

### Fiction and Literature

**The Tin Drum** by Günter Grass — Set in Danzig (now Gdansk) across the Nazi and wartime period, this Nobel Prize-winning novel captures the fractured history of the Polish-German borderlands better than almost anything else.

**Solaris** by Stanisław Lem — Poland's greatest science fiction writer. Solaris is the most accessible entry point, but Lem's other work rewards exploration.

**Flights** by Olga Tokarczuk — The 2018 Nobel laureate writes unconventional, essayistic fiction. *Flights* and *The Books of Jacob* are both translated into English and give a sense of Tokarczuk's particular Central European perspective.

**A Blessing on the Moon** by Joseph Skibell — A novel about the Holocaust in Poland, told from the perspective of a murdered villager. Unsettling and memorable.

### History

**Bloodlands: Europe Between Hitler and Stalin** by Timothy Snyder — Essential context for understanding what happened in Poland (and its neighbours) between 1933 and 1945. Not light reading, but nothing written on this period comes close to it.

**The Pianist** by Władysław Szpilman — The memoir that became Roman Polanski's film. A Warsaw Jewish pianist's survival through the war and the ghetto. Straightforward and devastating.

**Poland** by James Michener — A long historical novel spanning centuries of Polish history. Better as an introduction to the country's sweep than as literature, but useful.

### Travel and Reportage

**Borderlands** by Anna Reid — A journey through the territories that were once part of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth. Excellent for understanding the east of the country and its tangled history with Ukraine and Belarus.
