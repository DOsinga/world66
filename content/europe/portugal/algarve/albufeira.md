Albufeira is the Algarve's most popular resort town, and for good reason. Perched on cliffs above a string of golden beaches on Portugal's southern coast, it combines the feel of a traditional fishing village with the energy of a modern holiday destination. The old town, a tangle of narrow cobbled streets and whitewashed houses tumbling down toward the sea, still has the charm that drew the first visitors decades ago -- and surprisingly few tourists bother to explore it properly.

The heart of Albufeira is two worlds in one. By day, the old centre around the main square is relaxed and almost sleepy, with good local restaurants and small shops. By night, the famous Strip -- a long street of bars, clubs and restaurants running from Praia da Oura -- comes alive with an unmistakably British and Irish party crowd. If that is not your scene, the old town has quieter bars and cafes where you can eat well for very little.

The beaches are the real draw. The main Praia dos Pescadores (Fisherman's Beach) sits right below the old town, while Praia da Oura and Praia da Falesia stretch east along some of the most dramatic coastline in Portugal -- ochre cliffs topped with pine trees, dropping to wide sand. The water is clean and the swimming is good from late spring through autumn. Beyond the beach, boat trips to sea caves, dolphin-watching excursions and day trips to the historic towns of [Silves](/europe/portugal/algarve/silves) and [Faro](/europe/portugal/algarve/faro) are all easy to arrange.

Albufeira sits about 40 kilometres west of Faro airport. Buses and trains connect it to [Lisbon](/europe/portugal/lisbon) and the rest of the Algarve, and a rental car opens up the quieter western coast toward Sagres and Lagos.
