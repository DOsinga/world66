Cascais is a refined seaside resort on the Estoril Coast, about 30 km west of [Lisbon](/europe/portugal/lisbon), where the Tagus estuary meets the Atlantic. It has been a popular escape from the capital since the Portuguese royal family summered here in the nineteenth century, and the old fishing quarter, the historic centre, and the elegant seafront palaces still give it a genuine character that many Portuguese resorts lack.

The old town around the harbour and Praça Luís de Camões is the best part — cobbled streets, seafood restaurants, and the sound of the sea never far away. The Museu do Mar and the Museu da Casa de Santa Maria are both worth visiting. The beaches immediately around town (Praia de Cascais, Praia da Rainha) are busy in summer but attractive; the wilder beaches towards Guincho to the west are more dramatic and excellent for surfing and windsurfing.

Cascais is easily reached from Lisbon by the Linha de Cascais commuter train, which runs along the coast from Cais do Sodré in about 40 minutes. The train passes through Estoril — famous for its casino — one stop before Cascais.

Cascais is a refined seaside resort about 30 km west of [Lisbon](/europe/portugal/lisbon), easily reached by commuter train along the coast. Once a fishing village, then a royal summer retreat, it is now a polished little town with a pretty harbour, sandy beaches, and a pedestrianised centre full of restaurants and boutiques.

The coastline west of Cascais grows wilder quickly — the **Boca do Inferno** (Hell's Mouth) is a dramatic sea cliff a short walk from town, and the beaches at **Guincho**, a few kilometres further, are popular with windsurfers. The town itself is compact and walkable, with the **Cidadela** (old fortress) and the **Casa das Histórias Paula Rego** museum as highlights.

Cascais makes a comfortable day trip from Lisbon, but it is worth staying a night or two to enjoy the seafood restaurants in the evening, when the day-trippers have gone home.
