Fátima is one of the most important Catholic pilgrimage sites in the world. Every year more than six million people make their way to this small town in central Portugal, drawn by the 1917 apparitions of the Virgin Mary to three shepherd children. Whether you come for religious reasons or simply out of curiosity, the scale and sincerity of the devotion here is genuinely affecting.

The town itself is modest — a grid of hotels and souvenir shops radiating outward from the vast esplanade of the Sanctuary. The esplanade is deliberately larger than St. Peter's Square in Rome, designed to accommodate the enormous crowds that gather for the main pilgrimage dates: May 13 and October 13. On those days, hundreds of thousands of pilgrims fill the plaza, many completing the last stretch on their knees. If you can time your visit to coincide, it is an extraordinary sight — not kitsch, but raw and moving.

The Basilica of Our Lady of the Rosary (1953) anchors one end of the esplanade, the newer Basilica of the Holy Trinity (2007) the other. Between them is the Chapel of the Apparitions, the actual spot where the visions occurred, now enclosed in a small canopied structure. The tombs of the three visionaries — Lúcia, Francisco, and Jacinta — are inside the older basilica.

Outside the main pilgrimage dates, Fátima is quiet and easy to visit. The crowds thin, the candle smoke clears, and you can walk the grounds at your own pace. The Wax Museum tells the story of the apparitions if you want more context. Allow a half day, or a full day if you attend a service.
