Madeira is a volcanic island rising steeply from the Atlantic, roughly 1,000 km southwest of Lisbon and closer to Morocco than to mainland Portugal. Its dramatic terrain — deep ravines, vertiginous cliffs, and peaks that disappear into cloud — makes it unlike any other Portuguese destination. The capital, [Funchal](/europe/portugal/madeira/funchal), clings to an amphitheatre of hills above the south coast and is where most visitors base themselves.

The island has been welcoming travelers for centuries, and it shows in the quality of its hospitality. The climate is one of Madeira's great selling points: mild and spring-like year round, with little rain on the sunny south coast and more on the lush northern slopes. Flowers bloom everywhere — bougainvillea on the city walls, bird-of-paradise plants in the gardens, hydrangeas lining the mountain roads.

Walking is the main activity for independent travelers. Madeira is laced with a network of levadas — narrow irrigation channels built to carry water from the wet north to the dry south. Most levadas have a maintenance path alongside them, and these paths provide extraordinary access into the island's interior: through forests of laurisilva (an ancient laurel forest that is a UNESCO World Heritage Site), along cliff edges, and into valleys that would otherwise be inaccessible. The walks range from easy strolls to demanding mountain hikes.

Food and drink are particular pleasures here. The island has its own wine, Madeira wine, which ranges from dry to intensely sweet and keeps almost indefinitely once opened. The local cuisine leans on seafood: espada (black scabbardfish) is the island specialty, often served with banana. Espetada — beef or tuna skewered on a bay laurel stick and grilled over wood — is another dish worth seeking out, especially at a local taberna rather than a tourist restaurant.

Madeira has a reputation as a safe, quiet destination favored by older European visitors, and that reputation is largely deserved. But the island rewards the more energetic traveler equally well — there is serious hiking, swimming in natural lava pools, and whale and dolphin watching offshore. The sheer physical drama of the place, with its cliffs and ravines and sudden ocean views, makes it memorable well beyond its gentle-paradise reputation.
