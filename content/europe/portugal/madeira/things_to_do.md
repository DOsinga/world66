---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

Madeira's defining activity is walking the levadas — the ancient irrigation channels that criss-cross the island, carrying water from the wet north to the drier south. Each levada has a maintenance path alongside it, and these paths give walkers extraordinary access into the interior: through laurisilva forest (a UNESCO World Heritage Site), along sheer cliff faces, and into valleys that would be otherwise unreachable. Difficulty ranges from nearly flat, broad paths suitable for anyone to demanding mountain routes requiring good footwear and a head for heights.

Public transport doesn't reach most trailheads, but several local agencies run guided levada walks with transport included, which is a practical option if you don't have a hire car. The Cicerone guide to Madeira walks is worth picking up before you travel.

Beyond walking, the island offers whale and dolphin watching (year-round, with sperm whales resident offshore), sea swimming in natural lava pools along the coast, and mountain biking down from the peaks. Toboggan rides on traditional wicker sleds from Monte down to Funchal are a beloved if touristy tradition — the sledges are steered by two men in white linen who run alongside them.
