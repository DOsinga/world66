Mina de São Domingos is an abandoned open-pit mine in the Alentejo, one of the more haunting industrial ruins in southern Portugal. The site sits in the Iberian Pyrite Belt, a geological band stretching from southern Portugal into Spain that has been mined for copper, iron, gold, and silver since antiquity. The Romans worked this area intensively for around four centuries, and large-scale industrial extraction resumed in the nineteenth century under a British company, lasting until 1966.

What you see today is the aftermath of that long history: a vast open pit stained vivid shades of rust and orange, its walls streaked with iron oxides and sulfur compounds. The tailings and waste heaps have leached acid into the surrounding streams, turning the water an eerie turquoise. It is genuinely strange and beautiful in an industrial way — a landscape that looks more like Mars than rural Portugal.

The village of São Domingos, built to house the mine workers, still stands nearby and retains some of the ordered, slightly melancholy character of a company town. A small museum documents the mining history, and you can walk the perimeter of the pit on informal tracks.

The area is remote — about 20 km east of [Mértola](/europe/portugal/mertola) — and there is little infrastructure for visitors, but that is part of the appeal. It rewards the traveller who takes the trouble to get there.
