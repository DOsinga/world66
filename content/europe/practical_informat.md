Most visitors to Europe benefit from the Schengen Agreement, which covers 29 countries and works like a single border zone — one visa, one entry stamp, then move freely between member states. Citizens of the US, Canada, Australia, and many other countries get 90 days within any 180-day period without a visa. The EU's ETIAS system (similar to the US ESTA) is expected to launch soon and will require a pre-travel registration for visa-exempt travelers. Note that the [United Kingdom](/europe/unitedkingdom) is no longer part of the EU and has its own entry requirements post-Brexit — you'll go through passport control separately, and Schengen days don't count against your UK allowance.

The Euro is used in 20 countries, which makes hopping between [France](/europe/france), [Germany](/europe/germany), [Italy](/europe/italy), [Spain](/europe/spain), [Greece](/europe/greece), and [the Netherlands](/europe/netherlands) financially seamless. But plenty of European countries keep their own currencies — you'll need krona in [Sweden](/europe/sweden), krone in [Norway](/europe/norway), forints in [Hungary](/europe/hungary), and pounds in the [UK](/europe/unitedkingdom). Card payments are nearly universal in northern and western Europe; the Dutch and Scandinavians barely know what cash looks like anymore. In southern and eastern Europe, carry some local currency — smaller restaurants, markets, and rural areas often prefer or require cash.

English is widely spoken, but how widely varies enormously. In [the Netherlands](/europe/netherlands), Scandinavia, and [Germany](/europe/germany), you can get by entirely in English. In [France](/europe/france), [Italy](/europe/italy), and [Spain](/europe/spain), English is common in tourist areas but drops off quickly outside them — a few phrases in the local language go a long way and people genuinely appreciate the effort. In eastern Europe, younger people usually speak some English, while older generations often don't. Learning to say hello, please, thank you, and beer in the local language costs you nothing and buys you goodwill everywhere.

Europe is generally very safe for travelers. Violent crime against tourists is rare. Your real concern is petty theft — pickpockets work the metros and tourist crowds in [Paris](/europe/france/paris), [Barcelona](/europe/spain/barcelona), [Rome](/europe/italy/rome), [Prague](/europe/czechrepublic/prague), and [Amsterdam](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam). The usual advice applies: front pockets, zippered bags, don't flash your phone around, and be wary of anyone creating a distraction. Scams targeting tourists exist everywhere but are most common around major monuments and train stations. Use common sense and you'll be fine.

Connectivity across Europe is excellent. EU regulations mean your mobile plan from one EU country works across all of them without roaming charges — if you have a European SIM, data works the same in [Greece](/europe/greece) as it does in [Germany](/europe/germany). Travelers from outside Europe should pick up a local SIM or an eSIM before arrival; prepaid data is cheap and coverage is strong almost everywhere. Free Wi-Fi is available in most cafes, hotels, and public spaces. The [UK](/europe/unitedkingdom) and [Switzerland](/europe/switzerland) are outside the EU roaming zone, so check your plan before crossing those borders.

European electrical outlets use 230V and mostly Type C or Type F plugs (the round two-pin variety). The [UK](/europe/unitedkingdom) and [Ireland](/europe/ireland) use their own chunky three-pin Type G plug. A universal adapter handles all of this — pick one up before you go rather than hunting for one at your destination. Most modern electronics (phones, laptops, cameras) handle the voltage difference automatically, but check the label on anything with a heating element before plugging it in.
