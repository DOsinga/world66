Constanta is Romania's main Black Sea port and the largest city on the Romanian riviera. Founded in the 6th century BC by Greek merchants under the name Tomis, it was later developed by the Romans and was the city where the poet Ovid spent the last years of his life in exile — a statue of Ovid stands in the central square. The city took its modern name from Emperor Constantine.

The old city centre has a remarkable concentration of ruins and museums within walking distance: a **Roman mosaic** from the 4th century (one of the largest in the world, discovered during construction work in 1959), the **National History and Archaeology Museum**, the **Ovid Square** and its monuments, and a late 19th-century **Casino** on the waterfront. The seafront promenade and the adjacent resort of Mamaia — a strip of hotels and beaches about 5 km north — are the focus of Black Sea beach tourism in summer.

Getting there: Constanta is connected to Bucharest by train (2.5–3 hours) and highway.
