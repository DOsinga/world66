Transylvania is the large plateau that fills the centre and north of Romania, ringed on three sides by the great arc of the **Carpathian Mountains**. For a word so saturated with vampire associations in the English-speaking imagination, the reality is almost comically unlike it: rolling farmland, forested hills, beech woods that still shelter the densest populations of brown bear and wolf in Europe, and a collection of small, beautifully preserved medieval towns that would not look out of place in Austria or southern Germany. That resemblance is not a coincidence — from the 12th century until the end of the First World War, Transylvania was ruled by Hungarians, and colonised by German-speaking "Saxons" who built the fortified churches and the cobbled squares that still dominate its towns today.

The great draws are the **Saxon towns**. **Braşov**, beneath the wooded cone of Mount Tâmpa, has the best-preserved old town and a huge Gothic Black Church. **Sighişoara** is a hilltop citadel of pastel houses and a clock tower, famous as the birthplace of **Vlad Țepeș** — the Wallachian prince Bram Stoker borrowed for his novel. **Sibiu**, with its pair of linked squares and eyelid-dormered roofs, was European Capital of Culture in 2007. And scattered across the countryside between them, dozens of fortified Saxon villages — including the UNESCO-listed **Biertan**, **Viscri** and **Prejmer** — have churches with four-metre walls and towers built to resist Turkish and Tartar raids.

Of the castles, **Bran Castle** near Braşov is the one marketed to tourists as Dracula's (Stoker never saw it, and Vlad almost certainly never lived there); the much more interesting castles are **Corvin Castle** at Hunedoara, a 14th-century Gothic fortress that may be the finest in Romania, and **Peleș Castle** near Sinaia, a fairy-tale late-19th-century royal summer residence. Beyond the towns and castles, the **Apuseni Mountains** in the west hold caves and rural communities where the old ways of life survive, and **Maramureș**, just over the northern border, preserves wooden churches and a way of life unchanged for centuries.
