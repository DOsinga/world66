Belgorod is a mid-sized city in southwestern Russia, about 40 km from the Ukrainian border. It sits on the chalk hills above the Seversky Donets River, and the city's name — "white city" — comes from the white chalk outcrops that define the landscape. Founded as a frontier fortress in the 16th century, Belgorod was largely destroyed during World War II and rebuilt in Soviet style, so the architecture is mostly functional rather than beautiful.

The city centre has a few points of interest: the Diorama Museum commemorates the Battle of Kursk, with a massive panoramic painting of the 1943 tank battle at Prokhorovka. The Holy Trinity Cathedral, one of the few pre-war buildings to survive, dates from the 19th century. Belgorod State University gives the city a young, student-driven energy, and there are enough cafes and restaurants around Sobornaya Square to fill an evening.

Belgorod is not a typical tourist destination, but it serves as a base for visiting the Prokhorovka battlefield memorial, about 60 km to the north, which marks one of the largest tank engagements in history. The surrounding countryside of black earth farmland and chalk valleys has a quiet, open beauty.
