---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Russian cuisine is hearty, warming, and built around soups, bread, meat, and dairy. It is comfort food in the truest sense -- designed for long winters and big appetites.

**Soups** are central to Russian eating. Borscht, the famous beet soup served with a spoonful of smetana (sour cream), is eaten across the country year-round. Shchi, a cabbage soup, is even more traditionally Russian. Solyanka is a thick, sour soup made with pickles, olives, and meat or fish. In summer, okroshka -- a cold soup made with kvass (fermented rye drink) -- is refreshing and ubiquitous.

**Pelmeni** are Russian dumplings filled with minced pork, beef, or lamb, boiled and served with smetana or butter. They originated in Siberia and are found everywhere. Every family has a recipe, and the best are handmade.

**Blini** are thin yeasted pancakes, served with sweet or savory toppings. With caviar and smetana, they are celebratory. With jam or condensed milk, they are a simple treat. During Maslenitsa (Butter Week, before Lent), blini are everywhere.

**Pirozhki** are small baked or fried pastries stuffed with meat, cabbage, potatoes, or mushrooms. They are classic street food and a perfect snack. Larger versions -- pirogi -- are served at home and in restaurants.

**Kasha** (porridge) is breakfast for millions of Russians, made from buckwheat, oats, or millet. Buckwheat kasha also appears as a side dish at dinner.

Russian food culture is inseparable from smetana, dill, and black bread. Rye bread is taken seriously and varies by region -- some are dense and sour, others light. A meal without bread feels incomplete to most Russians.

In Kazan and other Tatar regions, the food shifts to include echpochmak (triangular meat pies), chak-chak (fried dough with honey), and other Tatar specialties that are worth seeking out.
