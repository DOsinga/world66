The State Borodino War and History Museum sits 120 km west of Moscow at the site of the Battle of Borodino, fought on 7 September 1812 between Napoleon's Grande Armee and Kutuzov's Russian forces. Founded in 1839, it is the oldest battlefield museum in the world.

The main museum displays original Russian and French uniforms, weapons, battle paintings, and personal belongings of the participants -- Kutuzov's field carriage, the tunic of Tsar Alexander I, and Napoleon's camp bed among them. A scaled model of the battlefield with troop positions gives you a sense of the enormous scale of the engagement.

But the real experience is the battlefield itself. The historic landscape has been preserved to look much as it did in 1812, and about 200 monuments dot the fields -- the Main Monument on the Raevsky battery site, graves of generals Bagration and Neverovsky, and markers for command posts used by both Kutuzov and Napoleon. The Borodino Savior Convent, founded in 1820, sits within the grounds and houses additional exhibitions.

The same fields saw fighting again in October 1941, when Soviet forces held the Mozhaisk defense line against the German advance on Moscow. Pillboxes, trenches, and memorials from that battle stand alongside the Napoleonic monuments.

**Getting there.** Take a local train from Belorussky Vokzal to Borodino station. From the station, turn left at the small square behind the building, then right along the road -- about a 30-minute walk to the museum.

**When to go.** Pick a dry day. The site is open-air and the paths between monuments turn to mud after rain. The first Sunday of September brings a large-scale reenactment of the 1812 battle, drawing participants from many countries. The last Sunday of May features a smaller reenactment that is less crowded but equally interesting.
