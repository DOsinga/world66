**Ulyanovsk** sits on the high western bank of the Volga River, about 700 kilometres east of Moscow, and it is a city defined by one fact: Vladimir Lenin was born here in 1870. The Soviet authorities never let anyone forget it. They renamed the city (it was called Simbirsk until 1924), built a vast memorial complex on the bluffs above the river, and preserved Lenin's childhood home with almost religious devotion. The result is a city unlike any other in Russia — a living museum of Soviet ideology, now slowly making peace with a post-Soviet identity.

Beyond the Lenin cult, Ulyanovsk rewards the curious visitor. The old merchant town of Simbirsk was a prosperous place, and a handful of handsome 19th-century streets survive around the historic centre. The writer Ivan Goncharov, author of *Oblomov*, was also born here, and the city has a good museum dedicated to him. The Volga itself is ever-present — wide, slow, and imposing — and in summer the riverbanks and the beaches on the far side draw locals out in numbers.

The city of around 600,000 is the capital of Ulyanovsk Oblast, a region that stretches across the Middle Volga forest-steppe. Roughly 73 percent of residents are Russian, with significant Tatar, Chuvash, and Mordvin minorities — a reminder that this part of the Volga has always been a meeting point between Slavic and Turkic worlds.

Ulyanovsk is not on the standard tourist trail, and that is part of its appeal. You can spend time in the Lenin Memorial Centre without irony or crowds, walk quiet streets where Soviet-era apartment blocks stand next to wooden merchant houses, and get a genuine sense of a Russian provincial city that has not been packaged for visitors.
