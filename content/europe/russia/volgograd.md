Volgograd is a Russian city of just over one million people, stretched for 80 kilometres along the west bank of the Volga River in the southern Russian steppe. It is known to the world almost entirely by its former name: Stalingrad. The Battle of Stalingrad, fought from August 1942 to February 1943, was the largest and bloodiest battle of the Second World War — an estimated two million casualties on all sides — and the point at which the German advance into the Soviet Union was broken. The city was essentially destroyed in the fighting and rebuilt from rubble after the war.

The war memorials are the reason most visitors come. Mamayev Kurgan, the hill above the city that was fought over for months and is now one of the great monumental complexes of the 20th century, culminates in The Motherland Calls — a colossal statue visible from kilometres away. The Panorama Museum at the base of the hill documents the battle with a vast circular painting and thousands of artefacts. The ruins of Mill No. 4, preserved in their battle-damaged state beside the museum, give the most visceral sense of what the city went through. Walking these sites, even for visitors with no particular interest in military history, is a powerful experience.

The city that was rebuilt after the war is a product of Stalinist urban planning — wide boulevards, monumental public buildings, and a long riverfront embankment designed for parades and promenades. The Volga here is very wide, and in summer the embankment is the social centre of the city. Volgograd is also notably long and narrow — a consequence of its geography — and the underground tram system (one of only a handful in the world) is an interesting quirk.

Note: Given Russia's invasion of Ukraine (2022) and resulting international sanctions and travel advisories, visitors should check their country's official guidance before planning travel to Russia.
