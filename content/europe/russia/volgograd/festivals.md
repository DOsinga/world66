---

**Volgograd Festivals** provide insight into the cultural fabric of Volgograd—one of Russia&rsquo;s most industrially developed cities. Volgograd is also the administrative capital of the district of Oblast. Volgograd gained eminence during World War II when it became an important manufacturing center for weapons. However, Volgograd is also popular for its vibrant festivals. Some of the most prominent of Volgograd festivals are discussed below:

**Multicultural Volgograd Festivals**—**Festival of Maslyanitsa**

During the last weeks of winter, a unique celebration called Shrovetide, or the Pancake Week, is celebrated in Volgograd. The Russian Pancake week is also called the Maslyanitsa. This festival usually arrives in the month of February. People of the city can be seen wearing costumes and carrying out small processions. This festival was started to welcome the spring season, but now it has gained a more multicultural significance wherein people of all communities take part in the celebrations. A few weeks after this festival, Red Hill is celebrated in Volgograd. Apart from the festivities, this day is considered the most auspicious for wedding ceremonies.

**Traditional Volgograd Festivals**—**Festival of Troitsa**

This is essentially a folk holiday that has assumed significance on par with a national festival. Keeping in touch with the traditions, roofs of houses are covered with branches of birch trees. Similarly, garlands made from birch trees are exchanged by the people. This festival is celebrated a few weeks after the Easter holiday, and always begins on a Sunday. It is often referred to as the Trinity Holiday, or the Pentecost.

**Jewish Volgograd Festivals**—**Festival of Purim**

This festival is celebrated by the Jewish community of Volgograd. Often referred to as the Purim Holiday, this festival is celebrated in accordance with the Hebrew calendar, during the holy month of Adar. This is essentially a religious festival that is aimed to unite the Jews living in Volgograd.
