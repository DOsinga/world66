Belgrade stands at the confluence of the Sava and Danube rivers, a position that has made it strategically vital and repeatedly contested throughout history. The city has been razed and rebuilt dozens of times by Celts, Romans, Byzantines, Hungarians, Ottomans, Habsburgs, and Serbs in turn. That turbulent past left surprisingly few ancient monuments — most of what visitors see dates from the 19th and 20th centuries — but the layers of history are still felt in the texture of the city.

The heart of Belgrade is Knez Mihailova, a long pedestrianised street of 19th-century buildings linking Terazije square to the Kalemegdan fortress. The fortress and its surrounding park occupy a dramatic promontory above the rivers and are the city's main historic monument: the fortifications incorporate Roman, Byzantine, medieval Serbian, and Ottoman elements. At the other end of Knez Mihailova, Republic Square opens onto the National Theatre (1868) and the National Museum. South of the centre, the enormous Temple of Saint Sava — one of the largest Orthodox churches in the world — dominates the Vračar plateau.

Belgrade has a serious reputation for nightlife. The splavovi — floating restaurants and clubs moored along the riverbanks — are particularly characteristic of the city, and the bar scene around Strahinjića Bana street and the cobblestoned Skadarlija quarter attracts visitors from across the region. The old bohemian restaurants of Skadarlija, with their live traditional music and hearty food, offer a different but equally authentic experience.
