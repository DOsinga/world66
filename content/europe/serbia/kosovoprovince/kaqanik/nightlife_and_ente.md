---
title: "Nightlife and Entertainment in Kaqanik"
type: section
---

Most of the town of Kaqanik winds down at about 10 p.m., but there can still be activities available. Here are some **Kaqanik – Nightlife and Entertainment** highlights.

**Titanik Bar Caffe**

Far from being a themed restaurant, the Titanik Bar Caffe and Restaurant is a tiny establishment tucked away in a corner of the town, serving up some caf&eacute; food and really good coffee. The interior d&eacute;cor is reminiscent of an old school American pub and diner, only far older and beaten up. The bar and caf&eacute; have seen better days, and although the interior design of the place is beginning to look really old, the food remains of a good quality. The old and new meet here in an interesting mix – hot mocha or cold beer is served up with traditional Albanian flatbread. Those looking for something a little more Western can opt for the variety of deep fried items – from cheese balls to fries to chicken nuggets. The place is packed on weekends and Friday nights, particularly with young people looking for a way to spend the evening as the town winds down. Be careful with your belongings though, since a crowded pub setting can be an invitation to pickpockets.

**RRAP Bar**

The RRAP reads that it is a bar and caf&eacute;, but it really is more of a pub. One of the cheapest places in Kaqanik to get a drink, the RRAP can be bursting at its seams on a weekend night, and still be moderately crowded on a weeknight. With disco lights and a house DJ playing up old school pop songs in a corner, the RRAP screams a tackiness that can be endearing to many visitors to the place. While here, be sure the try some the Kosovan beers – Peja is a common brand, and is a clear brew that is light on the palate. Wines are also available, and local wines are very popular and cheap in Kaqanik. Kosovars are predominantly Muslim, but drinking is generally not frowned upon here.

**Pellumbi**

The Pellumbi is one of Kaqanik&rsquo;s oldest restaurants, and still remains standing to this day. Those looking for a quieter night out can opt to go to the Pellumbi, serving traditional Albanian food. Cheese, olives, fish and lamb are a large part of the Albanian diet, and are reflected in the cooking of the Pellumbi restaurant. Be sure to try the specialty Fli pastry, a light, buttery, layered pastry, usually served with a lamb or chicken stew in the middle. Be careful with the prices here, though. They have the tendency to overcharge, so be sure to ask for a receipt with paying the bill here.
