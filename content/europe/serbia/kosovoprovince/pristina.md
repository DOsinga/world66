Pristina is the capital of Kosovo and a city that wears its complicated history on its sleeve. Ottoman mosques sit next to brutalist Yugoslav apartment blocks, which sit next to glass-and-steel EU-funded buildings. The result is not pretty in the conventional sense, but it is never boring.

The city has grown rapidly since Kosovo declared independence in 2008, and much of the center has been rebuilt or renovated. Mother Teresa Boulevard, the main pedestrian street, is lined with cafes and shops and fills with people every evening for the traditional *xhiro* — the slow promenade that is one of the Balkans' great social rituals. The population skews remarkably young, and the energy is palpable.

Pristina is not a city of grand monuments, but it has a few worth seeking out. The National Library, an extraordinary piece of brutalist architecture draped in metal lattice, is either magnificent or hideous depending on who you ask. The Imperial Mosque and the old bazaar area give a sense of the Ottoman city that once was. The Newborn monument, repainted every year on the anniversary of independence, has become the city's most recognizable symbol.

The cafe culture here is serious. Espresso is cheap, strong, and consumed in enormous quantities. On a warm evening, it can feel like the entire city is sitting outside with a macchiato. The nightlife scene punches well above its weight for a city this size, with a good mix of basement bars, live music venues, and proper clubs.
