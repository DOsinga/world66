Kotor is a fortified town at the head of the Bay of Kotor in Montenegro, wedged between the water and a sheer mountain wall. The setting is dramatic — the bay is the deepest natural harbour in the southern Mediterranean, surrounded by mountains that rise straight up to over 1,700 metres — and the old town inside the walls is one of the best-preserved medieval centres on the Adriatic. UNESCO listed it in 1979.

The old town is entered through the Sea Gate and is a maze of narrow stone lanes, small squares, and Romanesque churches. The Cathedral of Saint Tryphon, dating from 1166, is the centrepiece. But the defining Kotor experience is the climb up the fortress walls to the Castle of San Giovanni — 1,350 steps zigzagging up the mountainside to a ruined fortress 280 metres above the town, with views down the length of the bay that justify every step.

Kotor has become a popular cruise ship stop, and the old town can feel crowded when a large ship is in port. The trick is to come early in the morning or stay into the evening, when the day-trippers leave and the town empties back to its quieter self. The waterfront has good seafood restaurants, and the bars in the old town fill up after dark.

[Perast](/europe/serbiaandmontenegro/perast), a smaller Baroque town twenty minutes north along the bay, makes an easy side trip. The coastal road between the two, hugging the water beneath the mountains, is one of the most scenic drives in the Balkans.
