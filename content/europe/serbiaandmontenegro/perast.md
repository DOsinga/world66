Perast is a tiny, perfectly preserved Baroque town on the Bay of Kotor in Montenegro, squeezed between the mountains and the water with no room to grow and no reason to change. It has one main street, a row of stone palaces built by sea captains who grew rich in the Venetian maritime trade, and a view across the bay that stops you in your tracks.

The town's two defining landmarks sit offshore: Our Lady of the Rocks, an artificial island built by locals over centuries of dropping stones and sinking ships, and the natural island of St. George with its Benedictine monastery. Boats shuttle visitors to Our Lady of the Rocks from the waterfront — it takes five minutes and costs a few euros.

Perast is small enough to walk end to end in ten minutes. The Maritime Museum in the Bujovic Palace documents the town's seafaring history, when Perast's fleet was trusted to guard the Venetian navy's battle standard. The churches — there are sixteen for a town that never had more than a few hundred residents — are mostly locked, but their exteriors and bell towers are half the point.

There are a few restaurants along the waterfront, a handful of rooms to rent, and not much else. That is exactly the appeal. Most visitors come as a day trip from [Kotor](/europe/serbiaandmontenegro/kotor), which is twenty minutes south by car or bus.
