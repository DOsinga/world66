---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Slovak cuisine is hearty mountain food -- heavy on potatoes, cheese, pork, and dumplings. It is comfort food built for cold winters, and it is surprisingly distinctive.

**Bryndzove halusky** is the undisputed national dish: small potato dumplings smothered in bryndza (a tangy, salty sheep cheese unique to Slovakia) and topped with crispy bacon. The dish is simple, filling, and genuinely delicious. Bryndza has Protected Geographic Indication status in the EU -- the real thing can only come from Slovakia. It is traditionally washed down with zinzica, a fermented sheep's whey drink that is an acquired taste.

**Kapustnica** is the traditional Christmas cabbage soup, made with sauerkraut, smoked sausage, dried mushrooms, and sometimes plums. Every family has its own recipe, and debates about the correct version are fierce.

**Lokse** are thin potato pancakes, often served alongside roast duck or goose at festivals and Christmas markets. Spread with goose fat or stuffed with duck, they are addictive street food.

**Pirohy** (pierogi) filled with bryndza, potatoes, or cabbage are common throughout the country, particularly in eastern Slovakia. **Strapacky**, a dish of shredded potato dumplings with sauerkraut, is another winter staple.

Slovak beer is excellent and cheap. Zlaty Bazant, Saris, and Topvar are the main brands, but craft breweries are appearing across the country. Wine from the Small Carpathian region near Bratislava, particularly the whites, is well worth trying. And slivovica (plum brandy) is the traditional spirit, offered at celebrations and as a welcome.

For such a small country, Slovakia has a remarkably distinct food identity. The cheese, the dumplings, and the generous portions reflect a mountain culture where hospitality means making sure nobody leaves the table hungry.
