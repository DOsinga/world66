*Europe > Spain > Andalucia > Cordoba*

Cordoba is the place where you understand what al-Andalus actually meant. In the 10th century this was the largest, richest, and most learned city in Western Europe — half a million people, hundreds of public baths, libraries holding work that the rest of the continent had forgotten existed. Walk through it today and you can still feel that layered inheritance: a Roman bridge, an Umayyad mosque with a Renaissance cathedral wedged inside it, a medieval Jewish quarter, and a Christian fortress that once housed Columbus pitching his expedition to Ferdinand and Isabella.

The **Mezquita-Catedral** is the obvious place to start, and there is no real way to be prepared for it. You step through the orange-tree courtyard into a forest of red-and-white striped arches, eight hundred and fifty of them, and somewhere in the middle a full Gothic cathedral rises straight out of the prayer hall. Charles V, who authorised the cathedral's construction, is supposed to have walked in afterwards and told the canons they had destroyed something unique to build something you can find anywhere. He was right, and the result is still extraordinary.

Just north of the mosque is the **Judería**, the old Jewish quarter, a tangle of whitewashed lanes barely wider than your shoulders, with one of the three medieval synagogues left in Spain and a small statue of Maimonides whose worn-shiny foot tells you which corner the tour groups stop at. South of the mosque the **Roman bridge** crosses the Guadalquivir to the Calahorra tower, best at sunset when the whole city goes gold. West of it, the **Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos** is a sober Christian fortress with strikingly good Moorish-style gardens.

If you can time a visit for **early May**, do. The **Festival de los Patios** opens up the private courtyards of the old town for two weeks — geraniums spilling off whitewashed walls, fountains running, a competition that Cordoba takes very seriously. The same month brings the Cruces de Mayo and the Feria, and the city is essentially one long party. Summer is brutal — temperatures regularly past 40°C — so spring and autumn are the times to come.

Cordoba is smaller than Seville or Granada and easily done in two days, but it rewards a third. Stay inside the old walls, eat **salmorejo** (the thick, garlicky cousin of gazpacho that Cordoba invented) and a glass of **Montilla-Moriles**, and walk the city after dark when the day-trippers have gone back to their tour buses.
