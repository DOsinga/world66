Córdoba is one of the most extraordinary cities in Spain — and arguably in Europe. For two centuries under the Umayyad Caliphate (8th–10th century), it was the largest city in western Europe, a centre of learning, philosophy, medicine, and trade that left Charlemagne's empire looking primitive by comparison. The physical legacy of that golden age is the Mezquita, a mosque of such scale and beauty that it remains one of the most visited buildings in the world.

The **Mezquita-Catedral** is the centrepiece: a vast hypostyle mosque whose forest of 856 columns and red-and-white striped double arches once accommodated 25,000 worshippers. When the Christians reconquered Córdoba in 1236, they inserted a full Renaissance cathedral into the middle of it — a collision of civilisations in stone that is both jarring and unforgettable. The surrounding **Judería** (Jewish Quarter) is a tightly packed network of whitewashed lanes, one of the three remaining medieval synagogues in Spain, and a neighbourhood that hums with the atmosphere of the three faiths that once coexisted here.

Beyond the historic centre, Córdoba is a small, manageable Andalucían city with good food and wine, shaded patios, and a particularly lively May festival season. Summer is brutal — regularly above 40°C — and is best avoided. Come in spring, particularly May, for the famous Patio Festival when private courtyards are thrown open to visitors.
