Málaga is the unsung capital of the Costa del Sol — a working port city with a 3,000-year history, a Moorish fortress on its hill, and Picasso's birthplace on its central square. For decades it was treated as the airport you flew into on the way to the beach resorts. That is changing fast: a string of new museums, a tidied-up old town, and a polished waterfront have turned it into one of the most rewarding short-break cities in southern Spain.

The compact historic centre wraps around the Cathedral and the **Alcazaba**, a beautifully preserved Moorish fortress connected by a long curtain wall to the higher **Gibralfaro Castle** — the climb up is steep but the view back down over the bullring, the port, and the Mediterranean is the definitive Málaga photograph. Below, the old town is a tangle of pedestrian streets, tapas bars, and small plazas converging on the **Plaza de la Merced**, where Picasso was born in 1881. The Picasso Museum a few streets away holds 200-plus works donated by his family.

Eat **fritura malagueña** — a paper cone of tiny fried fish — at one of the *chiringuitos* (beach shacks) in **El Palo** or **Pedregalejo**, the old fishing barrios east of the centre. Drink the local sweet wine, which is made from Moscatel and Pedro Ximénez grapes grown in the hills behind the city. The climate is the warmest in mainland Europe: over 300 sunny days a year and a Mediterranean that stays swimmable into late October.

Málaga is also the gateway to the rest of the province — [Ronda](/europe/spain/andalucia/ronda) and its dramatic gorge, the white town of [Antequera](/europe/spain/andalucia/antequera), the prehistoric caves of Nerja, and the resort strip of the Costa del Sol all lie within an hour or two by car or train.
