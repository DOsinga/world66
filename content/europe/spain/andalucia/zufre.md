Zufre is a small white village in the Sierra de Huelva, clinging to the edge of a gorge above the Rivera de Huelva river. Unlike the famous white towns of Cadiz province, Zufre has not been polished for tourists — there are no souvenir shops, no tour buses, and the village feels genuinely lived-in rather than preserved. The streets are steep, narrow, and quiet, and the houses are whitewashed in the traditional Andalusian style.

The most striking feature is the Paseo, a walkway along the cliff edge with views down into the gorge and across the surrounding sierra. The parish church of Nuestra Senora del Puerto Inmaculada Concepcion, dating from the 16th century, is worth a look for its Mudejar tower. Zufre also has a remarkably well-preserved Arab wall and gateway, a reminder of the town's Moorish past.

The Sierra de Huelva around Zufre is prime Iberian pig country — the dehesas of cork and holm oak that surround the village produce some of the best jamon iberico in Spain. The area suffers from depopulation and unemployment, which keeps it authentic but also means facilities are limited. There is a bar or two on the main square, but do not come expecting much in the way of restaurants or services. Zufre is best visited as a day trip from [Aracena](/europe/spain/andalucia/aracena) or Seville.
