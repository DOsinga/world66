Mallorca is the largest of the Balearic Islands and, despite decades of mass tourism, still one of the most varied and rewarding places in the western Mediterranean. The cliché — that the island is a sun-and-sangria strip of British and German package resorts — is true of a few stretches of coast, and resolutely false of everywhere else. Behind the beach hotels lies an island of pine-covered mountains, terraced olive groves, hilltop monasteries, working fishing harbours, and a capital city that can hold its own against anywhere on the Spanish mainland.

[Palma](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/palma1) is the obvious starting point. Its enormous Gothic cathedral, La Seu, rises directly from the waterfront — Gaudí worked on the interior in the early twentieth century — and the old quarter that spreads behind it is full of Baroque mansions, Arabic baths, and narrow stone streets that reward an aimless morning. Es Baluard, the contemporary art museum built into the old sea walls, is excellent. The food scene is serious: try ensaïmadas (the spiral pastry that is the island's most famous export) for breakfast and sobrasada — soft, paprika-cured pork sausage — on bread later in the day.

The Serra de Tramuntana, the spine of mountains running along the northwest coast, is the island's most beautiful region and a UNESCO World Heritage landscape. The road from Palma to [Puerto de Sóller](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/puertodesoller) winds above the sea through orange groves and past Valldemossa, where Chopin and George Sand spent their famously miserable winter of 1838–39 in a converted monastery cell. Deià, a few kilometres on, was the home of Robert Graves and remains a writers' and artists' refuge with the look of a hand-built stone village clinging to the hillside. Travel between Palma and Sóller on the wonderful narrow-gauge railway built in 1912 — slow, scenic, and infinitely more enjoyable than the bus.

The east coast has the best beaches: long, calm-water arcs of soft sand at Puerto de Alcúdia and Cala Millor, and a string of smaller coves cut into the limestone cliffs further south. The Coves del Drac near [Porto Cristo](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/portocristo) are a system of underground caverns containing one of Europe's largest subterranean lakes — visited by classical music concerts performed by musicians in illuminated boats, which is as theatrical as it sounds. Inland, the small market towns of [Pollença](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/pollensa), Sineu, and Petra still feel genuinely Mallorcan, with weekly markets and Sunday morning life going on around the church square.

A warning and an enthusiasm. The warning: the southern strip — [Magaluf](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/magaluf), [Playa de Palma](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/playa_de_palma), [S'Arenal](/europe/spain/balearicislands/mallorca/arenal) — is package-holiday Mallorca at its most cartoonish, and you should know what you are getting into. The enthusiasm: the rest of the island, especially out of season, is one of the great places to walk, drive, eat, and look at things slowly in the Mediterranean. Spring and autumn are the best times — the heat is bearable and the crowds have not arrived or have gone home.
