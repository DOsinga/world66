Menorca is the quieter, less-developed of the major Balearic Islands — a deliberate choice, since the island was declared a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve in 1993, limiting large-scale development and preserving the interior farmland and coastline. The result is an island that feels genuinely unspoiled compared to its neighbours Mallorca and Ibiza: narrower roads, smaller resorts, and a rural landscape of dry-stone walls, holm oak forests, and wildflower meadows.

The capital Maó (Mahón in Spanish) sits above one of the finest natural harbours in the Mediterranean — deep, sheltered, and over five kilometres long. The British occupied the island for much of the 18th century, and their legacy shows in the Georgian architecture around the harbour and in the local fondness for gin, still produced on the island. The old capital Ciutadella, at the western end, has a grander feel: Baroque palaces, Gothic churches, and a harbour filled with fishing boats and yachts.

The island has a remarkable concentration of prehistoric monuments from the Talayotic culture: stone circles, burial chambers (navetes), and T-shaped megaliths called taulas, many of which can be visited along rural paths. Fort Marlborough near Es Castell is a well-preserved example of British military engineering. The beaches — particularly on the south coast, which is sheltered from the Tramontana wind — are among the best in Spain, with sand ranging from white to red.

Getting here is straightforward by air from most European cities, or by overnight ferry from Barcelona or Valencia. A hire car or bicycle is essential for exploring beyond the main towns.
