Cáceres has one of the best-preserved medieval old towns in Europe, and hardly anyone outside Spain seems to know about it. The Ciudad Monumental — the walled upper town — is a UNESCO World Heritage Site of golden stone towers, Renaissance palaces, and narrow lanes that have changed so little since the fifteenth century that film crews keep coming back to use it as a period set. If you have seen Game of Thrones, you have seen Cáceres standing in for King's Landing.

The old town is compact enough to walk in an hour, but dense enough to reward a full day. The Plaza de Santa María anchors everything, with the co-cathedral and several noble palaces around it. The towers are the signature — the Torre de Bujaco, the Torre de las Cigüeñas (the only one the Catholic Monarchs allowed to keep its battlements), the Torre de Sande — each built by rival families competing for height and prestige. Storks nest on many of them, their clattering bills the only sound on a quiet afternoon.

Outside the walls, the Plaza Mayor is where modern Cáceres lives: cafes, tapas bars, and the evening paseo. Extremaduran food is hearty — ibérico pork, migas, torta del Casar cheese — and cheaper than almost anywhere else in Spain. Cáceres is roughly equidistant from Madrid and Lisbon, connected to both by train, and makes a natural stop on any cross-Iberian route.
