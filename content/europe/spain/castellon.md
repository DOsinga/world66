Castellón is the northernmost of the three provinces that make up the Valencia region, stretching from the Costa del Azahar (Coast of Orange Blossoms) on the Mediterranean to the rugged interior highlands of the Maestrat. While less famous than its southern neighbours, it rewards travellers who look beyond the beaches: the province combines a long stretch of largely undeveloped coast with a well-preserved medieval interior.

The coastline is anchored by the resort town of **Benicàssim**, popular with Spanish holidaymakers for its beaches and its famous FIB music festival, and by **Peñíscola**, a fortified town on a rocky peninsula that served as the refuge of the Avignon antipope Benedict XIII (Papa Luna) in the early fifteenth century. Further north, **Vinaròs** is a working fishing port known for its large prawns. Inland, **Morella** is one of the most dramatic medieval towns in Spain — a walled city perched on a hilltop castle, with well-preserved streets and an important Gothic church. **Sant Mateu** was once the capital of the Maestrat region and retains a quiet, unhurried character.

The province is also known for its ceramics tradition, centred on the town of Onda, and for the Desierto de las Palmas natural park above Benicàssim, which offers good hiking in surprisingly wild terrain just minutes from the coast.
