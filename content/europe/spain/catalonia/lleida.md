Lleida (Lérida in Spanish) is the westernmost city of Catalonia, sitting on a rise above the Segre river in a broad agricultural plain that produces some of the best fruit in Spain — the orchards around the city turn spectacular in spring blossom. It is often overlooked by travelers rushing between Barcelona and Zaragoza, but those who stop discover a city with a proud history and one outstanding monument.

Dominating the old town from its hilltop is La Seu Vella, the old cathedral of Lleida — an extraordinary Gothic structure begun in the thirteenth century. After the War of Succession in 1707, the cathedral was converted into a military barracks (Felipe V's punishment for Lleida supporting the wrong side), and it was only returned to cultural use in the twentieth century. The result is a building that is half military fortress, half ecclesiastical grandeur, with cloisters that look out over rooftops toward the Pyrenees on clear days.

The lower city is lively and modern, with a university that keeps the bar and café scene energetic. The covered market and the pedestrian streets around Carrer Major are pleasant to wander. Lleida is also a useful base for the Pyrenean foothills and the medieval villages of the Pallars region to the north.
