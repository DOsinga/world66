Cáceres is one of the best-preserved medieval cities in Europe, and one of the least crowded. Its old city — a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986 — sits on a hill and contains an almost unbroken ensemble of palaces, towers, and fortified houses from the 14th to 16th centuries. Walk through the Arco de la Estrella into the Ciudad Monumental and the modern world largely disappears.

The city's layered history shows in its architecture. The Romans built the walls, the Moors strengthened them and added towers — about thirty survive, the most famous being the Torre del Bujaco on Plaza Mayor. Then the Christian reconquest brought feudal noblemen, who spent the following centuries constructing their palaces and fortified houses throughout the old city. The Catholic Monarchs eventually forced the removal of the defensive battlements from many towers, which is why so many of them have a peculiarly flat top.

The result is a remarkably coherent medieval streetscape: the Palace of Los Golfines de Arriba, the Casa de las Cigüeñas (House of the Storks), the Gothic church of Santa María with its cathedral status, the Convento de San Francisco. Arab, Gothic, and Italian Renaissance styles sit alongside each other without much fuss.

Cáceres is the capital of Extremadura, a region that produced a disproportionate share of the conquistadors who went to the Americas — Hernán Cortés and Francisco Pizarro were both from here. The wealth that came back from the New World funded some of the grander Renaissance palaces you see today.

The lower city around Plaza Mayor is livelier, with cafes and bars filling the square in the evenings. The old city itself is remarkably quiet, even in summer — the Spanish tourists who come here tend to be culture-minded, and the international crowds that swamp Toledo or Segovia have not yet discovered Cáceres in the same numbers.
