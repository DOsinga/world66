Guadalajara is a provincial capital about 55 km northeast of Madrid, well connected by commuter rail and fast trains that run every 30 minutes. It is often overlooked in favour of Toledo or Segovia, but it has genuine historical monuments that reward a half-day visit.

The city was founded by the Iberians, occupied by the Moors, and reconquered in 1085. Its most impressive building is the Palacio de los Duques del Infantado, built in 1461 by Juan Guas in a striking combination of Gothic, Renaissance, and Mudéjar styles — the façade covered in diamond-shaped stone bosses is unlike anything else in Castile. The Palacio de los Mendoza (1407) has two Plateresque doorways and a graceful Renaissance courtyard. Among the religious buildings, the church of San Ginés is Renaissance in style and the convent of Santa Clara dates to the 14th century.

Guadalajara is an easy day trip from Madrid and makes a pleasant alternative to the more tourist-heavy Castilian cities.
