Valladolid is the de facto capital of Castilla y León and one of the great historic cities of Spain's central plateau. It served as the capital of the Spanish Empire under Philip III in the early 17th century, and both Columbus and Cervantes lived here. The city still carries that weight — grand plazas, ornate churches, and the imposing National Museum of Sculpture housed in the Colegio de San Gregorio, one of the finest examples of Isabelline Gothic in the country.

The city centre is compact and walkable, centred on the Plaza Mayor — one of the first grand squares in Spain, which became the model for others across the country. The cathedral, designed by Juan de Herrera (architect of El Escorial), was never finished but is still worth visiting for its sheer ambition. The university quarter gives the city a youthful energy, and the tapas scene along Calle de la Correos and the surrounding streets is excellent — Valladolid takes its food seriously and prices are far lower than in Madrid or Barcelona.

Valladolid sits in the heart of the Ribera del Duero wine region. The surrounding countryside produces some of Spain's best red wines, and many bodegas are within easy day-trip distance.
