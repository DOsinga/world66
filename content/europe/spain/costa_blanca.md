The Costa Blanca stretches along the Alicante coast from Dénia in the north to Torrevieja in the south, its name — "white coast" — coming from the pale sand and the limestone cliffs that punctuate the shoreline. It is one of Spain's most popular beach holiday regions, drawing millions of visitors a year with reliable sunshine and warm Mediterranean water.

The coast has plenty of variety. Dénia and Jávea at the northern end are more Spanish and less developed, sitting beneath the rocky headland of the Montgo. [Benidorm](/europe/spain/andalucia/benidorm) is the high-rise resort everyone pictures, but it has good beaches and a surprisingly lively old town. Further south, Altea and Calpe are quieter, with the dramatic rock of the Peñón de Ifach rising straight out of the sea at Calpe. Villajoyosa, south of Benidorm, is known for its colourful waterfront houses and its chocolate factories.

Inland, the Alicante hinterland is surprisingly mountainous, and the city of Elche — with the largest palm grove in Europe, a UNESCO World Heritage site — is well worth a detour.
