---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Spain celebrates harder and more often than almost any country in Europe. Many festivals are deeply local — a single town's patron saint's day that draws the surrounding region — but a handful are internationally famous and worth planning a trip around.

**Las Fallas (Valencia, mid-March)** is five days of fireworks, massive satirical sculptures (called fallas), and a daily midday firecracker explosion called the mascletà. On the final night, almost all the sculptures are burned. Loud, smoky, and spectacular.

**Semana Santa (nationwide, Holy Week before Easter)** transforms cities across Spain with solemn religious processions, where brotherhoods carry enormous ornate floats through the streets. Seville's processions are the most famous — the combination of incense, candlelight, and saeta (flamenco lament) sung from balconies is unlike anything else in Europe. Málaga, Valladolid, and Zamora also have outstanding celebrations.

**Feria de Abril (Seville, two weeks after Easter)** shifts completely from solemnity to joy. A week-long fair with flamenco dancing, horse displays, and casetas (private tents) where sevillanas music plays from afternoon until dawn. Visiting without an invitation to a caseta requires some social engineering, but the atmosphere spills onto the streets.

**San Fermín (Pamplona, 6–14 July)** is much more than the running of the bulls. The week is a non-stop street party with music, dancing, and processions. The encierro (bull run) takes place each morning at 8am — watching from a balcony is safer and gives a better view than running.

**La Tomatina (Buñol, last Wednesday of August)** is exactly what it sounds like: a town-wide tomato fight, with over 100 tonnes of overripe tomatoes thrown in the streets. It sells out quickly; book tickets well ahead.

**La Mercè (Barcelona, 24 September)** is the city's main festival — free outdoor concerts, human towers (castellers), fire runs, and fireworks across multiple venues for several days.
