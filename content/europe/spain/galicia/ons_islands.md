The Illas Ons are a small, windswept archipelago in the Atlantic off the coast of Galicia, marking the seaward edge of the Ría de Pontevedra. They are part of the Illas Atlánticas National Park, Galicia's only national park, which also covers the better-known Cíes islands further south. Ons is quieter, emptier, and more atmospheric than its famous neighbours: a single main island with a handful of beaches, a lighthouse on a cliff, and the stone cottages of a village whose inhabitants made a living from octopus and shellfish until the 1970s, when most of them left.

The main draw is simply the walking. From the ferry pier at Area dos Cans, paths radiate out through low scrub and dune to the lighthouse, to the beach at Melide (one of the loveliest unspoilt beaches in Galicia), and south to the Buraco do Inferno — a sea-eroded pit where you can hear the Atlantic booming away below your feet through the rock. The whole island can be walked in a few hours, and the number of daily visitors is capped, so even at the height of summer it rarely feels crowded.

Boats run in the summer months (roughly June to September) from Sanxenxo, Portonovo, Marín, and Bueu — the crossings take about forty minutes. Day trips are the standard, but there is a controlled campsite on the island if you want to stay overnight, which is the best way to see Ons in the evening after the day boats have left. Bring water and food: there is a small café but limited supplies. And come prepared for the wind, which rarely takes a day off.
