Pontevedra is the capital of its province and one of the best-preserved medieval towns in Galicia. The old town is a tight cluster of granite arcades, stone squares, and narrow streets running down toward the Lérez estuary — and since the 1990s it has been almost entirely car-free, which makes it feel like a genuinely liveable place rather than a tourist stage set.

The Lérez River meets the sea at the Ría de Pontevedra, one of the Rías Baixas. The city grew up at this estuary crossing — the port gave it purpose, and the river still defines the western edge of the old town. Within a short walk you can reach the Castiñeiras Lake, the beaches of the O Morrazo peninsula, the fishing village of Combarro, and the island of A Toxa.

The museum is the main cultural draw. It occupies four historic buildings around Leña Square and covers Galician archaeology and art from prehistoric gold to 20th-century drawings. The Santa María Basilica, commissioned by the Guild of Mareantes in the 16th century, has a Plateresque façade that ranks among the finest in Spain. La Peregrina church, shaped like a scallop shell, stands at the southern gate of the old town on the Portuguese Camino route to Santiago.
