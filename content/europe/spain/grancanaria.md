Gran Canaria is often called a miniature continent, and the description is earned. In a single day you can drive from the golden dunes of Maspalomas in the south, through pine forests and volcanic ridges in the mountainous interior, to the lush green valleys of the north. Few islands this small pack in so much variety.

The capital, [Las Palmas](/europe/spain/grancanaria/laspalmas), is a proper city with a historic old quarter, one of the best urban beaches in the Atlantic, and a food scene that draws on both Spanish and Latin American traditions. Most package tourists head straight for the southern resorts around Playa del Ingles and Maspalomas, where the sun is more reliable and the beaches stretch for miles. But the real character of the island lies elsewhere — in the mountain villages of the interior, the cave houses of Artenara, the wild coastline around Agaete, and the surprisingly green north.

The interior of the island rises steeply to Pico de las Nieves at nearly 2,000 metres. The roads up are spectacular, winding through volcanic terrain with views that stretch across the entire island. Roque Nublo, a massive volcanic plug standing alone on a ridge, is the most iconic landmark and a worthwhile hike.

Gran Canaria sits in the eastern Canaries, a few hours by ferry from [Tenerife](/europe/spain/tenerife) and [Fuerteventura](/europe/spain/fuerteventura). The climate is mild year-round, though the south is distinctly drier and warmer than the north. Winter is peak season for Europeans escaping the cold, but spring and autumn are lovely and less crowded.
