Jaén is the olive oil capital of the world, and that is not an exaggeration. The province produces more olive oil than all of Greece, and the endless rows of olive trees covering every hillside are the defining image of this part of Andalusia. The city itself sits at the foot of the Cerro de Santa Catalina, crowned by a castle with sweeping views across the olive groves to the Sierra Morena mountains.

The Cathedral of Jaén is the city's masterpiece -- a monumental Renaissance building designed by Andrés de Vandelvira, with a grandeur that seems out of proportion for a provincial city of this size. The facade alone is worth the trip, and the interior holds what the church claims is the relic of the Holy Face (Santo Rostro). The cathedral served as a model for churches across Latin America, and you can see its influence from Lima to Oaxaca.

Above the city, the Castillo de Santa Catalina has been partly converted into a parador, and even if you are not staying there, the walk up through the old quarter rewards you with views that explain why this hilltop has been fortified since Carthaginian times. The Arab Baths (Baños Árabes) in the old town are among the largest and best-preserved in [Spain](/europe/spain), dating to the 11th century and rediscovered beneath a 16th-century palace.

Jaén is routinely bypassed by travellers heading to [Granada](/europe/spain/granada) or [Córdoba](/europe/spain/cordoba), which is a shame. It is a genuine, unhurried Andalusian city with excellent tapas -- many bars still serve a free tapa with every drink -- and none of the tourist crowds of its neighbours. The surrounding province also holds the Renaissance towns of Úbeda and Baeza, both UNESCO World Heritage Sites, making Jaén a strong base for a few days of exploration.
