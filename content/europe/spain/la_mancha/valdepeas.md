Valdepeñas is the wine capital of La Mancha, sitting at the southern end of the vast Castilian plateau where the endless vineyards produce more wine than any other single region in the world. The town has been making wine since at least the 12th century, and it shows — bodegas line the streets, and the local Tempranillo-based reds are honest, full-bodied, and remarkably cheap.

The Plaza de España is a handsome main square with arcades and the 16th-century church of La Asunción. The Museo del Vino, housed in a traditional bodega, walks you through the winemaking process and finishes with a tasting. Several bodegas in town offer visits — the most atmospheric are the older ones with rows of giant clay tinajas (amphorae) that were used to ferment wine for centuries before steel tanks arrived.

Valdepeñas sits on the N-IV between Madrid and Andalucía, and most people blow past it on the motorway. That is their loss. Stop for lunch, drink a glass of the local red with manchego cheese and migas (fried breadcrumbs with pork), and you will understand why La Mancha is more than just windmills and Don Quixote.
