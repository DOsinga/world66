Lorca sits in the dry southeast corner of Spain, a city of around 90,000 people that has been continuously settled since the Bronze Age. Its skyline is dominated by the Fortaleza del Sol, a castle complex perched above the old town that has served as a Roman, Visigothic, and Moorish stronghold over the centuries. The fortress has been extensively restored and is worth the climb for the views alone — the semi-arid landscape stretching out toward the coast is unlike anything in greener Spain.

The old centre below the castle is full of baroque churches and noble houses from Lorca's wealthy 17th and 18th centuries. The Colegiata de San Patricio, dedicated to Saint Patrick after the city was recaptured from the Moors on his feast day, anchors the main square with an ornate facade. The streets around it are pleasant for wandering, with a handful of good tapas bars tucked into the side streets.

Lorca is best known across Spain for its Semana Santa processions, which rival those of Seville in spectacle if not in fame. Two rival brotherhoods — the Blancos and the Azules — compete with elaborate biblical floats and thousands of costumed participants. The processions were badly disrupted by the 2011 earthquake that damaged much of the old town, but the city has rebuilt with determination. If you are in [Murcia](/europe/spain/murcia) during Holy Week, Lorca is well worth the 45-minute drive south.
