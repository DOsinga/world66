Pamplona is known worldwide for the San Fermin festival each July, when bulls charge through the old town streets and the city erupts into a week-long party. But reduce this place to a single week of chaos and you miss the point entirely.

The rest of the year, Pamplona is a handsome, walkable city of around 200,000 people, sitting on a plateau south of the Pyrenees. The medieval Casco Viejo is compact and well-preserved, ringed by massive star-shaped fortifications and green parks that follow the line of the old walls. The cathedral, tucked away in the quietest corner of the old town, has a Gothic cloister that ranks among the finest in Europe.

What really sets Pamplona apart is the food. Navarre's capital takes its pintxos seriously -- the bars along Calle San Nicolas and Calle Estafeta serve some of the best in northern Spain. The local cuisine leans on seasonal vegetables (white asparagus, piquillo peppers, artichokes), lamb from the Pyrenean foothills, and the excellent rosado wines of the region.

Pamplona is also a key stop on the Camino de Santiago. Pilgrims have been walking through here for a thousand years, entering through the medieval gate at the Portal de Francia and crossing the old town before heading west toward Puente la Reina. Known as Irunea in Basque, the city sits at the cultural boundary between Basque and Navarrese identity -- a tension that adds texture to the place without overwhelming it.
