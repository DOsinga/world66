El Bierzo is a comarca tucked into the far northwest corner of León province, cupped by mountains on three sides and open toward Galicia on the fourth. Geographically and culturally it sits between worlds — the meseta of Castile to the east, the green hills of Galicia to the west — and that in-between quality gives it a character unlike either neighbour.

The Camino de Santiago crosses El Bierzo, passing through Ponferrada and climbing to O Cebreiro at its western edge, so pilgrims have been moving through for a thousand years. Ponferrada's Templar castle is one of the finest in Spain, and the old town around it is worth an afternoon. But El Bierzo rewards those who linger: the slate-roofed villages of Las Médulas, where Roman gold mines carved an eerie landscape of red cliffs and lakes, are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and genuinely strange and beautiful.

The region also makes wine. The Mencía grape thrives on the mountain slopes and produces reds that are lighter and more aromatic than most Spanish reds — closer in spirit to a Burgundy than a Ribera del Duero. The DO Bierzo designation covers around 3,400 hectares; look for single-vineyard bottlings from the older, higher-altitude plots. White wines from Godello are increasingly impressive. This is not a place for cellar-door tourism on a grand scale, but the wine shops in Ponferrada are well stocked and the locals are proud of what they grow.
