Bilbao (pop. 350,000) is the largest city in the Basque Country and one of the most remarkable urban turnarounds in recent European history. For most of the twentieth century it was a gritty industrial port, its fortunes built on steel, shipbuilding, and the Nervión River that cuts through the city. When those industries collapsed in the 1970s and 80s, Bilbao faced a crisis that many similar cities never recovered from. Instead, it reinvented itself.

The catalyst was the Guggenheim Museum Bilbao, opened in 1997 in Frank Gehry's extraordinary titanium-clad building on the waterfront. The gamble paid off spectacularly. The museum drew visitors, the waterfront was redeveloped, and the city's self-confidence returned. Norman Foster's metro system, opened two years earlier, gave Bilbao a piece of public infrastructure so beautiful that its entrance canopies — nicknamed *fosteritos* — became a civic symbol.

But Bilbao is more than its architectural showpieces. The medieval Casco Viejo, a tight grid of pedestrian lanes in the old quarter, is genuinely alive: local bars, independent shops, and the vast covered Mercado de la Ribera on the riverbank. The pintxo culture here is among the best in the Basque Country — small bites of extraordinary quality, eaten standing at the bar with a glass of txakoli.

A day is enough to see the highlights; two days lets you settle in and eat properly.
