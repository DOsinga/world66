Pamplona (Iruñea in Basque) is the capital of the foral community of Navarra, a city of around 200,000 people sitting at the foot of the western Pyrenees, about 50 km south of the mountain passes into France. Outside Spain, it is known almost exclusively for one thing: the Fiesta de San Fermín in July, which includes the famous encierro — the running of the bulls through the narrow streets of the old town.

The nine-day festival each July is genuinely extraordinary — loud, chaotic, and all-consuming — and the city fills beyond capacity. Outside festival time, Pamplona is a pleasant, prosperous Spanish city with a well-preserved medieval old town (the casco antiguo), the large star-shaped citadel rebuilt in the 16th century, and a good food scene. The cuisine of Navarra is among the finest in northern Spain: the region produces excellent red wine, the white asparagus is famous, and the pintxos bars in the old town are serious business.

Navarra itself is worth exploring beyond the capital. It is unusually rich in Romanesque art and architecture — the pilgrimage route to Santiago de Compostela passes through the region, leaving a trail of fine churches. The Bardenas Reales, a semi-desert landscape in the south of the region, is one of the most remarkable and unexpected landscapes in Spain.
