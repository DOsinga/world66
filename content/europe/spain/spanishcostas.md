Spain's Mediterranean coastline stretches over two thousand kilometres from the French border to the Strait of Gibraltar, and each costa has its own character. The Costa Blanca around [Alicante](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/alicante) and [Benidorm](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/benidorm) draws visitors with reliably warm winters and long sandy beaches backed by palm-lined promenades. Inland, [Elche](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/elche) has the largest palm grove in Europe, a UNESCO World Heritage site, while [Valencia](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/valencia) combines futuristic architecture with one of Spain's liveliest food scenes -- paella was invented here.

Further south, the Costa del Sol in [Andalucia](/europe/spain/andalucia) runs from Malaga to Gibraltar, mixing purpose-built resorts with whitewashed fishing villages that have barely changed in decades. Beyond the beaches, the coast gives easy access to Moorish towns like [Vejer de la Frontera](/europe/spain/andalucia/vejerdelafrontera) and the ancient port of [Cadiz](/europe/spain/andalucia/cadiz), one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in western Europe. The Costa de la Luz, facing the Atlantic between Cadiz and [Tarifa](/europe/spain/andalucia/tarifa), is wilder and less developed -- rolling dunes, strong winds for kitesurfing, and views across the strait to Morocco.

What sets the Spanish costas apart from other Mediterranean beach destinations is what happens off the sand. The climate is generous enough for year-round outdoor life, and even small towns have tapas bars where you can eat well for very little. Local markets sell fresh seafood straight from the morning catch, and fiestas -- from Valencia's firework-mad Fallas to Cadiz's raucous carnival -- punctuate the calendar. You can spend a morning on the beach and an afternoon exploring a Gothic cathedral, a Roman amphitheatre, or a hilltop castle, all within a short drive.
