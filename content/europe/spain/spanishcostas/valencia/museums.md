It's difficult to classify the City of Arts and Sciences, but as the city's site lists two major parts as museums, it had better come here. One part is undoubtedly a museum, the Museo de las Ciencias Principe Felipe, whilst the Palacio de las Artes is a multi-halled establishment for all types of show. l'Hemisferic is an IMAX theatre and l'Umbracle combines botany and statues. That leaves the wonderful Oceanográfic, classified as a science museum on the city's site.

To call it an aquarium would be to belittle it. Even Lisbon's wonderful ocanarium comes nowhere near it. It has mammals [whales, walruses, sealions, seals, otters], amphibians [turtles], birds [scarlet ibisis, pelicans, black-winged stilts, flamingos, spoonbills] and, of course, masses and masses of fish. In all they claim 45,000 creatures of 500 different species. There are pavilions, all superbly architectured, for the Arctic, the Antarctic [unhelpfully called OCEAN on their website], Oceans [properly called OCEANOS], Metiterranean, Temparate and Tropical and Wetlands as well as the entrance building, erected to represent the head and tail of a whale, the Delfinarium [dolphin pools where there are three magnificent shows a day], the Red Sea auditorium with a giant screen. The Delfinarium alone has seating for over 2,200 people to watch the shows and 23 million litres of water! Even by world standards this is big and it's certainly the biggest of its kind in Europe.

See the website - www.cac.es - it takes a while for the different sections of the city to appear at the top.

### [Museo de Bellas Artes](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/valencia/museums/museo_de_bellas_ar)

The building is smashing. The museum itself is rated as one of the finest in Spain and contains some incredible ecclesiastic art. There is a significant El Greco and a wonderful Bosch. A personal highlight is Jose Benlliure Gil's 'Vision del Coliseo', a huge and dark canvas in the domed entrance hall. I strongly recommend taking your time over the first/second floor and leaving the more parochial later and lesser work on the third floor alone. Entry free.

### [Museo Nacional de la Cerámica](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/valencia/museums/museo_nacional_de)

The Ceramics Museum is praised at one of the TICS as 'one of our very nicest museums' and it thoroughly deserves the description.

There are some early ceramics from the Visigothic period and any number from the 13th to the 19th century with exhibitions of 21st century ceramics on the ground floor.

The building is in the baroque style and the façades could reasonably be considered way over the top.

### [Museo Fallero](/europe/spain/spanishcostas/valencia/museums/museo_fallero)

This is the museum mentioned on the main Valencia page where the ninots [floats] that are deemed the very best in both the open and the children's sections are kept out of the bonfires [fallas] and exhibited. Sadly, unlike most valencia museums, it doesn't allow non-flash photography.

Non-Spaniards will obviously not understand all the subtleties - as lampoonery of politicians and other prominent citizens plays a major part - but anybody must appreciate the artistry and craftsmanship that have gone into the exhibits.
