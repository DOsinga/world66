Arvidsjaur sits deep in Swedish Lapland, roughly 800 kilometres north of Stockholm, and it gets cold here — genuinely, bracingly cold. That's partly the point. Winter temperatures regularly plunge to -20°C and below, which is why the town draws car manufacturers for cold-weather testing and why the frozen lake doubles as a test track each January.

The town's most distinctive sight is Lappstaden, the oldest preserved Sami church town in Sweden. Built from the 18th century onwards, it consists of nearly 80 timber huts that Sami reindeer herders used when travelling to town for church services and market days. In winter it stands largely empty and blanketed in snow; in summer a handful of Sami families still use the huts, and the site is open as a living museum. Walking among the low wooden buildings gives a tangible sense of how seasonal Lapland life once worked.

Beyond Lappstaden, Arvidsjaur is a base for the classic Lapland outdoors: ice fishing on the lake, snowmobile safaris across the taiga, and dog sledding in the surrounding forest. In summer the same landscape offers hiking, canoeing, and the long Arctic evenings.
