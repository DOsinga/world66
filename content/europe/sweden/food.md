Swedish food has changed dramatically since the 1990s. The country now has a serious restaurant scene — Stockholm has more Michelin stars than any other Scandinavian city — alongside the traditional comfort food that Swedes actually eat at home.

## Traditional Dishes

**Meatballs (köttbullar)** with mashed potatoes, lingonberry jam, and pickled cucumber is the national comfort food. Yes, the ones at IKEA are a reasonable approximation; no, they're not the best version.

**Husmanskost** — traditional home cooking — means dishes like pea soup (ärtsoppa, eaten on Thursdays by tradition), pork with stewed vegetables, and Jansson's Temptation (a gratin of potatoes, onions, and pickled sprats).

**Smörgåsbord** is the format, not a dish: a spread of cold and hot dishes from which you help yourself in multiple rounds. The cornerstone is always pickled herring in various preparations.

**Crayfish parties (kräftskiva)** happen in August when the season opens. It's a social ritual as much as a meal: bibs, paper hats, schnapps, and singing. Get invited to one if at all possible.

**Surströmming** — fermented Baltic herring — is eaten mainly in northern Sweden in late August. It smells spectacularly bad and is genuinely an acquired taste. Hotels and ferries routinely ban it indoors.

## Fika

The Swedish institution of *fika* — a coffee break, usually with something sweet — happens mid-morning and mid-afternoon. It's a genuine social ritual, not just a break. Cinnamon buns (kanelbullar), cardamom buns, and prinsesstårta (a dome of sponge, cream, and green marzipan) are staples.

## New Nordic

Stockholm and Gothenburg have embraced the New Nordic cooking movement, with many restaurants focused on local, seasonal, and foraged ingredients. Noma's influence has filtered down to mid-range restaurants too: expect dishes built around rye, root vegetables, butter, and Scandinavian fish. It's genuinely excellent.

## Drinking

Sweden has tight alcohol regulations. Wine, spirits, and strong beer are only sold through Systembolaget, the state monopoly, which closes at 18:00 on weekdays. Bars and restaurants can serve alcohol but prices are steep. Beer (öl) is divided by strength: folk öl (max 3.5%) can be bought in supermarkets; anything stronger needs Systembolaget.

Aquavit (snaps) is drunk at celebrations — midsummer, Christmas, crayfish parties — usually in small shots accompanied by herring and song.

Coffee consumption in Sweden is among the highest in the world.
