Gotska Sandön is one of the most isolated islands in the Baltic Sea — a 9-by-6-kilometre slab of sand sitting 38 km north of [Gotland](/europe/sweden/gotland), entirely given over to a national park. The sand is about 70 metres deep, and the wind constantly reshapes the dunes, the highest of which reach 43 metres above sea level. Innumerable ships have wrecked on its shores over the centuries; the first lighthouse went up in 1858.

Most of the island is covered by a uniform pine forest, but in sheltered spots there are groves of deciduous trees with a richer flora, including orchids. Where the moving sand has overwhelmed the forest, ghostly stands of dead pines remain. The complete absence of grazing animals since conservation began has actually created a new problem — the dunes are stabilising, and the open sandy habitats that make the island special are slowly being reclaimed by vegetation.

People have visited since the Stone Age, but the island has never supported a permanent settlement — just fishermen and seal hunters staying temporarily. Tourism is strictly regulated. A limited number of visitors can stay overnight in small cottages or tents, and supplies should be brought in, since there are no shops. The reward is near-total solitude on endless white sand beaches.
