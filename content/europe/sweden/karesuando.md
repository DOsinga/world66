Karesuando is the northernmost town in Sweden, sitting on the Muonio River right at the Finnish border. The Finnish town of Karesuanto is just across the bridge, and the two communities share a culture that is as much Finnish and Sami as it is Swedish. The landscape up here is subarctic tundra and birch forest, flat and vast, with temperatures that drop below minus 40 in winter.

The town is small — a few hundred people — but it has an outsized place in Swedish history. Lars Levi Laestadius, the 19th-century botanist and preacher whose revivalist movement still has followers across northern Scandinavia, was the parish priest here. His parsonage is preserved as a museum. The Vita Huset cultural centre has exhibitions on Sami and Tornedalian culture.

Karesuando is a starting point for wilderness excitions into the Karesuando-Enontekio fell area, and in winter it is one of the best places in Sweden to see the northern lights. The midnight sun shines from late May to mid-July. If you are driving north through Swedish Lapland, this is quite literally the end of the road.
