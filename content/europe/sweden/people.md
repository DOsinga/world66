---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Sweden has a population of around 10.5 million. The country is less homogeneous than many visitors expect. Significant immigration since the 1960s — from Finland, the former Yugoslavia, the Middle East, East Africa, and elsewhere — has reshaped Swedish society. Today roughly one in five Swedes was born abroad or has at least one parent born abroad.

The indigenous Sámi people live primarily in the far north, in Swedish Lapland. They number around 20,000 in Sweden and maintain distinct cultural traditions including reindeer herding, language, and crafts.

One thing that strikes most visitors immediately: Swedes speak excellent English. It is taught from an early age and reinforced by undubbed TV and films. You will rarely encounter a situation where language is a barrier.

Swedes tend to be reserved with strangers but warm once the ice breaks. The concept of *lagom* — roughly meaning "just the right amount," neither too much nor too little — is often cited as a cultural cornerstone. It reflects an aversion to showing off and a preference for understatement and fairness.

The welfare state is a source of genuine pride. High taxes fund universal healthcare, free education through university level, generous parental leave, and an extensive social safety net. Politics leans pragmatic: both conservative and social democratic governments have maintained the core of the system.

Sweden ranks consistently near the top of gender equality indices. Women make up roughly half of parliament and are well represented in senior positions across business and government.

Religion plays a limited role in daily life. The Church of Sweden (Lutheran) claims a large nominal membership but regular attendance is low. Sweden is one of the most secular countries in the world.
