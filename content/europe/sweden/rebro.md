Örebro sits roughly halfway between [Stockholm](/europe/sweden/stockholm) and [Gothenburg](/europe/sweden/gothenburg), at a crossroads that has made it a market town since at least the 13th century. The east-west water route across the great lakes met the north-south land road here, and the town grew up around the traffic.

The castle is the centrepiece — a medieval fortress on an island in the River Svartån, expanded into a grand Renaissance castle in the 16th century. It still dominates the city centre and is open for guided tours. Along the river, Wadköping is an open-air quarter of old wooden houses saved from demolition in the 1960s and reassembled here, named after the fictional town in Hjalmar Bergman's novels.

Örebro is now a university town with a pleasant, walkable centre. The shoe factories that defined the city for a century are all gone. An oddity worth knowing: the world's first mushroom-shaped water tower was built here in 1958 — there is an exact copy in Riyadh.

Birdwatchers should head to Oset, the marshes where the Svartån flows into Lake Hjälmaren. It is one of Sweden's best birding sites, with hides and boardwalks through the wetlands.
