Österlen is the southeastern tip of Sweden — a gently rolling landscape of wheat fields, apple orchards, and long sandy beaches that feels a world apart from Stockholm. Technically it's not an official region at all, more a shared idea: the area roughly between Brösarp in the north and Ystad in the south, hugging the Baltic coast. What holds it together is light, quiet, and a certain unhurried pace that Swedes have been coming here for generations to find.

The coastline is the main draw. About 50 kilometers of sandy beach runs from Haväng down to Ystad, backed by dunes and low pine forest. Sandhammaren, near the southern tip, is one of the finest beaches in Scandinavia — wide, wild, and windswept. The water is cold by Mediterranean standards but swimmable in July and August. Outside high summer you'll often have stretches entirely to yourself.

Inland, the landscape is dotted with prehistoric monuments, medieval churches, and apple farms selling juice from roadside stalls. Ales Stenar — a 59-stone ship-shaped monument from around 600 AD — sits on a cliff above the sea south of Kåseberga and is worth the short walk from the car park. Glimmingehus is one of the best-preserved medieval fortresses in Scandinavia, still standing essentially as it was in 1500. Kivik is famous for its Bronze Age burial cairn and, incongruously, for producing much of Sweden's apple crop — the orchards blossom spectacularly in May.

Ystad, at the southern edge, is the main town. Most people know it as the setting for the Wallander crime novels, but it's a genuinely pretty place with a well-preserved medieval center and good ferry connections to Bornholm and Poland. Simrishamn on the east coast is smaller, quieter, and worth an afternoon.

Cycling is the best way to see the area. The roads are flat, the distances are manageable, and there are marked routes through the countryside. A car is useful for flexibility but not strictly necessary if you base yourself in Ystad.
