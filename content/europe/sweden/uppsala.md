Uppsala is Sweden's ancient spiritual and intellectual capital, a city where Viking burial mounds and a medieval cathedral sit alongside one of Europe's oldest universities. With a population of around 175,000, it is Sweden's fourth largest city, yet it has the feel of a small town — compact, walkable, and shaped by its 30,000 students.

The city sits along the Fyris River, just 70 km northwest of [Stockholm](/europe/sweden/stockholm). On the western bank, the massive red-brick Uppsala Cathedral dominates the skyline, flanked by the pink Uppsala Castle on its hilltop and the university's elegant Carolina Rediviva library. The eastern bank is flatter and more commercial, with shops and restaurants lining the pedestrian streets. A few kilometres north, the ancient royal burial mounds at Old Uppsala mark the site where Sweden's pre-Christian kings held court and worshipped the Norse gods.

Uppsala's character is defined by its university, founded in 1477. The student "nations" — regional clubs housed in grand 19th-century buildings — run their own pubs, restaurants, and events, forming a considerable part of the city's social life. On Walpurgis Eve (30 April), the students take over entirely: tens of thousands gather below the castle to watch white-capped graduates tumble down the hill, and the celebrations carry on late into the spring night.

The city is also inseparable from Carl Linnaeus, the father of modern taxonomy, who taught here for decades. His botanical garden and summer residence at Hammarby remain open to visitors, preserved much as he left them.
