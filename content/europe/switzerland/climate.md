---
title: "Climate"
type: section
---

Switzerland's climate varies significantly with altitude and region. On the central plateau, where most cities sit, the climate is temperate — warm summers (around 25°C in July), cold winters (around 0°C in January), and rain spread fairly evenly through the year. The Swiss plateau gets around 900–1,200 mm of precipitation annually.

In the Alps, temperatures drop roughly 2°C for every 300 metres of elevation gain. Above 2,700 metres, snow can fall in any month, and the highest peaks are permanently glaciated. Afternoon thunderstorms are common in the mountains from June through August — plan hikes for mornings.

The Ticino (Italian-speaking south) is the warmest region, with a Mediterranean influence: hot, dry summers and milder winters than the north. Lugano averages 230 days of sunshine per year.

Two notable winds shape Swiss weather: the **bise**, a cold northerly that brings clear, sharp conditions especially in winter, and the **foehn**, a warm, dry wind that funnels down from the Alps and can raise temperatures dramatically on the northern side of the mountains, sometimes contributing to wildfires and triggering headaches and fatigue in locals.
