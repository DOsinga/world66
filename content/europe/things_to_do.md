---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

Europe's cities are the main event for most visitors, and rightly so. The concentration of world-class museums is staggering — the Louvre and Musée d'Orsay in [Paris](/europe/france/paris), the Rijksmuseum in [Amsterdam](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam), the British Museum in [London](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/london), the Uffizi in Florence, the Prado in Madrid. Architecture spans two millennia, from Roman temples to Gothic cathedrals to buildings by Gaudí and Zaha Hadid. And European nightlife runs deep — [Berlin](/europe/germany/berlin)'s techno clubs, [London](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/london)'s pubs, Lisbon's fado bars, and the all-hours terrace culture of [Spain](/europe/spain) and [Italy](/europe/italy).

The Mediterranean coast and islands are reason enough to come. [Greece](/europe/greece)'s islands range from the party scene of Mykonos to the volcanic drama of Santorini to hundreds of quieter spots where you can still have a beach mostly to yourself. Croatia's Dalmatian coast combines crystal-clear water with medieval walled towns. The Amalfi Coast, the French Riviera, Sardinia, Mallorca — each has its own character. If you want Mediterranean atmosphere without Mediterranean crowds, look to [Albania](/europe/albania), [Montenegro](/europe/montenegro), or the Turkish Aegean.

Hiking in Europe ranges from gentle afternoon walks to serious mountain expeditions. The Alps offer some of the most spectacular trail networks anywhere — the Tour du Mont Blanc, Austria's Eagle Walk, and Switzerland's Haute Route are classics that take a week or more. For something different, walk a stretch of the Camino de Santiago across northern [Spain](/europe/spain), trek Scotland's West Highland Way, or explore the volcanic landscapes of [Iceland](/europe/iceland). Most European countries maintain well-marked trail systems, and you can often hike hut-to-hut without carrying a tent.

Winter sports are deeply woven into European culture. The Alps are the obvious draw, with mega-resorts in [France](/europe/france), [Switzerland](/europe/switzerland), and [Austria](/europe/austria) offering vast interconnected ski areas. But you'll find excellent and often cheaper skiing in the Dolomites in [Italy](/europe/italy), the Pyrenees between [France](/europe/france) and [Spain](/europe/spain), and the increasingly popular resorts of [Bulgaria](/europe/bulgaria) and [Romania](/europe/romania). Cross-country skiing and snowshoeing are huge in Scandinavia. Season typically runs December through March, though high-altitude glaciers extend into spring.

Food and wine alone justify a European trip. [Italy](/europe/italy) and [France](/europe/france) are the obvious culinary powerhouses, but every corner of the continent has something worth seeking out — pintxos bars in San Sebastián, smoked fish in Scandinavia, hearty stews in [Hungary](/europe/hungary), fresh seafood along the Portuguese coast. Wine regions are scattered across the south, from Bordeaux and Burgundy to Tuscany, Rioja, and the Douro Valley. Beer culture thrives in [Belgium](/europe/belgium), [Germany](/europe/germany), [Czechia](/europe/czechrepublic), and the [UK](/europe/unitedkingdom). Eat where locals eat, avoid restaurants with photos on the menu, and you'll rarely go wrong.

History is layered everywhere you look. Ancient Greek and Roman ruins anchor the Mediterranean, from the Acropolis in Athens to Pompeii near Naples. Medieval old towns survive intact across central Europe — [Prague](/europe/czechrepublic/prague), Bruges, Tallinn, Dubrovnik. The 20th century left its marks too: WWII sites from Normandy's beaches to Auschwitz, Cold War relics like the Berlin Wall remnants and the Iron Curtain Trail cycling route that follows the old divide from the Baltic to the Black Sea. Europe doesn't hide from its difficult history, and engaging with it makes for more meaningful travel.
