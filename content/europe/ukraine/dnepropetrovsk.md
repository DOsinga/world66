Dnipro (officially renamed from Dnepropetrovsk in 2016, as part of Ukraine's post-Maidan decommunisation) is Ukraine's fourth-largest city, sitting on a wide bend in the Dnieper River in the country's industrial heartland. With a population of around one million, it is the economic and administrative capital of the surrounding Dnipropetrovsk Oblast — a region defined by heavy industry, steel, and, for decades, an extraordinary secret: Dnipro was the city where the Soviet Union built its ballistic missiles.

From the 1950s until the fall of the USSR, Dnipro was a closed city. The Yuzhnoye Design Bureau here designed and produced intercontinental ballistic missiles and the rockets that launched Soviet satellites, including those that put Sputnik into orbit. Foreign nationals were barred from entering. When the city opened in 1991, this hidden industrial history became one of its most distinctive attractions — documented in detail at the aerospace museum, whose collection of actual missiles and spacecraft components gives a visceral sense of Cold War-era scale.

The city sits on high bluffs above the Dnieper, and the wide riverfront — with Monastyrsky Island in the middle of the river and a long embankment park — is the most attractive part. Soborna Square at the centre has the 18th-century Preobrazhensky Cathedral, one of the few buildings from Catherine the Great's founding of the city to survive the 20th century intact. Prospekt Karla Marksa, the main boulevard, remains the commercial axis. Dnipro has a large Jewish community and a substantial intellectual and university presence that gives the city a more cosmopolitan character than its industrial profile suggests.

Note: Given the ongoing war in Ukraine (Russia's full-scale invasion began in February 2022), travellers should consult current foreign government travel advisories before visiting any Ukrainian city.
