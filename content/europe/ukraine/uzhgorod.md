Uzhhorod (sometimes Uzhgorod, in older English) is the western-most city in Ukraine — closer to Prague, Vienna, and Budapest than it is to Kyiv — and the smallest of the country's regional capitals. It sits on the Uzh river at the foot of the Carpathians, and its whole character comes from its position on what has been one of the most contested borderlands in central Europe for the last thousand years. At various points it has been Rusyn, Hungarian (as Ungvár), Czechoslovak, Hungarian again, Soviet, and since 1991 Ukrainian. You can still walk streets signed in several alphabets and hear half a dozen languages in the cafés.

The heart of the city is the old town, which runs along both banks of the Uzh. The 9th-century **Uzhhorod Castle**, rebuilt by the Italian Drugeth counts in the 14th century, dominates a wooded hill above the river — inside the walls you'll find the Zakarpattia Museum of Local Lore and Bercsényi Palace. Next door, the **Museum of Folk Architecture and Life** is one of the most atmospheric open-air museums in eastern Europe: a hillside of wooden Carpathian churches, farmhouses, and windmills relocated from the surrounding villages, each with its own story about the Rusyn and Hungarian communities that built them. The 18th-century wooden church of St Michael, with its three onion domes rising out of shingle and carved wood, is the highlight.

The Uzh embankment is the town's promenade — a long linden-lined walkway that locals claim is the longest in Europe (it is at least in the conversation). The streets off it are full of Austro-Hungarian cafés, small bookshops, and Rusyn restaurants serving bograch (paprika soup), banosh (cornmeal porridge with bryndza cheese), and shashlik. The former synagogue, a striking Moorish-revival building from 1904, now houses the Transcarpathian Philharmonic Orchestra — a piece of the city's Jewish past, 18,000 of whom were deported from here to Auschwitz in the spring of 1944.

Since the full-scale Russian invasion of 2022, Uzhhorod has become a safer haven in a country at war: many displaced Ukrainians have moved here, and the city has taken on a quiet wartime seriousness while remaining physically undamaged. Check travel advisories before planning a visit. But if you do come, you'll find a small, handsome, Central European city that does not feel like anywhere else in Ukraine — a window into the old multi-ethnic Carpathians that elsewhere has mostly been lost.
