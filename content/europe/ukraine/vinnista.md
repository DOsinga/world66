Vinnytsia is a pleasant mid-sized city in central Ukraine, straddling the Southern Bug River. It is one of those Ukrainian cities that rarely appears on tourist itineraries but has more to offer than you would expect — a handsome riverside promenade, good food, and a couple of genuinely unusual sights.

The main attraction is the Roshen Fountain on the Southern Bug, one of the largest floating fountain complexes in Europe. On summer evenings it puts on a light-and-music show that draws crowds to the embankment. The Pirogov National Museum, a few kilometres south, preserves the estate and embalmed body of the 19th-century surgeon Nikolai Pirogov — a strange and fascinating place.

The city centre is walkable and well-kept, centred on Soborna Street with its mix of Habsburg-era and Soviet architecture. Vinnytsia also has a dark historical site: the remains of Hitler's "Werwolf" headquarters in the forest at Strizhavka, used briefly in 1942–1943 and blown up on retreat. Little remains above ground, but the concrete bunker ruins in the pines are an eerie visit.
