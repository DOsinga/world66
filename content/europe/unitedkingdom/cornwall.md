Cornwall is the south-westernmost county of England, a long peninsula jutting into the Atlantic with sea on three sides. The landscape shifts dramatically as you cross the county: the north coast faces the full force of the ocean with wide surf beaches and sheer clifftops, while the south coast is gentler — sheltered coves, wooded estuaries, and pretty fishing harbours. The Gulf Stream keeps the climate mild and gives Cornwall more than its share of sunshine for England.

The county has a distinct identity. Cornish culture and language are Celtic in origin, related more closely to Welsh and Breton than to the English spoken everywhere else, and that difference is real even today — in the place names, the food traditions, and the quiet pride locals take in not quite being England. Land's End, the most south-westerly point of mainland Britain, draws visitors for the symbolism as much as the scenery.

Highlights worth making time for include the dramatic cliffs and beach at [Bedruthan Steps](/europe/unitedkingdom/cornwall/bedruthansteps), the medieval castle ruins at [Tintagel](/europe/unitedkingdom/cornwall/tintagel) associated with the Arthurian legend, the tidal island of [St. Michael's Mount](/europe/unitedkingdom/cornwall/stmichaelsmount) near Penzance, and the surf beaches around [Newquay](/europe/unitedkingdom/cornwall/newquay). The Tate St Ives gallery brings serious modern art to the far south-west. The coastal path runs the entire length of Cornwall — over 300 miles — and walkers tackle sections of it every summer.

Don't leave without trying a Cornish cream tea: scones with clotted cream and jam. The county-wide argument over whether the cream or the jam goes on first (cream first in Cornwall, jam first in Devon) is entirely real and taken with surprising seriousness.
