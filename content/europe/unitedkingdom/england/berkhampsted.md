Berkhamsted (the older spelling "Berkhampsted" survives in historical sources but the town long ago dropped the "p") is a small commuter town in the Bulbourne valley in west Hertfordshire, half an hour up the West Coast Mainline from London Euston. It looks like a pretty Chiltern market town — a single high street of brick and flint, a Norman parish church, the canal running quietly behind it — but it has more history than the modern town quite advertises.

The most striking survival is **Berkhamsted Castle**, just behind the railway station: the grass-and-earth ruins of a great motte-and-bailey thrown up in 1066 by William the Conqueror's half-brother, where William received the formal submission of the Saxon nobility before riding south to be crowned in London. The town was briefly the de facto capital of England as a result. Later it was the childhood home of the poet **William Cowper**, the writer **Graham Greene** (whose father was headmaster of Berkhamsted School), and the painter **John Bratby**. The **Grand Union Canal** runs along the valley floor and is a fine flat walk in either direction, with good pubs every couple of locks.

Berkhamsted is a small market town in the Chiltern Hills, half an hour up the line from London Euston. Despite the commuter traffic and the smart restaurants on the High Street, it is a genuinely historic place — and the place where, in December 1066, the **Anglo-Saxon nobility surrendered the kingdom of England to William the Conqueror**. The Normans built a motte-and-bailey castle here within months of the conquest, and the surprisingly large earthworks still stand at the north end of town, with the ruined flint walls of the later stone keep wrapped in two concentric moats.

The High Street follows the route of the Roman Akeman Street and is lined with Georgian and Victorian shopfronts. Behind it run the parallel ribbons of the **Grand Union Canal** and the West Coast Main Line — the canal is one of the prettiest stretches near London, with narrowboats moored along towpath walks that reach Tring in one direction and the centre of London in the other. The novelist Graham Greene, whose father was headmaster of Berkhamsted School, grew up here and used the town's hedgerows and chalkpits as the backdrop for several novels.

Beyond town the **Ashridge Estate** — 5,000 acres of National Trust beechwoods and chalk downland — climbs the Chiltern escarpment, with bluebells in spring and free-roaming fallow deer year round. Berkhamsted works well as a half-day stop on the way to or from Tring or Aylesbury, or as a base for a weekend's walking in the Chilterns.
