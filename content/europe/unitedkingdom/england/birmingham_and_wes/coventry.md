Coventry is a city that refuses to be defined by its destruction. Heavily bombed in November 1940 — the raid was so total that "to coventrate" briefly entered the language as a verb meaning to obliterate — it was rebuilt from scratch in the 1950s and 60s. The result is a city-centre that is partly a period piece of post-war civic optimism and partly, in places, an object lesson in what went wrong with British planning. But Coventry has made something interesting of all this.

The ruined shell of the old cathedral, left standing as a war memorial, and Basil Spence's soaring new cathedral alongside it are the city's defining image — and genuinely worth the trip. The Herbert Art Gallery and Museum is one of the better regional museums in England. And the transport museums, particularly the Museum of British Road Transport, remind you that this was once a world centre of car and bicycle manufacturing.

Coventry was UK City of Culture in 2021, which brought real investment and a renewed sense of confidence. The city centre has been extensively pedestrianised, and the University of Warwick (technically in Coventry, despite the name) keeps the place young and lively. It's not pretty in the conventional sense, but it has genuine character and a lot more to offer than its reputation suggests.
