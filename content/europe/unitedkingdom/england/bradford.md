Bradford made its fortune in wool. By the mid-19th century it was the wool capital of the world, its mills churning out enough fabric to clothe an empire. The industry collapsed after World War II, but the evidence of that wealth is still visible: grand Victorian civic buildings, a world-class science and media museum, and Saltaire — a model mill village so intact it's now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The city sits on the edge of the Yorkshire Moors, the same landscape that shaped the Brontë sisters, who grew up in nearby Haworth. Bradford also has one of England's largest South Asian communities, with a Pakistani and Bangladeshi heritage that dates back to the 1950s, when workers came to keep the mills running. That heritage shows most clearly in the food: Bradford has a strong claim to having some of the best curry in England.

It's not a polished tourist destination, but that's part of the point. The National Science and Media Museum is genuinely excellent and free. The restored mill at Saltaire is a half-day in itself. And the moors are close enough to reach on foot from the suburbs.
