Stow-on-the-Wold sits at 244 metres, making it one of the highest towns in the [Cotswolds](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/bristol_plymouth/gloucestershire/cotswolds) — and notoriously one of the windiest. The old rhyme says "Stow-on-the-Wold, where the winds blow cold," which is accurate enough in winter. In summer, though, it is one of the most attractive market towns in England, built in the honey-coloured limestone typical of the region.

The town's large market square is flanked by handsome stone buildings, many now given over to antiques dealers. Stow has an unusually high concentration of antique shops and has long been a centre of the English antiques trade. The square was historically used for sheep fairs — at their height, tens of thousands of sheep changed hands here each year. Two medieval alleyways called "tures" lead into the square; these were designed to funnel the flocks through for counting.

St Edward's Church at the top of the square is worth a look inside. The north door of the church is flanked by two ancient yew trees, and the interior contains some striking memorials. In the Civil War, the church was used as a prison for hundreds of Royalist soldiers after the last pitched battle of the war was fought just outside the town in 1646.

Stow is a good base for exploring the northern Cotswolds. The villages of Bourton-on-the-Water, Burford, and Moreton-in-Marsh are all within a few miles.
