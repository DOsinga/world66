Cambridge is one of the two great English university cities, a small market town of about 145,000 people on the River Cam roughly 80 km north of London. The university was founded in 1209 by scholars fleeing a riot in Oxford, and for the eight centuries since then it has produced more Nobel laureates than almost any other institution on earth — Newton, Darwin, Rutherford, Crick and Watson, Turing, Hawking, and so on. Today Cambridge is a curious mixture of the 16th century and the 21st: medieval stone courtyards and punts drifting under willow trees, and, a mile up the road on the Madingley Road, the enormous science parks and biotech startups that have earned the region the nickname "Silicon Fen".

The city is built around the **31 colleges** of the University, each a self-contained community of students, Fellows, chapels and dining halls. A handful of the most famous — **King's**, **Trinity**, **St John's**, **Clare**, **Queens'** — sit along the river on the stretch known as **The Backs**, where the college lawns run down to the Cam through a succession of bridges. **King's College Chapel** (begun by Henry VI in 1446, completed by Henry VIII in 1515) is one of the great late-Gothic buildings in Europe, with the largest fan vault in the world and Rubens' *Adoration of the Magi* over the altar; the chapel choir's Christmas Eve service of **Nine Lessons and Carols** is broadcast live from here around the world every year. **Trinity College**, the largest, has the extraordinary **Wren Library** with its collection of Isaac Newton's and Ludwig Wittgenstein's manuscripts. **St John's** has the covered **Bridge of Sighs** across the river. Note that many colleges charge a small entrance fee in term time, and some close entirely during exam periods.

Beyond the colleges, the **Fitzwilliam Museum** is a surprisingly rich collection of Egyptian antiquities, Italian and Dutch masters, and French impressionism, all free and rarely crowded. The **Mathematical Bridge** at Queens' College is a famous wooden structure often incorrectly attributed to Newton. And the defining Cambridge experience — indispensable in summer, questionable in February — is **punting** on the Cam, either along the college Backs or upstream to the village of **Grantchester**, where you can moor up, have tea at the Orchard, and pretend for an afternoon that nothing has really changed since Rupert Brooke wrote about the place in 1912.
