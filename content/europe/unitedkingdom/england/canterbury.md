Canterbury is one of England's most historically significant cities, and it's been drawing pilgrims and visitors since Thomas Becket was murdered in its cathedral in 1170. The cathedral itself — a UNESCO World Heritage Site alongside the ruins of St Augustine's Abbey and St Martin's Church — is extraordinary: a long Gothic nave, medieval stained glass, and the shrine that made Canterbury the destination of Chaucer's pilgrims. The city built itself around that pilgrimage traffic for centuries, and tourism is still its dominant business.

Within the medieval walls, the old town is compact and walkable, with Roman-era remains visible at the Westgate Towers and the Canterbury Roman Museum. The Beaney House of Art and Knowledge is a good free museum covering the city's layered history. The streets around the cathedral get very crowded in summer — arrive early, or visit in spring or autumn when the city breathes easier. The city is an hour by train from London, which makes it an easy day trip.
