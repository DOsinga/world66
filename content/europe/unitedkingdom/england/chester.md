Chester is the most complete walled city in Britain, and the walls themselves — two miles of Roman and medieval stonework — are the best way to begin any visit. The circuit takes about an hour to walk and gives you a view over the rooftops of the city and across the River Dee to the Welsh hills on clear days.

The city's other defining feature is The Rows, a two-tier system of galleried shops running along the main medieval streets. The half-timbered galleries date in their current form to the 13th and 14th centuries and have no parallel anywhere else in England. The ground-level shops are modern; the elevated walkway above creates a distinctive covered arcade. Chester's black-and-white timber-framed architecture is striking throughout the centre — much of it is Victorian rebuilding in a medieval style, but it is convincingly done.

The Roman legacy is tangible. Chester was the legionary fortress of Deva Victrix, garrisoning up to 10,000 soldiers at its peak. The Roman amphitheatre, partially excavated on the south side of the city, is the largest discovered in Britain. The Grosvenor Museum covers the Roman and medieval history well, and further Roman remains turn up throughout the centre. The cathedral, a medieval foundation converted from a Benedictine monastery, is worth an hour for its intricately carved choir stalls.

Chester makes a practical base for exploring North Wales, the Peak District, and the Wirral. The city is popular and can be crowded in summer — midweek visits are more comfortable.
