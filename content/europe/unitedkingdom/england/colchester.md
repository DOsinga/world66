Colchester has a stronger claim to ancient fame than almost anywhere in Britain: it was the first Roman capital of the province — Camulodunum — and its streets still follow the Roman grid. The town walls, built around 65 AD and largely intact, are among the finest surviving Roman fortifications in the country. Boudica's revolt in 60 AD burned the city to the ground, and the ash layer archaeologists found beneath the modern town is some of the most vivid physical evidence of that rebellion anywhere.

The old town sits inside those walls, with Colchester Castle at its heart — a Norman keep built directly on the base of the Roman Temple of Claudius, making it the largest Norman keep in Europe by footprint. The Castle Museum inside is excellent, one of the best Roman collections in Britain. Beyond the castle, the Dutch Quarter preserves a cluster of timber-framed houses built by Flemish weavers who settled here in the sixteenth century, and the Balkerne Gate at the western end of the high street is the largest surviving Roman gateway in Britain.

Colchester is an easy day trip from London (under an hour from Liverpool Street) but rewards a night or two — the surrounding countryside is unexpectedly pretty, and the town itself has enough history to fill a weekend.
