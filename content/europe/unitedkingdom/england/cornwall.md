Cornwall is the westernmost part of England, a peninsula of dramatic coastline, ancient stone walls and granite farmhouses set against a canvas of gorse and green countryside dropping sharply down rugged cliffs to the sea. The coast is the main attraction — wild and varied, from the surfing beaches of the north coast to the sheltered coves and subtropical gardens of the south.

Derelict mine engine houses dot the landscape from Land's End to Gunnislake, witnesses to a time when Cornwall dominated the tin and copper markets of the world. That mining heritage is now a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The coast road from the resort of St Ives to Land's End is particularly scenic.

Cornwall has its own distinct identity — the Cornish language, revived from near-extinction, appears on signs and place names, and the pasty is a point of fierce local pride. The Eden Project, built in a former clay pit near St Austell, is one of England's most visited attractions.
