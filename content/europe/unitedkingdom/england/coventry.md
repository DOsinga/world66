Coventry is a city that earned its place in history the hard way. On the night of 14 November 1940, the Luftwaffe flattened the medieval centre in one of the most devastating raids of the Blitz, killing nearly 600 people and gutting the ancient cathedral. What the Germans left standing, postwar planners sometimes finished off — Coventry became a byword for brutal urban renewal. But the city has also produced something more interesting than any of its critics expected: a genuinely moving meditation on destruction and reconciliation, centred on the two cathedrals that now stand side by side, one ruined and one rebuilt.

It helps that Coventry was always a city with something to prove. In the Middle Ages it was one of England's most prosperous cities, home to guilds, cloth merchants, and a priory that rivalled the great monasteries. The transport museum tells the story of what came after: the bicycles, motorcycles, and cars that made Coventry the Detroit of Britain for most of the twentieth century. The world's two fastest cars — Thrust 2 and ThrustSSC, which broke the sound barrier in 1997 — both live here, which gives some sense of what Coventry thought of itself.

The centre is compact and easy to walk. The regeneration that has been coming in waves since the 1990s has, in places, actually worked: FarGo Village on Far Gosford Street is a genuine creative quarter, with independent shops, street food, and a brewery operating out of converted railway arches. The Canal Basin to the north has been slowly coming to life. Coventry was the UK City of Culture in 2021, and while that brought some overblown rhetoric, it also left behind real improvements. This is not a place to spend a week, but an overnight stop or a day trip from Birmingham — only 20 minutes by train — is well worth it.
