The Cambridge Music Festival lasts three weeks. It is held in November while the universities are in term to allow as many people to participate as possible. The next one is 11th to 29th November 2000.

  The overall aim of the festival is to involve as many different groups of Cambridge people in music making and listening; the result is a festival which is both community based and has a programme of the highest artistic aspirations. Within the theme of each festival music is so chosen as to be attractive and relevant to all those who might participate. Concerts are given by all the major Cambridge groups, city and university based. The main bulk of the programme, however, is professional, with musicians chosen to complement the local contributions around the agreed theme. In 1997 musicians appearing in the festival included Radu Lupu, the City of Birmingham Symphony Orchestra, the Lindsay Quartet, Sarah Walker, King's and St. John's College Choirs, the University's Chamber Orchestra, a specially formed University Festival Chorus, as well as young quartets and musicians playing in local hospitals, student concerts in local village venues, school children performing in the shopping centre, and college groups giving late night chamber recitals.

  Previous festivals have had a theme, for example Mozart (1991), Elgar (1994), and Schubert & Vienna (1997). Three important anniversaries will provide the core of the Festival's musical programme for 2000: the 250th anniversary of the death of J S Bach will be marked with concerts in King's College Chapel, Great St. Mary's and other city churches, as well as smaller events in college chapels; the 25th anniversary of the death of Shostakovich occasions a series of his string quartets performed in a startling array of unusual venues; the 100th anniversary of Elgar's Gerontius forms the foundation for a celebration of some of the most remarkable and popular musical monuments of the 20th century, ranging from Britten's War Requiem through Gershwin's Rhapsody in Blue to John Tavener's The Last Sleep of the Virgin. To salute Cambridge's technological future in the new Millennium, concerts will open to public view some of the amazing new architecture constructed for industry and the university.
