Gloucestershire sits in the west of England, straddling the River Severn, and contains some of the most recognisably English scenery in the country. The Cotswold hills in the east draw tourists in vast numbers — often too many — but get a few miles off the main roads and you find honey-stone villages where the main sound is birdsong.

The county splits into three distinct zones. The Severn Vale runs through the middle, home to [Gloucester](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/gloucestershire/gloucester) (the county town, with its Norman cathedral and revived Victorian docks), [Cheltenham](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/gloucestershire/cheltenham) (a confident Regency spa town with a good food and arts scene), and [Tewkesbury](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/gloucestershire/tewkesbury) (a small medieval market town at the confluence of the Severn and Avon). To the east, the [Cotswolds](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/gloucestershire/cotswolds) roll away in an endless procession of limestone walls and market towns. To the west lies the Forest of Dean, an ancient royal hunting forest still dense with oak and fern, and the Wye Valley, shared with Wales — one of England's most underrated landscapes.

If you have a car and a few days, base yourself in Cheltenham and work outward. The Cotswolds are best explored on foot, village to village, rather than by driving between car parks. The Forest of Dean rewards anyone willing to leave the main road.
