Beverley is one of the finest small towns in the north of England — compact, handsome, and easy to underestimate. It served for centuries as the county town of the East Riding of Yorkshire, and the medieval bones of that importance are still very much on show.

The town is anchored by two remarkable churches. Beverley Minster is the headline act: a Gothic masterpiece that rivals many English cathedrals in scale and ambition, with exceptional stone carving and a set of misericords that are worth the trip alone. St Mary's Church, a short walk north, punches well above its weight too, with exuberant carvings and a painted nave ceiling that stops most visitors in their tracks.

Beyond the churches, Beverley has a Saturday market that has been running since the twelfth century, a racecourse on the edge of town, and Saturday Market — one of the most attractive squares in the north, framed by Georgian buildings and anchored by the market cross. The North Bar, a fifteenth-century brick gate, is the only surviving medieval town gate in the county and marks the entrance to the old town from the minster side.

It is not a place that demands days, but a half-day or full day here rewards anyone who takes it.
