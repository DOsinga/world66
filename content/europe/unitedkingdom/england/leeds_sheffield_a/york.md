York is one of England's most visited historic cities, and for good reason. Its Roman walls, Viking past, and medieval streetscape are all visible to the naked eye. The city is compact enough to explore on foot in a day, though it rewards a longer stay.

The York Minster dominates the skyline and the imagination. It is the largest Gothic cathedral in Northern Europe, and its medieval stained glass — particularly the Great East Window — is among the finest in the world. The Shambles, a few minutes' walk away, is one of the best-preserved medieval streets in Europe: timber-framed buildings lean over the cobbles, so close together that neighbours could pass things between upper-story windows. It was the city's butchers' quarter; the iron hooks for hanging meat are still visible.

The city walls date from Roman foundations and are the most complete in England. The full circuit takes about two hours. The Castle Museum, built into the old prison, is excellent — its Victorian street reconstruction alone is worth the entrance fee. The JORVIK Viking Centre, built over the site of an extraordinary 10th-century excavation, does a convincing job of bringing the Viking city back to life.

York has good connections by train: London King's Cross in two hours, Edinburgh in two and a half. Castle Howard (the inspiration for Brideshead Revisited) is a 40-minute drive and worth a full day.
