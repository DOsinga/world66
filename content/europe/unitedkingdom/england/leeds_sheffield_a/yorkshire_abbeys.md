title: Yorkshire's Ruined Abbeys

The dissolution of the monasteries under Henry VIII left Yorkshire with one of the finest concentrations of medieval abbey ruins in Europe. A week of touring them — driving between, walking around, lunching at the village pubs nearby — is one of the great Yorkshire holidays.

**Fountains Abbey near Ripon** is the standout — a Cistercian foundation set in the elegantly landscaped grounds of Studley Royal, jointly a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Owned by the National Trust, with a substantial visitor centre. Allow at least half a day.

**Rievaulx Abbey** in the North York Moors is another striking ruin set deep in a wooded valley; the surrounding countryside is full of walks. **Byland Abbey**, west of Rievaulx, is well worth combining; the inn opposite is a good lunch stop.

**Bolton Abbey** sits on the Duke of Devonshire's estate by the River Wharfe near Skipton — the nave survived the Dissolution and remains in use as a parish church. Footpaths follow the river (mind the fast-flowing Strid with children) and climb into the hills near Simon's Seat.

**Jervaulx Abbey** in lower Wensleydale is more atmospheric than imposing; **Easby Abbey** on the Swale has a beautiful short walkable approach. **Kirkstall Priory** sits within Leeds itself and is often passed unnoticed by commuters; it has extensive remains and a museum. **Whitby Abbey**, on a clifftop above the seaside town, is photogenic rather than substantial — but inseparable from the place.
