The Yorkshire Dales are a region of steep-sided river valleys — the dales of the name — cut through the limestone uplands of northern England, between the Lake District and the North York Moors. The whole area was designated a national park in 1954, and for anyone who has ever spent time there it remains one of the most beloved landscapes in Britain: dry-stone walls dividing impossibly green pasture, flocks of hardy sheep, grey limestone villages with names like Buttertubs and Muker, and long open walks up onto the peat moors and the tops of the fells. It is gentler and more pastoral than the Lake District to the west, and more populated, and the people are reliably warm in that particular Yorkshire way.

Each dale has its own character. **Wharfedale**, in the south-east, is the most visited — and probably the most beautiful of the broader dales — with the 12th-century ruins of **Bolton Abbey** set in its bend of the River Wharfe, the river squeezing through the narrow rock chasm of the **Strid**, and the handsome grey-stone village of **Grassington**. **Malhamdale**, just west, has some of the most extraordinary limestone scenery in England: **Malham Cove** is a vast natural amphitheatre of pale limestone with a bare rock pavement on top, and **Gordale Scar** is a deep gorge where two waterfalls drop through ferns and overhanging cliffs. **Wensleydale**, in the centre, is slower and more agricultural, with the iconic cheese of the same name, the waterfalls at Aysgarth, and the famous village of Hawes at its head. **Swaledale**, the northernmost, is the quietest and the most perfectly unchanged, with a string of stone villages (Muker, Keld, Gunnerside) and the old lead-mining scars still visible on the hillsides.

The Dales are walking country. The **Dales Way**, an 80-mile long-distance trail, runs from Ilkley to Bowness-on-Windermere through the heart of the region. The **Yorkshire Three Peaks** challenge — climbing Pen-y-ghent, Whernside, and Ingleborough in a single day — is a classic long walk, roughly 24 miles with 5,200 feet of ascent. And the short walk up to the top of Malham Cove, where the River Aire mysteriously emerges from underground at the base of the cliff, is as good a half-day walk as you can take in the country.

Beyond walking, the Dales have an extensive network of underground caves — **White Scar Cave** and **Ingleborough Cave** are both show caves open to visitors, and experienced cavers come from across Britain for the wild systems of the Three Peaks area. The Settle–Carlisle Railway, one of the great scenic rail journeys in England, crosses the heart of the park via the 24-arch Ribblehead Viaduct. And the Yorkshire Dales cheese, the James Herriot museum in Thirsk (the vet whose books are set here), and the hundreds of stone-flagged country pubs with fires and real ale are the cherries on top. Come in May or June before the school holidays, or in October for the autumn colour on the slopes.
