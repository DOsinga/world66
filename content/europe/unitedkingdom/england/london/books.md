---
title: "Books about London"
type: section
---

London has inspired more writing than almost any other city. A few books that add something to a visit:

**Fiction**

*Oliver Twist* by Charles Dickens — the London of slums, workhouses, and Newgate Prison; much of the geography is specific enough to follow on foot. *The Picture of Dorian Gray* by Oscar Wilde covers a different London — the decadent West End of the 1890s. *Mrs Dalloway* by Virginia Woolf takes place over a single day in 1923, moving through Westminster and the parks; reread it after a walk through those same streets. *White Teeth* by Zadie Smith is the best modern novel about London's multicultural north, set in Willesden and Cricklewood.

**Non-fiction**

*London: The Biography* by Peter Ackroyd is a 700-page portrait of the city across its entire history, thematic rather than chronological — overwhelming but endlessly interesting. *Londoners* by Craig Taylor is built from interviews with a cross-section of the people who live and work in the city, from tube drivers to sex workers to City traders; it gives a better sense of what the place actually feels like than most guide books. *The East End* by Alan Palmer covers the history of the area east of the City from Roman times to the 20th century.

**Walking guides**

*Slow London* by Andrew White and *London Urban Walks* by Viv Nield both offer walks that get off the obvious tourist routes. The London Loop and the Capital Ring are long-distance walking routes that circle the city — the route guides double as introductions to areas most visitors never see.
