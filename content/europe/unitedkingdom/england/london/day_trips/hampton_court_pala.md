A great day trip out of London for history buffs is Hampton Court Palace where the 16 th century is alive again. A 30 minute train ride from London 's Waterloo Station brings you to this palace of Henry VIII 's, originally donated to him by Cardinal Wolsey. Wolsey had renovated the Palace to reflect his role as Lord Chancellor of England, and included rooms for Henry, Catherine of Aragon and their daughter Mary – all these rooms you can see today.

My 13 year old daughter and I had this on our must-see list since we started planning our trip to London , and were not disappointed. Passing through the iron gates from the street, the long, wide road leading straight to the Palace gateway gives you a taste of the grandeur to come. But first, you make a side detour to get your ticket, or in our case, to get our London Pass validated. Buy a guide book here as well, if you want more detail about the Palace and its history. There is a long queue, but it moves quickly, and you are soon at the entrance. A security check reminds you that you really do live in the 21 st century, but then you are free to immerse yourself in the 16 th century for the day.

Entering the Base Court , you get a sense of the scale of life when this Palace was used by the royal family of the day. Further on is the Clock Court which is dominated by its very complex astronomical clock that shows the hour, month, day, number of days since the beginning of the year and the phases of the moon – and which has the sun revolving around the earth, a reminder that we were indeed in the midst of the past. Yet further on, the Fountain Court is more cloistered and provides the view from the state apartments. We managed to get lost even with the map, and never quite made it to all the gardens, but we wandered rooms and apartments and supposedly haunted corridors at leisure.

Actors playing out life at the palace were an added treat, although we did jump at one stage when an imperious voice told us to ‘make way for the Queen’! Turning around, Catherine of Aragon and her entourage swept by us, her conversation with her ladies in waiting giving us a hint of the drama surrounding her life then. We watched some more as Henry VIII notice Anne Boleyn during a dance in the impressive Great Hall, which we 21 st century interlopers could join in if chosen by members of the court. We listened to the political intrigue of the time, and saw Cardinal Wolsey’s gift of Hampton Court to Henry backfire when he was given the burned out Richmond Palace in return. Even better was when the actor playing the Cardinal appeared beside us in the gift shop, buying some Palace souvenirs himself!

The main café at the Palace was very busy when we headed there for lunch, so wandered the gardens instead, checking out the swans and daffodils, and marveling at the impressive East Face of the Palace and the engineering of the canal or Long Water, built after Henry’s time by Charles II. We made our way to the second, smaller café and managed to get what we call in Australia a sausage in bread for a more or less reasonable price. A visit to the Tudor kitchens, watching meals being prepared and listening to the sounds of the kitchens in operation, followed by the Chapel Royal, which has been in use for over 450 years, and finally exploring the small but comprehensive Palace Museum which tells the history of Palace, rounded out the day.

Hampton Court Palace is a long but fascinating day trip, and provides a clear insight into the grandeur of life at the Palace and the life at court. There were plenty of people around when we visited in early March, but somehow the size of the Palace and its gardens meant that our visit was busy, but somehow equally peaceful. Being able to wander the Palace at your own pace means you can design your visit the way you want, and spend time doing what interests you.
