Loughborough is a market town in north Leicestershire, midway between Leicester and Nottingham, dominated by the long red-brick presence of **Loughborough University**. The university — particularly its sport science department — is the reason most outsiders have heard of the place: it has trained more Olympic medallists than any other in Britain, and the campus on the southern edge of town is more like a small American college town than an English provincial university.

Beyond the university, the town's main exports historically were lace and bells. **John Taylor & Co.**, founded in 1784, is the largest bell foundry in the world, and cast Great Paul (London's largest bell) and the carillon at Loughborough's own war memorial in Queen's Park. The fully-functioning **Great Central Railway**, the only mainline-gauge heritage steam railway in the UK, runs vintage trains from Loughborough Central Station down to Leicester North on summer weekends — well worth a couple of hours if steam railways are your thing.

Loughborough is a market town in Leicestershire, sitting on the Soar Valley between Leicester and Nottingham. Two things define it. The first is **Loughborough University**, one of the leading sports universities in the world — the British Olympic team has trained here for decades, and on any afternoon the campus tracks, pools and pitches are full of athletes in national kit. The second is bells: **John Taylor & Co.** has been casting church bells here since 1839 and is the only major bell foundry left in Britain. They cast Great Paul, the largest bell in the country, in 1881, and you can still tour the foundry on weekday afternoons.

The town centre is compact, with a busy market three days a week (the medieval charter goes back to 1221), the parish church of All Saints with its famous **carillon** in a war memorial tower in Queen's Park, and the **Great Central Railway** — the only mainline heritage steam railway in Britain, running restored locomotives down to Leicester North. For travellers Loughborough is mostly a pleasant Midlands stop: a half-day for the railway and the carillon, with Charnwood Forest and the rocky outcrops of Beacon Hill on the doorstep for walks.
