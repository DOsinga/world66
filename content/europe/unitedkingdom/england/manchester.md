Manchester is the great industrial city of northern England — the place where the modern world, for better and worse, was largely invented. The first passenger railway ran from here to Liverpool in 1830. The first programmable computer was built here in 1948. The cotton mills that powered the Industrial Revolution lined every river and canal in the city, and the wealth they generated built the grand Victorian warehouses, exchanges, and civic buildings that still define the centre. The mills are gone, but Manchester has reinvented itself as successfully as any post-industrial city in Europe: the music scene (Joy Division, The Smiths, Oasis, The Stone Roses), the football (two of the biggest clubs in the world, United and City), and a nightlife that regularly outpaces London's have made it the de facto second city of England.

The city centre is compact and walkable. **Deansgate** and the area around **St Ann's Square** are the commercial heart, with the handsome Victorian **John Rylands Library** — a neo-Gothic cathedral of books, free to enter, with medieval manuscripts and early printed Bibles — hidden just off the main shopping streets. The **Northern Quarter**, north of Piccadilly Gardens, is the independent, slightly scruffy creative district: vintage shops, record stores, street art, and the best café culture in the city. **Castlefield**, to the south-west, is where the canals, railway viaducts, and Roman ruins converge in a surprisingly atmospheric urban landscape.

Manchester's museums are excellent and mostly free. The **Museum of Science and Industry** (MOSI), in the world's oldest surviving passenger railway station, tells the story of industrialisation with working steam engines and a hands-on textile gallery. The **Imperial War Museum North**, Daniel Libeskind's fractured-aluminium building at Salford Quays, is one of the most powerful war museums anywhere. Nearby, **The Lowry** holds the largest public collection of L.S. Lowry's matchstick-men paintings of industrial Lancashire. And the **Manchester Art Gallery**, reopened after renovation, has an unexpectedly strong collection of Pre-Raphaelites alongside contemporary work.

The **Curry Mile** on Wilmslow Road in Rusholme — a long strip of South Asian restaurants, sweetshops, and shisha cafés — is one of the most famous food streets in Britain. Chinatown, just off Piccadilly, is the second-largest in the UK. And the craft-beer and cocktail-bar scene in the Northern Quarter and Ancoats is among the best outside London.
