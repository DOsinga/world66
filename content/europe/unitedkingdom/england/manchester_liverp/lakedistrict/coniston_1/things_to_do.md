Coniston rewards walkers and anyone with an eye for Victorian cultural history. The fells rise directly behind the village, the lake stretches south for five miles, and Ruskin's legacy is everywhere.

### Tarn Hows

It seems odd that in a district with so many natural lakes people should flock to see an artificial tarn, but **Tarn Hows**, owned by the National Trust, is one of Lakeland's most popular views. The roads are so narrow that they have become one-way, and there is an easy circular walk around the tarn, surrounded by peaks.

### Brantwood

John Ruskin lived the last three decades of his life at Brantwood, which he reckoned to be the best-situated house in the whole country for its views across Coniston Water. His tastes in refurbishing and furnishing the house reflected his closeness to the Arts and Crafts Movement, and the rooms are still full of his original furniture, feeling much as though it were still his home.

### John Ruskin Museum

The village museum holds works of art by Ruskin alongside ancient remains from the area and specimens of the local cottage industry in lace and linen. Described by Tolstoy as great by the standard of all nations at all times, Ruskin was a professor of fine art at Oxford before settling across the lake at Brantwood.

### Coniston Old Man

The most popular fell walk from the village, but do not just go straight up and back. The circuit via Goat's Water and Dow Crag, over the summit, and along the ridge to Wetherlam is one of the finest rounds in the southern Lakes.
