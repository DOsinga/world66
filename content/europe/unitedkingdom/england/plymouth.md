Plymouth is a naval city with a long memory. Its dockyard made it a prime bombing target in the Second World War, and the hurried post-war rebuilding left a centre that feels like a museum of late-1940s town planning. But look past the concrete and there is a city with real character, shaped by centuries of maritime history and a dramatic waterfront setting.

The Mayflower Steps on the Barbican mark where the Pilgrim Fathers departed for America in 1620, drawing a steady stream of American visitors. The Barbican itself is Plymouth's oldest quarter — cobbled streets, Elizabethan houses, and a working fishing harbour. Nearby, the Royal Citadel, built under Charles II, sits above the waterfront with more guns famously pointed at the city than out to sea — a reminder that Plymouth sided with Parliament during the Civil War.

Plymouth Hoe is the great public promenade, with sweeping views across the Sound to Drake's Island and the Cornish coast. Below it, the Tinside Lido — an art deco seawater pool — is one of the finest outdoor swimming spots in England. The city is also the gateway to south [Devon](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/devon), the southern reaches of Dartmoor, and southeast Cornwall via the road bridge to Saltash, which runs alongside Brunel's celebrated railway bridge.
