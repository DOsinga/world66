type: section
---

While visiting **England, practical information** about traveling includes money, visas, communications, accommodations and staying safe while you're there.

**
Money and Costs**

The currency of England is the pound sterling. Paper notes, or bills, come in denominations of &pound;5, &pound;10, &pound;20 and &pound;50. There are one and two pound coins, and these are quite handy to have when boarding buses and the underground subway. Major credit cards are accepted in all but the tiniest, most remote shops and restaurants. England is an expensive country; however, staying in hostels and self-catering can bring costs down to a reasonable level. Budget travelers can get by on as little as &pound;30—&pound;40 a day.

**
Visas and Entry Requirements**

Visa requirements for the UK change on a regular basis. It's wise to check with your embassy to find up to date information on what you need to go to England. As long as they are staying as tourists under a period of three months, and in some cases six months, Canadian, U.S., Australian and New Zealand nationals do not need visas but are not allowed to work. U.K. immigration officials might ask to see a return ticket, so it would be a good idea to have one just in case. Citizens of Asian countries such as China, India, Indonesia and Thailand do need a visa. Nationals of many Middle Eastern, African and South American countries would also have to present a visa on arrival.

**
Communications**

The international calling code for England is 44. There are pay phones all over the country that allow you to make international telephone calls to anywhere in the world. The phones accept pre-paid phone cards that can be bought in any newsagent (convenience) shop and Internet cafe. Just look for the red phone booths.

**
Accommodations**

Accommodations in England range from &pound;20 dorm beds to hotel suites that cost more than &pound;2,000 per night. Where you stay will depend on how deep your pockets are. Of course, in London you can expect to fork over tons of cash for hotels near the center of town. Fortunately, there are plenty of options for backpackers. If you arrive without a reservation, a good place to book a room would be at the hotel reservation kiosk in Victoria Station.

**
Staying Safe**

England is a remarkably safe country, but take the same precautions as you would anywhere else. There are pockets of crime all over London and in the smaller cities. Unlicensed minicabs are best avoided, because they are known for driving jet-lagged tourists around in circles and then demanding a huge payment. Medical treatment is available to everyone in the state-run NHS hospitals, but take travel insurance just to be safe.
