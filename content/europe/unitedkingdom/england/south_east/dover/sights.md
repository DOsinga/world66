title: 'Sights'
type: section
---

**Dover Castle** towers above the town and is one of the most impressive fortresses in England. The medieval keep was built by Henry II in the 1180s, but the site has been fortified since the Iron Age. During the Second World War, the tunnels beneath the castle served as the headquarters for the Dunkirk evacuation. The tunnels are open to visitors and bring that history vividly to life. Within the castle grounds, you will also find a Roman lighthouse (pharos) and the Saxon church of St Mary in Castro.

**The White Cliffs** stretch along the coast on both sides of Dover. The National Trust manages a clifftop walk east of the port, starting from the visitor centre and running along the cliff edge with views across the Channel. On a clear day you can see France. Shakespeare referenced these cliffs in King Lear, and they have been a symbol of England ever since.

**Dover Museum** in the town centre houses the Dover Bronze Age Boat, one of the oldest known seagoing boats in the world, dating to around 1550 BC. The museum also covers the town's long history as a port and garrison town.
