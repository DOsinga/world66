Eastbourne sits on the Sussex coast where the South Downs meet the English Channel, a Victorian seaside resort that still feels like one — in the best possible way. The seafront is grand: a long promenade, carpet gardens, a bandstand, and a pier that has survived fires and storms since 1870.

The real draw is the landscape. Beachy Head, the highest chalk sea cliff in Britain, rises 162 metres just west of town. The view from the top is genuinely dramatic — white chalk dropping vertically to the sea, with the red-and-white lighthouse far below. The Seven Sisters chalk cliffs continue westward toward [Seaford](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/south_east/seaford), and the walk along them is one of the finest coastal paths in England.

The town itself is pleasant without being exciting. The Towner Art Gallery, a striking modernist building near the station, has a strong collection of British art and regularly hosts good exhibitions. The old town, slightly inland, has more character than the seafront — independent shops, a few decent pubs, and the medieval church of St Mary. Eastbourne has a reputation as a retirement town, which keeps things quiet, but the students from the University of Brighton campus add some life.
