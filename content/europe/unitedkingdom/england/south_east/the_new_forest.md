The New Forest is one of England's newest national parks, designated in 2005, but the forest itself is anything but new. William the Conqueror established it as a royal hunting ground in 1079, and his son William Rufus was killed here in a hunting accident (or perhaps not an accident) in 1100. The Rufus Stone marks the spot where he is said to have fallen.

Today the forest covers about 220 square miles of heathland, ancient woodland and bog in south-west Hampshire. Its most famous residents are the New Forest ponies, which roam freely across roads and through villages. Donkeys, cattle and pigs also wander at will under ancient commoners' rights. You will need to drive carefully, especially at night.

The forest is wonderful for walking and cycling, with hundreds of miles of tracks and paths. The villages of Lyndhurst (the unofficial capital), Brockenhurst and Burley make good bases. Burley is known for its cider, its witchcraft connections and its tea rooms.

The coast runs along the southern edge, with the Beaulieu River and its estuary, the ruins of Beaulieu Abbey and the National Motor Museum at Beaulieu. The Solent shoreline offers sailing, birdwatching and mudflat walks.

The New Forest is easily reached from [Southampton](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/south_east/southampton) or [Portsmouth](/europe/unitedkingdom/england/south_east/portsmouth), and trains from London Waterloo stop at Brockenhurst.
