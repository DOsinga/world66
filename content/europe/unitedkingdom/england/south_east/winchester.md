Winchester was the capital of England before London claimed the title, and the city carries that ancient dignity with quiet confidence. Alfred the Great ruled his kingdom of Wessex from here, and the city remained a seat of royal power through the Norman period and beyond. Today it is a prosperous cathedral city in Hampshire, compact enough to explore on foot and rich enough in history to hold your attention for days.

Winchester Cathedral dominates the city center as it has for nearly a thousand years. The longest medieval cathedral in Europe, it holds the bones of Saxon kings, the grave of Jane Austen, and an extraordinary collection of medieval floor tiles. The cathedral's setting, surrounded by lawns and enclosed by the old city walls, is unusually peaceful for a building of this importance. The Winchester Bible, a 12th-century illuminated manuscript, is displayed in the cathedral library and is one of the finest surviving examples of Romanesque book art.

The Great Hall is the only surviving part of Winchester Castle, and its main attraction is the Round Table that hangs on the western wall. It is not actually King Arthur's, of course -- dendrochronology dates it to the 13th century -- but the painted table, with its Tudor rose and portrait of Arthur, has been a tourist attraction for over 500 years. Henry VIII brought Emperor Charles V here specifically to see it, and it remains oddly compelling despite its known provenance.

The city beyond the major monuments rewards wandering. The water meadows along the River Itchen, accessible from the city center, provide walks that inspired Keats to write "To Autumn." The High Street climbs from the medieval Westgate to the Butter Cross, passing independent shops and the excellent city museum. Winchester's food and pub scene benefits from its affluent commuter population, and the market days on the cathedral green are some of the best in southern England. If you are visiting southern England and can only add one cathedral city to your itinerary, Winchester makes a strong case for itself.
