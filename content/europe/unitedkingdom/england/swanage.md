Swanage is a small seaside town on the Isle of Purbeck in Dorset, tucked into a sheltered bay at the eastern end of the Jurassic Coast. The sandy beach is one of the best on the south coast — clean, gently sloping, and backed by Victorian seafront buildings rather than amusement arcades.

The town has a quirky character. Much of Victorian Swanage was built using architectural salvage brought back as ballast in the stone-carrying ships that exported Purbeck marble. The town hall facade came from the Mercers' Hall in London; a Wellington Clock Tower arrived from London Bridge. George Burt, the local stone merchant responsible for most of this, essentially decorated his hometown with London's cast-offs.

The real draw is the surrounding landscape. The South West Coast Path passes through town, and the walk to Old Harry Rocks — chalk sea stacks at the tip of the Purbeck ridge — is one of the finest short coastal walks in England. The Swanage Railway, a preserved steam line, runs through the Purbeck countryside to Corfe Castle, whose dramatic ruins sit on a hill above the village.
