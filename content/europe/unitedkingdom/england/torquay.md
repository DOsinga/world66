Torquay is the largest of the three towns that make up the English Riviera, a sweep of coast in south Devon that earned its name from the palm trees and mild winters. The comparison to the Mediterranean is optimistic, but on a good day — blue water in the harbour, pastel-coloured villas climbing the hillside, yachts bobbing at anchor — you can see where they got the idea.

The town developed as a fashionable resort in the Victorian era, and the grand hotels and stuccoed terraces of that period still define its character. Agatha Christie was born here in 1890, and the town makes the most of the connection with a trail linking sites from her life. The museum in Torre Abbey has a good collection, and the harbour area is pleasant for wandering.

The beaches are Torquay's main attraction. Meadfoot Beach is a sheltered cove below the cliffs, Oddicombe Beach is reached by a cliff railway, and Babbacombe offers red-sand charm. The coast path in both directions is excellent walking — south towards Brixham and its working fishing harbour, or north towards Teignmouth along red sandstone cliffs.
