London is one of the world's great cities — not just for its history, but for the sheer density of things going on at any given moment. Museums that would be highlights in any other city are free and quietly astonishing here. The food scene has shaken off its old reputation entirely. And the parks — vast, green, genuinely central — make the city feel less claustrophobic than its size suggests.

It's also a city of neighbourhoods, each with a distinct character. The City of London is the old square mile of finance, ancient in its street plan and oddly deserted on weekends. Westminster is all pomp and government. To the east, Shoreditch and Hackney have become the centres of creative London, full of galleries, street art, and coffee shops that take themselves very seriously. South of the river, Brixton pulses with music and food from the Caribbean diaspora; Borough Market draws food obsessives from across the country.

The sheer scale can be daunting. London is enormous — bigger than any European city except Moscow — and navigating it takes planning. The Tube is fast and reliable, but you'll also spend a surprising amount of time walking. That's not a bad thing: some of the best discoveries happen when you stray from the tourist routes into the quieter streets of Islington, Bermondsey, or Notting Hill.

London rewards the traveller who doesn't try to see everything. Pick a few neighbourhoods, walk them slowly, eat well, and end up in a pub that's been serving beer in the same room since before the United States existed.
