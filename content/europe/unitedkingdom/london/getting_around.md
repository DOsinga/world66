---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

London's public transport network is extensive and largely reliable, though expensive by European standards. The Oyster card (or a contactless payment card) gives you access to the whole network — Tube, bus, Overground, Elizabeth line, DLR, and most National Rail services within London zones — at significantly lower fares than buying paper tickets.

**The Underground (Tube).** Fast and frequent on most lines, but crowded during rush hour (8–9:30am and 5:30–7pm), and swelteringly hot on some older lines in summer. The map is a design classic but distorts geography significantly — some stations shown far apart are walking distance. Don't automatically take the Tube for short hops; often walking or taking the bus is faster.

**Buses.** Slower than the Tube but far more scenic, and you get to see the city. The night bus network is good and the only option when the Tube shuts (around midnight, though the Night Tube runs all night on several lines on Fridays and Saturdays).

**The Elizabeth line.** Opened in 2022, this east-west line is a revelation — air-conditioned, spacious, fast. It links Heathrow and Reading to the west with Shenfield and Abbey Wood to the east, stopping at Paddington, Bond Street, Tottenham Court Road, Farringdon, City, and Canary Wharf.

**Cycling.** The Santander Cycles hire bikes (colloquially "Boris Bikes") are available at docking stations across most of central London. Good for daytime cycling in quiet conditions; cycling in heavy traffic takes confidence.

**Taxis.** Black cabs are the genuine article and licensed to a demanding standard, but expensive. Uber and other apps operate widely. Don't hail minicabs off the street; always book.

**Walking.** More of London is walkable than the Tube map suggests. The South Bank from Tower Bridge to Westminster is one of the best urban walks in Europe.
