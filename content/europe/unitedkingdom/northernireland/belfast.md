Belfast has transformed itself from a city defined by conflict to one of the most interesting destinations in the British Isles. The Troubles left deep scars — the peace walls, the murals, the sectarian geography — but they also gave Belfast a resilience and dark humour that makes it unlike anywhere else in the UK or Ireland.

The Titanic Quarter, built on the shipyard where the Titanic was constructed, is now home to a world-class museum and the city's most ambitious regeneration project. The Cathedral Quarter has become the cultural heart of the city, with bars, restaurants, and street art filling the Victorian warehouse district. The Victorian and Edwardian architecture along the main avenues is impressive — the City Hall, the Grand Opera House, and the Crown Liquor Saloon (a gin palace preserved by the National Trust) are all worth seeing.

The black taxi tours of the murals and peace walls are essential for understanding the city's recent history, and the people you will meet — on both sides — are unflinchingly honest about what happened and what has changed. Belfast is friendly, affordable, and full of good craic. Go before everyone else figures this out.
