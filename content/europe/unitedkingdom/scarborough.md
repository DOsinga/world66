Scarborough is the original British seaside resort. Long before Brighton or Blackpool got in on the act, people were coming here for the sea air and the mineral springs, and the town has never quite lost that sense of being first. It sits on the Yorkshire coast, draped over a headland that divides two sweeping bays, with the ruins of a Norman castle perched on top like a reminder that this place has been worth fighting over for a thousand years.

South Bay is the classic seaside: arcades, ice cream, a harbour full of fishing boats, and the elegant Victorian Spa building curving along the waterfront. North Bay is quieter, more residential, with Peasholm Park and its miniature naval battles providing one of the most charmingly eccentric attractions in England. The two bays are connected by Marine Drive, a road carved into the base of the castle headland that is worth walking just for the views.

The town punches above its weight culturally. The Stephen Joseph Theatre, housed in a former Odeon cinema, is where Alan Ayckbourn has premiered most of his plays. Anne Bronte is buried in the churchyard of St Mary's, having come here in her final days for the sea air. The fish and chips are excellent — you are on the Yorkshire coast, after all. And if you are a walker, Scarborough is the starting point for the Cleveland Way, a 175-kilometre national trail that traces the edge of the North York Moors before turning inland across the heather.
