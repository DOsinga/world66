---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

Scotland has a rich literary tradition spanning history, fiction, and travelogue. For historical context, Michael Lynch's *Scotland: A New History* is thorough and readable; Fitzroy Maclean's *A Concise History of Scotland* is shorter. John Prebble's books on the Highland Clearances are passionate and devastating.

James Boswell's *Journal of a Tour to the Hebrides with Samuel Johnson* covers Skye, Coll, and Mull and remains one of the finest Scottish travelogues. Sir Walter Scott's *Rob Roy* and Robert Louis Stevenson's *Kidnapped* (with scenes on Mull and in Edinburgh) are the essential historical novels.

For modern fiction, Irvine Welsh's *Trainspotting* captures Edinburgh's darker side unflinchingly. James Kelman's *How Late It Was How Late* (1994 Booker Prize) and Alasdair Gray's *Lanark* are essential Glasgow reads. Muriel Spark's *The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie* is a sharp portrait of 1930s Edinburgh. Compton Mackenzie's *Whisky Galore* is lighter relief — the comic tale of a whisky shipwreck on a Hebridean island.
