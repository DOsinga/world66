The capital of Scotland is one of the most architecturally dramatic cities in Europe. Two cities sit beside each other on the same stretch of volcanic rock: the **Old Town**, a tangle of medieval closes and tenement spires draped along the ridge from the castle to Holyrood; and the **New Town**, a vast and rigorously planned 18th-century grid of Georgian terraces and crescents. Together they form a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and you can walk between them in five minutes.

The whole city is built around the Castle Rock, the 350-million-year-old plug of an extinct volcano that the medieval town huddled against for protection. From the **Royal Mile** running down its spine, narrow wynds drop steeply into the valleys on either side. To the south, **Greyfriars Kirkyard**, the underground vaults beneath South Bridge, and the Grassmarket; to the north, **Princes Street Gardens** in the drained valley of the old Nor Loch, with the Georgian streets rising beyond. East of all this, **Holyrood Park** and the volcanic peak of **Arthur's Seat** give you a 250-metre hill walk that ends in the middle of the city.

Edinburgh has been a writer's town since the Enlightenment — Hume, Adam Smith, Walter Scott, Robert Louis Stevenson, Conan Doyle, Muriel Spark, Irvine Welsh, JK Rowling — and it remains one. It has also become a festival city to a degree that beats almost anywhere else. For three weeks in **August** the place more than doubles in size as the **Edinburgh International Festival**, the **Fringe**, the **Tattoo** and the **Book Festival** take over every available room and stage; the December and January **Hogmanay** celebrations bring a torchlight procession, a vast street party and fireworks off the castle. Outside those windows the city is much quieter and feels, again, like a working capital of half a million people.

The weather is honest about itself: changeable, often cold, frequently wet, and bracing in a way that suits the city's temper. Bring proper shoes, a waterproof, and a few extra days. Three is the minimum, five is better, and a long weekend with a hangover and a walk up Arthur's Seat in the rain is one of the great European city breaks.
