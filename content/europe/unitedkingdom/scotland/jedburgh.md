Jedburgh is a small market town in the Scottish Borders, set in a shallow valley carved by the River Jed Water. It is one of the more rewarding stops on the Border Abbeys trail — the ruined 12th-century Jedburgh Abbey dominates the town from its rocky promontory, and in decent light it is genuinely spectacular.

The abbey is Augustinian and was founded around 1138. Enough of the nave and tower survives to give a clear sense of how grand it once was, and the on-site museum does a good job of filling in the rest. Equally worth your time is the Mary Queen of Scots House, a 16th-century fortified townhouse where Mary stayed in 1566 on a journey through the Borders. It tells her story clearly and the building itself — thick-walled, crow-stepped, and beautifully preserved — is the main attraction.

The town centre is compact and easy to explore on foot. The Borders landscape around Jedburgh — rolling hills, river valleys, and the odd peel tower — rewards a short drive or a walk along Jed Water. It makes a good base for visiting the other Border Abbeys at [Kelso](/europe/unitedkingdom/scotland/kelso), Melrose, and Dryburgh.
