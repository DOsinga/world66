Moray is a rural council area on the southern shore of the Moray Firth in north-eastern Scotland, tucked between the Cairngorm Mountains and the sea. It is sheltered enough from the Atlantic weather to enjoy one of the driest and sunniest microclimates in Scotland — the coast here gets less rain than most of southern England — and the combination of fertile farmland, long sandy beaches, quiet fishing villages, and the Speyside whisky country makes it one of the more rewarding and least-visited corners of the Highlands.

The region has two main draws. The first is **whisky**. More than half of Scotland's single malt distilleries sit in the valley of the River Spey, which flows through the southern half of Moray, and the **Malt Whisky Trail** is a signposted driving route that takes in nine of the best: Glenfiddich, The Glenlivet, Cardhu, Aberlour, Strathisla, Dallas Dhu, Benromach, Glen Grant, and the working Speyside Cooperage that still makes the oak casks all of them mature in. **Dufftown**, the self-proclaimed "whisky capital of the world," has seven distilleries within a mile of its market square and an old saying: if Rome was built on seven hills, Dufftown was built on seven stills. The **Speyside Whisky Festival** every May is one of the best ways to experience all of this at once.

The second draw is the **coast**. Long sandy beaches at Findhorn, Lossiemouth, and Roseisle give way to dramatic red sandstone cliffs and a string of painted fishing villages: **Findochty**, whose brightly coloured cottages look more Mediterranean than Scottish; **Portknockie**, with the striking sea arch of the Bow Fiddle Rock just offshore; and **Cullen**, whose fishing community gave its name to **Cullen skink**, a creamy smoked-haddock soup you will find on menus across the region. The **Moray Firth** itself is home to one of only two resident populations of bottlenose dolphins in Britain — boat trips from Buckie and Lossiemouth, and patient watching from the shore, are often rewarded.

The main town of Moray is **Elgin**, the medieval cathedral city whose 13th-century cathedral (the "Lantern of the North") is one of the most beautiful ruins in Scotland. Nearby **Pluscarden Abbey**, founded in 1230, is still a working Benedictine monastery — one of the few surviving in Britain — and visitors are welcome at services. Outside Forres, the 23-foot-high Pictish **Sueno's Stone** is one of the finest standing carved monuments in northern Europe. And inland, the Cairngorms National Park edge is an easy drive away, via the high **Tomintoul** village and the Lecht ski area.

Moray is an excellent base for a slow-travel Scottish week — distilleries, dolphins, walking on the coast path, and some of the best seafood in the country.
