Oban is where western Scotland tips into the sea. This compact harbor town in Argyll serves as the main departure point for the Inner Hebrides — ferries to Mull, Lismore, Colonsay, Coll, Tiree, Islay, and the islands beyond leave from the bay throughout the day — and the waterfront has the purposeful, slightly salty energy that comes with a working port. Fishing boats, CalMac ferries, and yachts share the harbor, and the hills behind the town rise steeply, closing it in from the east and giving Oban the feeling of a place that turns toward the water as a matter of necessity.

The single most famous activity in town involves no travel at all. The seafood stall on the harbor pier sells langoustines, oysters, crab claws, and dressed crab directly off the boats. Queuing here in all weathers with a polystyrene tray is something of a rite, and the quality justifies every minute of the wait. Oban's seafood restaurants carry the same tradition indoors, and the town punches well above its size for the quality of what comes out of its kitchens.

McCaig's Tower, the colosseum-like granite folly that crowns the hill above the town, was commissioned in 1897 by a local banker partly as a job-creation scheme for unemployed stonemasons. It was never finished and its interior has always been an open garden, but its presence on the skyline is unmistakable. Climbing up to it takes about ten minutes from the town center and the view over the bay, the islands, and the mountains of Mull on the far side of the sound justifies the effort entirely.

The Oban Distillery sits in the middle of town, literally enclosed by the urban fabric — one of the smallest production facilities of any major Scotch distillery. It has operated on this site since 1794, and the guided tour is one of the better distillery experiences in Scotland. The whisky itself — lightly peated, maritime, with a characteristic sweetness — reflects the landscape around it. From Oban you can reach Mull in under an hour, Islay in two and a half, and the entire western island chain is effectively at your feet: few places in Britain offer such immediate access to such extraordinary scenery.
