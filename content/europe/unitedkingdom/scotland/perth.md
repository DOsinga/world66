Perth sits on the River Tay at the southern edge of the Highlands, a position that made it one of the most strategically important towns in medieval Scotland. It was a royal residence throughout the Middle Ages and served as the effective capital of Scotland until the 15th century — hence the unofficial title "The Ancient Capital of Scotland." The town centre retains its medieval street pattern, flanked by two large parks (the North and South Inches) where clan conflicts and the 1715 Jacobite uprising left their marks.

St John's Kirk, the oldest building in the centre, is where John Knox preached the inflammatory sermon in 1559 that sparked the Scottish Reformation. A few miles north, Scone Palace occupies the site where Scottish kings were crowned on the Stone of Destiny for centuries. The palace grounds are open to visitors and the state rooms contain a good collection of furniture and art.

Perth makes a natural gateway to the southern Highlands. Dunkeld and Birnam, 20 km north by train or road, are worth a half-day: the ruined cathedral, the Beatrix Potter Garden, and the woodland walks at the Hermitage all cluster in a small area. The town itself is notable for its flowers — a long tradition of public planting that gives even the commercial streets a distinctive seasonal character.
