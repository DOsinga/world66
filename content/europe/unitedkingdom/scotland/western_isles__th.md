The Outer Hebrides — also known as the Western Isles or the Long Island — are a chain of islands off the northwest coast of Scotland, stretching about 200 km from Lewis and Harris in the north to Barra in the south. Despite appearances on a map, Lewis and Harris are a single island divided by mountains, not a strait; the other main islands are North Uist, Benbecula, South Uist, and Barra, linked by bridges and causeways.

The landscape is unlike anywhere else in Britain: vast peat moors, sea lochs cutting deep into the land, and on the west-facing coasts, dazzling white sand beaches backed by machair — a low-lying grassland that bursts into flower in early summer. The Callanish Standing Stones on Lewis, erected around 2900 BC, rival Stonehenge in scale and are far less crowded. Gaelic is still spoken widely, particularly on the more southerly islands.

Caledonian MacBrayne ferries connect the islands to the mainland — from Ullapool to Stornoway, from Uig on Skye to Tarbert (Harris) and Lochmaddy (North Uist), and from Oban to Barra and South Uist. Island-hopping tickets are available for multi-island journeys. The islands are quiet, the weather unpredictable, and the pace slow — which is precisely the point.
