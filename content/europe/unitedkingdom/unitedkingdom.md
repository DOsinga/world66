The United Kingdom is formed by [England](/europe/unitedkingdom/england), [Scotland](/europe/unitedkingdom/scotland), [Wales](/europe/unitedkingdom/wales) and [Northern Ireland](/europe/unitedkingdom/northernireland) — four nations with distinct identities, languages and cultures bound into one state. Don't call a Scot English, or a Welsh person British if they'd rather you didn't. These distinctions matter here.

Separated from continental Europe by only a few miles of water, the UK is nonetheless permeated by a strong sense of its separateness. Measures, road habits, humour and food all diverge from European norms. The country drives on the left, prices its goods in pounds sterling, and takes a particular pride in its eccentricities.

Politically, the UK has been reshaping itself. Devolution brought parliaments and assemblies to Edinburgh, Cardiff and Belfast in the late 1990s, and the 2016 Brexit vote to leave the European Union has continued to redefine the country's relationship with its neighbours. Northern Ireland remains constitutionally complex, with ongoing debates about its future that are impossible to ignore if you travel there.

Despite all this, there is continuity. The country is extraordinarily rich in accumulated history — from Neolithic stone circles and Roman villas through Norman castles, Tudor manor houses and the industrial landscapes of the north to the modernist landmarks of the 21st century. A good heritage infrastructure makes most of it accessible and well interpreted.

For sheer urban energy, [London](/europe/unitedkingdom/london) is unmissable — one of the great cities of the world, with world-class museums, theatre, food and nightlife concentrated in a metropolis of nearly 10 million people. Beyond London, [Edinburgh](/europe/unitedkingdom/scotland/edinburgh) and [Glasgow](/europe/unitedkingdom/scotland/glasgow) offer a different, sharper urban character. [Wales](/europe/unitedkingdom/wales) and the Scottish Highlands provide genuine wilderness.
