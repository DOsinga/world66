---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Almost everything worth seeing in Vatican City is concentrated in two areas: the basilica complex and the Vatican Museums. Between them, they can easily fill a full day — or two if you want to take your time.

**St. Peter's Basilica** is the centrepiece of the Vatican and one of the largest churches in the world. Construction began in 1506 on the site of the original 4th-century basilica commissioned by Constantine, and was completed over the following 120 years. Michelangelo took over as chief architect in 1547 and designed the great dome; Bernini added the baldachin, the colonnade of St. Peter's Square, and much of the interior decoration. Admission is free but the dress code is strictly enforced.

**The Vatican Museums** contain one of the world's great art collections, accumulated over five centuries of papal patronage. The collection spans ancient Greek and Roman sculpture, Egyptian antiquities, Renaissance paintings, tapestries, maps, and modern religious art. Highlights include the Gallery of Maps, the Raphael Rooms, the Pio-Clementine Museum (home to the Laocoön and the Apollo Belvedere), and the Pinacoteca (picture gallery). The museums lead to the Sistine Chapel.

**The Sistine Chapel** needs little introduction. What often surprises visitors is the scale — the ceiling is about 20 metres high and covers some 500 square metres. Michelangelo completed the ceiling between 1508 and 1512, working largely alone. The Last Judgement on the altar wall was completed 29 years later, in 1541. Earlier frescoes by Botticelli, Ghirlandaio, and Perugino line the lower walls. No photography.

**The Vatican Gardens** cover about half of Vatican City's total area and are accessible only by guided tour, bookable through the museums website. The gardens contain Renaissance and Baroque fountains, topiary, a replica Lourdes grotto, and the Casina of Pius IV, a small Renaissance palace now used for Vatican academic events.

**Castel Sant'Angelo**, though technically just across the border in Rome, is connected to the Vatican by the Passetto di Borgo — a covered corridor built in 1277 that allowed popes to flee the Vatican in emergencies. The castle is now a museum and offers good views over the Tiber and St. Peter's.
