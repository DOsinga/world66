---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Vatican City has a population of around 800, making it the world's least populous sovereign state. Most residents are clergy, members of the Papal Swiss Guard, and employees of the Holy See. Very few have citizenship by birth — Vatican citizenship is functional rather than hereditary, granted to those who work or live there in an official capacity, and it lapses when they leave.

The Swiss Guard, founded in 1506, is the world's oldest active military corps still in service. Its members must be Swiss Catholic men between 19 and 30, at least 174cm tall. They guard the Vatican's entrances and the Pope's personal security. The colourful Renaissance-era uniforms (credited, incorrectly, to Michelangelo) are ceremonial; the Guard also uses modern equipment.

Italian is the working language of Vatican City. Latin retains official status and is used in formal Church documents. Many residents speak multiple languages given the international nature of the Church's administration.

The Vatican is not a place most people live in any ordinary sense. The daily reality of the state is the bureaucracy of the Holy See — a diplomatic entity with embassies in over 180 countries — and the logistical operation of serving several million visitors per year.
