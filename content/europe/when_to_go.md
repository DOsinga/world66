---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

There's no single best time to visit Europe — it depends entirely on where you're going and what you want to do. The continent spans from Arctic [Norway](/europe/norway) to subtropical [Greece](/europe/greece), and the seasons play out very differently across that range. What you can count on: July and August are peak season almost everywhere, which means higher prices, bigger crowds, and the need to book well in advance.

The Mediterranean summer is glorious but intense. [Southern France](/europe/france), [Italy](/europe/italy), [Spain](/europe/spain), and [Greece](/europe/greece) bake under relentless sun from late June through August, with temperatures regularly pushing past 35°C. Coastal towns and islands fill up completely — you'll compete for beach space, restaurant tables, and patience. If the Mediterranean is your goal, the shoulder seasons of May to mid-June and mid-September to October are far more pleasant: warm enough for swimming, dramatically fewer crowds, and prices that don't make your eyes water.

Northern Europe comes alive in summer. From June through August, Scandinavia, the [UK](/europe/unitedkingdom), [Ireland](/europe/ireland), and the Baltics enjoy long daylight hours — nearly 24 hours of light in the far north around midsummer. This is festival season across the continent, from music festivals in the [UK](/europe/unitedkingdom) to midsummer celebrations in [Sweden](/europe/sweden) and [Finland](/europe/finland). [Amsterdam](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam), [Berlin](/europe/germany/berlin), and [Copenhagen](/europe/denmark/copenhagen) are at their best when outdoor cafe culture takes over the streets. Just don't expect guaranteed sunshine — northern European weather keeps you honest.

Winter brings its own rewards. The Alps offer world-class skiing from December through March, with resorts across [Austria](/europe/austria), [Switzerland](/europe/switzerland), [France](/europe/france), and [Italy](/europe/italy). Christmas markets transform cities across [Germany](/europe/germany), [Austria](/europe/austria), and central Europe into twinkling, mulled-wine-scented wonderlands from late November through December. Head far enough north — [Norway](/europe/norway), [Finland](/europe/finland), northern [Sweden](/europe/sweden) — and you can chase the northern lights between September and March. Southern European cities like [Rome](/europe/italy/rome), [Lisbon](/europe/portugal/lisbon), and [Barcelona](/europe/spain/barcelona) are mild and uncrowded in winter, making them excellent off-season destinations.

Eastern Europe experiences continental climate extremes that catch some visitors off guard. Summers in [Hungary](/europe/hungary), [Poland](/europe/poland), and [Romania](/europe/romania) can be genuinely hot, while winters bring serious cold and snow. Spring and autumn are the sweet spots — [Prague](/europe/czechrepublic/prague) in May or [Budapest](/europe/hungary/budapest) in September are hard to beat. These shoulder seasons also mean lower prices in a region that's already far cheaper than western Europe.

Pricing follows the crowds predictably. Flights and accommodation spike during school holidays (July-August across most of Europe, plus two weeks around Christmas and Easter). Book early or travel off-peak and you'll save substantially. Shoulder season isn't just cheaper — it's often the most enjoyable time to travel, with pleasant weather, shorter queues, and locals who aren't yet exhausted by the tourist tide.
