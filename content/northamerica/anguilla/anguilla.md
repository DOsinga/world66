Anguilla is a small British Overseas Territory in the Eastern Caribbean that has quietly become one of the region's most celebrated beach destinations. It is 26 km long and about 5 km wide, flat and scrubby, sitting directly north of St. Martin. What it lacks in dramatic landscapes it more than makes up for in beaches: the island has 33 of them, and several rank among the best in the Caribbean.

The island draws two kinds of traveler — those who come for the luxury resorts clustered along Meads Bay and Maunday's Bay, and those who come for the beaches and the food and are happy to stay somewhere simpler. Both can be satisfied here. Anguilla's restaurant scene is remarkably strong for an island of roughly 18,000 people, earning it a reputation as the culinary capital of the Caribbean.

Anguilla has been a British colony since 1650, though the Arawaks were here thousands of years before that. The island's most dramatic modern moment came in 1967, when Anguillans revolted against federation with St. Kitts and Nevis and eventually won the right to remain a British territory — a revolution achieved almost entirely without bloodshed, and celebrated to this day on Anguilla Day (May 30).

The Valley is the small capital at the centre of the island. Most visitors arrive by ferry from nearby St. Martin, docking at Blowing Point in the south. The island is flat enough that driving it end to end takes less than half an hour.
