---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Anguilla punches well above its weight in the kitchen. The island's food culture draws on West African, Caribbean, British, and French influences — the last partly attributable to the proximity of French St. Martin, whose chefs and produce cross regularly on the ferry.

Seafood is central. Lobster, conch, crayfish, snapper, and grouper appear on menus across the price spectrum. **Lobster Fest**, held in autumn, celebrates the start of lobster season with a week of special menus at restaurants island-wide. Conch is prepared dozens of ways — stewed, fried, in chowder, in fritters.

The local staples are simpler: **johnny cakes** (deep-fried dough), saltfish (salt cod prepared in stews and with vegetables), rice and peas, and **souse** (pickled pork). The Welches Festival in the village of Welches each year is the best place to eat these dishes as home cooks rather than restaurants make them.

The roadside barbecue scene is excellent and easy to miss if you stick to resort dining. On weekends especially, stalls outside The Valley and in Stoney Ground set up grills and sell ribs, chicken, and fish for a few dollars. Arriving early means better choice; arriving late means the coals are at their best. Either works.

At the higher end, Anguilla has a cluster of genuinely accomplished restaurants. **Tasty's** in South Hill has been a local institution for years, serving Creole and Caribbean food in a relaxed setting. **Blanchard's** on Meads Bay is the best-known fine-dining option, run by American couple Bob and Melinda Blanchard (who wrote a memoir about building it). The **Dune Preserve**, Bankie Banx's beach bar on Rendezvous Bay, is more atmosphere than cuisine but is not to be missed.

The **Anguilla Culinary Experience (ACE)** festival in May brings visiting chefs to the island for a week of dinners, beach barbecues, and cooking demonstrations.
