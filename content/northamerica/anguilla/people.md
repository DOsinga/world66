---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Anguilla has a population of around 18,000, the majority of whom are descended from enslaved West Africans brought to the island during the colonial period. A smaller number of residents are British expatriates, Americans, and migrants from other Caribbean islands — the island's relative prosperity draws workers from across the region.

Anguillans have a strong sense of identity shaped in part by the 1967 revolution. When Britain federated Anguilla with St. Kitts and Nevis, the island's residents — unwilling to be governed from St. Kitts — expelled the Kittitian police, held a referendum, and declared independence. Britain eventually responded not with force but with negotiation, and Anguilla remained a separate British Overseas Territory. The revolution was remarkably peaceful; it is remembered locally with more pride than most historical events of this size would warrant.

The culture is relaxed and outwardly cordial. People greet each other in passing; unhurried conversation is the norm. Boat racing — a tradition dating back to the trading schooners of the 19th century — is taken seriously enough to be considered a national sport. The wooden sailboats are still built by hand, and the racing community spans generations.

Music is important. Anguilla has produced Bankie Banx, a reggae and blues musician who has been performing and recording since the 1970s and who runs the Dune Preserve, a legendary beach bar and performance space on Rendezvous Bay. His Moonsplash festival, running continuously for over 30 years, is one of the oldest independent music festivals in the Caribbean.
