Barbuda is [Antigua's](/northamerica/antiguaandbarbuda) quieter, flatter, and almost empty sister island, lying about 50 kilometres to the north. Fewer than 2,000 people live here, nearly all of them in the single village of Codrington. The rest of the island is scrubland, lagoons, and some of the most spectacular beaches in the Caribbean.

The western shore is one long stretch of pink-tinged sand — the colour comes from crushed shells — that runs for kilometres without a building in sight. On most days you will have it entirely to yourself. The Codrington Lagoon, a large shallow body of water in the island's centre, is home to one of the largest frigate bird colonies in the western hemisphere. A boat trip into the mangroves during nesting season (September to February) puts you face to face with hundreds of males inflating their vivid red throat pouches.

Barbuda was devastated by Hurricane Irma in 2017, which damaged or destroyed nearly every structure on the island and forced a complete evacuation. The island has been slowly rebuilding since, and while tourism infrastructure remains minimal — a handful of guesthouses and a couple of beach bars — that is part of the appeal. A day trip by boat or small plane from Antigua is the most common way to visit, though staying overnight is the only way to appreciate just how quiet a Caribbean island can be.
