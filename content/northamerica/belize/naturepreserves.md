Belize has protected roughly 40% of its land area — an extraordinary commitment for a small country. Nature reserves and wildlife sanctuaries cover the jungle interior, the coastline, and the reef.

**Cockscomb Basin Wildlife Sanctuary** in southern Belize is the world's only jaguar reserve, protecting 150 square km of lowland tropical forest. Jaguars are present but elusive; most visitors see tracks rather than cats. What's reliable: birding, tapirs, coatis, and the waterfalls in the reserve. Good trail network.

**Crooked Tree Wildlife Sanctuary**, north of Belize City, is a lagoon system and bird-watching destination. In the dry season (March–May), the lagoons contract and birds concentrate in large numbers. Jabiru storks — the largest flying birds in the Americas — nest here.

**Community Baboon Sanctuary** near Bermudian Landing, a short drive from Belize City, protects black howler monkeys in a community-managed reserve. Morning is the best time to hear (and see) them. Easy half-day trip from the city.

**Guanacaste National Park**, just outside [Belmopan](/northamerica/belize/belmopan), is a small park at the river junction with a pleasant swimming hole and easy forest walks.

**Rio Bravo Conservation Area** in northwest Belize is managed by the Programme for Belize and protects 100,000 hectares of jungle, including some remote Maya sites. Primarily for organized tours and researchers.

The Mountain Pine Ridge Forest Reserve, south of [San Ignacio](/northamerica/belize/sanignacio), is a highland plateau with granite hills, pine forest, and waterfalls — very different from the lowland jungle. Hidden Valley Falls (also called Thousand Foot Falls) is one of the highest waterfalls in Central America.
