---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

There are no car rentals in Bermuda — visitors are not permitted to rent cars at all. This forces a genuinely pleasant mode of travel: public buses, ferries, mopeds, taxis, or your own feet.

**Buses** are the backbone of island transport. The pink-and-blue buses are frequent, reliable, and cover the entire length of the island. You need exact change in coins to pay the fare; bus tokens and passes are available from the Central Bus Terminal in Hamilton and from some hotels.

**Ferries** are faster than buses for certain routes and far more scenic. The Blue Route connects Hamilton with the Royal Naval Dockyard in about 20 minutes — a beautiful crossing of the Great Sound. Other routes serve Paget, Warwick, and the Somerset area. Ferry tickets and passes are interchangeable with bus passes.

**Mopeds and scooters** are the classic Bermudian way to explore. Rental shops are found around Hamilton, at major hotels, and near the Dockyard. You need to be at least 16 years old, wear a helmet, and drive on the left. Be aware: Bermuda's roads are narrow and winding, and enough visitors come off their bikes that "road rash" is a well-known local term. Take it slow until you are comfortable.

**Taxis** are metered and available at the airport, large hotels, and Hamilton. Drivers are knowledgeable and many are happy to offer informal island tours by the hour — the meter is switched off and an hourly rate negotiated. This is a good option for first-timers who want commentary along with transport.

Walking is practical within towns — Hamilton, St. George's, and the Dockyard area are all walkable — but walking between towns is not recommended as the narrow roads mostly lack pavements.
