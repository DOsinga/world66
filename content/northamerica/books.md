---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

Reading before you travel will deepen your experience considerably. North America has inspired some extraordinary writing — novels, journalism, poetry, and memoir that capture landscapes and cultures better than any guidebook.

**United States and Canada.** John Steinbeck's *Travels with Charley* is the classic American road trip account, still worth reading for its observations of a changing country. Jack Kerouac's *On the Road* defined a certain kind of American wandering. For the American West, Wallace Stegner's *The Big Rock Candy Mountain* and Ivan Doig's *This House of Sky* are essential. Colson Whitehead's *The Underground Railroad* and Toni Morrison's *Beloved* give essential historical depth to the American South. For Canada, Farley Mowat's *Never Cry Wolf* captures the northern wilderness, and Mordecai Richler's *The Apprenticeship of Duddy Kravitz* brings [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal) to life.

**Mexico.** Mexican literature is world-class. Juan Rulfo's *Pedro Paramo* is a short masterpiece of magical realism set in a ghost town and unlike anything else you'll read. Carlos Fuentes' *The Death of Artemio Cruz* covers the arc of the Mexican revolution. Octavio Paz's *The Labyrinth of Solitude* remains the essential meditation on Mexican identity. For something more contemporary, Valeria Luiselli's *Lost Children Archive* deals with migration at the US-Mexico border with remarkable intelligence and empathy. *Like Water for Chocolate* by Laura Esquivel captures provincial Mexican life in a more romantic vein.

**Central America.** Paul Theroux's *The Mosquito Coast* is set in the jungles of [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), and his *The Old Patagonian Express* covers his journey through [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) and Central America. Aldous Huxley's *Beyond the Mexique Bay* is a classic 1930s account of traveling through [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Belize](/northamerica/belize), and Mexico. For understanding the region's political upheavals, Joan Didion's *Salvador* remains a devastating and compact piece of reporting. *I, Rigoberta Menchu* gives a first-person account of indigenous life and resistance in Guatemala.

**The Caribbean.** V.S. Naipaul's *The Middle Passage* offers a sharp, sometimes uncomfortable portrait of Caribbean societies in the early 1960s. Derek Walcott's epic poem *Omeros* reimagines Homer on the shores of [Saint Lucia](/northamerica/saintlucia) and won the Nobel Prize. Jamaica Kincaid's *A Small Place* is a fierce, short meditation on [Antigua](/northamerica/antiguaandbarbuda) and the legacy of colonialism — read it before you visit. For [Cuba](/northamerica/cuba), Leonardo Padura's Havana Quartet novels capture the atmosphere of the city better than any guidebook.

**Food writing.** If food is part of why you're going — and it should be — pick up *Pati's Mexican Table* by Pati Jinich for a warm introduction to Mexican home cooking, or *Provisions* by Michelle and Suzanne Rousseau for Jamaican cooking. Anthony Bourdain's *A Cook's Tour* covers his early travels through Mexico and elsewhere in typically irreverent style.
