---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

Central America and the Caribbean have inspired some extraordinary writing, and reading before you travel will deepen your experience immeasurably. The region's turbulent history, cultural richness, and sheer beauty have attracted novelists, journalists, and poets for centuries.

For the mainland, start with Paul Theroux's *The Mosquito Coast*, set in the jungles of [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), and his travel memoir *The Old Patagonian Express*, which covers his train journey through [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) and Central America. Aldous Huxley's *Beyond the Mexique Bay* is a classic 1930s account of traveling through [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Belize](/northamerica/belize), and [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico). For understanding the region's political upheavals, Joan Didion's *Salvador* remains a devastating and compact piece of reporting. *I, Rigoberta Menchu* gives a first-person account of indigenous life and resistance in [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala).

The Caribbean literary tradition is deep and world-class. V.S. Naipaul's *The Middle Passage* offers a sharp, sometimes uncomfortable portrait of Caribbean societies in the early 1960s. Derek Walcott's epic poem *Omeros* reimagines Homer on the shores of St. Lucia and won the Nobel Prize. Jamaica Kincaid's *A Small Place* is a fierce, short meditation on [Antigua](/northamerica/antiguaandbarbuda) and the legacy of colonialism — read it before you visit. For [Cuba](/northamerica/cuba), Leonardo Padura's Havana Quartet novels capture the atmosphere of the city better than any guidebook.

Mexican literature deserves special mention. Carlos Fuentes' *The Death of Artemio Cruz* and Juan Rulfo's *Pedro Paramo* are pillars of Latin American fiction. For something more contemporary, Valeria Luiselli's *Lost Children Archive* deals with migration at the US-Mexico border with remarkable empathy. And no reading list for [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) would be complete without the poetry of Octavio Paz — *The Labyrinth of Solitude* is essential for understanding Mexican identity.

For practical inspiration, the Rough Guides and Lonely Planet books for individual countries remain useful for trip planning, though details go out of date quickly. Moon Guides are particularly strong for [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) and [Belize](/northamerica/belize). If you're interested in the natural world, Les Beletsky's field guides to the wildlife of Central America and the Caribbean are compact and well-illustrated. And for food — which is reason enough to visit this region — pick up a copy of *Pati's Mexican Table* by Pati Jinich or *Provisions* by Michelle and Suzanne Rousseau for Jamaican cooking.
