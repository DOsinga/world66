Banff is the postcard of the Canadian Rockies — a small resort town sitting in a valley of ragged peaks, with turquoise lakes, glaciers, and elk wandering down the main street. It was the first piece of Canada set aside as a national park, in 1885, and the spectacular scenery that drew the Canadian Pacific Railway to build its baronial Banff Springs Hotel here still draws four million visitors a year. In summer it is crowded; in winter the crowds are at Lake Louise, Sunshine Village, and Mount Norquay for the skiing.

The town itself is compact and walkable. **Banff Avenue** — the main drag — has the outfitters, the restaurants, and a view straight down the street to the towering mass of Cascade Mountain. The **Banff Park Museum** (a 1903 wooden building packed with Victorian-era taxidermy) is a strange and wonderful small museum; the **Whyte Museum** has the art and history of the Rockies. The **Banff Upper Hot Springs**, on the mountainside above town, have been open to bathers for more than 130 years.

The real reason to be here, though, is the surrounding **Banff National Park**. A short drive from town, the classic short walks include **Bow Falls**, the **Hoodoos**, **Johnston Canyon** (up to its two waterfalls through a deep slot canyon), and the hilltop viewpoints at **Tunnel Mountain** and **Sulphur Mountain** (the Banff Gondola saves you the climb on the latter). Further afield, **Lake Louise** (57 km north) and **Moraine Lake** are the two most photographed lakes in Canada and genuinely live up to the pictures. The **Icefields Parkway**, running north from Lake Louise to Jasper, is one of the most beautiful drives in the world — past **Peyto Lake**, **Bow Lake**, **Crowfoot Glacier**, and the **Columbia Icefield** where you can walk on the Athabasca Glacier.

Wildlife is abundant: elk and deer in and around town (give them space — the bulls are dangerous in the autumn rut), black and grizzly bears and bighorn sheep on the mountainsides, and wolves, cougars, and moose for the lucky and the patient. Dawn and dusk drives along the Bow Valley Parkway are the best chance of seeing the larger animals.

Summer (June–September) is the main season, with the warmest weather and access to all trails. Winter (December–March) is world-class ski terrain. The shoulder seasons — May and October — are cold but much less crowded, and autumn colour along the Bow River in late September is extraordinary.
