British Columbia is Canada's westernmost province and its most scenically extravagant. Mountains crowd the coast from north to south, old-growth rainforests spill down to the Pacific, and the Interior opens into semi-arid valleys where vineyards thrive beside desert canyons. It's a place that regularly stuns first-time visitors and keeps drawing people back.

The province holds extremes: Vancouver is one of the world's most livable cities, polished and cosmopolitan; three hours north by ferry you're in the territory of grizzly bears and no cell service. The Okanagan Valley is sun-baked wine country. Haida Gwaii is a remote archipelago with its own Indigenous culture and ecology. Whistler is world-class skiing. Tofino is wild surf beaches and ancient cedars.

Getting around takes effort — BC is nearly twice the size of France — but the reward is scale. You can drive the Sea-to-Sky Highway with Howe Sound glittering below, take the BC Ferries through the Gulf Islands, or fly into a float-plane dock at the edge of the wilderness. The province's infrastructure is good enough that most of it is genuinely accessible.

Wildlife is everywhere and surprisingly visible: orcas off Vancouver Island, grizzlies along the Chilko River, thousands of bald eagles in the Squamish Valley in winter. The temperate rainforest supports a density of life that's hard to find anywhere else.

Climate varies wildly. The coast is mild and rainy year-round; the Interior gets hot dry summers and cold winters; the mountains get enormous snowfall. Summer (June–September) is the easiest time to travel broadly, but winter is perfect if skiing or wildlife watching in the snow is your goal.
