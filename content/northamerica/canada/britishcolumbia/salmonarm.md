Salmon Arm sits on the long fjord-like southern arm of **Shuswap Lake**, in the rolling interior plateau of British Columbia between the Okanagan and the Rockies. The town itself is a small commercial centre — a single main street rising from the lakeshore — but the setting is the draw: a wide sheet of clear cold water rimmed by forested ridges, with one of the longest freshwater wharves in Canada running out into the bay. The lake is the centre of the local houseboat economy, and in summer the marinas on every arm rent out floating cabins for week-long cruises through the Shuswap's quiet coves.

The other thing Salmon Arm is known for is salmon. Every four years (the next dominant run is well-publicised locally) the **Adams River sockeye run** at nearby Roderick Haig-Brown Provincial Park turns the river red with millions of returning fish — one of the great wildlife spectacles in North America. Even in off-years a smaller run comes through every autumn. Off the lake, the surrounding hills are good walking and cycling country, with the **Larch Hills** Nordic ski trails turning into mountain-bike country in summer.

Salmon Arm is the largest town in BC's **Shuswap region**, set on the long crooked finger of Shuswap Lake from which it takes its name. The town has the standard look of a small interior BC settlement — a single main street, a clutch of malls along the highway, the lake glittering at the bottom of a wooded valley — but its setting is the draw: Shuswap is one of British Columbia's great houseboating lakes, and on summer weekends the marinas at Salmon Arm and nearby Sicamous send a steady stream of rentals out into the four arms of the lake.

The most famous local sight is the **Adams River sockeye salmon run**, an hour's drive west at Roderick Haig-Brown Provincial Park. Every four years, in October, several million bright red salmon return to spawn in shallow gravel runs — one of the most spectacular wildlife events in North America. The next dominant runs are due in 2026 and 2030; smaller runs occur in the years between.

Otherwise Salmon Arm is a quiet farm town with a good Saturday-morning market, a marsh-and-pier walk on the lake foreshore that doubles as a birding hotspot (look for western grebes), and easy access to the Trans-Canada Highway. Most travellers see it as a stop on the long drive between Calgary and Vancouver, or as a base for a few days on the lake.
