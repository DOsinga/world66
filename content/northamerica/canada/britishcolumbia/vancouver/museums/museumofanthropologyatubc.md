The Museum of Anthropology at UBC is a great place to spend a rainy afternoon. Their website is quite good. It includes lots of pictures and even a virtual tour, where you will get a sense of the majesty of some of the works.

There are extensive exhibits from various coastal native communities, including large carvings, totem poles, masks, and an outdoor Haida longhouse. Along with the hundreds of native antiquities on display, the MOA has something like 13,000 items in "open storage" --many of them in drawers with minimal or no documentation. This is a good way to see some museum pieces--just look and appreciate.

This museum houses one of the world’s finest displays of Northwest Coast First Nations art in an award-winning building overlooking mountains and sea. See poles, feast dishes and canoes inside the soaring glass and concrete structure of the museum’s Great Hall. Wander through the Masterpiece Gallery with its dramatic views and intricately carved works in silver, gold, stone and wood. Explore over 15,000 objects from around the world in the museum’s innovative Visible Storage Galleries. Stroll through the museum grounds where two Haida houses and ten poles capture the dramatic beauty of traditional Northwest Coast architecture and design. Contemporary Works by First Nations Artists in various media, such as photography, painting and textiles, by artists from the Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, Gitxsan and Nisga'a nations. See the Philippine Pottery from the Tecson Collection in the only collection of indigenous Philippine pottery in Western Canada. Three Case Studies Objects such as turn-of-the-century paddles and contemporary works by master artist Dempsey Bob highlight different aspects of Northwest Coast art. Attributed to Edenshaw: Identifying the Hand of the Artist Basketry, gold, silver, argillite and wood carvings by Haida artists Charles and Isabella Edenshaw.
