Winnipeg sits almost exactly in the longitudinal centre of North America, at the confluence of the Red and Assiniboine Rivers — a meeting place used by Indigenous peoples for six thousand years before the Hudson's Bay Company built a fort nearby in 1738. Today it is the capital of Manitoba, a city of 750,000, and the gateway to western Canada for travellers coming overland.

The city has a long, cold, unpretentious character. Locals call it "Winterpeg" for reasons that become obvious in January, when temperatures regularly hit minus thirty. But the summer is glorious, the arts scene — Royal Winnipeg Ballet, Winnipeg Symphony, Folklorama — is remarkable for a city its size, and the **Canadian Museum for Human Rights**, a Daniel Libeskind-designed landmark that opened in 2014, is one of the most ambitious museum projects in the country.

**The Forks**, where the rivers meet downtown, has been rebuilt into a market, riverside walk, and historic site. The **Exchange District**, a compact grid of early-20th-century brick warehouses, is the best collection of pre-1914 commercial architecture in Canada. The **Manitoba Museum** is the grandfather of local museums, with a full-sized replica of the Nonsuch, the ship that started the fur trade. Further out, the **Royal Canadian Mint** produces Canada's circulating coinage and runs tours.

The food scene is better than the city's reputation — Winnipeg is one of Canada's most culturally diverse cities per capita, and the restaurants reflect it. Filipino, Ukrainian, Icelandic, Chinese, Thai, and Indigenous cuisines are all strongly represented.
