St. Andrews-by-the-Sea is one of the prettiest small towns in Atlantic Canada, perched on a peninsula at the mouth of Passamaquoddy Bay where the St. Croix River meets the Bay of Fundy. It was founded by Loyalists in 1783, and many of the original buildings survive — the town has more pre-1880 buildings than almost anywhere else in the Maritimes.

Water Street, the main thoroughfare, runs along the harbour and is lined with galleries, craft shops, and restaurants serving fresh lobster and scallops. The Kingsbrae Garden, a 27-acre horticultural showpiece, is the main in-town attraction and well worth an hour or two. At the tip of the peninsula, the Algonquin Resort — a grand Tudor-style railway hotel — has dominated the skyline since 1889.

The real draw is the bay. Whale watching tours leave from the town wharf and regularly spot minke whales, finback whales, and harbour porpoises. Ministers Island, accessible by a sandbar at low tide, has a fascinating abandoned estate built by a railway baron. The Bay of Fundy's extreme tides — the highest in the world — are the backdrop to everything here, and the shoreline changes dramatically between high and low water.
