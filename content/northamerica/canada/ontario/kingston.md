Kingston sits at the point where Lake Ontario meets the St. Lawrence River, with the mouth of the Rideau Canal just above it — a geography that made it strategically vital and historically rich. It was Canada's first capital city after Confederation in 1841, and the limestone buildings from that era give downtown a solidity and grandeur unusual for a mid-sized Ontario city.

The city's character is shaped by three overlapping worlds: the military (Canadian Forces Base Kingston, Fort Henry, the Royal Military College), the academic (Queen's University dominates the west end), and the maritime (the waterfront is genuinely lovely, with the Thousand Islands archipelago beginning just downstream). The result is a city that feels lively year-round, with a student population that keeps the restaurants and bars busy and a tourist draw that fills the historic sites in summer.

The Limestone City, as it is often called, rewards slow exploration on foot. Princess Street is the main commercial strip, lined with independent shops and cafes; the waterfront path runs along the lake with views of the outer islands; and the Market Square has been a gathering place since the 18th century.
