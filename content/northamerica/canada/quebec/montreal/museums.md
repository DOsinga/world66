---
title: 'Museums'
type: section
---

Montreal punches well above its weight for museums. The city has world-class institutions alongside quirky smaller collections, and many offer free or reduced admission on certain evenings.

**Pointe-a-Calliere** (350 Place Royale) is the city's museum of archaeology and history, built directly on top of Montreal's founding site. You walk through actual archaeological ruins beneath the modern building while multimedia displays bring a thousand years of history to life. The permanent exhibition is excellent, and the temporary shows are consistently strong. One of the best museums in Canada.

**Montreal Museum of Fine Arts** (1380 Rue Sherbrooke Ouest) is the oldest art museum in the country, spread across five buildings. The permanent collection of over 44,000 works spans ancient to contemporary, with particular strength in Canadian and Inuit art. The permanent collection is free. Temporary exhibitions are ticketed and usually worth it.

**Musee McCord** (690 Rue Sherbrooke Ouest) tells the social history of Montreal through photographs, clothing, toys, sports equipment, and First Nations objects. The photographic archives are outstanding -- the Notman collection alone contains hundreds of thousands of images of Montreal from the 1850s onward.

**Chateau Ramezay** (280 Rue Notre-Dame Est) is a former governor's residence in Old Montreal, now a museum tracing the city's history from pre-contact Indigenous life through the French and British colonial periods. The building itself, dating from 1705, is as much an exhibit as its contents.

**Montreal Biodome** (4777 Avenue Pierre-De Coubertin) recreates four ecosystems of the Americas under one roof -- tropical rainforest, Laurentian maple forest, Gulf of St. Lawrence marine environment, and sub-Antarctic islands complete with penguins. Built inside the 1976 Olympic velodrome. Especially good for families.

**Redpath Museum** (859 Rue Sherbrooke Ouest) is a small, free natural history museum on the McGill University campus. Victorian display cases hold everything from dinosaur bones to Egyptian mummies to mineral specimens. Delightfully old-fashioned.

**Centre d'histoire de Montreal** (335 Place d'Youville) is a compact museum exploring the city's past through everyday stories and oral histories. Less famous than the bigger institutions but worth a visit if you want to understand what Montreal felt like in different eras.

**Cinematheque Quebecoise** (335 Boulevard de Maisonneuve Est) is essential for film lovers -- a museum and screening venue dedicated to the history of cinema, television, and new media, with a focus on Quebec filmmaking.
