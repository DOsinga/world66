title: "Geography"

---

Costa Rica sits on the Central American land bridge between [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua) to the north and [Panama](/northamerica/panama) to the south. It covers roughly 51,000 square kilometers — about the size of West Virginia — and at its widest spans around 270 km. Despite its small size, the terrain is dramatically varied.

A chain of volcanic mountains runs down the center of the country from northwest to southeast, dividing the Pacific and Caribbean slopes. The Central Valley sits at around 1,000 meters elevation and has the most comfortable climate in the country; most of the population lives here. Mount Chirripó, in the Talamanca Range in the south, is the highest peak at 3,821 meters — on a clear day you can see both oceans from the summit.

On the Pacific side, two major peninsulas jut into the ocean: Nicoya in the north (with the Golfo de Nicoya behind it) and Osa in the south (with the Golfo Dulce). The Osa Peninsula contains Corcovado National Park, one of the most biodiverse areas on earth. The northern Pacific lowlands (Guanacaste) are dry and savanna-like; the southern Pacific is wetter and more forested.

The Caribbean coast is flatter and wetter, with a narrow coastal plain and extensive wetlands in the north around Tortuguero — a labyrinth of jungle canals accessible only by boat. The Talamanca mountains back the southern Caribbean coast, where several indigenous communities live.
