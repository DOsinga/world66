Costa Rica is one of the oldest continuous democracies in Latin America, with its first free election in 1889. The current constitution dates from 1949 and is notable for one unusual provision: it permanently abolished the military. The savings were redirected to education and healthcare.

The president, elected to a single four-year term (no immediate re-election), holds executive power and appoints a cabinet. The unicameral Legislative Assembly has 57 members elected for four-year terms. The Supreme Court (*Corte Suprema de Justicia*) comprises 22 magistrates elected by the Assembly.

The Constitutional Chamber of the Supreme Court, known as "La Sala IV," is particularly influential. It rules on the constitutionality of laws and can be petitioned directly by any citizen — a feature that has produced some significant legal reversals over the decades.

Costa Rica's democracy is genuinely functioning, with regular peaceful transfers of power and an independent judiciary. Political culture tends toward moderation rather than extremes.
