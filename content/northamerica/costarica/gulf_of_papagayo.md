The **Golfo de Papagayo** cuts into the northern Pacific coast of Guanacaste province, sheltered enough that its waters stay calm even when the open ocean is rough. That calm, combined with one of Costa Rica's longest dry seasons — sunshine dominates from November through May — has turned the peninsula into the country's most concentrated resort corridor.

The development here is deliberately upscale. The Papagayo Peninsula itself is a government-designated tourism zone where large international resort hotels occupy private stretches of beach. Outside the resort gates, smaller beaches along the gulf's northern and eastern shores are mostly deserted, reached by dirt roads that still discourage casual visitors.

The gulf's real draw beyond the beach is what lies beneath: the warm, nutrient-rich waters around the Islas Murciélagos (Bat Islands), a short boat ride offshore, attract bull sharks, manta rays, and schools of tuna. Dive operators based at the marinas run day trips there regularly. Sport fishing, kayaking the rocky coastline, and boat excursions to secluded coves fill out the water-based options. The closest airport is in Liberia (LIR), about 35 km to the east, which receives direct international flights during high season.
