Heredia sits just ten kilometres north of San José, close enough to the capital that many residents commute daily, but distinct enough in character to feel like its own city. Founded in 1705 and nicknamed the City of Flowers for the gardens that fill its residential streets, Heredia is compact, walkable, and notably calmer than the capital. Its university population — the National University of Costa Rica is based here — gives it a lively café culture and a young, unpretentious energy.

The historic centre is built around the Parque Central, a pleasant square shaded by mature trees. On its eastern edge stands the Iglesia de la Inmaculada Concepción, a colonial church dating to 1797 with walls two metres thick — a precaution that has made it the only large-scale colonial building still standing in the Central Valley after centuries of earthquakes. Its 20 stained glass windows were made in France. Just north of the park, El Fortín is Heredia's most distinctive landmark: a cylindrical stone tower built in 1876 under President Tomás Guardia as part of a military headquarters that was never completed. Its loophole slots face inward rather than outward, a construction error that has become something of a local joke, and it appears on the Costa Rican 500-colón coin.

The Central Valley setting makes Heredia an excellent base. The coffee plantations of the surrounding hills can be visited on half-day tours from the city, and the Barva Volcano — a dormant crater within Braulio Carrillo National Park — is reachable in under an hour. La Paz Waterfall Gardens, with its chain of five falls through cloud forest, is a popular half-day trip. The Sibu Chocolate facility runs tours where visitors can trace cacao from bean to bar using a recipe said to date to the 1500s.

For travellers who want to experience Costa Rica's Central Valley without dealing with San José, Heredia is the more agreeable choice — quieter, safer, and with a colonial centre that rewards an afternoon of wandering.
