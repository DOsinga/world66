Manuel Antonio is a small stretch of the central Pacific coast of Costa Rica, about three hours south of San José, and one of the country's most visited destinations. The national park here — **Parque Nacional Manuel Antonio** — is tiny (just 16 square kilometres, the smallest in Costa Rica's park system) but packs extraordinary biodiversity into a postcard-perfect setting: white-sand beaches backed by dense tropical forest, with monkeys, sloths, iguanas, and toucans visible from the main trails within minutes of entering.

The park has four short, well-marked trails through the forest and along the coast. **Playa Manuel Antonio**, inside the park, is regularly named among the most beautiful beaches in the world — calm turquoise water in a sheltered cove, white sand, and squirrel monkeys in the trees at the edge. The **Cathedral Point** trail leads to a viewpoint with ocean on both sides. Capuchin monkeys are everywhere and bold; do not feed them, and guard your bags. The park limits daily visitors to around 600, so arrive early (it opens at 7am) or book ahead. Closed on Tuesdays.

Outside the park, the road between the village of Quepos and the park entrance is lined with hotels, restaurants, and tour operators. Quepos is the working town — cheaper, less scenic, with a fishing harbour and a good local market. Between the two, the hillside is covered with accommodation at every price point, most with views down through the canopy to the ocean. Activities beyond the park include sportfishing, sea kayaking, whitewater rafting on the Savegre or Naranjo rivers, and canopy zip-line tours.

Come in the dry season (December–April) for the best weather, or in the green season (May–November) when prices drop, the park is quieter, and the forest is at its most lush.
