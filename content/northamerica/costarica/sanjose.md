San José is the capital and beating heart of Costa Rica, sitting in the fertile Central Valley at around 1,100 metres elevation — high enough to enjoy a mild, spring-like climate year-round. It is inevitably a transit hub for travellers moving between the coasts and the highlands, but the city rewards a day or two in its own right.

The centre is walkable and lively. The Plaza de la Cultura is the social nucleus: street performers, shoeshine vendors, and the underground entrance to the extraordinary Gold Museum, which houses the finest pre-Columbian gold collection in Central America. Nearby, the National Theater — funded by 19th-century coffee barons — is one of the most ornate buildings in Central America and offers daily tours. The National Museum, in a bullet-scarred former fortress, is worth a couple of hours.

Beyond the sights, San José is best experienced through its public spaces. The Mercado Central near Avenida Central is a dense, fragrant labyrinth of butchers, spice sellers, and cheap sodas (the local casual restaurants). The city's parks and plazas — particularly Parque Morazán and Parque España — fill with office workers at lunchtime and families in the evenings. Joséfinos are direct and opinionated; conversations about politics, football, and the state of the roads start easily.

The Barrio Amón neighbourhood, northeast of the centre, has the best concentration of Victorian and Art Nouveau architecture from the coffee-boom era, and a growing number of boutique hotels and restaurants occupy its old mansions. La California and Barrio Escalante, east of the centre, are where San José's restaurant and nightlife scene has evolved in recent years.
