Remedios is one of Cuba's oldest and best-preserved colonial towns, tucked away on the north coast of Villa Clara province. Founded in 1514, it is among the first seven cities established by the Spanish on the island, yet it has largely escaped the tourist circuit — which is precisely what makes it worth visiting.

The town centers on a handsome main square, the Plaza Mayor, flanked by two churches facing each other across the cobblestones — an unusual arrangement that hints at Remedios's long history of internal feuds. The streets are quiet, the houses painted in faded pastels, and the pace of life slow enough that you notice things: a rocking chair on a porch, children playing in the dust, the smell of tobacco drifting from a doorway.

Remedios is also famous throughout Cuba for Las Parrandas, a festival held each December that turns the town into a battleground of competing neighborhoods, floats, fireworks, and music. Outside of December it is a place to wander rather than tick off sights, though the Iglesia de San Juan Bautista alone is worth the detour.

The nearby town of Caibarién, 8 km to the east, is the starting point for the 48 km causeway to Cayo Santa María — one of Cuba's finest beach keys. Remedios makes a good base if you want a colonial atmosphere alongside beach access.
