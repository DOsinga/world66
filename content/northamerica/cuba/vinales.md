Viñales and its surrounding valley are among the absolute top attractions of Cuba. Set about 40 km north of [Pinar del Río](/northamerica/cuba/pinardelrio) in the island's westernmost province, it is a place where the landscape alone justifies the journey.

The valley is extraordinary. Flat red-soiled land planted with tobacco and palms is scattered with abrupt rocky hills called mogotes — the eroded remnants of an ancient limestone plateau, eaten away over millions of years until only these steep-sided towers remain. The effect is something like [Cappadocia](/asia/turkey/cappadocia) turned green: every mogote is dense with vegetation, their sheer faces climbing 20 to 30 metres out of the fields.

The village itself is small and unhurried. The colonial main street is lined with low porticoed houses, and the atmosphere is genuinely friendly. People here are used to travelers but not yet exhausted by them. The real draw is getting out into the valley — on horseback, on foot, or simply by sitting somewhere and watching the light change on the mogotes.
