Las Terrenas sits on the north coast of the Samaná Peninsula, a former fishing village that found itself on the map when a road was cut through the mountains in 1946. The French and Italians arrived first, then everyone else. Today it is one of the Dominican Republic's most cosmopolitan small towns — a place where you can eat fresh-caught lobster for lunch and proper French pastries for breakfast.

The appeal is straightforward: long coconut-palm beaches, warm water, and a town that still feels like a town rather than a resort. Playa Las Terrenas stretches west from the village centre, Playa Bonita lies a short drive further along the coast, and Playa Cosón — arguably the best of the three — runs for several unbroken kilometres to the east. The surf is gentle, the sand is golden, and the palm trees lean dramatically over the water.

The town itself is walkable and slightly chaotic. The main road, Calle Duarte, runs parallel to the beach and holds most of the restaurants, shops, and bars. Side streets are unpaved and flood after rain. Motoconchos buzz everywhere. The mix of Dominican, French, Italian, and Haitian residents gives the place an unusual character — more Mediterranean fishing village than Caribbean resort, with merengue playing from the colmados to remind you where you actually are.

Las Terrenas works well as a base for exploring the peninsula. The [Samaná](/northamerica/dominicanrepublic/samana) bay, where humpback whales gather from January to March, is less than an hour away. El Limón waterfall is a half-day trip on horseback. And the town itself is pleasant enough that doing nothing at all for a few days feels like a perfectly good plan.
