El Salvador is Central America's smallest country and its most densely populated, a fact that becomes obvious the moment you start driving. What it lacks in size it more than compensates for in variety: in a single day you can hike the rim of an active volcano, swim at a black-sand Pacific beach, and walk the cobblestone streets of a colonial town.

The country spent the 1980s in a brutal civil war that killed 75,000 people, and a reputation for violence has dogged it since. That reputation is now substantially outdated. A sweeping crackdown on gang activity that began in 2022 has transformed public safety, and El Salvador has become one of the fastest-growing tourist destinations in the region. Travelers who visit tend to be pleasantly surprised.

The Pacific coast draws surfers to the breaks around [La Libertad](/northamerica/elsalvador/lalibertad), while the mountainous interior rewards hikers and coffee lovers alike. [San Salvador](/northamerica/elsalvador/sansalvador) is a chaotic, energetic capital that reflects a country still rebuilding and reimagining itself. [Santa Ana](/northamerica/elsalvador/santaana), the second city, is smaller and calmer, a good base for the western highlands.

The food is cheap and satisfying. Pupusas — thick corn tortillas stuffed with cheese, beans, or chicharrón — are eaten at every meal and cost less than a dollar. Salvadorans are direct, proud of their country, and generally pleased to see visitors. The US dollar is the official currency, which simplifies things for travelers from North America.
