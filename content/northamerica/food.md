---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

North America contains some of the most exciting food cultures on earth, from Mexican cuisine — now recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage — to the barbecue traditions of the American South to the extraordinary diversity that immigration has brought to cities like New York and Toronto.

**Mexico** is the continent's culinary crown. Real Mexican food bears little resemblance to the Tex-Mex versions exported north. The cuisines of Oaxaca, Yucatan, Puebla, and Veracruz are each distinct regional traditions. Oaxacan mole negro takes days to make, with dozens of chiles, chocolate, and spices. Yucatecan cochinita pibil — slow-roasted pork marinated in achiote and citrus — comes from a pre-Columbian tradition. The taco, in its street form, is a perfect food: the trompo al pastor, the carnitas, the barbacoa on Sunday mornings. [Mexico City](/northamerica/mexico/mexicocity) is one of the great dining cities in the world, with everything from decades-old market stalls to internationally recognized fine dining.

**The United States** is more culinarily serious than its reputation suggests. New Orleans has a genuine regional cuisine — gumbo, jambalaya, red beans and rice, beignets — rooted in the intersection of French, Spanish, African, and Native American traditions. The barbecue traditions of the South vary dramatically by region: Texas brisket, Carolina pulled pork, Memphis ribs, each with fierce local partisanship. The Pacific Coast runs on fresh seafood, produce, and the kind of ingredient-led cooking that California essentially invented for the modern world. [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork) has the most diverse restaurant scene on earth, reflecting its immigrant history. The American burger and hot dog, done well, should not be dismissed.

**Canada's** food scene reflects its multicultural cities. [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto) and [Vancouver](/northamerica/canada/britishcolumbia/vancouver) have exceptional Asian food — ramen, dim sum, Korean barbecue — alongside strong indigenous-influenced restaurants that are reintroducing bannock, bison, and wild game to fine dining. Quebec has its own distinct tradition: poutine (fries, cheese curds, gravy) is the local comfort food, but Quebec also has serious French-influenced restaurant cooking and a thriving maple syrup culture. Maritime seafood — lobster rolls, scallops, dulse — is as good as anywhere.

**Central America** doesn't get enough credit. The food is simple but honest. [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala) does extraordinary things with black beans, corn, and chiles. [Costa Rica](/northamerica/costarica)'s rice and beans (gallo pinto) is ubiquitous and done right. [Belize](/northamerica/belize) has a Caribbean coastal tradition of seafood stews and rice dishes with African and Maya roots. [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua)'s vigorón — yuca, chicharrón, and cabbage salad — is street food that rewards a stop. The street food everywhere on the isthmus is the best value eating on the continent.

**Caribbean cuisines** vary enormously by island but share a foundation of rice, beans, seafood, and slow-cooked meat with African and European influences. [Jamaica](/northamerica/jamaica) contributes jerk — meat marinated in scotch bonnet peppers and allspice and cooked over pimento wood — which may be the most internationally influential cooking tradition to come out of the Caribbean. [Trinidad](/northamerica/trinidadandtobago)'s food reflects its Indian heritage: doubles (fried flatbread with curried chickpeas) are the national street food and are extraordinary. Barbadian flying fish and cou-cou is the national dish of [Barbados](/northamerica/barbados). Cuban food is simpler than you might expect — ropa vieja (shredded beef), congri (rice and black beans), lechón asado — but done well it's deeply satisfying.

**Drinks.** Mexico produces mezcal and tequila, both worth exploring seriously — artisanal mezcal from Oaxaca in particular has become a genuine craft spirit. The [Caribbean](/northamerica/thecaribbean) runs on rum, from the aged rums of Barbados and Jamaica to the white rums mixed into Cuba libres and daiquiris. The US and Canada have strong craft beer cultures; the Pacific Northwest and New England are particularly good for local brews. Coffee from [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala) and [Costa Rica](/northamerica/costarica) is genuinely excellent — both countries produce high-altitude arabica that rivals anything from Ethiopia or Colombia.
