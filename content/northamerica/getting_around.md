---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

North America is built around the car. The [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates) has an enormous Interstate highway system that connects every major city, and outside of a handful of urban cores, driving is simply how life works. If you're visiting from Europe or Asia, the distances will shock you — it's a five-hour drive from [Los Angeles](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/losangeles) to [San Francisco](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfranciscobayarea/sanfrancisco), and that's within the same state. Renting a car is essential for most of the US and [Canada](/northamerica/canada), and fuel is cheap by European standards. The Trans-Canada Highway stretches over 7,800 km coast to coast.

[Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) has an excellent long-distance bus network that puts Greyhound to shame. First-class lines like ADO, ETN, and Primera Plus run modern coaches with air conditioning, reclining seats, and onboard entertainment between all major cities. Driving in Mexico is fine on the toll highways (autopistas), which are well maintained but expensive. Free highways are slower and rougher. In cities, Uber works well and is often safer than hailing a random taxi.

Central American buses are an experience unto themselves. In [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), and [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua), the iconic "chicken buses" — retired US school buses painted in wild colors — are the backbone of local transport. They're cheap, crowded, and stop everywhere. For longer distances, shuttles aimed at travelers connect the main tourist routes and are worth the extra cost. Driving the mainland from Mexico through Central America is doable but demanding — road conditions vary wildly, border crossings take time, and you need insurance for each country separately.

Flying is the practical choice for covering big distances. The US and Canada have massive domestic airline networks, and budget carriers like Southwest, Frontier, Spirit, and Flair keep prices competitive if you book ahead. Major hubs like Chicago O'Hare, Atlanta, Dallas-Fort Worth, and [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto) Pearson connect to almost everywhere. From [Mexico City](/northamerica/mexico/mexicocity), Volaris and VivaAerobus offer cheap flights throughout Mexico and Central America. For reaching Central American capitals from the US, airlines like Avianca, Copa, and the US majors run frequent routes through hubs in Miami, Houston, and Panama City.

The Caribbean runs on a mix of inter-island flights and ferries. [LIAT](https://www.liatairline.com/) and Caribbean Airlines connect the smaller islands, though schedules can be unreliable and fares add up fast. Ferries link some island pairs — Sint Maarten to Anguilla, St. Kitts to Nevis, Guadeloupe to Dominica — but there's no island-hopping ferry network like the Greek islands. Within the islands themselves, you'll find everything from shared minivans (called route taxis or "tap-taps" in [Haiti](/northamerica/haiti)) to rental cars, though driving on small islands means narrow roads and often left-hand traffic on the former British colonies.

Trains exist, but they're not a major way to get around. Amtrak covers much of the US, though service is infrequent and often slower than driving. That said, some journeys are genuinely spectacular — the California Zephyr through the Rockies, the Coast Starlight along the Pacific, the Empire Builder across the northern plains. The Northeast Corridor between Boston, [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork), and Washington is the one route where trains actually compete with flying. In Canada, VIA Rail runs useful corridor service between [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal), Ottawa, and [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto). Long-distance buses remain the budget option in the US and Canada — Greyhound and FlixBus are slow but cheap and fine for shorter hops.

[Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) is a different world entirely. There are no roads connecting towns — the ice sheet and rugged terrain make it impossible. You fly between settlements on Air Greenland, or in summer you can take the coastal ferry Sarfaq Ittuk, which is an adventure in itself. Helicopters serve the smallest communities. Don't expect to just drive around; travel here requires planning and flexibility, especially when weather grounds flights.
