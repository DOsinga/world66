---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

North America is built around the car. The [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates) has an enormous Interstate highway system that connects every major city, and outside of a handful of urban cores, driving is simply how life works here. If you're visiting from Europe or Asia, the distances will shock you — it's a five-hour drive from [Los Angeles](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/losangeles) to [San Francisco](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfranciscobayarea/sanfrancisco), and that's within the same state. Renting a car is essential for most of the continent, and fuel is cheap by European standards. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) is the same story, with the Trans-Canada Highway stretching over 7,800 km coast to coast.

Flying is the practical choice for long distances. The US and Canada have massive domestic airline networks, and budget carriers like Southwest, Frontier, Spirit, and Flair keep prices competitive if you book ahead. Major hubs like Chicago O'Hare, Atlanta, Dallas-Fort Worth, and [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto) Pearson connect to almost everywhere. Domestic flights are routine here in a way they aren't in smaller countries — people fly the way Europeans take trains.

Trains exist, but they're not the backbone of travel here. Amtrak covers much of the US, though service is infrequent and often slower than driving on many routes. That said, some Amtrak journeys are genuinely spectacular — the California Zephyr through the Rockies, the Coast Starlight along the Pacific, and the Empire Builder across the northern plains. The Northeast Corridor between Boston, [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork), and Washington is the one route where trains actually compete with flying. In Canada, VIA Rail runs a transcontinental route and useful corridor service between [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal), Ottawa, and [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto).

Long-distance buses are the budget option. Greyhound still operates across both countries, and FlixBus has expanded into North American routes. They're slow but cheap, and perfectly fine for shorter hops between nearby cities. Within major cities, public transit varies wildly — [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork) and [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal) have excellent subway systems, [Chicago](/northamerica/unitedstates/illinois/chicago) and [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto) are solid, but most Sun Belt cities barely have functioning bus networks.

[Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) is a different world entirely. There are no roads connecting towns — the ice sheet and rugged terrain make it impossible. You fly between settlements on Air Greenland, or in summer you can take the coastal ferry Sarfaq Ittuk, which is an adventure in itself. Helicopters serve the smallest communities. Don't expect to just drive around; travel here requires planning and flexibility, especially when weather grounds flights.
