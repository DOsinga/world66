---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Greenlandic food is built around what the land and sea provide: seal, whale, reindeer, musk ox, Arctic char, halibut, capelin, and in summer, berries from the tundra. The collective term for this local food is *kalaalimernit* — literally "Greenlandic food" — and it carries strong cultural weight. Eating local is not a trend here; it is identity.

**Suaasat** is the national dish: a simple soup of boiled seal, whale, or seabird meat, often with onion and rice. It is straightforward and deeply traditional. **Mattak** — raw whale skin with a thin layer of blubber — is an acquired taste but worth trying; it has a mild, slightly nutty flavor and is rich in vitamin C, which historically made it invaluable in a land without vegetables for much of the year.

The **kalaaliaraq** (known as "The Board") operates in towns across Greenland as an open-air meat and fish market. Hunters and fishers bring in the day's catch — seal, reindeer, fish, berries — and sell it directly to buyers. It is one of the most authentic and photogenic aspects of daily life in Greenland. Look for signs or ask at your hotel.

Restaurant options are limited outside of Nuuk and Ilulissat, but most towns have at least one place to eat. Imported food is available in supermarkets, though expensive. Fruit and vegetables are particularly pricey. Dairy products, by contrast, are relatively affordable.

Alcohol is available in restaurants and bars. Prices are high: expect around DKK 50–70 for a beer, DKK 300–400 or more for wine in a restaurant. The minimum age for purchasing alcohol is 18.
