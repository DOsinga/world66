---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Greenland has a population of around 56,000, making it one of the most sparsely inhabited places on earth relative to its size. The majority are Greenlandic Inuit — descended from the Thule people who migrated from what is now Arctic Canada roughly 1,000 years ago. A significant minority are Danish or of mixed Greenlandic-Danish heritage; most Danes came as administrators, teachers, or workers during the 20th century.

The Greenlandic language, Kalaallisut, is the official language and the primary language of the majority. It belongs to the Inuit language family and bears no resemblance to Danish or other European languages. Danish is the second official language and widely understood. The two communities are not always fully integrated — many Danes in Greenland live in a somewhat separate social world — but younger generations increasingly identify as simply Greenlandic regardless of ancestry.

Most Greenlanders are Lutheran Christians, a legacy of 18th-century Danish missionary activity. Religion is present but not dominant in everyday life.

Society is changing fast. The move from small settlements to larger towns has accelerated since the mid-20th century; many traditional skills — dog sledding, kayaking, ice fishing — survive but are no longer universal. Hunting and fishing remain central to identity and to the economy outside urban centers.

The question of independence from Denmark is the central political conversation of this generation. Greenland has had self-governance since 2009 and controls most domestic affairs, but defense and foreign policy remain with Denmark. Economic dependence — Greenland receives a substantial annual block grant from Copenhagen — is the main practical obstacle to full independence, and there is real debate about whether new revenues from tourism and resource extraction can replace it.
