Grande-Terre is the eastern half of Guadeloupe, separated from Basse-Terre by the narrow Rivière Salée. Where Basse-Terre is volcanic, mountainous, and lush, Grande-Terre is flat, dry, and lined with beaches — the two halves of the butterfly-shaped island could hardly be more different.

The south coast from Sainte-Anne to Saint-François has the best beaches: long stretches of white sand backed by sea-grape trees. Plage de la Caravelle and Plage du Bourg are popular and well-served; the Pointe des Châteaux headland at the eastern tip offers dramatic cliffs and a wild Atlantic coastline.

Pointe-à-Pitre, Guadeloupe's economic capital, sits where the two islands meet. The city is noisy and chaotic but has a lively market and the Mémorial ACTe, a striking museum dedicated to the history of slavery in the Caribbean.
