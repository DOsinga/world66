Ceibal (also written El Ceibal or Seibal) is a Maya archaeological site in the Petén jungle, about 60 km south of [Flores](/northamerica/guatemala/flores) along the Río de la Pasión. It is far less visited than Tikal but has exceptional carved monuments.

The Ceibal stelae are among the finest and best-preserved sculptures of the Late Classic Maya period, earning the site its informal designation as a "Maya art gallery." The circular Temple A-3 is unusual in Maya architecture and points to influence from Gulf Coast civilizations in the site's final phase. The last datable stela at Ceibal depicts a figure with distinctly non-Maya (possibly Toltec or Gulf Coast) features, suggesting a dynastic shift around 900 AD.

The site's occupation began around 800 BC, making Ceibal one of the earlier continuously inhabited Maya centers. The jungle setting is dense and the paths between structures are atmospheric.

Getting there: most easily reached by boat along the Rio de la Pasión from the town of Sayaxché, itself about 60 km south of Flores by road. Tour operators in Flores combine Ceibal with visits to other Petén sites.
