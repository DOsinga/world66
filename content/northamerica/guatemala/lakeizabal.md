Lake Izabal is Guatemala's largest lake, a broad expanse of freshwater hemmed in by the Sierra de las Minas to the south and the Santa Cruz range to the north. The lake empties east through the Rio Dulce gorge, one of the most dramatic stretches of waterway in Central America, before reaching the Caribbean at Livingston.

The lake and surrounding wetlands are rich in wildlife. Manatees (fresh-water sea cows) inhabit the lake — one of the few viable freshwater manatee populations remaining. Tarpon, snook, and snapper make it a destination for sport fishermen. Birding in the surrounding forest and mangroves is excellent.

The main base is Río Dulce town, at the eastern end of the lake, accessible by road from Guatemala City or by boat from Livingston. The Castillo de San Felipe, a 17th-century Spanish fort at the narrow passage between the lake and the river, is worth a stop. Several sailing yachts are anchored in the river here, and it is possible to arrange water transport north or south.

The Rio Dulce gorge — the river section between the lake and Livingston — is the highlight: limestone cliffs draped in jungle rise over the water, with birds overhead and small waterfalls emerging from the rock. The only practical way to see it is by boat. Regular lanchas make the 2-hour journey between Río Dulce town and Livingston, passing through the gorge.
