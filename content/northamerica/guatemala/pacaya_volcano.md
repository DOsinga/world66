Standing at 2,552 metres, the highly active Pacaya Volcano is perhaps the most climbed volcano in Guatemala. Its current active phase began in 1965, and it has been almost continuously erupting since — activity ranges from quiet steam vents to explosions powerful enough to hurl bombs 12 kilometres and force the evacuation of surrounding villages.

The easiest way to visit is with a tour operator from [Antigua Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala/antiguaguatemala), where groups depart daily for around $15–20 per person. If you have your own transport, head south from [Guatemala City](/northamerica/guatemala/guatemalacity) on the highway to Escuintla, then turn off toward San Vicente Pacaya and Pacaya Volcano National Park before reaching Palín. The sign is not prominent, so watch carefully.

The gentler trail starts at **San Francisco de Sales**, where you pay admission (around Q50–80 for foreign visitors) and find parking, refreshments, and restrooms. The trail is well maintained and patrolled by *guardabosques* (rangers). It brings you to the rim of the old **Cerro Chino** crater, where the volcanic cones of Acatenango, Fuego, and Agua are visible. From here the final steep ascent begins — it is genuinely difficult, a constant process of two steps up and one sliding back in the ash and loose volcanic rock.

The view from the top is worth every step. Pacaya is an active volcano and two hazards demand respect: avoid the sulphur clouds of smoke, which are extremely uncomfortable to breathe, and watch where you sit, as small active vents emit heat that can burn. The descent is the fun part — you practically ski down the ashy slope.
