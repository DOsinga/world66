title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Guatemala packs an extraordinary range of attractions into a country roughly the size of Tennessee. Mayan ruins, colonial cities, highland volcanoes, jungle lakes, and Caribbean coast all sit within a day's travel of each other.

[Tikal](/northamerica/guatemala/tikal) is the headline. This vast Maya city in the northern jungle has temples rising above the rainforest canopy, and the atmosphere at dawn — howler monkeys and toucans in the trees, mist over the plaza — is unlike anything else in Central America. Plan at least two full days.

[Antigua Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala/antiguaguatemala) is Guatemala's most visited city for good reason. The well-preserved colonial center, framed by three volcanoes, has magnificent ruined churches, a lively central park, and some of the best food and accommodation in the country. It makes an excellent base.

[Lake Atitlán](/northamerica/guatemala/lakeatitlan) is surrounded by volcanoes and ringed with Maya villages, each with a distinct character. Panajachel is the tourist hub; San Juan La Laguna is known for its weavers' cooperatives; San Marcos La Laguna draws a more spiritual crowd. The lake is best explored by lancha, hopping between villages at your own pace.

Chichicastenango hosts Central America's most famous indigenous market every Thursday and Sunday. Beyond the tourist stalls is a genuine commercial and ceremonial hub where Maya traders come from across the western highlands.

[Quetzaltenango](/northamerica/guatemala/quezaltenango) (Xela) is Guatemala's second city and the heartland of K'iche' Maya culture. It is quieter and more working-class than Antigua, with excellent Spanish schools and access to the volcanic landscape of the western highlands, including the active Santa María volcano.

The Pacific coast offers black-sand beaches and one of the best sea turtle nesting sites in the world at [Monterrico](/northamerica/guatemala/monterrico). The Caribbean side has the laid-back Garífuna town of [Livingston](/northamerica/guatemala/livingstone), reachable only by boat, and the spectacular Rio Dulce gorge connecting [Lake Izabal](/northamerica/guatemala/lakeizabal) to the sea.

Smaller ruins worth seeking out include Quiriguá (a UNESCO site with enormous carved stelae, easily combined with a bus journey east) and Iximché, the former Kaqchikel capital near Tecpán, which sees far fewer visitors than Tikal but has a genuine ceremonial significance still observed by local Maya.
