Tikal is the greatest Maya ruin in Guatemala and one of the most impressive archaeological sites anywhere in the Americas. Located in the northern Petén jungle, 65 km from [Flores](/northamerica/guatemala/flores), it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — designated both for its cultural and natural significance.

At its peak between 200 and 900 AD, Tikal was one of the most powerful cities in the Maya world, with a population perhaps exceeding 100,000. The site covers over 550 square kilometres, with more than 3,000 mapped structures in the excavated central area alone. The signature sight is the Great Plaza, flanked by Temple I (the Temple of the Grand Jaguar) and Temple II facing each other across a stone plaza. Temple IV, the tallest structure at 70 metres, rises above the forest canopy — climb to the top at dawn for one of the most remarkable views in Central America, with temples protruding from a carpet of jungle.

The forest within the park is thick with wildlife. Spider monkeys and howler monkeys are guaranteed. Toucans, parrots, oscillated turkeys, and occasionally the great curassow are common. Coatis wander the plazas. Jaguars are present but rarely seen.

**Practicalities:** The park entrance fee is substantial for foreign visitors (around Q150–Q200). Arrive as early as possible — by 06:00 — to see the site before tour buses arrive from Flores mid-morning. The ruins stay open until 18:00, and sunset from Temple IV is almost as good as dawn. At least one full day is needed; two is better. Camping and basic lodges are available inside the park, which allows you to experience the site in the evening quiet.

Getting there: colectivos (shared minivans) and shuttle buses run from Flores/Santa Elena throughout the morning, taking about 1 hour.
