Cap-Haïtien is located on historic ground: Columbus made landfall nearby and celebrated the first Christmas in the New World in 1492. Today it is Haiti's second city, a port town of around 300,000 people with a well-preserved colonial street grid and a more relaxed atmosphere than Port-au-Prince.

The city has long supported a strong community of visual artists — painters, sculptors, and craftspeople whose studios and small galleries dot the old town. Some of Haiti's finest beaches are nearby, particularly Labadie and Cormier Plage to the north. But the main reason to visit Cap-Haïtien is to see the Citadelle Laferrière and the ruins of Sans-Souci Palace at Milot, about an hour's drive to the south — two of the most remarkable historical monuments in the Americas, and together a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Cap-Haïtien has been more accessible than Port-au-Prince during recent years of unrest, and is currently the main entry point for most visitors to Haiti. Its international airport (CAP/MTCH) has remained open when Toussaint Louverture in the capital has not.
