---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

**Carnival (Kanaval).** Held in February or early March, the three days before Ash Wednesday. The biggest celebration in the Haitian calendar. Port-au-Prince's carnival is massive and raucous, centered on competing musical floats (chars) blasting kompa, rara, and zouk. Jacmel's carnival is smaller, more artistic, and internationally famous for its extraordinary handmade papier-mâché masks and costumes — a tradition dating to the 19th century.

**Rara season.** In the weeks between Ash Wednesday and Easter, rara bands take to the countryside. These processional groups — led by bamboo vaksin horns, drums, and a crowd of followers — roam the roads at night, a fusion of Vodou practice, social gathering, and street theater. Rara is most visible in rural areas and is deeply connected to Vodou societies.

**Fet Gede (Guédé Festival).** November 1–2 (All Saints and All Souls Day). The most important Vodou observance in the public calendar. Celebrants dressed in purple and black flood the National Cemetery in Port-au-Prince and cemeteries across the country, making offerings to the Guédé — the spirits of the dead. Baron Samedi, the most famous lwa, is honored here. The atmosphere is both solemn and celebratory; the Guédé are known for their crude humor alongside their power over death.

**St. Jacques (Ogou) Pilgrimage, Plaine du Nord.** In late July, thousands of Vodou devotees travel to Plaine du Nord, near Cap-Haïtien, to bathe in a mud pit sacred to Ogou, the lwa of war and iron. One of the most striking religious gatherings in the hemisphere.

**New Year / Independence Day, January 1.** Haiti's independence day coincides with New Year's Day. Families gather, soup joumou is eaten, and celebrations mark both the new year and the 1804 declaration of independence — still a profound source of national identity.
