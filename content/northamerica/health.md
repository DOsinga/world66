---

Mosquito-borne diseases are the biggest health concern across Central America and the Caribbean. Dengue fever is present throughout the region and cases spike during the rainy season. Zika, while less in the headlines than a few years ago, still circulates. There's no vaccine for either — prevention means covering up at dawn and dusk, using repellent with DEET or picaridin, and sleeping under a net or in air-conditioned rooms when possible. Malaria risk exists in lowland rural areas of [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua), and parts of [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) and [Panama](/northamerica/panama). Talk to a travel doctor about prophylaxis if you're heading to remote areas.

Water safety varies. Tap water is safe to drink in [Costa Rica](/northamerica/costarica) and [Panama](/northamerica/panama) City, but stick to bottled or purified water everywhere else on the mainland. In [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico), even locals drink purified water — every town has purification stations where you can refill bottles cheaply. Ice in tourist restaurants is usually fine; ice from street vendors may not be. The Caribbean islands generally have safe tap water, but check locally. Traveler's diarrhea is common in the first few days — carry rehydration salts and give your stomach time to adjust.

Check vaccination recommendations with your doctor well before departure. Hepatitis A and typhoid vaccines are recommended for most travelers to the region. Hepatitis B is worth considering if you haven't had it. Yellow fever vaccination is required if you're arriving from a country where it's endemic, and some countries may ask for proof. Routine vaccinations — tetanus, measles, and the like — should be up to date. Rabies pre-exposure vaccination makes sense if you plan to spend time in rural areas or around animals.

Altitude is a factor in parts of [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) and [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala). Mexico City sits at 2,240 meters (7,350 feet) — enough to leave you short of breath and headachy for a day or two if you arrive from sea level. Take it easy on arrival, drink plenty of water, and go light on the mezcal for the first night. The highlands of Guatemala around Lake Atitlan and Quetzaltenango are similarly elevated. Most of the Caribbean and coastal Central America is at sea level, so this won't be an issue there.

Sun and heat are easy to underestimate, especially in the Caribbean where a sea breeze masks the intensity of tropical UV. Wear sunscreen, reapply it after swimming, and drink far more water than you think you need. Heat exhaustion can sneak up on you during active days of ruin-climbing or city walking. A hat and a refillable water bottle are non-negotiable. Medical facilities are generally decent in capital cities and tourist areas, but can be basic in rural regions — travel insurance with medical evacuation coverage is well worth the cost.
