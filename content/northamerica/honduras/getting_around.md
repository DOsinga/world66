---
title: "Getting Around"
type: section
---

Honduras is not an easy country to get around quickly, but patient travelers will find it manageable.

**Buses** are the main form of transport. There are three tiers: local chicken buses (slow, cheap, crowded), rapiditos (shared minibuses on local and regional routes), and long-distance ejecutivo or lujo coaches (air-conditioned, faster, and only a little more expensive). On any longer route, the ejecutivo bus is worth it for comfort and safety. Reputable companies include **Hedman Alas** and **Viana Transportes**. Fares are low — typically US$1–2 per hour of travel. Inter-city services are reasonably frequent in the morning but thin out after midday; aim to be at your destination by 4pm.

**Safety on buses:** Local urban buses in San Pedro Sula and Tegucigalpa have a poor security record and should be avoided by tourists. Long-distance buses have been subject to armed robbery on some routes; many now carry a soldier on board. Always travel by day on intercity routes and never on local buses after dark.

**Taxis** are the safest way to get around cities. Always use radio-dispatched taxis rather than flagging one down on the street, and agree on the fare before you get in. Most major hotels can call a reliable cab.

**Ferries** link La Ceiba to Roatán (about 1 hour) and Utila (about 45 minutes). The Galaxy Wave and the Utila Dream run scheduled services. Check departure times carefully — there are only a few crossings per day.

**Car rental** is available in the cities and at the airports. Road conditions vary: the main highways between Tegucigalpa, San Pedro Sula, and La Ceiba are reasonable; elsewhere expect potholes, unmarked speed bumps, and poor lighting. Driving at night is not recommended anywhere in the country.

**Internal flights** connect Tegucigalpa and San Pedro Sula to Roatán, La Ceiba, and a few other destinations. These can be surprisingly affordable and save a lot of time if you're on a short trip.
