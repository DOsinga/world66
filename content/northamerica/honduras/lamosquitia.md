La Mosquitia is the wild eastern corner of Honduras — a vast, roadless expanse of rainforest, wetlands, and coastal lagoons stretching from the Río Plátano to the Nicaraguan border. It is one of the least accessible regions in Central America and one of the most biodiverse places on the continent.

The Río Plátano Biosphere Reserve, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is the centrepiece. Howler monkeys, jaguars, and harpy eagles live in forests that have been continuously inhabited by the Miskito, Pech, and Tawahka peoples for centuries. Getting here typically means a small plane to Brus Laguna or Palacios, followed by boat travel along rivers and lagoons.

This is not casual tourism territory. Infrastructure is minimal, guides are essential, and the region has security concerns related to drug trafficking routes. But for those willing to make the effort, La Mosquitia offers something increasingly rare: genuinely wild country.
