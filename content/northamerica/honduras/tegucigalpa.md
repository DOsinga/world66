Tegucigalpa — known as Tegus — is the capital of Honduras, a city of around 1.4 million people spread across hilly terrain at about 1000m elevation. It is not a beautiful city, but it has more to offer than its reputation suggests, and as a starting point for exploring the south and centre of the country it serves well enough.

The colonial centre around Parque Central has a few worthwhile churches and museums. The Basilica de Suyapa, about 5 km east of the centre, draws pilgrims from across Central America and is one of the most important Catholic shrines in the region. The Parque Nacional La Tigra, a cloud forest reserve about 22 km northeast of the city, is accessible as a day trip and has good birdwatching.

Day trips from the capital include [Comayagua](/northamerica/honduras/comayagua), the old colonial capital about 80 km north. It is well worth an afternoon.

Safety in Tegucigalpa requires real attention. Stick to the main tourist and commercial areas, use radio-dispatched taxis, and avoid walking in unfamiliar neighbourhoods at night. The city has improved significantly in recent years but remains a place where situational awareness matters.
