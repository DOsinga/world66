Cockpit Country is one of the most distinctive landscapes in the Caribbean: a roughly circular area of collapsed limestone karst covering about 1,300 square kilometres in the parishes of Trelawny, St James, St Elizabeth, and Manchester. From the air it looks like a field of inverted bowls — steep forested hills separated by rounded sinkholes called "cockpits." On the ground it's a dense, disorienting maze of forest and rock.

This terrain made Cockpit Country the stronghold of the Leeward Maroons — formerly enslaved Africans who escaped and fought the British to a standstill in the early 18th century. The British were eventually forced to sign a peace treaty in 1739, granting the Maroons autonomy. Their descendants still live in the area; Accompong, the main Maroon town, hosts an annual festival on January 6th celebrating the anniversary of the peace treaty.

The most accessible entry point for visitors is Windsor, in Trelawny parish, where Windsor Cave provides shelter for ten species of bat and several cave-adapted invertebrates. Guides are essential — the cave system is extensive and disorienting. The Windsor Research Centre, run by naturalists, can provide information on the area's remarkable biodiversity.

Cockpit Country has no resort infrastructure. It rewards the adventurous traveller with solitude, extraordinary wildlife, and genuine historical depth.
