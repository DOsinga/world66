Jamaican food is one of the best things about the island. It's bold, spicy, and deeply rooted in the country's African, Asian, and European heritage. Don't limit yourself to hotel buffets.

## Street Food and Jerk

Jerk is Jamaica's defining cooking style: meat — usually chicken or pork — marinated in a mixture of Scotch bonnet peppers, allspice (pimento), thyme, ginger, and garlic, then slow-cooked over pimento wood. The best jerk comes from roadside stands, not restaurants. Boston Bay near Port Antonio is regarded as the home of jerk cooking and worth a detour. Look for stands where locals are eating and the smoke is rising — that's the sign of a real jerk operation.

The **Jamaican patty** — a flaky pastry filled with spiced beef (or chicken, vegetables, or ackee) — is the island's iconic fast food. Juici Patties and Tastee are the main chains and are reliably good. Eat one with a hard dough bread roll (a "coco bread") for the full experience.

## National Dishes

**Ackee and saltfish** is the national dish, typically eaten for breakfast or brunch. Ackee is a fruit that looks and cooks like scrambled eggs when ripe; combined with salted codfish, onions, and tomatoes, it's mild and satisfying. It's only safe to eat when fully ripe — the unripe fruit is toxic — so don't try to pick your own.

**Rice and peas** (cooked with kidney beans and coconut milk) is the staple accompaniment to almost everything. **Escovitch fish** (fried fish in a spiced vinegar pickle) is excellent at coastal towns. **Oxtail** stewed with butter beans is a Sunday staple.

## Drinks

**Red Stripe** is the local beer — light, well-chilled, and ubiquitous. Harder not to drink than to find.

**Ting** is a grapefruit soda made from real Jamaican grapefruit, without preservatives. It doesn't travel well, so drink it on the island.

**Rum** is the obvious local spirit. Appleton Estate is the most respected Jamaican distillery; their aged rums are worth trying neat. The Appleton factory in the Nassau Valley offers tours. Rum punch and rum cocktails are served everywhere; quality varies.

**Blue Mountain coffee** is among the best in the world, grown in the mist-covered mountains east of Kingston. Genuine Blue Mountain coffee is expensive and most of the export is bought by Japan. What's sold cheaply in tourist shops is rarely the real thing — if you want to buy some to take home, buy from a reputable roaster in Kingston.

## Eating Locally

For the cheapest and most authentic eating, look for local cookshops (small roadside restaurants serving rice, protein, and vegetables at very low prices) or food markets. Coronation Market in Kingston and the open-air markets in Ocho Rios have stalls selling prepared food and fresh produce. In tourist zones, prices at restaurants are similar to what you'd pay in Europe or North America.
