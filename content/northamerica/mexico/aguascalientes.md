Aguascalientes is one of Mexico's smallest states and its capital city of the same name is a pleasant, prosperous colonial town that most travellers pass through without stopping. That is a mistake. The historic centre is handsome — arcaded plazas, a baroque cathedral, and the Palacio de Gobierno with its dramatic murals by the Chilean artist Oswaldo Barra Cunningham.

The city is best known for the Feria Nacional de San Marcos, held every April and May. It is one of Mexico's largest and oldest fairs, running since 1828, with bullfights, concerts, rodeos, and enough mezcal to float a small navy. Outside fair season the city is quiet and walkable, with good food markets and thermal baths (the name means "hot waters") fed by underground springs.

The Museo Nacional de la Muerte, dedicated to Mexico's relationship with death, is one of the more unusual museums in the country and worth a visit if the subject does not put you off your lunch.
