When visiting **Ciudad Juarez**, **Budget Travel** will always be available because of the good air travel and highway systems that Mexico has to offer. Here are some budget travel ideas from Ciudad Juarez.

**Last Minute Air Fare Deals**

The Abraham Gonz&aacute;lez International Airport (also known as the Ciudad Juarez Airport, IATA airport code: CJS) is served by four main airlines, including discount airlners Viva Aerobus and Interjet. On a weekly basis, these two companies offer last-minute air-fare deals through their websites and email newsletters. It is a good idea to sign up for their email newsletters before leaving for Ciudad Juarez so that you are able to be up to date on their latest promotions. Interjet offers direct flights to Mexico City (also known as D.F., Distrito Federal, or Ciudad de Mexico) and Viva Aerobus offers direct flights to Guadalajara and Monterrey.

**Driving to Monterrey**

If you already have a rental car or access to a car, it is a good idea to drive to Monterrey because the highway system in northern Mexico is more modern and in much better conditions than most roads in southern Mexico. Take advantage of the more developed roadways and make a road trip from Ciudad Juarez heading south to Chihuahua, then southeast to Torreon, passing through Saltillo and arriving to Monterrey. During your drive to Monterrey, you will have to decide whether to take the paid highway or the free highway. If you take the free highway, you will have to travel at lower speeds and travel will take longer, but you won't have to pay any road tolls. If you take the paid highway, you will travel much faster but the road toll fees quickly add up. Once you arrive to Monterrey, you will experience one of the most modern cities in northern Mexico. This city offers great Mexican style BBQs, amazing shopping centers, nearby natural parks and great night clubs.

**Visiting and Eating at the City Market**

Bargain hunters need to look no further than Ciudad Juarez daily city market, located on the busiest sector of the city. You can get to the market through the city trolley or just jump on a cab. Make sure not to attract unnecessary attention to yourself by not wearing expensive clothes, jewelry or sunglasses, by not carrying large amounts of money, and by not carrying expensive photo and video equipment. The idea is to blend in and enjoy negotiating over quality crafts and great souvenirs to take back home. Be assertive and be prepared to say 'no, than you' a lot. Don't feel bad and be open to negotiation. Haggling over a price is part of Mexican culture and it is a rewarding experience to walk away really "getting your money's worth." Never accept the first price and don't be afraid to ask for a 40 percent discount, it's all part of the experience. There are several local eateries and improvised food stands all around the city market. It's a good idea to eat fried and cooked foods over uncooked ones such as any kind of ceviche. Try as much as possible only to take out bills smaller than 100 pesos (e.g. 20 and 50 peso bills), otherwise vendors will conveniently not have change and you will end up overpaying for things. Avoid using U.S. dollars because you will never be given the exact exchange rate as vendors often use a $1 = 10 pesos exchange rate.
