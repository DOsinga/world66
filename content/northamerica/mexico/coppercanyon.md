Copper Canyon — Barrancas del Cobre in Spanish — is not a single canyon but a vast network of more than 20 interconnected gorges carved through the Sierra Madre Occidental in the northern state of Chihuahua. Together they cover over 20,000 square miles and run deeper than the Grand Canyon, though far fewer people have heard of them.

The region is famous for two things: the El Chepe railway, one of the most spectacular train journeys in the Americas, and the Tarahumara (Rarámuri) people, who have lived in these cliffs and caves for centuries and are renowned for their extraordinary long-distance running ability. You'll encounter them selling crafts at train stops along the route.

The main hub for visitors is the mountain town of Creel, which sits at around 2,300 metres and makes a good base for hikes into the canyon. Los Mochis on the Pacific coast and Chihuahua City are the two ends of the rail line. Most travellers do the journey over two or three days, stopping at Creel or the canyon rim at Divisadero.
