Culiacan is the capital of Sinaloa and a city most travelers pass through rather than linger in. That is understandable — it lacks the colonial charm of nearby Mazatlan or the beach appeal of the Pacific coast — but the city has more going on than its reputation suggests. The food alone is worth a stop. Sinaloa is one of Mexico's agricultural powerhouses, and Culiacan eats exceptionally well as a result.

The city sits in a fertile valley where the Tamazula and Humaya rivers meet to form the Culiacan River. The climate is hot and semi-arid, with scorching summers and mild winters. The historic center retains a few handsome buildings around the cathedral and the main plaza, though much of the city has the sprawling, car-dependent feel common to mid-sized Mexican cities.

Culiacan is also, unavoidably, associated with the Sinaloa drug cartel. The city is safer than headlines suggest for ordinary visitors, but it pays to be aware of your surroundings and avoid ostentatious displays of wealth. Stick to well-traveled areas, especially after dark.

The best reason to visit is the food scene. Culiacan is famous across Mexico for its seafood — aguachile, ceviche, and shrimp prepared every way imaginable. The city also has a surprisingly good art museum and a botanical garden worth an afternoon. Most visitors use it as a base for exploring the Sierra Madre foothills or as a stopover between Mazatlan and Los Mochis.
