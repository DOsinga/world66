Dolores Hidalgo is where Mexican independence began. On the morning of 16 September 1810, the parish priest Miguel Hidalgo rang the bell of the Parroquia and delivered his Grito — the cry for freedom from Spanish rule — to a crowd of largely indigenous villagers. The ragged army that formed that day marched to San Miguel, then Celaya, Salamanca and finally [Guanajuato](/northamerica/mexico/guanajuato), growing to 20,000 men along the way. Hidalgo was eventually captured, executed and beheaded; his head hung for years from a corner of the Alhóndiga de Granaditas in Guanajuato. A colossal statue at the outskirts of town now greets visitors in his honour.

Today the town is known for two things: Talavera ceramics and unusual ice cream. Small factories and shops selling hand-painted ceramic tiles, plates and figurines line the streets — the tradition was started by Padre Hidalgo himself, who taught the craft to his parishioners. The ice cream stands around the main plaza compete to offer the most unlikely flavours: avocado, corn, fried pork skin, tequila, pulque and shrimp, alongside the more conventional options. Trying something improbable is part of the visit.

Dolores Hidalgo sits between [San Miguel de Allende](/northamerica/mexico/sanmigueldeallende) and Guanajuato in the colonial heartland of central [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico), and makes a rewarding stop on a circuit of the region.
