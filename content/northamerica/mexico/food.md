---

Mexican food is a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage, and for good reason: it is one of the world's great cuisines. The version exported globally — burritos, nachos, hard-shell tacos — bears little resemblance to what Mexicans actually eat. Each region has its own distinct traditions shaped by indigenous ingredients, local agriculture, and history.

**Street food** is how most Mexicans eat, and it is some of the best food in the country. Tacos al pastor (pork marinated in dried chiles and achiote, shaved from a vertical spit), tacos de canasta (basket tacos, braised fillings in soft tortillas), tlayudas in Oaxaca (large crispy tortillas with beans, cheese, and meat), elotes (corn on the cob with mayonnaise, chili, and lime), tamales, quesadillas — these are all staples best eaten at busy street stalls or markets.

**Regional highlights:**

*Oaxaca* is the country's most celebrated food destination. The famous seven moles are complex, multi-ingredient sauces served over meat or poultry; mole negro, made with chocolate, chiles, and dozens of spices, is the signature. Tlayudas, memelas, and chapulines (toasted grasshoppers with lime and salt) are worth seeking out. Oaxacan cheese (quesillo) and mezcal complete the picture.

*Puebla* gave Mexico mole poblano and chiles en nogada (a roasted poblano pepper stuffed with minced meat and fruit, covered in walnut cream sauce, garnished with pomegranate seeds — the colors of the Mexican flag). The cemita sandwich is the city's great street food.

*The Yucatan* has a cuisine rooted in Maya tradition. Cochinita pibil — pork slow-roasted in a pit with achiote and bitter orange — is the signature dish, served in tacos or tortas with pickled red onion. Sopa de lima (lime broth with fried tortilla strips) and panuchos (fried tortillas stuffed with black beans) are local staples.

*Northern Mexico* is cattle country. Carne asada — charcoal-grilled beef, often served with flour tortillas — is the dominant tradition. Cabrito (roasted kid goat) is the specialty of Monterrey. The north also has excellent machaca (dried, shredded beef) and burritos in the form Mexicans recognize: large flour tortillas with simple fillings.

*Veracruz* blends indigenous, Spanish, and African influences. Huachinango a la veracruzana (red snapper in a tomato, olive, and caper sauce) is the classic. The port city's café de olla and fresh coffee reflect the region's coffee-growing history.

**Drink**

Mezcal and tequila are both made from agave; tequila is a specific type of mezcal produced from blue agave in a defined region of Jalisco. Oaxaca is the heart of artisanal mezcal production — small distilleries (palenques) use traditional methods and native agave varieties. Mezcal is meant to be sipped, not slammed.

Mexican beer is ubiquitous. Local lagers like Corona, Modelo, and Pacifico are what the country drinks with food. Craft beer has grown dramatically in the past decade, with breweries in [Guadalajara](/northamerica/mexico/guadalajara), Mexico City, and [Tijuana](/northamerica/mexico/tijuana) leading the scene.

Agua fresca — fresh fruit juice diluted with water and sweetened lightly — is the everyday cold drink. Horchata (rice milk with cinnamon) and jamaica (hibiscus flower) are the classic flavors. Café de olla (coffee brewed with cinnamon and piloncillo in a clay pot) is how the country takes its coffee in traditional settings.
