Guadalajara is Mexico's second city and the capital of Jalisco -- the birthplace of mariachi music, tequila, and the wide-brimmed charro hat. It wears its cultural identity proudly, and visitors notice right away: this is a city that feels deeply, unapologetically Mexican.

The historic centre is built around a cross of four plazas radiating from the twin-spired Cathedral, a structure started in 1561 and rebuilt so many times it mixes Gothic, Baroque, Moorish, and Neoclassical styles. Walk south to the Hospicio Cabanas, a former orphanage turned cultural centre where Jose Clemente Orozco's murals -- especially the fiery "Man of Fire" on the chapel dome -- are worth the trip alone. The building is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Beyond the centre, Guadalajara sprawls. Tlaquepaque, once a separate village, is now absorbed into the metro area but keeps its cobblestoned streets and artisan workshops turning out hand-blown glass and Talavera pottery. Zapopan has its own basilica and a quieter pace. The Colonia Americana neighbourhood, centred on Avenida Chapultepec, is where you will find the best cafes, bookshops, and street life.

The climate is near-perfect: warm days, cool nights, and a rainy season from June to October that greens everything up without washing out your plans. Come in October for the Fiestas de Octubre or late August for the International Mariachi Festival, when the plazas fill with competing troupes and the whole city seems to be singing.
