Guaymas is a working port city on the Sea of Cortez in the state of Sonora, northwestern Mexico. Most visitors pass through on the way to the ferry to Santa Rosalia on the Baja California peninsula, but those who linger find a city with genuine character — a natural harbour framed by dramatic volcanic mountains, a colonial streetscape along Serdan Street, and some of the richest diving waters in the world just offshore.

The Sea of Cortez, which Jacques Cousteau famously called "the aquarium of the world," reaches its peak for diving between August and the cooler winter months, with hammerhead sharks, sea lions, manta rays, and an extraordinary variety of fish. The nearby town of San Carlos, about 20 kilometres to the northwest, has become a resort hub with marinas, crescent beaches, and water sports facilities. Guaymas itself is less polished but more authentic — the Malecón waterfront at sunset, with the mountains turning pink behind the bay, is genuinely beautiful.

Serdan Street preserves the most interesting architecture: the old Municipal Palace, the Banco de México, the Church of San Fernando, and the decommissioned Carcel Municipal — a fortress-like jail with dome towers that looks more like a castle than a prison. The Statue of the Fisherman on the Malecón, inscribed with a local song about fishermen sailing to heaven in the boat of Guaymas, captures the city's relationship with the sea.

The best time to visit is during the Carnival in February (roughly timed with Mardi Gras), when the city puts on one of Mexico's better small-city carnivals: floats, fireworks over the bay, folk music, and mariachi in the plazas. Winter and spring (October to May) are the most comfortable months; summer temperatures frequently top 40°C.
