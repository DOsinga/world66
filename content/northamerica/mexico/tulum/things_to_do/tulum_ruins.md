The walled Maya city of Tulum is the most dramatically sited archaeological site on the Yucatán Peninsula. Built on a limestone cliff above a small cove, its temples look out directly over the Caribbean — a view that has barely changed since the city was at its height between the 13th and 15th centuries. El Castillo, the main pyramid, crowns the cliff and served as a lighthouse, guiding canoes through a gap in the reef below. The Temple of the Frescoes preserves some of the best-surviving Maya murals in the region, with intricate figures painted in blue and black that show connections to the Paris Codex. Come as early as possible — the site opens at 8am and the first tour buses from Cancún typically arrive by 10am.

story: >
  The small opening in the reef directly below El Castillo is no accident. Ancient Maya mariners navigated by aligning the light from two windows in the temple's upper chamber — creating one of the earliest known lighthouse systems in the Americas.
