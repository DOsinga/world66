---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

The single most compelling reason to visit Montserrat is Plymouth, the abandoned capital buried under volcanic ash and now accessible only with a licensed guide. Walking the edge of the old city — past church spires poking above ash fields, rooftops half-submerged in grey debris — is unlike anything else in the Caribbean. Zone V, closest to the volcano, is off-limits to the public. Parts of Zone A can be visited on guided tours; your guide will take you to viewpoints and into streets where buildings still stand partially intact. It is sobering and unforgettable.

The Montserrat Volcano Observatory (MVO) is worth a visit for context. Staff explain the science of what happened and what the monitoring equipment tracks today. Panoramic views of the exclusion zone from the Observatory are some of the best on the island.

Garibaldi Hill and St George's Hill both offer dramatic elevated views of the volcano, Plymouth, and the surrounding seas. St George's Hill was closed for decades and has recently reopened under controlled access — check current conditions before planning a visit.

Away from the volcano, the island has good hiking. The walk to Rendezvous Bay, the only white-sand beach, takes about an hour from Little Bay along a coastal trail with strong views. Most other beaches on the accessible northern coast have dark volcanic sand. Sea turtles — Green, Leatherback, and Hawksbill — nest on island beaches between May and December.

The Montserrat National Museum in Little Bay covers the island's history from pre-Columbian times to the present, including the volcanic crisis and its aftermath. Small but worthwhile.
