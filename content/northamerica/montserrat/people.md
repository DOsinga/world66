---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Montserrat's population is around 5,000, a fraction of the 12,000 who lived here before the 1997 eruptions. Most inhabitants are descended from enslaved West Africans brought to the island during the plantation era. A significant minority has Irish heritage — Irish settlers arrived from the 1630s onward and at their peak made up the majority of the European population. The result is a culture that blends African and Irish threads in ways found nowhere else: surnames like O'Brien and Sweeney, a faint Irish lilt in the local accent, and a St Patrick's Day celebration that serves as a commemoration of African resistance rather than a tribute to the saint.

The island was a British colony and remains a British Overseas Territory, so English is the language and British institutions — courts, police, a governor — are part of daily life. The elected government handles most internal affairs.

The volcanic crisis of 1995–1997 shaped everything about contemporary Montserrat. Two-thirds of the population left, many to the UK, USA, and other Caribbean islands. Some have returned; many have not. The people who stayed or came back are understandably proud of what they have rebuilt, and most visitors find them welcoming and straightforward.

The island's most famous cultural export is music. Montserrat had an outsized influence on the global music industry in the 1980s through AIR Studios, a recording studio opened by George Martin (producer of the Beatles) in 1979. The Rolling Stones, Paul McCartney, Elton John, and many others recorded here before the studio was destroyed by Hurricane Hugo in 1989.
