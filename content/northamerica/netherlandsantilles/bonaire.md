Bonaire is a small, flat island in the southern Caribbean, part of the ABC islands alongside Aruba and Curaçao. It sits just 80km north of Venezuela and well outside the hurricane belt, which keeps its waters calm and its skies reliably clear. The island became a special municipality of the Netherlands in 2010 — technically the closest thing to Dutch soil in the western hemisphere.

What draws most visitors is the reef. Bonaire's coral is among the healthiest in the Caribbean, and the entire coastline is a marine park. You can wade in from the beach with a tank and be on the wall within minutes — no boat required for most sites. Divers have been making pilgrimages here since the 1970s, and the dive infrastructure shows it: every dive shop is efficient, the sites are well-marked, and locals treat the reef as something to be proud of.

Away from the water, Bonaire is quiet and unpretentious. Kralendijk, the capital, is a small port town of pastel-painted buildings and open-air restaurants. The north of the island is a national park of cactus scrub and flamingo breeding grounds. In the south, old salt pans still operate, and pink flamingos wade in the shallows alongside the solar evaporation pools.
