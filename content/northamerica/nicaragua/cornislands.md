Two small islands floating in the Caribbean, 70 kilometres off Nicaragua's Atlantic coast — the Corn Islands are among the most rewarding places in Central America that most people have never heard of. Big Corn and Little Corn together offer white sand beaches, healthy coral reefs, and an Afro-Caribbean culture that feels entirely distinct from the rest of Nicaragua.

Big Corn is the larger island: it has a proper airport, paved roads, ATMs, and a range of places to eat and sleep. It moves at a relaxed Caribbean pace but it is recognisably a working community with fishing boats, a hospital, and a supermarket. Little Corn, reached by a short but sometimes rough panga ride across the channel, has none of that — no cars, no paved roads, no ATMs. What it does have is some of the finest diving in the Caribbean, beaches where you can go a full morning without seeing another tourist, and an unhurried rhythm that is genuinely difficult to leave.

The islands were historically settled by Afro-Caribbean and Creole communities, many descended from Jamaican migrants. English-based Creole is widely spoken alongside Spanish, and the cultural identity here — the music, the food, the pace — leans more toward the Caribbean than toward Managua.
