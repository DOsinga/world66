El Castillo is a tiny riverside village on the Río San Juan in southeastern [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua), accessible only by boat. The journey itself is half the appeal — you take a panga from San Carlos at the eastern end of Lake Nicaragua and travel several hours downstream through dense tropical forest, past caiman-studded banks and howler monkey territory. There are no roads here, and the isolation is part of the charm.

The village is named for the Fortaleza de la Inmaculada Concepción, a 17th-century Spanish fortress built on a bluff above the river to stop pirates from reaching Granada via the waterway. The restored fort houses a small museum and offers sweeping views of the river and jungle. It is one of the most atmospheric colonial ruins in Central America, and the setting — surrounded by nothing but green — makes it feel genuinely remote.

There is not much to El Castillo beyond the fort, a handful of simple guesthouses, and a couple of riverside restaurants serving fresh fish. But that is exactly the point. You can arrange guided hikes into the Indio Maíz Biological Reserve across the river, one of the largest tracts of primary rainforest in Central America, home to jaguars, tapirs, and hundreds of bird species. Bring insect repellent, rain gear, and a good book for the hammock.
