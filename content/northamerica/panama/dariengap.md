The Darién Gap is the 100-km stretch of jungle, swamp, and mountain that interrupts the Pan-American Highway between Panama and Colombia — the only break in a road system that otherwise runs continuously from Alaska to Tierra del Fuego. No road has been built through it, and no serious proposal to build one has succeeded. The reasons are practical (extraordinary terrain and cost), environmental (the Gap is protected as Darién National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage Site), and epidemiological (the Gap has historically served as a barrier preventing foot-and-mouth disease from spreading north from South America into Central and North America).

The national park covers nearly 6,000 square kilometres of one of the most biologically diverse regions on earth. More than 900 bird species have been recorded, including the harpy eagle, Panama's national bird, which is reliably seen in the Pirre cloud forest. Jaguars, tapirs, howler monkeys, and giant anteaters live here in numbers that have long since disappeared from most of their former range. Two indigenous groups — the Emberá-Wounaan and the Guna — maintain communities within the park and along its borders, reachable by canoe.

Getting into the Darién requires accepting real effort and real conditions. From Panama City, the road runs east to Metetí and then to Yaviza — the literal end of the highway, a small town at the edge of the jungle where the pavement simply stops. Everything beyond Yaviza moves by dugout canoe or on foot. La Palma, the provincial capital on the Tuira River estuary, is accessible by small aircraft and serves as a base for boat trips into the park. The security situation in the Gap — which has historically seen activity by armed groups from Colombia — changes, and travellers should check current conditions seriously before planning anything remote.

For those who want to cross to Colombia, the maritime route is more practical and safer than overland: water taxis connect Puerto Obaldía (Panama) to Capurgana (Colombia), a small village with beaches and snorkelling, from where onward travel into Colombia is possible. The overland crossing — the romantic ambition of every motorcyclist attempting the full Pan-American route — is genuinely dangerous and should not be attempted without expert guides and up-to-date security intelligence.
