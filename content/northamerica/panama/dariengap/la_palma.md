La Palma is the administrative capital of Darién Province, sitting on a narrow peninsula where the Tuira River meets the Gulf of San Miguel. It's a small, functional town — the largest settlement in the Darién and the last point with reliable services before you head deeper into one of the most remote and ecologically rich regions in the Americas.

For travellers heading into the Darién, La Palma is where you sort out last-minute supplies, arrange permits for Darién National Park, and negotiate with boatmen for water-taxi transport upriver or along the coast. The town itself has basic accommodation, a market, and government offices. The Gulf of San Miguel outside the town is wide and dramatic, and the mangroves lining the rivers are worth exploring if you have time before moving on.

The Emberá and Wounaan indigenous communities are the main reason people pass through the Darién; La Palma is often the point of departure for river journeys to their villages. The experience — dugout canoe, dense forest, traditional life — is among the most striking in Central America, though it requires advance planning and ideally a local guide.
