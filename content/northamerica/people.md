---

Central America and the Caribbean are among the most ethnically and culturally diverse regions on earth. Centuries of indigenous civilization, European colonization, the African slave trade, and waves of immigration from Asia and the Middle East have produced societies where multiple traditions live side by side. The mix varies dramatically from country to country and even town to town. What unites the region is a warmth toward visitors and a deep pride in local identity.

Indigenous Maya peoples are very much alive and thriving, particularly in [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala) and the highlands of southern [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico). In Guatemala, more than forty percent of the population is indigenous, speaking over twenty Maya languages. Visit the highland towns around Lake Atitlan or the markets of Chichicastenango and you'll see traditional textiles, ceremonies, and customs that trace directly back to pre-Columbian times. In Mexico's Yucatan, Chiapas, and Oaxaca, indigenous communities maintain distinct languages and cultural practices. [Belize](/northamerica/belize) has Mopan and Q'eqchi' Maya communities in its southern districts, and the Kuna (Guna) people of [Panama](/northamerica/panama) govern their own territory in the San Blas Islands.

The Garifuna people are one of the most distinctive cultures in the region. Descended from West African and indigenous Carib and Arawak peoples, the Garifuna live along the Caribbean coast of [Belize](/northamerica/belize), [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), and [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua). Their language, music, and dance traditions — especially punta — were recognized by UNESCO as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity. Garifuna communities like Livingston in Guatemala and Hopkins in Belize are well worth visiting for their food, drumming, and the chance to experience a culture unlike any other in the Americas.

Caribbean island societies reflect their colonial histories in complex ways. [Jamaica](/northamerica/jamaica), [Trinidad and Tobago](/northamerica/trinidadandtobago), [Barbados](/northamerica/barbados), and much of the English-speaking Caribbean have populations of predominantly African descent, with cultural traditions rooted in the slave era and transformed into something entirely new — reggae, calypso, soca, and carnival all came from this history. Trinidad has a large population of Indian descent, brought as indentured laborers in the nineteenth century, creating a unique Hindu-Caribbean culture. The French Caribbean islands carry their own Creole traditions. The Dutch ABC islands blend African, Indigenous, and European influences. [Cuba](/northamerica/cuba) and the [Dominican Republic](/northamerica/dominicanrepublic) are deeply shaped by the intersection of Spanish and African heritage, producing musical traditions — son, merengue, bachata — that have conquered the world.

Religion across the region is predominantly Catholic on the mainland, a legacy of Spanish colonization, but practiced in ways that often blend Christian and indigenous or African traditions. Evangelical Protestantism has grown significantly in recent decades, especially in [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala) and [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras). In the Caribbean, you'll find everything from Anglicanism to Rastafari to Santeria to Hinduism. Music and dance are not just entertainment here — they are the primary vehicles of cultural expression, community identity, and resistance. Whether it's a Maya marimba ensemble, a Garifuna drum circle, a Cuban son band, or a Trinidadian steelpan orchestra, you're never far from music that tells you exactly where you are.
