title: "People"

---

North America is one of the most ethnically and culturally diverse regions on earth. Centuries of indigenous civilization, European colonization, the African slave trade, and successive waves of immigration from every part of the world have produced societies where multiple traditions live side by side. The mix varies dramatically from country to country and even town to town — a Guatemalan highland market town, a Canadian prairie city, a Caribbean island, and a US suburb might all be on the same continent, but they feel like different worlds.

The [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates) has long styled itself a "nation of immigrants," and the phrase rings true even if the reality has always been more contested. Its population of around 340 million is predominantly white European in origin, but Hispanic and Latino Americans make up nearly 20 percent of the population and are the fastest-growing group. Black Americans, whose ancestors were brought as slaves, have shaped American culture — music, language, food, politics — far beyond their numbers. Asian Americans are the fastest-growing immigrant community in recent decades. Indigenous peoples were here long before any of them: hundreds of tribal nations with distinct languages and traditions survive, though often in reduced and difficult circumstances. The US is also extremely regional — a Texan, a New Yorker, and an Appalachian have visibly different cultures despite sharing a passport.

[Canada](/northamerica/canada) has embraced multiculturalism as a conscious policy, with high immigration levels that have made [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto) and [Vancouver](/northamerica/canada/britishcolumbia/vancouver) two of the most diverse cities in the world. French-speaking Quebec is culturally distinct — fiercely protective of its language and identity — while Indigenous peoples across the country have won increasing recognition of treaty rights and self-governance. The relationship between settler Canada and First Nations, Métis, and Inuit peoples remains a defining and unresolved national question.

[Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) is predominantly mestizo — of mixed indigenous and Spanish heritage — but indigenous identity runs deep, particularly in Oaxaca, Chiapas, and the Yucatan. Over sixty indigenous languages are officially recognized. Indigenous Maya peoples are very much alive and thriving, particularly in [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala) and the highlands of southern Mexico. In Guatemala, more than forty percent of the population is indigenous, speaking over twenty Maya languages. The highland markets of Chichicastenango and the towns around Lake Atitlan give you a direct connection to pre-Columbian traditions that have never been erased.

The Garifuna people are one of the most distinctive cultures in the region. Descended from West African and indigenous Carib and Arawak peoples, the Garifuna live along the Caribbean coast of [Belize](/northamerica/belize), [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), and [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua). Their language, music, and dance traditions — especially punta — were recognized by UNESCO as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity. Garifuna communities like Livingston in Guatemala and Hopkins in Belize are well worth visiting.

Caribbean island societies reflect their colonial histories in complex ways. [Jamaica](/northamerica/jamaica), [Trinidad and Tobago](/northamerica/trinidadandtobago), [Barbados](/northamerica/barbados), and much of the English-speaking Caribbean have populations of predominantly African descent, with cultural traditions rooted in the slave era and transformed into something entirely new — reggae, calypso, soca, and carnival all came from this history. Trinidad has a large population of Indian descent, brought as indentured laborers in the nineteenth century, creating a unique Hindu-Caribbean culture. [Cuba](/northamerica/cuba) and the [Dominican Republic](/northamerica/dominicanrepublic) are deeply shaped by the intersection of Spanish and African heritage, producing musical traditions — son, merengue, bachata — that have shaped popular music worldwide.

Religion across the continent is predominantly Christian in various forms, but practiced in ways shaped by local history. Evangelical Protestantism has grown rapidly in recent decades in Central America. In the Caribbean you'll find everything from Anglicanism to Rastafari to Santeria to Hinduism. Music and dance are not just entertainment here — they are vehicles of cultural identity and resistance. Whether it's a Maya marimba ensemble, a Garifuna drum circle, a Cuban son band, or a Trinidadian steelpan orchestra, you're never far from music that tells you exactly where you are.
