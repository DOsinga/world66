Visa requirements vary widely across the continent. Most European, Australian, and Japanese travelers can enter the [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates) without a visa through the ESTA program — apply online at least 72 hours before departure, pay $21, and get authorization valid for two years. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) has a similar system called the eTA (CAD $7, usually approved within minutes). Both countries are straightforward for visitors from wealthy nations, but the US border can be intimidating — agents ask pointed questions and have broad discretion. Answer honestly, have your accommodation details handy, and don't joke around. [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) issues a free tourist permit (FMM) on arrival for most nationalities, valid for up to 180 days. [Guatemala](/northamerica/guatemala), [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras), [El Salvador](/northamerica/elsalvador), and [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua) share the CA-4 agreement — one visa covers all four countries for 90 days. [Cuba](/northamerica/cuba) requires a tourist card, available from the airline or embassy. Most Caribbean islands allow visa-free entry for short stays, though requirements differ island to island — check before you go. [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) follows Danish visa rules, so Schengen visa holders are covered.

The US dollar is the dominant currency across the continent — not just in the [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates), but widely accepted in [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico), Central America, and much of the Caribbean. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) uses the Canadian dollar (typically worth 20-30% less than USD). Mexico's peso is stable and widely used; you'll get better prices paying in pesos than in dollars. Central American currencies vary — Guatemala's quetzal, Honduras's lempira, Costa Rica's colón — but US dollars work in tourist areas everywhere. [Belize](/northamerica/belize) and [Panama](/northamerica/panama) essentially use the US dollar alongside their own currencies. Credit and debit cards work virtually everywhere in the US and Canada, and contactless payments are standard. In Central America and the Caribbean, carry cash for smaller businesses and rural areas.

Tipping is deeply embedded in [US](/northamerica/unitedstates) culture and is not optional: 18-20% at restaurants, $1-2 per drink at bars, 15-20% for taxis. Service workers depend on tips for their income. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) tips similarly though slightly less aggressively. In Mexico and Central America, 10-15% at restaurants is standard and appreciated. In the Caribbean, check whether service is included on the bill — at many resort restaurants it already is. In [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland), tipping is not expected.

English dominates in the US and Canada, but North America is far more multilingual than visitors expect. In Quebec, French is the primary language — [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal) is genuinely bilingual, but in smaller Quebec towns you'll want at least basic French. Spanish is the main language from [Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) through Central America, and increasingly a co-language across the US Southwest, Florida, and [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork). In the Caribbean, you'll hear English, Spanish, French, Dutch, Haitian Creole, and various patois depending on which island you're on — sometimes several on the same island. In [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland), Kalaallisut is the main language, though Danish is widely understood. Learning even a few phrases of the local language makes a noticeable difference to how people receive you, especially in Latin America.

Safety varies enormously. The US and Canada are generally safe for travelers, though the US has its own realities — gun ownership is widespread, and urban crime varies dramatically by neighborhood. Ask locals where not to wander at night. Mexico is safe in most tourist areas but has regions with serious cartel violence — stick to well-traveled corridors and check current advisories. Central America ranges from [Costa Rica](/northamerica/costarica) (quite safe) to parts of [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras) and [El Salvador](/northamerica/elsalvador) where caution is warranted. The Caribbean is mostly relaxed, but petty theft and opportunistic crime exist, particularly in port cities. Across the whole continent, wilderness hazards are serious: bears in the Rockies, extreme heat in the deserts, tropical diseases in lowland Central America, and riptides on Pacific beaches everywhere. Don't underestimate distances or weather in remote areas.

Cell coverage is excellent in populated areas across the US and Canada, and free Wi-Fi is available at most cafes, hotels, and libraries. Once you leave cities and highways, coverage drops fast — large parts of the Mountain West, northern Canada, and Alaska have no signal at all. Mexico and Costa Rica have decent coverage in tourist areas; elsewhere in Central America, expect spotty service outside cities. Caribbean islands generally have coverage near towns but data can be pricey on roaming. If you're doing a road trip through rural areas anywhere on the continent, download offline maps. Prepaid SIM cards are easy to get at airports throughout the region. [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) has limited mobile coverage, mostly around towns.

North America uses 120V electricity with Type A/B plugs (two flat prongs) — this applies across the US, Canada, Mexico, Central America, and most of the Caribbean. If you're coming from Europe, Asia, or Australia, you'll need an adapter. Most modern phone chargers and laptops handle both 120V and 220-240V automatically — check the label on your charger before you travel. A few Caribbean islands that were French or Dutch colonies may occasionally have European-style outlets, but this is increasingly rare.
