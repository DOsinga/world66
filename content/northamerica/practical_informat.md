---
title: "Practical Information"
type: section
---

Most European, Australian, and Japanese travelers can enter the [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates) without a visa through the ESTA program — you apply online at least 72 hours before departure, pay $21, and get authorization valid for two years. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) has a similar system called the eTA, which costs CAD $7 and is usually approved within minutes. Both countries are straightforward for visitors from wealthy nations, but the US border can be intimidating — agents ask pointed questions and have broad discretion. Answer honestly, have your accommodation details handy, and don't joke around. [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) follows Danish visa rules, so Schengen visa holders are covered.

The US runs on the dollar (USD) and Canada on the Canadian dollar (CAD), which is typically worth 20-30% less. Credit and debit cards work virtually everywhere in both countries — many places have gone cashless entirely. Contactless payments are standard. Tipping is deeply embedded in [US](/northamerica/unitedstates) culture and not optional: 18-20% at restaurants, $1-2 per drink at bars, 15-20% for taxis. Service workers depend on tips for their income. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) tips similarly, though slightly less aggressively. In [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland), tipping is not expected.

English dominates, but North America is more multilingual than many visitors expect. In [Canada](/northamerica/canada), French is the primary language in Quebec — [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal) is genuinely bilingual, but in smaller Quebec towns you'll want at least basic French. Across the [US](/northamerica/unitedstates), Spanish is increasingly a co-language, especially in the Southwest, Florida, and [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork). In [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland), Kalaallisut is the main language, though Danish is widely understood.

The [United States](/northamerica/unitedstates) is generally safe for travelers, but some realities are worth knowing. Gun ownership is widespread, and mass shooting events, while statistically rare for any individual, are a real feature of American life. Urban crime varies enormously by neighborhood — ask locals where not to wander, especially at night. [Canada](/northamerica/canada) has significantly lower violent crime rates. In both countries, wilderness hazards are serious: bears in the Rockies and Pacific Northwest, extreme heat in the Southwest, and hypothermia anywhere in winter. Don't underestimate distances or weather in remote areas.

Cell coverage is excellent in populated areas across both the [US](/northamerica/unitedstates) and [Canada](/northamerica/canada), and free Wi-Fi is available at most cafes, hotels, and libraries. Once you leave cities and highways, coverage drops off fast — large parts of the Mountain West, northern Canada, and Alaska have no signal at all. If you're doing a road trip through rural areas, download offline maps. Prepaid SIM cards from T-Mobile, AT&T, or Canadian carriers are easy to get at airport shops. [Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) has limited mobile coverage, mostly around towns, and data can be expensive.

North America uses 120V electricity with Type A/B plugs (two flat prongs). If you're coming from Europe, Asia, or Australia, you'll need an adapter and possibly a voltage converter for older appliances, though most modern phone chargers and laptops handle both voltages automatically. Check the label on your charger before you travel.
