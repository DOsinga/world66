---
title: "Food and Drink"
type: section
---

Puerto Rican cuisine — *cocina criolla* — blends Taíno, Spanish, and African influences into something distinct and satisfying. The food is built around plantains, pork, rice, beans, and fresh seafood, seasoned with *adobo* (garlic, oregano, and vinegar) and *sofrito* (a sauce base of peppers, onions, garlic, and herbs).

**What to eat**

*Mofongo* is the island's most iconic dish: green plantains fried and then mashed with garlic and pork crackling, shaped into a ball or cup and typically stuffed with stewed chicken, seafood, or vegetables. It's heavy and delicious. Every restaurant has its version; quality varies enormously.

*Arroz con gandules* — rice cooked with pigeon peas in sofrito and seasoned with *sazón* — is Puerto Rico's unofficial national dish. Usually served alongside *lechón asado*, slow-roasted whole pig cooked over an open wood fire. The lechón corridor along Route 184 near Guavate in the mountains is the pilgrimage destination, especially on weekends.

*Tostones* (twice-fried green plantain slices) appear as a side dish everywhere and are usually excellent. *Amarillos* (fried ripe sweet plantains) are softer and sweeter. *Pasteles* — masa dough filled with seasoned pork and wrapped in plantain leaf — are traditionally a Christmas food but found year-round in good *fondas*.

Street food is outstanding. *Bacalaitos* (salt cod fritters), *alcapurrias* (plantain-and-yuca fritters filled with ground beef), and *empanadillas* are all sold at beach kiosks and roadside stands. The kiosk strip at Luquillo beach, east of San Juan, is the best-known spot to eat this way — over a dozen food stalls, cheap prices, usually with live music.

**Drinking**

Puerto Rico is where rum was perfected. Bacardí is the famous name, but local distilleries like Don Q and Ron del Barrilito produce excellent rums worth seeking out. The *piña colada* was invented in San Juan — the Caribe Hilton and Barrachina restaurant in Old San Juan both claim the original, depending on who you ask.

*Malta* is a popular non-alcoholic malt beverage. Fresh *coconut water* from roadside vendors is everywhere and very good. Coffee from the mountain regions (particularly around Maricao and Yauco) is some of the best in the Caribbean.

**Where to eat**

*Fondas* are small, unpretentious local restaurants serving traditional home cooking at low prices. They're the best value on the island and worth finding over tourist-facing restaurants. In San Juan, the Santurce neighborhood has developed into a serious dining scene with a range of Puerto Rican and international restaurants.
