---
title: "People"
type: section
---

St. Barts has a population of around 10,600, making it one of the smallest inhabited territories in the Caribbean. The permanent residents are a mix of long-established Creole families, descendants of white French settlers who arrived in the 17th century (known as "z'oreilles" or sometimes as the original Saintoisians), and a substantial contingent of French and other European expats who arrived more recently for the tourism economy.

The island's unusual history shows. France originally settled it in 1648, then ceded it to Sweden in 1784 in exchange for trading rights in Gothenburg. Sweden held it for nearly a century, founding Gustavia as a free port and naming the capital after King Gustav III. A referendum in 1878 returned the island to France, but the Swedish chapter left its mark: the three crowns of the Swedish coat of arms appear in the island's own crest, Gustavia kept its name, and some Swedish-origin family names survive on the island.

Despite that history, the culture is thoroughly French. Daily life runs in French, food is French-inflected, bureaucracy is French, and the calendar follows French public holidays. There is also a strong Creole culture present in the music, the cooking, and the festivals — particularly at Carnival, when the streets of Gustavia come alive with soca, zouk, and elaborate costumed processions.

The high season brings a very different demographic: wealthy visitors, largely from the United States and Europe, who arrive for the beaches and the restaurants and give the island its glossy international character. The celebrity profile is real — St. Barts has been a winter destination for the famous and the wealthy for decades — but the permanent population lives alongside this without much apparent resentment, having built their economy on it. Outside the tourist areas, particularly in the eastern and northern parts of the island, a quieter, more local St. Barts is accessible to anyone who takes the time to find it.
