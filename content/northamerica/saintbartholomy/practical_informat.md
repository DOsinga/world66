---
title: "Practical Information"
type: section
---

**Visas.** St. Barts is a French overseas collectivity, not a French department, which affects visa requirements. Americans, Canadians, and most EU citizens can visit without a visa for up to 90 days. British citizens also enter visa-free for tourism stays up to 90 days. Travellers from other countries may need a French visa with the specific notation for Saint-Barthélemy and Saint-Martin — the standard French Schengen visa does not cover it. Check with the French embassy in your home country before travelling. All visitors need a valid passport with at least six months remaining, plus a return or onward ticket.

**Currency.** The euro (€). Despite being in the Caribbean and close to dollar-based islands, everything here runs on euros. ATMs are available in Gustavia. Credit cards are widely accepted at hotels, restaurants, and shops. Cash is useful for smaller purchases and beach snack bars.

**Language.** French is the official language and the daily language of the island. English is widely spoken in tourist-facing businesses — restaurants, hotels, car rental offices — but making an effort with basic French is always appreciated. Away from the main strip, French is more necessary.

**Money.** St. Barts is expensive, full stop. It is one of the costliest destinations in the Caribbean. A meal at a decent restaurant will run €40–80 per person without wine. Even grocery shopping at the local supermarket is pricier than most visitors expect. Gustavia and the area around the airport have a concentration of duty-free luxury boutiques — perfume, jewellery, spirits — where prices can be competitive with Paris.

**Safety.** The island is very safe by any Caribbean standard. Petty theft does occur — don't leave valuables in a parked car — but violent crime is rare. The main hazards are practical: the airport approach, narrow mountain roads, strong Atlantic surf on the island's eastern beaches, and the occasional powerful swell at Toiny and other exposed spots.

**Health.** St. Barts has a hospital (Hôpital de Bruyn) in Gustavia and a handful of medical clinics. Serious cases may be evacuated to Guadeloupe or Martinique. Travel insurance with medical evacuation coverage is recommended. Sun, heat, and dehydration are the most common issues; the tropical sun is stronger than it feels.

**Electricity.** 220V, 50Hz, with French-style two-round-pin plugs. American devices need a plug adapter and possibly a voltage converter.

**Time zone.** Atlantic Standard Time (UTC-4). St. Barts does not observe daylight saving time.
