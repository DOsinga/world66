Saint Barthélemy — known to almost everyone as St. Barts or St. Barths — is a small French Caribbean island of about 25 square kilometres sitting roughly 35 kilometres southeast of Saint Martin. It is one of the most expensive destinations in the Caribbean, unabashedly so, and has built its reputation on white-sand beaches, excellent French food, and a clientele that arrives by superyacht and private jet. If that sounds rarefied, it is — but the island itself is genuinely beautiful, and the money has not ruined it. It is remarkably understated for what it costs.

The island belongs to France as an overseas collectivity, sharing a legal status with a handful of other French territories. You pay in euros, speak French (or get by in English), and eat well. But the history is more layered than it first appears: Sweden owned St. Barts for nearly a century, from 1784 to 1878, and left behind the island's capital name — Gustavia — along with traces of Scandinavian influence in the coat of arms and a few street names. The French have since reclaimed the culture thoroughly.

Gustavia, the capital, is a small harbour town with red-roofed wooden houses climbing the hillside above a port full of serious yachts. It has duty-free shopping, good restaurants, and a compact colonial quarter worth walking through. The beaches — there are 22 of them — range from the long arc of Anse des Flamands on the northwest to the wilder, Atlantic-facing surf of Anse du Grand Fond in the southeast. Flamands, Gouverneur, and Saline are consistently rated among the Caribbean's best.

The island does not have a backpacker scene. Budget travellers will struggle: the cheapest accommodation is expensive, restaurants are serious, and the ferry from Saint Martin adds transport costs. Come prepared for what it is, or go somewhere else. For those who fit the profile — or can splurge for a week — it delivers on its reputation without much fuss or pretension.
