Castries is the capital and main port of Saint Lucia, a bustling town of roughly 70,000 people wedged between the green hills of the island's north and a natural harbour busy with cruise ships and inter-island ferries. It is not a pretty city in the colonial sense — fires in 1796, 1813, 1927, and 1948 destroyed most of the old wooden architecture — but it has an honest, working energy that feels less curated than many Caribbean destinations.

The heart of the city is the central market on Jeremie Street, a lively covered hall where vendors sell local produce, spices, and hand-crafted souvenirs. Saturday morning is the best time to visit, when farmers from across the island bring in fresh fruit, dasheen, breadfruit, and local hot sauces. Derek Walcott Square, named for the Saint Lucian Nobel laureate, provides a shaded green in the middle of town, overlooked by the Cathedral of the Immaculate Conception with its painted interior. The square was called Columbus Square until 1993, when it was renamed in honor of Walcott winning the Nobel Prize in Literature.

Most visitors pass through Castries quickly, using it as a base or stopping only for the market. That is a reasonable approach — the real draws of Saint Lucia are the Pitons to the south, the rainforest interior, and the beaches — but the city rewards an hour or two of slow walking, particularly around the Vigie Peninsula and the yacht-lined inner harbour.
