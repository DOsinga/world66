title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**The Pitons**

Gros Piton and Petit Piton are the defining image of Saint Lucia — twin volcanic plugs rising from the sea near Soufrière. Together they form the Pitons Management Area, a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2004. You can hike Gros Piton in a morning. The base village of Fond Gens Libre provides local guides, whose fee is included in the park entrance charge.

**Soufrière and the Sulphur Springs**

The town of Soufrière is Saint Lucia's oldest European settlement and the most rewarding place on the island for a day visit. The road passes through the Sulphur Springs geothermal field — a landscape of steaming vents and coloured mineral pools — before reaching the Soufrière Estate, where Diamond Falls drop through mineral-stained rock and warm bathing pools are open to visitors. Allow half a day to cover both.

**The Rainforest**

Saint Lucia's interior is a 19,000-acre tropical forest covering the volcanic highlands. The endemic Saint Lucia parrot lives here, as do several other bird species found nowhere else. The Barre de l'Isle trail is the main walking route across the island. The Forest Department organises guided tours; going with a guide is worth it for the wildlife spotting.

**Rodney Bay**

Rodney Bay in the north is the island's most developed tourist area — shopping, restaurants, a marina, and the long white arc of Reduit Beach. It's busy but the marina area has good restaurants and bars, and the beach is genuinely excellent. The ruins of Fort Rodney on Pigeon Island, now a national park connected to the mainland by a causeway, give a good overview of the bay.

**Marigot Bay**

A narrow inlet about 12km south of Castries, almost invisible from the sea — which is why Admiral Rodney used it to hide from the French. Today it's peaceful and scenic, with a small ferry between the two sides of the bay, a marina, and good snorkelling. Far less crowded than Rodney Bay.

**Choiseul**

A small fishing village in the south, Choiseul is known for its crafts. The craft market sells locally made baskets, pottery, and woodwork. The nearby Balenbouche Estate is a historic plantation house set in grounds that include a river, petroglyphs, and an organic farm.

**The East Coast**

Most tourism is concentrated on the calmer west coast. The Atlantic east coast is rougher and less visited, which gives it a different character — fishing villages, open sea views, and nesting leatherback turtles at Grande Anse from March to July.
