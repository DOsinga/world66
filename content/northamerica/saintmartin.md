Saint-Martin is the French half of a 37-square-mile island it shares peacefully with the Dutch territory of Sint Maarten. Together they form the smallest landmass in the world divided between two sovereign nations. The French side is slightly larger — 21 square miles to the Dutch 16 — and holds the capital Marigot and the gastronomic hub of Grand Case.

The island sits in the northeastern Caribbean, roughly midway between Puerto Rico and Guadeloupe, with Anguilla just a short ferry ride to the north and Saint-Barthélemy to the southeast. Its location made it a prize worth fighting over: the Spanish, French, and Dutch all staked claims before the two sides settled into the informal partition that has held, more or less, since 1648.

The island is known above all for its food, its beaches, and the easy movement between two very different European cultures. On the French side, the pace is slightly more relaxed and the cooking distinctly better. On the Dutch side, the casinos, duty-free malls, and Maho Beach — where planes pass terrifyingly low overhead — draw a different crowd. Visitors who stay a week can sample both without formality: there is no border control crossing between the two sides.

Hurricane Irma struck in September 2017 with catastrophic force, destroying around 90% of the island's infrastructure. Recovery was slow but real. By the early 2020s, most hotels, restaurants, and beaches had reopened, the airport terminal was rebuilt and reopened in 2023, and the island was broadly back to normal. Some shells of damaged buildings remain visible, particularly in the less-visited parts of Sint Maarten. The French side recovered somewhat faster.
