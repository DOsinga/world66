---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Saint-Martin has an outsized reputation for food given its size, and it is largely deserved. The island calls itself the "Culinary Capital of the Caribbean" — a claim that is slightly self-promoting but not entirely wrong. The French influence on the French side is real: you will find proper bistros, wine lists, and serious cooking alongside Caribbean and Creole options.

**Grand Case** is the centre of the island's food scene. The main street running along its small beach is lined on both sides with restaurants — French, Italian, Creole, and various fusions. Prices are high by Caribbean standards. At the northern end of the strip, the **lolos** offer a cheaper and more local alternative: open-air grills serving fresh fish, chicken, ribs, and rice. Both ends of the street are worth exploring.

Local Creole cooking draws on the island's African and Caribbean heritage. Look for grilled fish, oxtail stew, rice and peas, johnny cakes (fried dough), and crab and rice. These are more available at lolos and local spots than at the tourist-facing restaurants.

Marigot has a decent restaurant scene and a morning market on the waterfront where local produce, spices, and street food are sold. Get there before 10am.

On the Dutch side, Philipsburg and the Maho area have a large number of restaurants aimed squarely at the cruise ship and resort market — plenty of choice, but less distinctive. If food matters to you, eat on the French side.

A good bottle of French wine is easier to find here than almost anywhere else in the Caribbean, and priced more reasonably than you might expect.
