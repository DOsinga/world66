---
title: "People and Culture"
type: section
---

Saint-Martin's population is genuinely diverse — the island is home to people from over 100 nationalities, the product of centuries of colonial settlement followed by large waves of Caribbean, Latin American, and more recently Asian and African immigration. The original Arawak inhabitants were here for thousands of years before European contact; almost nothing of their presence survives.

The island was divided between France and the Netherlands by the Treaty of Concordia in 1648, signed atop Mount Concordia after Spanish forces had abandoned their garrison. According to a much-repeated local story, the boundary was set by a walking race: a Frenchman walked north and a Dutchman walked south from the same point, and where they met again determined the border. The French walker apparently covered more ground — explaining why France ended up with the larger share. The story is probably apocryphal but is told with some pride.

The official language split — French on the north side, Dutch on the south — has little to do with what people actually speak. English functions as the island's common language, used by most residents in daily life and by virtually all businesses. Papiamentu (a Creole language of the Dutch Caribbean) and French Creole are also widely spoken.

The French side became an overseas collectivity of France in 2007, separate from Guadeloupe. This gives it some degree of local autonomy while remaining part of France and the EU.

The cultural mix shows up most clearly in the food and in the music. Zouk, reggae, and calypso are all present; local festivals include Carnival (held on the French side in spring, different timing to the Dutch side's Carnival in February) and various sailing regattas in the yacht-heavy waters around the island.
