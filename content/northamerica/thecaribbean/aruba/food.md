title: "Food"
type: section
---

Aruban food is the product of centuries of layered migration: Arawak, Spanish, Dutch, African, Portuguese, Chinese and South American influences all show up in the local cooking. The result is a genuinely distinct cuisine that most visitors miss entirely because they eat at the resort buffets.

The word for delicious in Papiamento is "dushi" — and it applies.

**Local dishes to look for**

*Keshi yena* is the most iconic Aruban dish: a hollowed-out gouda or edam rind stuffed with spiced minced meat, onions, peppers, olives and raisins, then baked. The name means "stuffed cheese" in Papiamento. It originates from enslaved Africans who took the cheese rinds discarded by Dutch colonists and filled them with whatever they had. It's rich, savory and deeply satisfying.

*Pastechi* are fried pastry pockets filled with cheese, ham, tuna or chicken — eaten at breakfast or as a snack throughout the day. You'll find them at roadside stands and small bakeries. Don't skip them.

*Funchi* is a cornmeal porridge, roughly like polenta, served as a side dish with fish, stews and almost everything else. *Pan bati* is a slightly sweet, pancake-like flatbread that accompanies soups and seafood.

Fresh fish features prominently in local cooking: wahoo, snapper and mahi-mahi appear on menus across the island. The waterfront market in Oranjestad, where Venezuelan boats sell fresh fish and produce, is a good place to see what's in season.

**Where to eat**

The resort strip along Palm Beach is well-stocked with international options. For more authentic local food, head into Oranjestad or to San Nicolas. Small local spots and lunch counters in both towns serve keshi yena, pastechi and fresh fish at prices well below the hotel restaurants.

Aruba has a notably diverse restaurant scene for its size, with Indonesian, Chinese, Dutch, Venezuelan and Brazilian options available alongside local cooking.
