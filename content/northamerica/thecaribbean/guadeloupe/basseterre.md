Basse-Terre is the administrative capital of [Guadeloupe](/northamerica/thecaribbean/guadeloupe), a small city on the southwestern coast of the island's volcanic western wing. It sits at the foot of La Soufrière, an active volcano that rises to 1,467 metres and looms dramatically over the town. The city is quieter and considerably less touristy than Pointe-à-Pitre, with a colonial old town of narrow streets and colourful Creole houses, a 17th-century fort, and easy access to the Guadeloupe National Park.

The main attraction is the hike up La Soufrière itself, which takes about two hours from the Bains Jaunes trailhead above town. The summit is often shrouded in cloud, but on clear days the views stretch across the archipelago. The surrounding national park is lush and tropical, with waterfalls — the Chutes du Carbet are the most famous — and well-marked trails through the rainforest. Fort Delgrès, the imposing 17th-century fortification overlooking the harbour, is worth a visit for its history and panoramic views.

The waterfront has a modest selection of restaurants serving Creole cuisine — colombo de poulet, accras de morue, and fresh grilled fish. Basse-Terre is not a nightlife destination by any stretch, but it is a genuine Caribbean town with a relaxed pace and a strong sense of place. If you are interested in hiking, nature, and Creole culture rather than beach resorts, this is the better side of Guadeloupe to base yourself.
