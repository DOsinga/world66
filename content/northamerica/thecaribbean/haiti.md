Haiti occupies the western third of Hispaniola, the island it shares with the Dominican Republic. It was the first Black republic in the world, winning independence from France in 1804 after the only successful slave revolution in history — a fact that shapes its identity, its fierce national pride, and its complicated relationship with the outside world.

The country is strikingly beautiful. Dramatic mountains drop to turquoise coastline, and the culture is among the most distinctive in the Caribbean — a fusion of African, French, and indigenous Taino traditions expressed through Vodou, vibrant carnival, intricate painting, iron sculpture, and a cuisine unlike anything else in the region.

**Travel warning.** Haiti is not currently safe to visit. Gang violence has escalated sharply since 2021, with armed groups controlling large parts of Port-au-Prince and surrounding areas. Kidnapping, armed robbery, and random violence are serious risks. Most governments advise against all travel. This guide describes Haiti as it has been and can be again — for when conditions improve.

Despite the crisis, Haitians continue to build and create. The countryside is scattered with remnants of the wars for independence — forts, ruins, and battlefields that tell the story of how enslaved people defeated Napoleon's army. The adventure traveler who makes it here when conditions allow will find a country that rewards curiosity and a tolerance for rough infrastructure.

[Port-au-Prince](/northamerica/haiti/portauprince) is the sprawling, intense capital — chaotic and overwhelming, but full of art, music, and street life. [Cap-Haïtien](/northamerica/haiti/caphaitien) in the north is smaller, more manageable, and the gateway to the Citadelle Laferrière, the largest fortress in the Americas and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. [Jacmel](/northamerica/haiti/jacmel) on the southern coast is the country's arts capital, known for its papier-mâché carnival masks and crumbling French colonial architecture. [Gonâve Island](/northamerica/haiti/gonaveisland) sits in the bay west of Port-au-Prince, remote and rarely visited.
