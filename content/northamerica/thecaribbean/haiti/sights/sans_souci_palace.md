Built between 1810 and 1813 by Henri Christophe, the self-declared King of Haiti, Sans Souci was once the most lavish palace in the Caribbean. Modeled loosely on Versailles, it featured gardens, an aqueduct system that piped water to every floor, and a printing press. An earthquake in 1842 destroyed much of the structure, and the ruins have stood open to the sky ever since.

The palace sits at the foot of the mountains near Milot, about 25 km south of Cap-Haïtien. Most visitors combine it with a trip to the Citadelle Laferrière, the enormous hilltop fortress visible from the palace grounds. Together they form a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Guides are available at the entrance and are well worth hiring for the history they bring to the crumbling walls.
