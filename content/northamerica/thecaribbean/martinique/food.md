---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Martinican cuisine blends French technique with African, Indian, and Creole traditions. The result is genuinely distinctive — richer and more complex than most Caribbean cooking, and often spiced more boldly than French mainland food. Eating well here is one of the pleasures of the island.

**Accras de morue** (salt cod fritters) are ubiquitous as a starter or street snack. Crisp outside, tender inside, and usually served with sauce chien — a sharp dressing of chillies, garlic, lime, and herbs. **Féroce d'avocat** is another classic starter: mashed avocado mixed with salt fish and chilli, historically made with cassava flour. Spicy and rich.

**Colombo** is the closest thing to a national stew: chicken or pork (sometimes goat) slow-cooked with a spice blend brought to the island by Indian indentured workers in the 19th century, similar to curry but distinctly local. Served with rice and vegetables. **Blaff** is a lighter fish dish — fillets poached in a broth of lime, garlic, and chilli — popular for lunch near the coast. **Chatrou** is octopus stew, common in coastal restaurants.

**Boudin créole** — blood sausage seasoned with local spices — is sold at roadside stalls and markets and eaten hot, often standing up. It is worth seeking out.

For dessert, **blanc manger coco** is a coconut milk jelly flavoured with vanilla and lime, served cold. Tropical fruit — mangoes, passion fruit, soursop, guava — is excellent and cheap at markets.

**Ti Punch** is the essential drink: white rhum agricole, a squeeze of lime, and a touch of cane syrup. You assemble it yourself — the bartender brings the components. The rum is poured first and the ratio of lime to syrup is personal. Do not confuse it with a daiquiri; it is much stronger and meant to be sipped slowly. More elaborate aged rum punches are also common.

Restaurants range from French-style bistros in Fort-de-France to small Creole places (often called lolos) at the beach, which are usually the better option for local food at reasonable prices. Fort-de-France has good options for all budgets. The highest concentration of beach restaurants is around Sainte-Anne and the Anse Mitan area south of Fort-de-France.
