Montserrat is a small British Overseas Territory in the eastern Caribbean, and one of the most unusual islands in the region. It was colonised largely by Irish settlers in the 17th century — an origin that has left a distinctive cultural mark, including shamrocks on the national seal and a St Patrick's Day celebration unique in the Caribbean.

The island is volcanic, and the Soufrière Hills volcano has shaped modern Montserrat profoundly. In 1995–1997 a series of major eruptions buried Plymouth, the former capital, under ash and pyroclastic flows. The southern half of the island is now an exclusion zone, and Plymouth remains a ghost town visible from the hills above — an eerie and sobering sight. The northern part of the island, centred on Little Bay and Brades, continues to function normally. Montserrat is a genuinely beautiful island with forested peaks, rivers, and waterfalls, and its off-the-beaten-track status means hiking and nature watching take place without the crowds found elsewhere in the Caribbean.

Access is by small plane from Antigua, or by ferry. The island's character — quiet, green, slightly melancholy in places — is unlike anywhere else in the region.
