---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

The food on St. Barts is, by Caribbean standards, remarkable. The island has over 80 restaurants for a resident population of around 10,000 — an absurd concentration — and the competition keeps standards high. The dominant style is French technique applied to local Caribbean ingredients, with Creole influence running through much of the menu.

Lobster grilled over a wood fire is the signature dish of the island. Conch — called lambi locally — appears as a fricassée or grilled, often with Creole rice and local vegetables. Fresh tuna and other local fish are staples. Serious provisions fly in from Paris regularly, so charcuterie and cheese boards at dinner can be surprisingly authentic. The bakeries produce proper baguettes and croissants from early morning.

Gustavia has the highest concentration of restaurants, from elegant dinner rooms to casual harbour-side bistros. The area around St. Jean beach has a cluster of beach restaurants where you can eat with your feet in the sand. For the full experience, a dinner at one of the serious tables — Grand Cul de Sac, Toiny, or inside Gustavia — is worth the splurge if you are already there for the price of a room.

The Gourmet Festival, held each autumn since 2013, brings in guest chefs from France and elsewhere for a week of dinners, tastings, and events. It has become one of the Caribbean's better food events.

Budget eating exists but requires looking. The food truck area in Gustavia and the small local snack bars (lorlos) scattered around the island serve solid Creole food at prices that are merely high rather than eye-watering. The supermarkets — there are several — stock good French wine and cheese for self-caterers.
