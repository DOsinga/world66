---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

St. Barts has two seasons: a dry season from roughly December to April, and a wet season from May through November that coincides with the Atlantic hurricane season.

**December to April** is high season. The weather is at its best — temperatures hover around 26–28°C, trade winds keep things comfortable, rainfall is minimal (40–50mm per month in the driest stretch), and the sea is calm and clear. This is when the island is at full throttle: New Year's is the peak of the peak, with prices and occupancy at their highest. The Carnival runs over the days leading up to Fat Tuesday in February or March. The sailing regattas — Bucket and Les Voiles — take place in April. If you can only go once and money is not the primary concern, January through early April is the best window: weather reliable, island alive, but quieter than the holiday crush.

**May to June** offers an underrated window. The crowds thin out after Easter, prices drop, the weather is still good (warming slightly, some afternoon showers), and most businesses remain open. These months have the best combination of decent weather and relative peace.

**July to November** is low season and hurricane season. Temperatures reach 30–32°C and humidity rises. Short, sharp tropical showers are common, though they often pass quickly. From August through October, hurricane risk is real — not likely on any given day, but the season's storms track through this part of the Caribbean and the island took a direct hit from Hurricane Irma in September 2017, causing substantial damage. The island has largely recovered, but the risk exists. Travel insurance is essential if you visit in this window.

**Water temperature** is pleasant year-round, ranging from about 26°C in February to 29°C in September. Swimming is good in all months.

The island follows French public holidays — Bastille Day (July 14) and St. Barthélemy Day (August 24, the island's patron saint day) are both worth knowing about if you're there at the time.
