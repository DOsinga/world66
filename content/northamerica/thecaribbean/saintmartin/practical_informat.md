## Currency and money

The French side uses the **euro**; the Dutch side uses the Netherlands Antillean guilder (ANG), though US dollars are accepted nearly everywhere on both sides. In practice, most businesses on the French side also accept dollars, especially in tourist areas. Credit cards are widely accepted.

## Language

French is the official language of Saint-Martin; Dutch is official on Sint Maarten. In practice, **English is the common language** throughout the island and you will rarely need anything else in tourist areas. Many locals also speak Papiamentu and various Creole languages.

## Entry requirements

Citizens of EU countries, the US, Canada, UK, and most Western nations do not need a visa for stays up to 90 days. Since Saint-Martin is an overseas collectivity of France, EU visitors have additional rights. A valid passport is required — check requirements for your specific nationality before travel.

## Health

No specific vaccinations are required. Tap water on the French side is safe to drink. The island has a hospital in Marigot (Centre Hospitalier Louis-Constant Fleming) and clinics on both sides. Travel insurance with medical coverage is strongly recommended.

## Electricity

The French side uses European standard 220V/50Hz with two-pin round plugs (Type E/F). American visitors need adaptors and possibly voltage converters for older devices.

## Communications

French side uses French mobile networks; Dutch side uses Dutch Caribbean networks. Coverage is good across the island. International roaming is straightforward for most European carriers on the French side; US travellers should check roaming rates or buy a local SIM.

## Safety

Saint-Martin is generally safe for tourists. The usual precautions apply: don't leave valuables in rental cars, be aware of your surroundings at night, and stick to busier areas after dark. The Dutch side's nightlife strip around Maho can get rowdy late at night.
