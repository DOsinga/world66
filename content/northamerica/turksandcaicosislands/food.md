---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Local cuisine in Turks and Caicos centres on what comes out of the surrounding sea: conch, lobster, and fish. The giant queen conch — a large sea snail with a pink shell — is the unofficial mascot of the islands and appears on almost every local menu in several forms. Conch fritters are the most common: the meat is chopped, seasoned, and fried in batter. Conch salad is served raw, dressed with lime juice, onion, and pepper — the acid effectively cures the meat. Conch chowder is a heartier option and varies considerably by cook.

Cracked conch, battered and fried whole, is the staple of many beach shacks and local restaurants. Grilled lobster tail and whole fried snapper round out the standard repertoire. Chicken souse — a stew-like dish with lime and allspice — is another local favourite, typically served with johnnycake, a dense fried bread that shows up alongside most meals.

On Providenciales, the restaurant scene has expanded considerably over the past two decades and now includes high-end international options alongside local spots. The best local eating is often at small, informal places away from the Grace Bay hotel strip. **Da Conch Shack** on Blue Hills Road is a well-known open-air spot on the beach specialising in freshly harvested conch and jerk chicken. **Omar's Beach Hut** in the Five Cays fishing village is another reliable option for unpretentious, very fresh local food.

On the outer islands, options are limited. Grand Turk has a few local restaurants in Cockburn Town. South Caicos, as a fishing community, sometimes has fresh seafood if you ask around, but don't rely on finding a restaurant open at any given time. On Middle and North Caicos, bring supplies from Providenciales.

The influence of Haitian, Jamaican, and broader Caribbean cuisine is evident in the spicing and preparations, particularly in the kitchens of the large immigrant workforce on Provo.
