## Grace Bay Beach

Grace Bay on Providenciales is the reason most people come to Turks and Caicos. The beach runs for 12 miles along the northern coast of the island, with sand so fine it squeaks underfoot and water that grades from pale turquoise to deep blue as the reef shelf drops away. The waves are gentle, the current minimal, and the swimming is reliably excellent. It gets busy in high season but is wide enough that it never feels crowded.

## Diving the Caicos Wall

The outer edge of the Caicos Bank drops dramatically into the Atlantic, creating a series of vertical walls that rank among the best dive sites in the Caribbean. The West Caicos wall is the most spectacular, with coral formations starting at 15 metres and dropping past 600. Even shallower dives at Northwest Point on Providenciales turn up reef sharks, eagle rays, turtles, and nurse sharks resting on the sandy bottom. The visibility regularly exceeds 30 metres.

## Humpback Whale Watching

Between January and March, humpback whales migrate through the Turks Island Passage on their way to and from calving grounds further south. Boat trips out of Grand Turk and Salt Cay offer some of the most accessible whale watching in the Caribbean. On good days you can snorkel alongside them.

## Grand Turk

The capital island is a different pace from Providenciales — quieter, older, and less polished. Cockburn Town has a compact colonial centre with salt-era Bermudian architecture, a small but well-kept national museum, and a reef wall that begins less than a kilometre from shore. The cruise ships that stop here briefly are no reason to rush a visit; the town is worth a night or two.

## The Outer Islands

North Caicos and Middle Caicos are the most accessible of the quieter islands, connected by a causeway and reachable from Providenciales by ferry in about 30 minutes. North Caicos has flamingos, wetlands good for birding, and plantation ruins. Middle Caicos has the Conch Bar Caves — a large limestone cave system with stalactites and stalagmites — and a nearly empty beach at Mudjin Harbour, one of the most dramatic stretches of coastline in the islands.

South Caicos is a working fishing village where the boats go out early and the conch and lobster you eat for lunch was in the water that morning. The diving around it is largely undeveloped and often exceptional.
