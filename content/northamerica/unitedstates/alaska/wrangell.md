Wrangell is one of Alaska's oldest and least-visited towns, sitting on the northern tip of Wrangell Island in the Inside Passage. It is a working fishing and timber community with a history that stretches back further than almost anywhere in Alaska — it has been under three flags (Russian, British, and American) and was built on land that the Tlingit people had occupied for thousands of years. The Stikine River, one of the fastest-flowing navigable rivers in North America, empties into the sea nearby and provides access deep into the interior.

The town is small but has real character. Petroglyphs Beach, a short walk from town, is scattered with ancient Tlingit rock carvings — you can wander among them freely at low tide. The Anan Wildlife Observatory, accessible by boat or floatplane, is one of the best places in Alaska to watch black and brown bears fishing for salmon. Wrangell gets far fewer cruise ships than Ketchikan or Juneau, which means you are much more likely to encounter genuine community life rather than a town performing for tourists.
