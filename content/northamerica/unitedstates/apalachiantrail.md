The Appalachian National Scenic Trail is a 2,175-mile (3,500 km) footpath threading along the ridge crests and across the major valleys of the Appalachian Mountains, from [Katahdin](/northamerica/unitedstates/maine/katahdin) in northern Maine to Springer Mountain in north Georgia. The trail passes through fourteen states — Maine, New Hampshire, Vermont, Massachusetts, Connecticut, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Maryland, West Virginia, Virginia, Tennessee, North Carolina, and Georgia — and is one of the longest continuously marked footpaths in the world.

The A.T. began as the vision of forester Benton MacKaye, was built by volunteers, and opened as a continuous trail in 1937. In 1968 it became the first National Scenic Trail designated under the National Trails System Act. Today, more than 99 percent of the route is protected by federal or state land ownership or rights-of-way, and over 4,000 volunteers contribute some 175,000 hours of trail maintenance each year.

Hikers use the trail in several ways: day hikes to a summit or shelter, weekend backpacking trips, section hiking over multiple seasons, or the full thru-hike — completing the entire trail in a single continuous journey, typically taking five to seven months. Around 3,000 people attempt a thru-hike each year, though only a fraction finish. Northbound thru-hikers traditionally start at Springer Mountain in late March or April to stay ahead of the seasons; southbound hikers begin at Katahdin in summer, constrained by Baxter State Park's early closing date.
