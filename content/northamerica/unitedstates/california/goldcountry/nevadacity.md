Nevada City is one of the best-preserved Gold Rush towns in California — a tight cluster of Victorian storefronts and brick buildings stacked up a hill above Deer Creek, 60 miles northeast of Sacramento. It doesn't feel like a museum piece. The downtown is genuinely alive with restaurants, galleries, and independent shops, and the surrounding hills draw hikers, cyclists, and wine tourists year-round.

The town grew explosively after gold was discovered in the region in 1849, peaking at around 10,000 residents by 1856. What survives today is a compact but remarkably intact historic core. Broad Street, the main drag, looks much as it did in the 1860s. The Nevada Theatre at 401 Broad Street — opened in 1865 and still running — is California's oldest continuously operating theater building; Mark Twain and Jack London both performed here.

North of town, about 26 miles on Tyler Foote Road, Malakoff Diggins State Historic Park preserves the largest hydraulic gold mine in California. Monitors blasted up to 100,000 tons of gravel a day at its peak in the 1870s, carving a vast rust-and-ochre canyon into the hills. The environmental damage eventually led to the country's first federal injunction against a private mining company, issued in 1884. The canyon is genuinely dramatic and worth the drive.
