type: location

Panning for gold in California's Gold Country delivers a Mother Lode of fun. Some gold-panning locations are set up with benches for panning from troughs. This arrangement minimizes bending down. Follow the steps described below.

  * Start out at a bend in a creek with a light flow of water. Choose the low pressure side of the bend. Look behind neighboring rocks, fallen tree limbs, and uprooted trees for a good spot to begin digging for material.

 * The beginner will be comfortable using an 8- to 10- inch pan and a pick and shovel to start. An old screwdriver can be used to pick material out of crevices. When you dig a hole looking for material, go as deep into the hole as you can. Follow any exposed bedrock down as far as possible. Finding black sand can be an indicator that gold may be in the area.

 * Fill your pan 3/4 full with material. Put it under the water, stirring the contents with your hand to liquify it. As you liquIfy the material, pick out the big rocks. Concentrate the material in your pan by quickly shaking it back and forth or side to side at least 20 times.

 * Dip the forward rim of your pan into the water (if your pan has rings, make certain they are pointed forward in the water). Sweep the water over the contents so the lighter material can float out. Then concentrate the material again.

 * Continue to alternate between these two procedures until you have approximately one tablespoonful of material left in your pan. Tip your pan, ring side up, leaving sand and gravel at the top. Gently swirl the water across the material to uncover the gold left in your pan.

 * Transfer gold from the pan to a vial. Fill a vial full of water. With a dry fingertip, press on the gold in the pan, then touch gold to the water in the vial and it will fall into the vial. (Be certain you perform the transfer over your pan so you don't risk losing the gold!)

 Author and gold prospector Ralph Shock developed the only college-accredited gold-panning course in the world.
