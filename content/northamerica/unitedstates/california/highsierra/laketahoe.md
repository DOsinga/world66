Lake Tahoe straddles the California–Nevada line at 6,225 feet in the Sierra Nevada — a 22-mile-long, cobalt-blue alpine lake set in a basin of pine forest and granite peaks. It is one of the deepest lakes in the world (1,645 feet), and on a still day the water is so clear that boulders are visible 70 feet down. Mark Twain, who saw it in 1861, called it "the fairest picture the whole earth affords." A century and a half of cabins, casinos and ski resorts later, the picture is more crowded but still spectacular.

The lake has two faces. The **California side** — Tahoe City, Homewood, the south end at South Lake Tahoe — is the resort and outdoor side: a horseshoe of ski areas above the West Shore, the granite-rimmed turquoise of **Emerald Bay** with its little island and ruined teahouse, the trailheads for the **Tahoe Rim Trail** and the dozens of high-country lakes around Desolation Wilderness. The **Nevada side** — Crystal Bay and Stateline — is where you'll find the casinos: the state line runs straight through the resort towns and gambling stops the moment you cross westbound.

Winter is the headline season. Within an hour of the lake there are more than a dozen ski resorts, including the connected giants of **Palisades Tahoe** (formerly Squaw Valley, 1960 Winter Olympics) and **Heavenly**, which straddles the state line and offers night skiing with the lake glittering 3,000 feet below. Summer is just as good: swim in cold water at Sand Harbor, kayak from the dock at Camp Richardson, hike the Rubicon Trail along the bluff above Emerald Bay, or ride a chair to the top for a view that takes in the whole basin.

Tahoe is busy. Reno is an hour away, Sacramento two, San Francisco three and a half — which means weekends and the four big holidays can be a slog. Time a visit for midweek, or for the shoulder seasons of late September and early June, when the water is at its clearest and the meadows full of wildflowers.
