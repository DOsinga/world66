Mammoth Lakes sits at 7,880 feet on the eastern escarpment of the Sierra Nevada, in a wide volcanic basin ringed by peaks. It is a year-round mountain town whose personality changes completely with the seasons: a serious ski destination in winter, a hiker's and biker's paradise in summer.

Mammoth Mountain itself is the centrepiece — one of California's premier ski resorts, with over 3,500 acres of terrain and a season that stretches from November well into spring (sometimes June). In summer the mountain transforms into a major bike park, with gondola-accessed descents drawing riders from across the West.

The surrounding landscape rewards exploration. [Devil's Postpile National Monument](https://www.nps.gov/depo/), just west of town, is a striking formation of near-perfect hexagonal basalt columns left by a lava flow some 100,000 years ago — and the adjacent Rainbow Falls drops 101 feet into a red-rock canyon. To the north, the June Lake Loop offers four alpine lakes in a glacially carved valley. [Mono Lake](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/highsierra/monolake), to the east on US-395, is one of the oldest lakes in North America and famous for its eerie tufa towers.

The town itself is modest but functional: a good mix of restaurants, gear shops, and lodging strung along Main Street and Old Mammoth Road. It gets crowded on winter weekends and in peak summer, but the scale remains human. This is a place people come to be outside, not to be seen.
