Eureka is the largest city on the California coast north of San Francisco, sitting on Humboldt Bay about 270 miles from the Golden Gate. It is a logging and fishing town that has never quite shaken that identity — and is better for it. The waterfront smells like salt and diesel, the Victorian architecture downtown is genuine rather than restored-for-tourists, and the fog rolls in most mornings without apology.

Old Town is the heart of Eureka's appeal: a stretch of late-19th-century commercial buildings along the waterfront that have survived largely intact. The Carson Mansion on M Street, built in 1886 for lumber baron William Carson, is one of the most photographed Victorian houses in the United States — a riot of turrets, gables, and ornamental woodwork that looks almost too elaborate to be real. Much of that woodwork was produced at the Blue Ox Millworks, which still operates as a working Victorian crafts mill and is open for tours.

Humboldt Bay itself is the attraction that doesn't get enough credit. It's the second-largest coastal bay in California, sheltered and calm, with a small fleet of fishing boats and a harbor cruise that gets you out on the water cheaply. The bay and its surrounding wetlands are important habitat for shorebirds and waterfowl; birders come from a long way for it.

The city has a university — Cal Poly Humboldt, formerly Humboldt State — which keeps it from feeling entirely like a town in economic decline. There are good restaurants and a few worthwhile bars. [Arcata](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/northcoast/arcata), just 8 miles north, is livelier and has more of the college-town energy; [Ferndale](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/northcoast/ferndale), 20 miles south, is a smaller Victorian town worth the side trip.
