Redding sits at the northern end of the Sacramento Valley, where Interstate 5 begins its long climb toward the Oregon border and three great waterways — the Sacramento River, the Pit River, and Clear Creek — converge in the surrounding landscape. It is hot here in summer, reliably over 100°F, but the heat has not stopped Redding from becoming an unlikely outdoor recreation capital. The city claims more miles of trail per capita than almost anywhere in the United States, and the surrounding terrain gives you somewhere to go: Shasta Lake to the north, Whiskeytown Lake to the west, and Lassen Volcanic National Park about 50 miles east.

The downtown is modest but improving. The Santiago Calatrava–designed Sundial Bridge, which opened in 2004, spans the Sacramento River and functions as the world's largest sundial — it actually tells time, accurately, on sunny days. The bridge anchors the Turtle Bay Exploration Park and connects to the Sacramento River Trail, which winds through riparian woodland along both banks.

Redding is a practical base for exploring a corner of California that most visitors skip. The scenery is volcanic and dramatic: jagged peaks, ancient lava fields, cold mountain lakes, and rivers full of trout. If you are driving I-5 between San Francisco and Portland, it is worth stopping for a night rather than pushing through.
