The [Castro](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/castro_district) is the heart of San Francisco's LGBTQ+ community and one of the most historically significant neighbourhoods in the city. It is also, on a weekday evening, one of the most pleasant places in San Francisco to spend a few hours — walkable, neighbourhood-scaled, and full of places that feel like they belong to the people who use them.

Start at [Harvey Milk Plaza](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/harvey_milk_plaza) at the corner of Castro and Market. The large rainbow flag here marks the unofficial entrance to the neighbourhood and the site of Milk's camera shop at 575 Castro. The neighbourhood's history — the first openly gay elected officials in California, the assassinations of 1978, the AIDS epidemic, the marriage equality fight — is embedded in the streets and the buildings.

If you can time it right, a film at the [Castro Theatre](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/castro_theatre) is worth planning your evening around. The 1922 movie palace with its Spanish Colonial Revival interior is one of the last great single-screen theatres in America, and its programming — classic films, repertory double features, film festivals — is consistently interesting. The Wurlitzer organ rises from the pit before showings.

Drinks after at [Moby Dick](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/moby_dick) on 18th Street or any of the neighbourhood's older bars, which tend toward the unpretentious and local. The Castro is not a nightclub district — it is a neighbourhood — and the bars reflect that.
