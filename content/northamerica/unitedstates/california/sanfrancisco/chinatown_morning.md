San Francisco's Chinatown is the oldest in North America and the most densely populated urban neighbourhood west of Manhattan. It is also one of the most visited — which is why morning, before ten, is the only time to really experience it. The produce vendors are setting out their stands, the butchers are opening their shops, and the streets belong to the people who live and work here rather than the tour groups.

Enter through the Dragon Gate on Bush Street and walk up Grant Avenue, the main tourist corridor, then turn off into the side streets — Waverly Place, Ross Alley, Spofford Lane. These are the narrow alleys where the neighbourhood's daily life runs: temple incense, the clack of mahjong tiles from the social clubs, the smell of roasting duck.

Ross Alley has the [Golden Gate Fortune Cookie Factory](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/golden_gate_fortune_cookie_factory) at number 56 — a tiny operation where you can watch the cookies being folded by hand on a griddle and buy a bag still warm from the press. It is one of the few remaining operations of this kind and is not a tourist recreation.

The produce markets on Stockton Street, one block west of Grant, are where the neighbourhood actually shops. The range and quality of vegetables, dried goods, and fresh seafood is remarkable. Breakfast: [Far East Cafe](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/far_east_cafe) on Grant has been open since 1920 and serves dim sum from early morning in a room of dark wood booths that has not changed much in decades.
