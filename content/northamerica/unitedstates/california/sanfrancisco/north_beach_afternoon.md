[North Beach](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/north_beach) is the neighbourhood most worth spending an afternoon in. It has the feel of a genuine Italian quarter — Columbus Avenue lined with cafes, trattorias, and delicatessens — and a literary history that gives it more character per block than almost anywhere in the city.

Start at [City Lights Bookstore](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/city_lights_books) at 261 Columbus. Lawrence Ferlinghetti opened it in 1953, and it became the centre of the Beat Generation — Ginsberg, Kerouac, and Corso all passed through. The poetry room upstairs is where Ginsberg's *Howl* was published, and you can still buy it there. Browse without agenda.

Walk through [Jack Kerouac Alley](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/jack_kerouac_alley) alongside the bookstore — a narrow passage with lines from Kerouac, Ginsberg, Ferlinghetti, Maya Angelou, and Confucius embedded in the pavement stones. It comes out at Vesuvio, the bar where the Beats drank and where you can still sit at the window table upstairs with a beer and watch Columbus Avenue.

Walk uphill to [Coit Tower](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfrancisco/coit_tower) at the top of Telegraph Hill — the climb is steep but the murals inside are worth it, and the views from the observation deck take in the bay, the bridges, and the city below. Come back down via the Filbert Street steps, where wild parrots nest in the cypresses and gardens tumble down the hillside in defiance of urban logic.

Dinner: anywhere on Columbus or the side streets. The Italian restaurants are consistent rather than exceptional, but the atmosphere makes up for it.
