---

Thousands came to the Santa Clara County Fairgrounds on Saturday for
the Tet Festival, one of several celebrations where the South Bay's
Vietnamese community marks the lunar new year.

The
compressed, two-day version of Tet, which in Vietnam is often
celebrated over a two week period, has been a fixture of San Jose's
cultural life for 24 years.

Lion dances punctuated seven sports competitions.

Judges also chose Miss Tet Festival during a beauty pageant.

The
centerpiece of the event, organized by the Coalition of Nationalist
Vietnam Organizations, was an hours-long reenactment of an historic
moment in the country's history.

Young people wore
traditional head dresses and carried traditional swords as they
recreated Vietnam's victory over China during an 18th century war.

That
event might seem like ancient, and perhaps unimportant history, but Van
Le with the Vietnam American Cultural Foundation said the festival was
an ideal vehicle for connecting Vietnamese American children with a
history and heritage all too easily lost in the American melting pot.

"We
really want to keep our culture, to really pass on to our younger
generation, to remember our ancestors, to remember our traditions, to
keep it going for the next generation," Le told KCBS reporter Betsy
Gebhart.

"We get a lot of people to remember," she said. "We don't forget our roots."

San
Jose hosts one of the largest Vietnamese populations in the United
States, and the festival has also served as an important bridge between
the immigrant community and the rest of the city.
"We want to continue to bring in a lot of flavor to America," Le said. "It's a place of all diversity."
