Saratoga sits at the base of the Santa Cruz Mountains in the southern Bay Area, a quiet, affluent town that feels a world away from the tech campuses of nearby Cupertino and San Jose. The village centre along Big Basin Way has a relaxed, almost small-town character — good restaurants, wine bars, and boutiques tucked between the redwoods — while the surrounding hillsides conceal some genuinely surprising cultural treasures.

The town's origins are industrial. A sawmill established in 1847 exploited the dense redwood forests above the valley, and the settlement briefly went by the name Bank Mills before residents voted in 1865 to rename it Saratoga, after the mineral springs resort in New York. Agriculture followed the lumber trade: apricots, cherries, and French prunes thrived in Saratoga's mild climate, and the 680-acre Glen Una Ranch between here and Los Gatos was once the world's largest prune producer. By the 1890s French immigrant Paul Masson had planted vines along Pierce Road, convincing himself — correctly — that the hillside terroir could produce champagne-quality fruit.

The wineries and orchards gave way to suburban homes after World War II as Silicon Valley's aerospace and electronics industries drew a new population. Saratoga became a refuge for the wealthy: elegant hillside estates, private schools, and a notably low-density streetscape. Senator James Phelan's palatial Villa Montalvo, built in 1912, set the tone and is now a celebrated arts centre and arboretum. Hakone Gardens, created the same year, survives as the oldest Japanese-style residential garden in the western hemisphere.

For visitors, Saratoga is an easy half-day from San Francisco or a relaxed base for exploring the Santa Cruz Mountains. The village strip rewards a slow walk, and both Hakone and Montalvo justify the detour on their own.
