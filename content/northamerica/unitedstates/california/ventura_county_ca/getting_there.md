---
title: "Getting There"
type: section
---

The major freeway for Ventura County is US Highway 101 (the Ventura
Freeway), which runs more-or-less east-west through the "urban" heart
of the area. Other freeway access can be had on California State
Routes 118 and 126. There are two small regional airports, in
Oxnard and Camarillo, which handle small commuter jets as well as
private craft. There is also an airport in Santa Paula for
private aircraft only. AmTrak and the local MetroLink trains have
stations in Simi Valley, Moorpark, Camarillo, Oxnard and Ventura.
The Vista bus system operates along the 101 corridor, providing mostly
local commuter service, but it can be used by those coming from the San
Fernando Valley area of Los Angeles; there is a stop in Woodland
Hills. Greyhound also provides service to the Oxnard
Transportation Center in the middle of town.

For those coming from Los Angeles, the straight-forward access is to
head west (the signs read north-bound, but the direction of travel is
west, due to the geography of the area) on the 101 freeway. From
the Burbank Airport, the 101 is due south a couple of miles; from LAX
(Los Angeles International), take I-405 (the San Diego Freeway) north
about 13 miles to the 101 junction.

The 101 exits Ventura County near the town of Carpinteria, after a few
miles of travel past communities with names such as Mussel Sholls and
Faria Beach: shades of Frankie Avalon and Annette Funicello! The
101 eventually turns northward at Gaviota, a half-hour past Santa
Barbara, and reaches San Francisco 5-6 hours later. Those coming
from anywhere along the Pacific Coast will find the 101 to be a
much-more interesting drive than I-5, at least south of the
Cascade/Siskiyou Mountains region.

There are alternates to the Ventura Freeway. CA 118 runs along
the northern edge of the San Fernando Valley, and enters Simi
Valley. At the western end of Simi, the freeway bends to the
south and becomes CA 23, which links with the 101 about five miles to
the south. This route is often a lot less crowded than the 101,
and is recommended for those coming from the Burbank Airport, or
driving in from points north and east on CA 14, through Lancaster and
Palmdale.

Those who have to land at LAX late in the afternoon of a weekday will
likely find the San Diego Freeway to be a parking lot. For them,
taking I-10 (the Santa Monica Freeway) westward through Santa Monica
may save time, and it will certainly be far more scenic. I-10
drops you onto Pacific Coast Highway (CA 1: PCH to the locals), which
runs along the shore through Malibu, past several State Parks and
Beaches, and eventually past Point Mugu and Mugu Rock: Oxnard, Port
Hueneme (h'Why-knee-me) and Ventura are straight ahead, and Camarillo
is to your right, when PCH passes the western end of the Santa Monica
Mountains.

Those driving southward through California's Central Valley will find
that taking CA 118 westward (the junction is in Santa Clarita, just
north of Magic Mountain, which is easily visible just south of the
junction) is much quicker than going all the way into the San Fernando
Valley. The 118 is peppered with a number of fresh produce
stands, the most-famous of which is at Rancho Camulos, where Helen
Jackson got the idea for her novel, ***Ramona***.
Construction a few years ago widened and straightened out many of the
narrow curves that made this road a tricky and even dangerous
drive. It is now one of the safest highways in Southern
California. It passes through the tiny community of Piru, and the
towns of Fillmore (be especially-certain to obey speed limits here!)
and Santa Paula before ending in Ventura at the 101.

Finally, for those who enjoy a bit of a driving challenge, with some
outstanding mountain scenery, consider driving down CA 33, from the
Central Valley. After a half-hour of travel through the Cuyama
Valley, this road begins to wind into the Santa Ynez Mountains, past
Ozena Fire Station, Pine Mountain and several Wilderness Areas in the
Los Padres National Forest. After 10 miles or so in the narrow
valley at the top of the range, the road begins to wind back down,
through Wheeler Gorge. At the top of the Gorge are some stunning
views of the ocean and the Channel Islands. The road winds around
on three sides of the gorge, with some hairpin turns. This route
is not for the squeamish, but it will reward you with a number of spots
to pull over and enjoy some of the best of Ventura County's natural
beauty. After most of the descent is completed, you'll pass the
Wheeler Gorge NFS campground, then pass through a tunnel. Just a
mile or so ahead is the town of Ojai, home of one of the premiere
classical music festivals in the world, the pre-eminent amateur tennis
tournament in the world and a number of artist's galleries, unique
shops and eateries, and several exclusive private schools. The
last few miles of the 33 are easy and the highway ends at the 101, near
Old Ventura and the mission.
