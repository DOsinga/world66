Estes Park sits at 7,500 feet in a high mountain meadow on the eastern flank of the Colorado Rockies, where four roads from the plains converge on the gateway to **[Rocky Mountain National Park](/northamerica/unitedstates/colorado/rockymountain)**. The town is small — a few thousand permanent residents — but it swells dramatically through the summer with hikers, climbers, and bus-tour traffic heading west onto Trail Ridge Road, the highest paved through-road in the United States.

The setting is genuinely spectacular: from the main street you look straight up at **Longs Peak**, the only fourteener in the park, with the granite walls of the Front Range crowding in on every side. Elk graze on the golf course in the autumn rut, when the bulls come down from the high country and bugle through the night. The town itself is more functional than picturesque — souvenir shops, fudge counters, river-tubing rental — with one large exception: the **Stanley Hotel**, a white Georgian pile from 1909 that inspired Stephen King's *The Shining* after he stayed there in the off-season. Even if you do not stay, it is worth a drink on the porch for the view alone.

Come outside the July–August peak if you can. September and early October — elk season, with golden aspens lining the canyons — is the best time of year here.
