Telluride sits in a dramatic box canyon in the San Juan Mountains of southwestern Colorado, ringed on three sides by steep peaks that rise to over 13,000 feet. The setting is extraordinary — waterfalls cascade down the cliffs at the end of town, and the peaks close in so tightly that the town gets very little direct sun in winter. It started as a rough mining camp in the 1870s and has since evolved into one of Colorado's most beautiful mountain towns.

The town itself is small, just a dozen blocks long, with a mix of restored Victorian buildings and newer construction along Colorado Avenue. It is walkable, quiet, and surprisingly unpretentious for a ski town of its caliber. A free gondola connects Telluride to Mountain Village, the resort area on the other side of the ridge, making it easy to go between the two without a car. The skiing is excellent — steep terrain, reliable snow, and far fewer crowds than the big resorts closer to Denver.

In summer, Telluride comes alive with festivals. The Bluegrass Festival in June is legendary, the Film Festival in September draws serious cinema crowds, and the Jazz Festival in August fills the town with music. Between festivals, the hiking is superb. The trail to Bridal Veil Falls, the tallest free-falling waterfall in Colorado, starts right at the end of the main street. The Bear Creek Trail and the hike to Blue Lake are both excellent half-day outings. For food, La Marmotte serves refined French cooking in a converted ice house, and Brown Dog Pizza is the local go-to for something casual.
