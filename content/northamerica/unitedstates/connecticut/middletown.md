Middletown sits on a broad bend of the Connecticut River, roughly in the geographic center of the state. It is a university town — Wesleyan University has been here since 1831 — and that shapes almost everything about it: the bookshops, the cafes, the arts scene, the general sense that people here have opinions about things.

Main Street is the obvious spine of the city. It runs uphill from the river, long and wide enough to feel genuinely urban for a city this size, with a mix of old brick storefronts, independent restaurants, and bars that fill up on weekend evenings. The area around Wesleyan's campus bleeds into it naturally, so there is a lively undergraduate energy even when classes are not quite in session.

The Connecticut River itself is underused as an attraction, which is part of its appeal. You can walk or cycle along the bank, rent a kayak, or simply watch the water. The river here is wide and slow, and on a quiet afternoon it feels like the rest of Connecticut barely exists.

Middletown is not a destination that people cross continents to reach, but it rewards a day or an overnight if you are traveling through central Connecticut. It is more interesting than its reputation suggests, and considerably livelier than the surrounding suburbs.
