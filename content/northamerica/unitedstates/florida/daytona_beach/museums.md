Located only minutes south of Daytona Beach in the quaint coastal town of Ponce Inlet, the Ponce de Leon Inlet Lighthouse stands like a quiet sentinel of days gone by. Considered by many to be one of the most beautiful and well proportioned lighthouses in the United States, this National Historic Landmark is located on the north side of Ponce Inlet where the Atlantic Ocean, Halifax, and Indian Rivers merge.

Visitors are provided with oportunity to climb 203 steps to the top of the lighthouse where they are rewarded with breathtaking views of the inlet, Atlantic coastline, and converging river systems below. Carressed by a clean salt breeze, visitors can look out and easily imagine what life was like here during simpler times when the peninsula was a remote and isolated location renowned for its excellent sport fishing and balmy climate.

At ground level, guests are provided with the rare opportunity to explore one of the last light stations in the United states that still possess all of its original structures. Each building, lovingly restored to its original condition, houses numerous exhibits that focus on different aspects of the light station's and local area's history. Visitors can take either a self-guided tour of the historic grounds or ask for a guided tour led by one of the lighthouse's volunteer docents.

No tour of the light station is complete without a visit to the Aryes Davies Lens Exhibit Building. Considered by many to be the finest Fresnel Lens exhibit in the nation, the Aryes Davies Exhibit houses several rare and beautiful Fresnel Lenses that visitors can examine up close. Included in the collection are the 1st order revolving Fresnel Lens that orignally sat atop the Cape Canaveral Lighthouse to the south and the 1st order fixed Fresnel Lens that warned mariners at sea from the lantern room of the Ponce Inlet Lighthouse until being replaced in 1933. Visitors will also learn about the evolution of lighthouses and beacons from crude wooden fires lit atop sand dune to the magestic towers that dot our coastlines today.

Once your visit to the light station is complete, visitors can grab a bite to eat at any of the excellent seafood restaurants located nearby. Those who crave a little more excitement can charter a fishing boat and any of the nearby marina, rent a jet ski at the inlet, or fish off the stone jetty just down the road. A trip to Ponce Inlet and its historic lighthouse will provide the visitor with a remarkable change of pace that is only minutes away from downtown Daytona Beach.

Known as Mosquito Inlet before tourism and the Florida landboom of the early 20th Century encouraged residents to change its name to Ponce de Leon Inlet in hopes of attracting more visitors, the area has been watched over by this magestic 175 ft. tower since 1887.
