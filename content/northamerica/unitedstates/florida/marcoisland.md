Marco Island is the largest of the Ten Thousand Islands — the maze of mangrove keys and shell-sand beaches that runs down the south-west corner of Florida between [Naples](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/naples) and the **Everglades**. The northern half of the island has been comprehensively built out into a resort town of low-rise condominiums and golf courses, but the long crescent of **Crescent Beach**, broad and pure white, remains genuinely beautiful, and the gulf sunsets here are some of the best on the Florida coast.

The southern tip is the much wilder **Tigertail Beach**, separated from the open gulf by Sand Dollar Island — a sandbar accessible by wading at low tide and known for its shelling. Even better, the small launch at the harbour runs day trips down into the maze of the **Ten Thousand Islands National Wildlife Refuge**: a tangled archipelago of uninhabited mangrove islets where you can fish, kayak past dolphins and manatees, and land on shell beaches that have not been built on since the **Calusa**, the indigenous people whose midden mounds still rise above the mangroves.

Marco Island is the largest of the Ten Thousand Islands, the maze of mangrove keys that drape the southwest Gulf coast of Florida between Naples and the Everglades. It is also the most developed by a wide margin: the north end of the island is wall-to-wall high-rise condos and beach resorts along **Crescent Beach**, a four-mile sweep of fine white shell sand that is the main reason most visitors come.

Step away from the resort strip and Marco quickly becomes interesting. The southern tip preserves quiet residential canals and **Tigertail Beach**, where a tidal lagoon shelters wading birds and a long sandbar reaches out into the Gulf. From the marinas you can hire boats or join guided tours into the **Ten Thousand Islands National Wildlife Refuge** and **Everglades National Park** — manatees in the channels in winter, dolphins in the wakes year-round, and the chance to land on undeveloped shell beaches like Cape Romano with its half-sunken dome houses standing in the surf.

Marco was inhabited for at least 1,500 years by the Calusa people, whose shell mounds underlie much of the island; the small **Marco Island Historical Museum** tells that story and houses the famous wooden Key Marco Cat, one of the finest pre-Columbian artefacts found in North America. Most travellers come for sun and beach; pair it with a couple of days in **Everglades City** to the south for a fuller picture of the region.
