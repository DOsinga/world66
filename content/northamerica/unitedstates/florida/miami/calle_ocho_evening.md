Arrive on [Calle Ocho](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/little_havana) — SW 8th Street — around five in the afternoon, when the light is going golden and the street is busy but not overwhelmed. Start at Máximo Gómez Park, known universally as Domino Park, between 14th and 15th Avenues. Under the covered pavilion, elderly Cuban men play dominoes with the focused seriousness they have brought to this corner since the 1970s. You can watch from outside the fence. It is one of the most genuinely Miami things you can do, and it costs nothing.

Walk west along Calle Ocho. Stop at El Titan de Bronze at 1071 SW 8th for a hand-rolled cigar — the rollers work in the window and you can watch before you buy. Pick up a cafecito from one of the ventanitas as you go: walk-up windows dispensing small cups of intensely sweet Cuban espresso, the correct fuel for the evening ahead.

[Café La Trova](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/cafe_la_trova) at 971 SW 8th is the first stop of the evening. The front room is a classic café; walk through to the back and you are in a 1950s Havana supper club — live bolero music, bartenders in guayaberas, daiquiris that are among the best in Miami. Have one drink here and soak in the room.

For the rest of the evening: [Ball & Chain](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/ball_and_chain) two blocks east at 1513 SW 8th. This 1935 venue has been a jazz club, a biker bar, and a shuttered ruin before its current incarnation as Calle Ocho's most atmospheric live-music spot. Salsa bands play most nights, the mojitos are strong, and the back patio under the lights is one of the best places in Miami to end up on a warm evening.
