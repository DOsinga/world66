The north end of South Beach — above Lincoln Road, roughly between the Convention Center and Collins Park — holds a cluster of museums that together make one of the better museum afternoons in Florida. None is enormous; all three can be done comfortably in a single afternoon without feeling rushed.

Start at the [Wolfsonian-FIU](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/wolfsonian) on Washington Avenue, which opens at noon on most days. This is the most distinctive of the three: a museum of decorative and propaganda arts covering 1850 to 1950, housed in a magnificently restored 1927 storage facility. The collection — over 180,000 objects — spans industrial design, political posters, architectural drawings, and furniture, all focused on how design shapes political and social movements. The exhibitions are consistently thoughtful and the building itself, with its ornate Mediterranean Revival facade, is one of the best-preserved interiors in South Beach.

A five-minute walk north brings you to the [Holocaust Memorial](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/holocaust_memorial) on Meridian Avenue, whose address — 1933-1945 — is itself intentional. The 42-foot bronze arm reaching skyward, covered with life-size bronze figures, is one of the most arresting Holocaust memorials in the country. The surrounding garden, reflecting pool, and tunnel of names create a sequence that takes about 30 minutes to move through properly. Free to visit.

Finish at the [Bass Museum of Art](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/bass_museum) in Collins Park — Miami Beach's contemporary art museum, in a 1930s Art Deco building expanded by Arata Isozaki. The permanent collection spans Old Masters to contemporary photography, but the rotating exhibitions are the draw: ambitious, site-specific installations in the dramatic central gallery that make the Bass worth checking before you visit to see what is showing.

Dinner from here: Lincoln Road is a five-minute walk south — a pedestrian street with restaurant options in every direction. Choose carefully; the tourist-facing places on the main strip are average, but the side streets off Lincoln have better options.
