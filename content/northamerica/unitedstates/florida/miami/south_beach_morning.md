Set your alarm. South Beach in the morning is a different city from the South Beach of brochures and nightlife footage. Get to Ocean Drive by seven and you will have the [Art Deco Historic District](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/art_deco_district) largely to yourself — the pastel facades catching the early light, the hotel lobbies quiet, the street empty except for joggers. The 1930s and 40s architecture that lines Ocean Drive, Collins, and Washington Avenue between 5th and 23rd Streets is the largest concentration of Art Deco in the world, and it looks extraordinary in the long morning light before the awnings go up and the restaurant touts appear.

Walk north along Ocean Drive, then double back down Collins. Detour one block west to Washington Avenue to see the [Wolfsonian-FIU](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/wolfsonian) — it does not open until ten, but the 1927 storage-facility-turned-museum is worth seeing from the outside. The building's Mediterranean Revival facade on Washington has decorative ironwork and carved stone details that reward attention.

The beach itself is west of Ocean Drive — wide, white, and well-maintained, with lifeguard stations painted in the same Deco colours as the hotels. Swim before nine and the water is calm and the sand almost empty. By ten the sun is already strong; by noon the beach is full. Come back in the afternoon if you must, but you will have missed the best of it.

Breakfast after the swim: there are ventanitas — walk-up windows serving Cuban coffee — throughout South Beach. A cortadito and a pastelito at a sidewalk counter is the correct Miami morning.
