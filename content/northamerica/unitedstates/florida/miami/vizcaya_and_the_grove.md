Go early. [Vizcaya](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/vizcaya) opens at nine-thirty and the gardens are best before the tour groups arrive. The estate — an Italian Renaissance villa built in 1916 by industrialist James Deering, surrounded by ten acres of formal gardens tumbling down to Biscayne Bay — is one of Miami's most extraordinary places, and it rewards time. The house has 34 rooms filled with European furniture and tapestries spanning four centuries, but the gardens are the reason to come: box parterres, fountains, grottoes, a stone-carved barge breakwater sitting in the water. In the morning light, with the bay behind it, the place looks like it was airlifted from the Italian coast.

Allow two hours for Vizcaya, then drive or walk north along the bayfront into [Coconut Grove](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/coconut_grove). Miami's oldest neighbourhood has a leafy, unhurried character that stands apart from the rest of the city — streets canopied by banyans, bougainvillea on the fences, Biscayne Bay visible between the houses. Peacock Park at the waterfront is a good place to stop and watch the boats.

A short walk from the park: the [Barnacle Historic State Park](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/barnacle_historic), where Miami's oldest house still on its original site sits behind a hardwood hammock on the bay. The 1891 house was built by Commodore Ralph Munroe to catch bay breezes and survive hurricanes — both of which it has done for over 130 years. The grounds are shaded and quiet; the guided tours run on weekends and are worth taking. The walk down to the waterfront is one of the calmest minutes you will find in Miami.

Lunch in Coconut Grove after: the side streets around Grand Avenue have small restaurants that serve the neighbourhood. Sit outside if you can.
