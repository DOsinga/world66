Saturday morning, and [Wynwood](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/wynwood) is still half-asleep. Start at [Enriqueta's Sandwich Shop](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/enriqueta_s) on NE 29th Street — a cash-only Cuban diner that has not changed much in decades despite the transformation happening around it. Breakfast here is the pan con bistec: a steak sandwich that arrives fast and costs very little. There is outdoor seating on the sidewalk, and on weekend mornings the place is full of the mix of people that Wynwood still manages to attract despite the rents.

From Enriqueta's, walk south to [Panther Coffee](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/panther_coffee) on NW 2nd Avenue — the original Wynwood location of Miami's best specialty roaster, and effectively the neighbourhood's living room. Get the espresso or the cold brew. The large open-air space, half indoors and half out, is a good base from which to plan the rest of the morning.

The [Wynwood Walls](/northamerica/unitedstates/florida/miami/wynwood) are two blocks away: a curated outdoor museum of murals on the walls of former warehouses at NW 2nd Avenue and 25th Street. The work rotates and the scale is large enough that you need time to really see it. Free to walk past; there is a small admission to enter the main courtyard. Beyond the Walls, nearly every surface in Wynwood carries art — walk north and south along NW 2nd Avenue and turn down any cross street.

By noon, the galleries start opening. The Rubell Museum on NW 29th Street — one of the world's largest private contemporary art collections in a converted DEA warehouse — is the most substantial stop. By early afternoon, when the heat becomes serious and the crowds arrive, Wynwood's restaurant density makes lunch an easy decision. The neighbourhood has some of Miami's best casual eating, in spaces that reflect where the art world and the warehouse district have converged.
