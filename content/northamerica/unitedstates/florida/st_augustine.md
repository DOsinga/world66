St. Augustine is the **oldest continuously occupied European settlement** in what is now the United States, founded by the Spanish in 1565 — more than half a century before the Pilgrims landed at Plymouth Rock. Tucked on the Atlantic coast of northern Florida about halfway between Jacksonville and Daytona Beach, it retains a genuinely un-American feel: the cobbled lanes, coquina-stone fortifications, and colonial houses of its old town make it look and feel more like a small Spanish colonial city in Latin America than anywhere else on the US east coast.

The central monument is the **Castillo de San Marcos**, an enormous 17th-century star-shaped fort built of coquina (a soft local limestone of compacted shells) that proved remarkably effective at absorbing British cannonballs during several sieges and still dominates the harbour. Behind it, the **Plaza de la Constitución** is one of the oldest public squares in the United States. Running south from the plaza, **St. George Street** is the pedestrianised historic core — an easy half-day wander past the old Spanish garrison quarters, the **Ximenez-Fatio House**, **Peña-Peck House**, and the reconstructed **Spanish Quarter** living-history museum. The outer city holds a cluster of extraordinary Gilded Age buildings left by the railroad magnate **Henry Flagler**, who developed St. Augustine as a winter resort in the 1880s — his former **Ponce de Leon Hotel** (now Flagler College, open for tours) is a Moorish-Spanish revival masterpiece by the architects Carrère and Hastings. Across the street, the **Lightner Museum** (in the former Alcazar Hotel) has an eclectic Gilded Age collection.

St. Augustine also has good beaches — **Anastasia State Park** is the best — and is a useful overnight stop for anyone driving between the Carolinas and Miami. Allow at least a full day inside the old city, more if you want to walk the beach and visit the **St. Augustine Lighthouse**.
