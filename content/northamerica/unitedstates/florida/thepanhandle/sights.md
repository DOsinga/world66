---
title: "Sights"
type: section
---

Pensacola has a number of interesting museums relating to the city's history. Don't mention St. Augustine while in Pensacola; the locals are quite violently convinced that their town deserves the "oldest city" crown. 

Immediately south of Pensacola is Gulf Breeze, a quiet beach town with a history of UFO sightings. Maybe this is because of the Navy air bases nearby, maybe it's the local vibe, maybe it's the fact that one of the town's residents is good with a Polaroid. Stay long enough and you might see something yourself, but don't hold your breath. 

East from Gulf Breeze along the Miracle Strip Parkway are mile after mile of pure white sand. Don't bother resisting the temptation to stop every couple of miles and wiggle your toes in it. You'll soon pass through Eglin Air Force Base and into Fort Walton Beach. There isn't much in Fort Walton Beach; it's a pleasant military community with six or seven towns of variously bizarre name-Mary Ester, Niceville, Shalimar, Valparaiso… Across Choctawhatchee Bay (literally, Black Water) is the town of Destin, one of the current hot spots for vacationing and retirement. Nice climate, nice beach--but you don't need to stop. 

Further along the road comes the infinitely more interesting town of Grayton Beach, recently named the best beach town in America. Stop here. There's not a lot to do--it is a beach town, after all, you're supposed to go to the beach--but it's a fun place to wander through and have lunch. 

Not too far down the road is Seaside, a new community planned by Andres Duany and Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk of the University of Miami, according to the principles of neo-traditional town design. The 1998 movie "The Truman Show" was filmed here. You'll want to explore-and you should do it on foot, as Duany and Plater-Zyberk intended.

The next town of note will be Panama City Beach--you'll know you're there because there is an enormous brown ship rising out of the water, which is in fact a decent restaurant. Fair warning: the streets will be clogged during the spring, and the beach will be packed with college students. If you're a college student, this is pretty cool, and is probably the reason you're here anyway. If you're not a college student, this will be mildly frightening and you probably shouldn't linger.

For those who tire of the beach but still want to play in the water, there is Shipwreck Landing, a water park across the intracoastal waterway in the city of Panama City itself. One of the better water parks in the state, Shipwreck Landing is usually pretty full but is well worth the admission price. 

On down the highway you'll pass through the town of Port St. Joe. Port St. Joe used to be a thriving paper mill town, but you'll note as you drive through that the mill is closed. Inexplicably, the company that owned the mill is called St. Joe Paper, so you'd think they'd keep the mill in Port St. Joe operating. Do the local economy a favor and stop for lunch or gas or something as you pass through. Port St. Joe is slowly developing a tourism economy, pinning their hopes on the sparkling beaches of the St. Joe Peninsula. If you haven't had enough of the beaches yet, this is a nice one, but facilities are still somewhat limited and there aren't many places to stay. 

Apalachicola will come next. This is the place to east oysters if you want them. The raw ones are an acquired taste--try them fried or steamed instead. If you like hunting through antiques shops, Apalachicola is your dream come true. Adventuresome types may not find much of note here, though. You can, however, take the causeway across to St. George island for one last round of beautiful beaches and crystal clear waters.
