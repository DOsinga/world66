Americus is a small city in southwest Georgia, about 130 miles south of Atlanta. It sits in the heart of Georgia's peanut country, surrounded by flat farmland — not a typical tourist stop, but it earns its place on the map for a few very specific reasons.

The most compelling is proximity. [Andersonville National Historic Site](/northamerica/unitedstates/georgia/andersonville) is 10 miles north — one of the most sobering places in the American South, where nearly 13,000 Union prisoners died during the Civil War. You don't visit Andersonville lightly, and Americus is the natural base for it. Fifteen miles west lies [Plains](/northamerica/unitedstates/georgia/plains), the tiny hometown of Jimmy Carter, where you can walk through the old railroad depot that served as his 1976 campaign headquarters and visit the Jimmy Carter National Historical Park.

Downtown Americus itself has a dignified, slightly faded quality. The restored Windsor Hotel (1892) dominates the main street — a grand Victorian pile that opened when Americus was a prosperous cotton trading center and has been carefully brought back to life. The surrounding historic district has a good collection of antebellum and late-19th-century commercial buildings worth a slow walk.

One surprise: Habitat for Humanity was founded here in 1976, and the organization's Global Village and Discovery Center, just outside town, tells the story of the movement and its work worldwide. It's more interesting than it sounds.

Americus is honest about what it is — a working small city, not a polished tourist destination. But if you're making the Andersonville pilgrimage, which you should, spend a night here rather than driving back to Atlanta the same day.
