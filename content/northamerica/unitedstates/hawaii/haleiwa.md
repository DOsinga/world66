Haleiwa is the gateway to Oahu's North Shore and the unofficial capital of Hawaiian surf culture. The small town sits at the mouth of the Anahulu River, where a photogenic rainbow bridge marks the start of a two-lane strip of surf shops, shave ice stands, food trucks, and art galleries. It feels a world apart from the high-rises of [Honolulu](/northamerica/unitedstates/hawaii/honolulu) and Waikiki, an hour's drive to the south.

In winter, the North Shore transforms. The beaches between Haleiwa and Sunset Beach — including Pipeline, the most famous wave in surfing — produce swells that draw the world's best surfers and crowds of spectators who line the sand to watch. The Banzai Pipeline breaks just offshore, close enough to see the faces of riders dropping into the barrel. Even if you do not surf, watching from the beach at Ehukai Beach Park on a big day is unforgettable.

In summer the ocean calms and the same beaches become gentle enough for swimming and snorkelling. Shark's Cove, a few miles up the coast, has some of Oahu's best reef snorkelling in calm conditions. Back in town, Matsumoto's shave ice has been serving its rainbow-coloured cones since 1951 and always has a queue, and the garlic shrimp trucks along Kamehameha Highway between Haleiwa and Kahuku are worth the drive on their own.
