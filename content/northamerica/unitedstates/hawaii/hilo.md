Hilo is the largest town on the Big Island of Hawaii and the quieter, rainier, more local counterpart to the tourist-heavy Kona coast on the other side. It rains here. A lot. Hilo averages more than three metres of rain a year, which makes it one of the wettest cities in the United States — and also one of the greenest, with lush gardens, wild waterfalls, and a general feeling that the jungle is always about to reclaim the place. That climate keeps the beach-resort crowds away and leaves Hilo as the Big Island at its most authentic: a low-rise, slightly faded plantation-era town where the rhythms of everyday Hawaiian life still set the pace.

The town itself is small and walkable. **Downtown Hilo**, lined with handsome wooden storefronts from the early 20th century that survived a devastating 1946 tsunami, has independent bookshops, thrift stores, galleries, and the excellent **Hilo Farmers' Market** at the corner of Kamehameha Avenue and Mamo Street, open daily but at its fullest on Wednesdays and Saturdays. **Liliuokalani Gardens**, on the north-eastern edge of Hilo Bay, is the largest Japanese-style garden outside Japan — a quiet grid of koi ponds, stone lanterns, and arched bridges built in honour of Hawaii's immigrant Japanese community. Just beyond it, **Coconut Island** is reached by a small pedestrian bridge and makes a pleasant stop for a swim and a view back at the town.

The real reasons to come to Hilo are outside the town. **Hawaii Volcanoes National Park**, 45 minutes south, is one of the most extraordinary national parks in the country — with a drive-in caldera, active lava flows (conditions depending), and walks through steaming craters and lava tubes. **Akaka Falls**, twenty minutes north, drops 135 metres in a single plume through the rainforest. **Rainbow Falls**, just outside Hilo itself, is the go-to morning stop when the sun catches the mist. And **Mauna Kea**, Hawaii's highest mountain at 4,207 metres, is an hour's drive up the saddle road — the summit observatories and the stargazing programme at the visitor centre at 2,800 metres are among the finest in the world.

Every April, Hilo hosts the **Merrie Monarch Festival**, the most important hula competition in the world — hula halau (schools) from across the Hawaiian islands and the wider Pacific compete for a week in both ancient and modern forms. Book accommodation months ahead if you want to come for it. And eat the local food: loco moco (apparently invented here), malasadas at the bakeries on Kilauea Avenue, poke from the supermarket counter, and shave ice from the old-fashioned stand downtown.
