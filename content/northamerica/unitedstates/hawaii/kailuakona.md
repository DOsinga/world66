Kailua-Kona sits on the dry, leeward side of the Big Island — the side that doesn't get the rain. While the rest of Hawaii can be lush and overcast, Kona is reliably sunny, which is exactly why it became the island's tourist hub. The town stretches along Alii Drive, a seaside road lined with shops, restaurants, and lava-rock shores where the Pacific crashes in hard.

This is a working town as much as a resort one. Kona coffee grows on the slopes above the coast, where the elevation and morning clouds create near-perfect conditions. The coffee farms are worth a visit — the contrast between the beachside town and the cooler, greener highlands a few miles up the mountain is striking.

The underwater life here is exceptional. The calm, clear waters on the leeward side draw divers and snorkelers year-round, and the manta ray night dives off the coast are genuinely unforgettable — one of those experiences that's hyped up but actually delivers. Whale watching runs through the winter months when humpbacks arrive from Alaska.

Kona is also the finish line for the Ironman World Championship, held every October. If you're here for it, the town buzzes with an energy unlike anything else. If you're not, you'll still notice the unusual number of lean people in spandex jogging along Alii Drive at all hours.
