Kaua'i is the oldest and northernmost of the main Hawaiian Islands — an almost circular volcanic island of about 1,430 square kilometres, less developed and far greener than either Oʻahu or Maui. Its nickname, the **"Garden Island"**, is more than marketing: the interior is a near-continuous sheet of jungle and waterfall-filled valleys, and **Mount Waialeale** at the centre is one of the wettest spots on earth, averaging some 9,500 mm of rain a year. The result is that Kaua'i is the most dramatic-looking of the Hawaiian islands. It was used as the backdrop for *Jurassic Park*, *King Kong*, and *South Pacific*, among many others.

The three regions of the island each have their own character. The **south coast**, around Poʻipū, has the most reliable sunshine, the gentlest surf, and the most family-friendly resorts. The **east coast**, around Kapaʻa and the capital **Līhuʻe**, is the workaday side of the island — airport, ferry wharf, the old plantation towns. The **north shore**, around Hanalei and Princeville, is the postcard Kaua'i: rainy, lush, and spectacular, with the perfect crescent beach at **Hanalei Bay** and the taro fields of the **Hanalei National Wildlife Refuge**. And on the far west side, beyond the road's end, lies the **Nā Pali Coast** — 27 kilometres of sheer green cliffs plunging straight into the Pacific, accessible only by foot on the 17-km **Kalalau Trail**, by kayak in summer, or by boat and helicopter tours from Port Allen.

The two other unmissable Kaua'i sights are both inland. **Waimea Canyon**, sometimes called the "Grand Canyon of the Pacific", is a 16 km long, 1 km deep gorge in dramatic red and green — a drive up Highway 550 leads to half a dozen lookouts. The road continues to **Kōkeʻe State Park** with trails and the extraordinary **Kalalau Lookout** over the Nā Pali coast. Kaua'i is smaller and less developed than Maui or Oʻahu — no building is taller than a coconut palm, by county law — and for that reason feels closer to what the rest of Hawaii used to be.
