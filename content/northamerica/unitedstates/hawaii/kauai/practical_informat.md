[Kauai](/northamerica/unitedstates/hawaii/kauai) is the 4th largest and oldest of the Hawaiian Islands. It is 552
square miles and is a circular shape. In 2000,
the population of the island 58,303. The Kauaian dialect of the Hawaiian language is know for being very
specific with many similarities
to old Polynesian.

Kauai was formed from
volcanoes and has the highest peaks at Kawaikini, reaching 5,243 feet
up, and Mount Waialeale measuring in at 5148 feet. Many waterfalls are
located on the east side of Mount Waialeale due to it being one of the
wettest places known on Earth. Waimea Canyon, some 3,000 feet deep, was
formed by the Waimea River that runs through the island.

In
2007, over 1 million tourists visited Kauai, primarily from the U.S.
and Japan. Clearly tourism is a very large contributor to the Kauai
economy. Most jobs held on the island are hotel/tourism/food service
jobs. Government and agriculture jobs also play an important roll in
Kauai society. Sugar was a large export, but many of the plantations
have been shut down and replaced by ranches. Guava, coffee and
pineapple are also grown on this island.

Kapaa is the largest
city on the island with a population of about 10,000. The old capital,
Waimea, was the first place in Hawaii that the famous Naval Captain
James Cook reached, just one year before his death.

The luau is popular all
over the islands of Hawaii. Tropical fruit-flavored cakes,
spare ribs, chicken and pork stew, and barbecue of any sort are popular
foods in Hawaii. During a luau, the native Hawaiian women
will dance in their traditional grass skirts.

Since
Hawaii is a state of the United States, the U.S. dollar is used as the monetary
unity and no passport is needed for American travelers.
