Oahu is Hawaii's most visited and most populous island, home to Honolulu and the state capital. It's where the tourist infrastructure is concentrated — Waikiki's tower-block hotels, Pearl Harbor, and the North Shore's legendary surf breaks — but also where much of Hawaii's genuine cultural and culinary life happens.

Waikiki, the beach resort neighbourhood east of Honolulu, is the arrival point for most visitors. The beach itself is beautiful — warm, calm, and backed by Diamond Head volcano — though inevitably crowded. Beyond Waikiki, the island opens up considerably. The North Shore is a 45-minute drive away: Haleiwa town, the Banzai Pipeline, Sunset Beach, and Waimea Bay are all here, with the world's best big-wave surfing from November to March and calm, swimmable water in summer. Kailua on the windward (eastern) coast is a more residential beach town with excellent kayaking and consistently good swimming conditions.

Pearl Harbor — the USS Arizona Memorial and Battleship Missouri — is the island's most visited historical site: the memorial is sober and affecting. The Bishop Museum in Honolulu is the best museum of Hawaiian and Pacific culture in the world. Hanauma Bay, now carefully protected, offers the best snorkelling easily accessible from Honolulu. The Honolulu Academy of Arts has a remarkable permanent collection.

The H1, H2, and H3 freeways connect the main parts of the island. Bus service (TheBus) covers most tourist destinations. Traffic in and around Honolulu is notorious; plan accordingly.
