Here is an important list of practical things to know when visiting Hawaii.

**
**

**Locals versus Hawaiian**

In general, when referring to local people in Hawaii, refer to them as
locals not Hawaiians. Not all locals are native Hawaiians because Hawaii has an eclectic
mix of Eastern (Japanese, Chinese, Korean) and Western (Portuguese, Puerto
Rican) cultures. When something is specific to the native Hawaiian culture,
locals will surely point it out to you. Also, don't be afraid of the haole term
(used to refer to people of white skin complexion). Even though this term used
to be quite derogatory in the past, it has become a common term in day to day
Hawaiian culture.**

The Bus**

The Bus refers to the public bus transportation system in Oahu.
Oahu is the only Hawaiian island that has a
formal bus system. The current bus fare is $2.00 and it allows you to get one
transfer. Buses usually stop running after midnight so remember this if you're
planning to go out late at night and come back to your hotel using the bus. Ala Moana Center, Waikiki and
Downtown are major hubs for connecting bus lines. **

Hawaiian Language**

There are certain words that you need to know to make your stay in Hawaii easier. Mahalo
means thank you. You will find this word on most trash cans; it is a common
mistake that tourists think that mahalo is trash in Hawaiian. Aloha does not
only mean hello, it also means good bye and "to love" or love. When
saying aloha, extend the pronunciation of the "o", the longer the
"o", the more aloha you express. Wahine can mean woman, women or
female. All of the University
of Hawaii female sports
team use this word, for example the Wahine Volleyball Team. Kokua means
cooperation. You will frequently hear mahalo for your kokua , which would
translate to thank you for your cooperation. Pidgin is the way that locals
speak English. "Ono" means delicious and "ono grinds"
usually means tasty snacks.

**

First Friday and Last Friday**

Friday is one of the most popular days of the week, but in Oahu
it's extra special because here the locals celebrate the first and the last
Friday of each month with two special events. The First Friday refers to the
movement of downtown businesses and restaurants to make special events every
first Friday of the month to attract more visitors. These events range from art
exhibitions with wine tastings, street performances and special discounts and
promotions. The Last Friday refers to the monthly "Art After Dark"
event from the Honolulu Academy of Arts at 900 SouthBeretania Street, where the
museum turns from 6 p.m. to 9 p.m. into one giant, extravagant party with a
different theme every month.

**The Aloha Way

**Locals always will refer to the special way that they do things in Hawaii as the aloha way.
Ranging from driving (Drive with Aloha ) to living (Living Aloha ), you will
find that locals are aware of the importance of preserving the traditions,
customs and culture that thousands of visitors come to discover every year in Hawaii. Hawaii is very dependent
in the tourism industry and locals are very aware of that. That is why they
welcome visitors to Hawaii
with open hearts. It is important for visitors to be aware of local traditions
and customs so that these wonderful islands maintain not only its natural
beauty but also its culture.**

Makai versus Mauka**

A common way to give directions in Hawaii
involves the use of makai and mauka. Makai means heading towards the ocean or
shore, while mauka means heading towards the mountain or inland.
