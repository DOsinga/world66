Twin Falls sits on the rim of the **Snake River Canyon**, a 500-foot gash that splits southern Idaho east to west. The town itself, built on the irrigated plains of the Magic Valley, is unassuming — a college town of about 50,000 with a tidy downtown and an agricultural economy of dairy farms, sugar beets and potatoes. The reason to come is what is hidden below the rim.

A few miles east of town, the river plunges 212 feet over **Shoshone Falls** — taller than Niagara, and in spring runoff much wider too. The falls were a major obstacle for early travellers on the Oregon Trail and remain a startling sight to anyone arriving from the dry sagebrush plateau. Closer to town, the **Perrine Bridge** spans the canyon at 486 feet and is one of the few places in the United States where BASE jumping is legal year-round; on a fair afternoon you can stand on the bridge and watch jumpers leap off and parachute to the canyon floor.

The canyon was also the site of **Evel Knievel's** botched 1974 attempt to jump the Snake on a steam-powered rocket; the launch ramp is still visible on the south rim, a slumping earth mound that has become a low-key pilgrimage site. Beyond the canyon, Twin Falls makes a useful base for visits to **Craters of the Moon National Monument** and the lava plains to the north, or as a stop on the long drive between Salt Lake City and Boise.
