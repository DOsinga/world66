Alton is a small city perched on limestone bluffs above the Mississippi River, about 15 miles north of downtown St. Louis. The setting is dramatic — the river bends sharply here, and the town climbs up the bluffs in tiers of 19th-century brick buildings. It has the feel of a river town that has seen better days economically but retains genuine character, with a growing arts scene and a lively restaurant strip along Broadway.

The town has deep historical roots. The last of the famous Lincoln-Douglas debates took place here in 1858, and a larger-than-life statue of Robert Wadlow — the tallest person in recorded history, who grew up in Alton — stands near the southern end of town. The Alton Museum of History and Art covers the city's role in the Civil War and the Underground Railroad. Elijah Lovejoy, an abolitionist newspaper editor, was murdered here by a pro-slavery mob in 1837, an event that galvanized the abolition movement.

Broadway and the surrounding blocks are lined with antique shops, galleries, and restaurants. My Just Desserts is known for its cakes and pastries, and the annual Restaurant Week in mid-January is a good excuse to sample locally-owned places at friendly prices. The Meeting of the Great Rivers Scenic Byway runs along the river bluffs and offers striking views, particularly in autumn when the trees turn. The Clark Bridge, a cable-stayed span across the Mississippi, is a handsome piece of engineering and connects Alton directly to Missouri.
