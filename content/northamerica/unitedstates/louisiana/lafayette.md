Lafayette is the capital of Cajun country, a mid-sized Louisiana city where French is still spoken in homes, zydeco and Cajun music fill the dance halls on weeknights, and the food alone justifies the trip. This is where Acadian culture — the descendants of French colonists expelled from Nova Scotia in the 1700s — is most alive and most accessible.

The food scene is extraordinary for a city this size. Boudin, cracklins, crawfish étouffée, and gumbo are everyday staples, not tourist attractions. The best meals are often at gas station counters and family-run plate-lunch spots rather than formal restaurants. Johnson's Boucanière and Don's Specialty Meats are local institutions. On weekends, live Cajun and zydeco music at places like the Blue Moon Saloon or Randol's gets everyone — locals and visitors alike — on the dance floor.

Vermilionville, a living-history museum on the banks of Bayou Vermilion, recreates Acadian and Creole life with restored buildings and cooking demonstrations. The surrounding prairies and bayous are worth exploring for their birdlife and quiet beauty. Lafayette is about two hours west of [New Orleans](/northamerica/unitedstates/louisiana/neworleans) and makes a strong case for being the more authentic Louisiana experience.
