Lafayette is the heart of Acadiana — the stretch of southern Louisiana settled by French-speaking Acadians expelled from Nova Scotia in the 18th century. More than anywhere else in America, this is where Cajun culture survived and flourished: the food, the music, and the French-inflected English spoken by older residents are all distinctly local.

The cooking is serious business here. Crawfish étouffée, boudin (a pork-and-rice sausage sold at gas stations and specialty shops alike), andouille, gumbo — Lafayette eats well, and the restaurants range from roadside joints to places that take reservations. The Saturday morning Cajun and Zydeco music at Vermilionville, the living history village on the bayou, gives a good introduction to both the culture and the music. At night, the dance halls around town fill with Zydeco bands and people who know how to two-step.

Lafayette is also a practical base for exploring the surrounding bayou country: Breaux Bridge (the crawfish capital), Breaux Bridge's neighbour St. Martinville (where Cajun history runs deep), and the Atchafalaya Basin — the largest river swamp in the United States — are all within easy reach.
