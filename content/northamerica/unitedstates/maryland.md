Maryland packs a remarkable amount of variety into a small state. From the Chesapeake Bay -- the largest estuary in the United States -- to the Appalachian mountains in the west, and from the rowhouse neighborhoods of [Baltimore](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/baltimore) to the rural Eastern Shore, the state feels like a cross-section of the entire mid-Atlantic region compressed into a few hours' drive.

[Annapolis](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/annapolis), the state capital, is one of the best-preserved colonial towns in America and home to the US Naval Academy. [Baltimore](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/baltimore) is the state's largest city, with a revitalized Inner Harbor, world-class museums, and a distinctive blue-collar culture that sets it apart from the polished federal feel of nearby Washington, DC. On the Eastern Shore, towns like [St. Michaels](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/stmichaels) and [Tilghman Island](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/tilghman_island) offer a window into the Chesapeake's watermen culture -- crabbing, oystering, and a pace of life that has not changed much in decades.

The Chesapeake Bay defines Maryland more than anything else. Blue crabs are practically the state religion -- steamed with Old Bay seasoning and eaten off brown paper at a crab house is the quintessential Maryland experience. [Ocean City](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/oceancity) on the Atlantic coast draws summer beach crowds, while western Maryland around [Cumberland](/northamerica/unitedstates/maryland/cumberland) has mountain scenery, the C&O Canal towpath for cycling, and a slower, more Appalachian character.
