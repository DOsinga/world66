Baltimore gets a bad rap, and some of it is earned. But this is a city with serious character — gritty, unpretentious, and fiercely loyal to its own identity. It is not Washington, D.C., and it does not want to be.

The Inner Harbor draws the crowds, but the real Baltimore lives in its neighbourhoods. Federal Hill has brick rowhouses and rooftop views of the skyline. Fells Point is the old waterfront — cobblestone streets, dive bars, and some of the best seafood in the Mid-Atlantic. Hampden is where Baltimore gets weird, in the best possible way, and Charles Village around Johns Hopkins has bookshops, cafes, and a university-town feel.

Baltimore is a food city. Crab cakes are the headline, but the restaurant scene runs deep — Afghan, Ethiopian, Italian, Korean, and everything in between. The bar scene is no-nonsense: cheap drinks, good jukeboxes, and nobody checking what you are wearing.

The arts punch above the city's weight. The Baltimore Museum of Art and the Walters Art Museum are both free. Fort McHenry is where the national anthem was written. And the American Visionary Art Museum is unlike anything you have seen anywhere else.

It is also, unmistakably, the city of John Waters, Edgar Allan Poe, and *The Wire*. That combination tells you most of what you need to know.
