Boston Common is impossible to avoid on a visit to Boston, but happily it is the most pleasant of crossroads. The Common dates from the founding of the city in the 1630s, and is the country's oldest public park. It was the 'living room' of the city then, just as it is now, except with then it was where the cows grazed.

Today, festivals, performances, protests, and demonstrations frequently occupy the lawn at the corner of Charles and Beacon streets; indeed, rare is the weekend in the spring, summer, or fall, where there isn't something happening. Shakespeare or opera in the park? You'll find it on the Boston Common.

There are tree-lined pathways and benches, hills, and softball fields. There are several monuments and statues as well scattered across the Common, as well as a graveyard on the Boylston St. side. The Frog Pond is a wading pool for kids to cool off in the summer, and a popular skating rink in the winter.

Access to the Common is easy, as is surrounded by major thoroughfares: Beacon, Charles, Tremont, and Boylston streets. The Park Street T station is located at the eastern corner, and is a central and convenient stop; the Boylston station on the T Green Line is a short walk away as well, at the southeast corner. A parking garage with reasonable (by Boston standards) rates is underneath the Common, accessible via Charles St.
