New Bedford is a small city on the south coast of Massachusetts, an hour south of Boston. In the 19th century it was the whaling capital of the world — the wealth generated by the trade built much of the city's handsome Federal and Greek Revival architecture that survives in the historic district downtown. Herman Melville shipped out of New Bedford in 1841 on his first whaling voyage, and the opening chapters of Moby-Dick are set here.

The New Bedford Whaling National Historical Park encompasses the downtown waterfront area. The New Bedford Whaling Museum on Johnny Cake Hill is the largest of its kind in the world: its collections include a half-scale replica of the whaleship Lagoda, scrimshaw, logbooks, and material about the industry's global reach and its reliance on Pacific Islanders and Cape Verdeans as crew. The Seamen's Bethel across the street, where Melville prayed before his voyage, still holds services.

The city today is a working fishing port with a significant Portuguese and Cape Verdean population. The food reflects this — good bacalhau and caldo verde in the local restaurants.
