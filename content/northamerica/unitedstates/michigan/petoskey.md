Petoskey sits on Little Traverse Bay on the northeastern shore of Lake Michigan, about 280 km north of Grand Rapids. A town of around 6,000 people, it has been a summer destination since the late nineteenth century, when the railroad brought vacationers up from Chicago and Detroit. That heritage is still visible in the downtown gaslight district, a compact grid of Victorian storefronts that caters mostly to well-heeled summer visitors.

The town is known in geological circles for Petoskey stones — fossilized coral from a Devonian sea that once covered Michigan. The smooth, grey stones with their distinctive honeycomb pattern wash up on the beaches along the bay and are prized by collectors. Searching for them along the shoreline at Magnus Park or Petoskey State Park is a popular way to spend an afternoon.

Ernest Hemingway spent his childhood summers in the Petoskey area, fishing and camping around the nearby lakes. A small plaque near the Bay View neighborhood marks his connection to the town, and several of his early Nick Adams stories are set in the woods and water of this part of Michigan.

Summer brings the most activity, with the marina full and the downtown lively. The shoulder seasons are quieter and the lake takes on a different quality — cold, grey, and worth seeing if you don't need a crowd.
