---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Though the capital city of Minnesota is not as well known as its
bigger and glitzier twin, Minneapolis, it has its fair share of
restaurants in all price ranges and a multitude of cuisines. Visitors
to St. Paul will want to try out these neighborhoods in particular,
for sampling the city’s variety.

**Downtown St. Paul**

First, downtown St. Paul is located on a bend in the Mississippi
River but, unfortunately, has little riverside dining. Two areas on
opposite ends of the city center offer possibilities, however. Rice
Park, a verdant square surrounded by cultural institutions and St.
Paul’s oldest hotel, is the epicenter of the city’s restaurant life.
The St. Paul Grill, Kincaid’s Fish, Chop, Steak House, and Pazzaluna
are located in the hotel or across the street. A block or so away are
the new funky, Pop!, several bistros and pubs, and two Japanese sushi
bars. Within easy walking distance of the park is Mickey’s Diner, a St.
Paul institution featured in the Prairie Home Companion movie.

The neighborhood around the Farmers Market a few blocks east of Rice
Park offers further choices including an authentic Japanese noodle
shop, Tanpopo, a Thai restaurant and an increasing selection of coffee
shops and sandwich spots.

**Grand Avenue**

Grand Avenue is up the hill from downtown St. Paul and over the past
thirty years has transformed itself from a quiet residential
thoroughfare into a bustling retail and gastronomic ribbon stretching
several miles. The intersection of Grand and Victoria, known as
Victoria Crossing, has the highest concentration of family-owned and
chain restaurants. The most popular is Café Latte, an art-infused,
gourmet cafeteria famous for its turtle cake and other irresistible
desserts. Other highlights of the avenue, starting on the eastern end
and heading west, include Tavern on Grand (former Soviet premier
Mikhail Gorbachev savored the walleye here), Grand Old Creamery (ice
cream flavors change daily), the Lexington (an old-style supper club),
the Uptowner (breakfasts into the wee hours), Everest on Grand
(Nepali/Tibetan), the original Dunn Bros (a locally-owned coffee shop
that roasts their own beans), and Coffee News Café (full of nearby
college students who’ve had enough of dining hall fare).

**Highland Park**

A little further from the city center is the residential
neighborhood of Highland Park with its own shopping area dating from
the 1940s. Highland Park has one of only two movie theaters still
operating within the city limits and there are plenty of places to have
dinner before or after a movie: Punch Pizza where there is always a
line (call before you go to get on the wait list – they don’t take
reservations), the Highland Grill (upscale burgers and blue plate
specials directly opposite the theater), Cecil’s Deli for soups and
sandwiches, and for those with a bigger pocket book, Ristorante Luci or
its sister, Luci Ancora.

**University Avenue**

University Avenue runs west from behind the Capitol Building all the
way to the University of Minnesota campus in Minneapolis. The eastern
end, known as Frogtown, attracted thousands of refugees from Vietnam,
Laos, and Cambodia starting in the 1970s. This strip of University
Avenue is now home to myriad Asian supermarkets and Vietnamese,
Cambodian, and even Korean restaurants. One of the most upscale is Ngon
Vietnamese Bistro, but a citywide favorite, not the least for its
reasonable prices, is Saigon.
