Thompson Falls sits in a tight river valley in far-western Montana, where the Clark Fork cuts through the Cabinet Mountains before crossing into Idaho. It is a small, unhurried town — county seat of Sanders County, population around 1,300 — that most travelers pass through on US-2, but it rewards a stop.

The namesake falls no longer roar. They were submerged in 1914 when the Thompson Falls Dam was built, creating a long reservoir on the Clark Fork. The dam itself is worth a look, and the small Thompson Falls State Park on the riverbank is a pleasant place to stretch your legs. In summer, fishing and floating on the river draw visitors from across the region.

The town takes its name from David Thompson, the Scottish-Canadian fur trader and cartographer who built Saleesh House nearby in 1809 — one of the first European trading posts in what is now Montana. A historical marker near the river commemorates the site. Thompson mapped much of the Pacific Northwest with extraordinary precision, and his journals remain essential reading for anyone interested in the early exploration of the region.

Thompson Falls is a practical base for exploring the Cabinet Mountains Wilderness, which begins just to the north. The wilderness is roadless and relatively uncrowded, with good hiking to alpine lakes and decent fishing in its streams.
