Wendover sits on the Utah-Nevada border, on the edge of the vast Great Salt Lake Desert. To many it is little more than an exit off Interstate 80 — a cluster of casinos and gas stations where you can pull over before the long haul across the desert to Salt Lake City or Wells. But that is being too dismissive.

Pilot Peak, rising to the south, was a famous landmark for the 19th-century wagon trains crossing the desert. The Bonneville Salt Flats nearby are where land speed records have been set since the 1930s — the blinding white expanse stretching to the horizon is an otherworldly sight. During World War II, Wendover Airfield was where the crew of the Enola Gay trained in secret before dropping the atomic bomb on Hiroshima; a small museum at the old airfield tells the story.

South of Wendover, the country gets lonelier and stranger: the ghost town of Gold Hill, the Goshute Reservation, and the remote settlement of Ibapah. It is compelling in a desolate, hard-to-explain way.
