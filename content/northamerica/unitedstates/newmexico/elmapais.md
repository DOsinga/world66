El Malpais means "the badlands," but this national monument near [Grants](/northamerica/unitedstates/newmexico/grants) is full of surprises. Lava flows, cinder cones, pressure ridges, and lava tube cave systems up to 17 miles long dominate a landscape that looks genuinely alien — jet-black basalt fields broken only by the occasional ponderosa pine that found a foothold in the rock. Sandstone bluffs and mesas line the eastern edge, giving the whole scene a layered drama that photographs cannot quite capture.

For more than 10,000 years people have lived with this landscape. The Puebloan peoples of Acoma, [Laguna](/northamerica/unitedstates/newmexico/laguna), and [Zuni](/northamerica/unitedstates/newmexico/zuni), along with the Ramah Navajo, maintain spiritual ties to El Malpais and continue ancestral uses of the land. Some of the lava flows are relatively recent — the McCarty's flow to the north is only about 3,000 years old, young enough that Pueblo oral histories may contain accounts of its eruption.

Hiking ranges from easy paved nature trails to serious backcountry routes across the raw lava. The 7.5-mile Zuni-Acoma Trail is an ancient trade route that crosses four distinct lava flows; it is beautiful and strenuous. Bring far more water than you think you need — the dark lava absorbs heat and there is no shade. The national monument visitor center is in Grants.
