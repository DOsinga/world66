New York State is two places at once. There's New York City — dense, loud, overwhelming in the best possible way — and then there's everything else: 54,000 square miles of farmland, forest, lakes, and mountains that most visitors never see. The state stretches from the Atlantic coast to the Great Lakes, from the Catskills to the wild peaks of the Adirondacks, and its scale tends to surprise people who've only ever been to the city.

The Hudson Valley and the Catskills are within an hour or two of the city and have evolved into serious destinations in their own right — art, food, old estates, hiking, and a stream of people who fled Brooklyn and never went back. Further north, the Adirondack Park is one of the largest protected areas in the contiguous United States, with more than 2,000 miles of hiking trails and some of the best paddling in the Northeast.

Upstate New York has a different rhythm entirely. Rochester and Buffalo are post-industrial cities with real culture — strong food scenes, good music, cheap rents, and a gritty pride that's earned. The Finger Lakes, running south of Syracuse and Rochester, are serious wine country: the Rieslings in particular have international reputations. Cooperstown, Saratoga Springs, and Ithaca each have their own strong identities and are worth more than a passing stop.

What makes New York State work as a destination is the variety. You can spend a week in the city and never scratch the surface, or you can disappear into the Adirondack backcountry for days without cell service. Most people do something in between — the state rewards those willing to get a little lost.
