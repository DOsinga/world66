New York City is five boroughs, eight million people, and an argument that has never quite been settled. It is overwhelming in the best possible way: dense, loud, expensive, and unlike anywhere else. You can spend weeks here and still feel like you've barely scratched the surface, which is partly the point.

Manhattan gets most of the attention, and for good reason — the concentration of world-class museums, theaters, restaurants, and architecture is unmatched. But the outer boroughs reward the curious. Brooklyn has supplanted Manhattan as the place most people actually want to live, with neighborhoods like Williamsburg, Park Slope, and Red Hook each worth a full afternoon. Queens holds the most diverse collection of restaurants anywhere in the country. The Bronx has the best zoo in America and, if you're lucky, a Yankees game.

The grid makes Manhattan navigable even on your first visit. Above 14th Street, numbered streets run east-west and avenues run north-south. Below 14th, the old Dutch street plan takes over and things get more interesting and more confusing. The subway goes almost everywhere and runs all night — get a MetroCard and use it. Taxis and Uber work well too, though midtown traffic can be punishing.

The best seasons are spring (late April through May) and fall (September through October), when the weather is mild and the city is at its most energetic. Summer is hot, humid, and very crowded; winter is cold but has its own attractions, particularly around the holidays. Whenever you go, budget more time than you think you need — New York has a way of expanding to fill it.

Some things to know before you arrive: everything costs more than you expect, especially accommodation. Book ahead. The city is physically compact enough that walking is often faster than any vehicle, particularly in Midtown. And despite its reputation, most New Yorkers are perfectly willing to help if you ask — the impatience comes from the pace, not from any hostility toward visitors.
