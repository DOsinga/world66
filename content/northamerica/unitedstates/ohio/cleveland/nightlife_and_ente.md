Cleveland has a number of great entertainment venues, and a pretty vibrant nightlife scene. The Flats, located on the banks and at the mouth of the Cuyahoga, were once the hyped-up district to head to for nightlife and entertainment, but it no longer reigns king of the party scene in Cleveland. Still a few remnents of the Flats remain. The Nautica Entertainment Complex contains several establishments worthy establishments in an old converted electric plant on the West Bank of the Flats. Shooters is a restaurant, bar, and sometimes dance club with an excellent deck for dining during warmer months and excellent views of the skyline. This is a real Cleveland institution and an annual destination for many Clevelanders. The old strip of bars and restaurants on the East Bank which is now a ghost town is being torn down to make room for a multi-million development neighborhood to be completed in 2009.

The party scene has effectively moved up the hill from the east bank of the river closer to Public Square. Just west of Public Square along W.6th, St. Clair, and W.9th is the Warehouse District, which is undoubtedly the place to "be seen" in Cleveland at the moment. This district restored the beautiful neoclassical Victorean-Era facades of old warehouses, gutted the interiors, and filled them with contemporary, chic, and trendy restaurants and clubs. The sidewalks are filled with tables and most establishments offer valet parking at entrances. Many of the people here in the evenings and well into the morning are young professionals who actually live in the Warehouse District loft apartments, but most of the people are drawn to the district from all over the region. Many of the Warehouse District restaurants are pretty upscale, so be prepared to dress appropriately and to pay quite a bit by the end of the night. There are however, a few fairly reasonably priced places, and everyone should feel welcome here in the Warehouse District.

The party has been going on in the Warehouse District for a few years now, but the party is slowly spilling over or perhaps shifting to an intersection just east of Public Square at E.4th and Euclid. While this is certainly a nightlife district with clubs like V Lounge and View Night Club (two of Cleveland's most exclusive), it is known most for the House of Blues concert venue, and Pickwick and Frolic cabaret and comedy complex. Both these venues have popular restuarants and bars as well. Cleveland's celebrity chef Micheal Symon is moving his infamous restaurant Lola's here to E.4th Street, and a new bowling alley and martini bar concept is also in the works for E.4th Street. East Fourth Street is a narrow alley street, paved with bricks, and lights strung across the buildings. It has a lot of atmosphere and worthy establishments, so don't entirely look it over by soley frequenting the Warehouse District when in town.

In addition to the Flats, Warhouse District, and E.4th hotspots, downtown Cleveland also offers a generous theater district known as Playhouse Square and a sports district called Gateway where the Cavs play in the Q Arena and the Indians play at Jacobs Field.

Ohio City just west of downtown along W.25th Street and just north of Lorain Avenue you will find a really nice "neighborhood" alternative to the "downtown" nightlife scene. People are a little more laid back and a little more casual, but still have taste and high standards. All different people from all over flock to the West Side Market to buy fresh goods during the day, and then stop in the restaurants and bars in the neighborhood. Take for instance that you will find the Great Lakes Brewing Company here, known for its famous brew, and the company's strict adherance to natural ingredients and methods according to Bavarian Purity Laws. Great Lakes is certainly a must when in Cleveland. Its brew is highly ranked, loved by by beer conosieurs, and distributed throughout the country somewhat exclusively. A quality wine bar, a coffee bar showing classic films, a belgian beer bar, a popular hookah bar, and an excellent classic rock bar add to the mix of laid back nightlife establishments with concern for quality and a touch of class in Ohio City.

Cleveland has a considerable college-student population downtown, especially in University Circle, and in some east side suburbs. A lot of the college students, as well as nearby high-schoolers, and other liberally inclined people tend to congregate in Coventry Village in Cleveland Heights on Coventry Road just south of Mayfield Road on weekends. You will find sports bars, vegitarean-inclined restaurants, little books shops, funky boutiques, coffee shops, and a great intimate music venue with a good line-up called the Grog Shop. It has been compared to Greenwhich Village in New York, but on a much smaller scale.

While the artists in Cleveland like Ohio City and Coventry, they would probably most prefer a neighborhood called Tremont just south-east of Ohio City. It is cut off from the rest of the city by freeways and the Cuyahoga River which creates a kind of isolated island-like quality to the neighborhood, but it remains in the heart of the city. Oddly arranged streets around the nieghborhood park, a diverse housing stock, high concentration of architecurally notable churches, art galleries, dive bars, ultra-chic lounges, old world cuisine, and cutting-edge contemporary cuisine create a diversity and a quality of randomness that could hardly be duplicated.

When the artists residing on the east side of town would want to stay a little closer to home, they might go to Little Italy around the interesection of Mayfield and Murray Hill Road. Little Italy is a bit more mainstream than Tremont, but like Tremont it is a popular place to open art galleries, and old-world cuisine mixes with contemporary interpretations. Little Italy and Tremont both host popular and regular Artwalks in the summer. During the summer parking can be quite problem in Little Italy. While people love to go to Little Italy in the afternoon and early evening for art galleries and restaurants, they aren't open too late and most people would probably head down Mayfield Road to Coventry Village later on the evening for nightlife.

Cleveland has several districts that are perfect for dinner and a movie. Tower City is one such place where downtown you can get something to eat a restaurant and then see a block buster movie at the movie theater right there in the center. However, if you are downtown and near Tower City, you might want to consider taking the Rapid from Tower City directly to Shaker Square for dinner and a movie instead. Shaker Square is a beautiful Georgian-style outdoor shopping center completed in the 1920s. The Square has gone through numerous transformations and it has a variety of restaurants that range from affordable and family-style to novelty, upscale, and classy. One of the most charming delightful theaters is the Shaker Square Cinema located right in the square. Its Art Decco interior has been restored, and its theaters have been transformed into state-of-the-art quality. Shaker Square makes dinner and a movie a special treat, and not just an average thing to do on the weekends.
