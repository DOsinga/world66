---
title: 'Getting Around in Oklahoma'
type: section
---

Oklahoma is car country. The interstate system connects the major cities well, and driving is by far the most practical way to get around the state. Traffic is light compared to most American states, and gas is cheap.

**By car** is the only realistic option for seeing the state properly. I-44 (the Turner Turnpike) connects [Oklahoma City](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/oklahomacity) and [Tulsa](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/tulsa). I-35 runs north-south through the center of the state. I-40 crosses east-west through Oklahoma City. Rental cars are available at both major airports.

**Route 66** is the classic way to see Oklahoma. With 48 towns and cities along the Mother Road, you can drive the full stretch from [Miami](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/miami) in the northeast to Texola on the Texas border. The principal cities from northeast to southwest are Miami, [Claremore](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/claremore), [Tulsa](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/tulsa), [Sapulpa](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/sapulpa), [Chandler](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/chandler), [Edmond](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/edmond), [El Reno](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/el_reno), and [Elk City](/northamerica/unitedstates/oklahoma/elk_city).

**Public transit** is limited. Oklahoma City and Tulsa have basic bus systems, but neither city has rail transit. Greyhound and Jefferson Lines serve the major cities. Amtrak's Heartland Flyer runs daily between Oklahoma City and Fort Worth, Texas.
