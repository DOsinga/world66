Pawhuska is the capital of the Osage Nation and the county seat of Osage County, the largest county in Oklahoma. Set in the rolling tallgrass prairie of north-central Oklahoma, it is a small town with a big story. David Grann's *Killers of the Flower Moon* brought international attention to the Osage Reign of Terror of the 1920s, when Osage people were systematically murdered for their oil wealth. Martin Scorsese's film adaptation, shot partly in and around Pawhuska, further raised the town's profile.

The **Osage Nation Museum**, the oldest tribally owned museum in the country, is the place to start for understanding Osage history and culture. The **Tallgrass Prairie Preserve**, managed by the Nature Conservancy about 15 miles north of town, protects nearly 40,000 acres of native tallgrass prairie where bison roam freely. It is one of the best places in the country to experience what the Great Plains looked like before settlement.

Downtown Pawhuska has been revitalized in part by Ree Drummond's **Pioneer Woman Mercantile**, a restaurant, bakery, and general store that draws large crowds, especially on weekends. Beyond the Mercantile, the town has a walkable main street with local shops and a genuinely friendly atmosphere. Pawhuska rewards a full day: spend the morning at the prairie preserve, the afternoon in town, and eat well before heading back.
