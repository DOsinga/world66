South Dakota is the most unassuming of the Great Plains states — a flat, sparsely populated rectangle of wheat and buffalo grass on the upper Missouri River — but it contains an extraordinary amount of what makes the American West famous. The state is split in half by the **Missouri River**, which flows north to south down its middle. Everything east of the river is prairie farming country, small towns, and the state's economic capital **Sioux Falls**. Everything west of the river is what most visitors actually come for: the **Black Hills**, the **Badlands**, the **Pine Ridge** and **Rosebud** Lakota reservations, and the ghost-town gold rush settlements of the late 19th century.

The **Black Hills** — a small, densely forested mountain range rising out of the plains in the south-west corner of the state — are the main draw. **Mount Rushmore**, with its sixty-foot presidential portraits carved into a granite cliff, is the most famous American monument after the Statue of Liberty; a little further south, the even more ambitious (and still unfinished) **Crazy Horse Memorial**, begun in 1948 and intended as a Native response to Rushmore, is slowly being carved into another mountain. Between them, **Custer State Park** has the largest free-roaming herd of bison in North America, reliably seen on the 18-mile Wildlife Loop Road. Nearby, **Wind Cave** and **Jewel Cave** national parks protect two of the longest cave systems in the world, the **Badlands National Park** is a vast, otherworldly landscape of eroded buttes and spires, and the old gold rush town of **Deadwood** — where Wild Bill Hickok was shot dead during a poker game in 1876 — has been restored to a somewhat Disneyfied version of its 1870s self.

South Dakota is also Lakota country. The **Pine Ridge Indian Reservation**, home to the Oglala Lakota, contains the site of the **Wounded Knee** massacre of 1890 and the American Indian Movement's 1973 occupation. The experience of visiting the reservation is very different from visiting the parks — this is one of the poorest places in the United States, and visitors should come with respect, patience, and a willingness to learn. The **Crazy Horse Memorial** includes the excellent **Indian Museum of North America**, and the **Akta Lakota Museum** in Chamberlain is another good introduction to Lakota history.
