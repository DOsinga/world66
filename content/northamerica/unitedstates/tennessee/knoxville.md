Knoxville sits in the foothills of the Great Smoky Mountains, at the point where the Tennessee River bends south before heading toward Chattanooga. It is not a glamorous city, but it is an honest one — a working-class university town that has spent the last couple of decades rediscovering its own centre after decades of suburban drift.

The Old City and Market Square are the heart of things. Market Square is a genuine public space rather than a theme-park facsimile: people eat lunch there, farmers sell produce on weekends, and local bands play in the evenings. The Old City, just east of downtown, has bars and live music in old brick warehouses. The 1982 World's Fair left behind Sunsphere, an odd gold-tinted ball on a tower that still stands as a kind of accidental landmark, and World's Fair Park, which gives the city a decent patch of green near downtown.

The University of Tennessee campus runs along the river and gives the city its energy — and its intense football culture. On game days in autumn, Neyland Stadium fills with over 100,000 people in orange. It is one of the largest stadiums in the country and the noise carries across the whole city.

The Smoky Mountains are less than an hour away, which makes Knoxville a useful base for anyone who wants city amenities alongside mountains and hiking. [Great Smoky Mountains National Park](/northamerica/unitedstates/tennessee/greatsmokymountainsnationalpark) is the most visited national park in the United States, and the drive from Knoxville to Gatlinburg or Townsend takes under an hour.
