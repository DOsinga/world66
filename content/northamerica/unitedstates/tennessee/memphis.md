Memphis isn't so much a place as it is a feeling, a sound, a taste. It's the muggy warmth of a southern summer evening. It's the slow moan of a blues song or the eternal echo of Elvis. It's the taste of barbecue, cornbread, and black-eyed peas.

To come to Memphis is to pursue those feelings. Almost everyone who visits goes to Graceland, the much-toured home of the King of Rock and Roll, and to Sun Studio, where he cut his first records. But Elvis was a product of this city, not its whole story. The music that came out of Memphis — blues on Beale Street, soul from the Stax label in Soulsville, gospel in the churches — shaped the sound of the twentieth century.

Take a walk down historic Beale Street and move from bar to bar hearing live blues. Eat at one of a hundred barbecue joints and join an age-old argument about whose ribs are best. The National Civil Rights Museum, built into the Lorraine Motel where Martin Luther King Jr. was assassinated, is one of the most powerful museums in America and not to be missed.

For two or three days, Memphis can offer even the most well-travelled visitor a genuine taste of the South — and if you find yourself staying longer, that's the old Memphis feeling taking hold.
