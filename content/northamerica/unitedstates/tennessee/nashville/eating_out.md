---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Nashville's food identity is built on two things: hot chicken and the meat-and-three. Hot chicken — fried chicken coated in a cayenne-heavy spice paste — originated at Prince's Hot Chicken Shack on Ewing Drive in the 1940s and is now the city's most celebrated dish. Order mild if you are cautious; Extra Hot is serious. The meat-and-three format, a Southern institution, means choosing one meat and three sides from a rotating steam table at a diner or cafeteria-style counter. Arnold's Country Kitchen on Eighth Avenue is the most reliable downtown option.

Beyond these traditions, East Nashville and the Gulch have a growing restaurant scene. The city leans heavily Southern — smoked brisket, biscuits and gravy, pimento cheese — but the dining options have diversified considerably. Most serious restaurants don't fill up until after 19:00.
