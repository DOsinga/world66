Galveston Island is a barrier island about an hour south of [Houston](/northamerica/unitedstates/texas/houston), stretched out along the upper Texas coast. Before the catastrophic hurricane of 1900 — still the deadliest natural disaster in American history — Galveston was the richest city in Texas and its main port. That history left behind an extraordinary collection of Victorian architecture that the rest of the Texas coast cannot match.

The Strand Historic District, a few blocks of ornate iron-fronted buildings near the port, was once known as the "Wall Street of the South." Today it holds shops, restaurants, and galleries, and is the centre of Galveston's annual Mardi Gras celebration, the third-largest in the United States. Nearby, the 1894 Grand Opera House still hosts performances.

The beaches stretch for 50 kilometres along the island's Gulf side. They are not the Caribbean — the water is warm but brown from Mississippi River sediment — but the wide sand is good for families, and Stewart Beach and East Beach are the most popular stretches. Seawall Boulevard, a 16-kilometre promenade built after the 1900 storm, runs along the waterfront.

Moody Gardens, at the island's west end, has three glass pyramids housing an aquarium, a rainforest exhibit, and a science museum. The adjacent Lone Star Flight Museum has an impressive collection of restored warbirds. For something quieter, Galveston Island State Park on the bay side offers kayaking through salt marshes and decent birding.

Getting here from Houston is straightforward — Interstate 45 runs directly to the island. A free ferry also connects the east end of Galveston to the Bolivar Peninsula.
