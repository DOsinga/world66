About thirty minutes south of [Seattle](/northamerica/unitedstates/washington/seattle), Tacoma juts northward into Puget Sound with Commencement Bay to the east and the Tacoma Narrows to the west. Mount Rainier looms on the southern horizon and the Olympic Mountains fill the western skyline — on a clear day the views here rival anything in Seattle.

Tacoma spent decades living down its “Smellcoma” reputation, earned from the paper-pulp mills that once dominated the waterfront. The mills are largely gone now, and the city has reinvented itself around the arts. Rental rates well below Seattle’s have drawn painters, glassblowers, and musicians south, and the results show in a walkable downtown anchored by the Museum of Glass, the Tacoma Art Museum, and a thriving gallery district along Fawcett Avenue.

The Museum of Glass, designed by Arthur Erickson and opened in 2002, is worth visiting for the architecture alone — a tilted steel cone inspired by the region’s lumber kilns. Dale Chihuly’s “Bridge of Glass” connecting the museum to the waterfront is a Tacoma landmark. Beyond the museums, the city has solid food and drink options and easy access to Point Defiance Park, one of the largest urban parks in the country.
