Pepin is a small village on the eastern shore of Lake Pepin, a 28-mile-long natural widening of the Mississippi River in western Wisconsin. It is best known as the birthplace of Laura Ingalls Wilder — the cabin site is marked just outside town, and September's Laura Ingalls Wilder Days draw fans of the books from across the country.

The village was settled in 1846, named after two French trappers, and grew as a steamboat stop on the Mississippi. Mark Twain knew it; wealthy Chicagoans summered on the lake. Today it is a quiet place with a handful of restaurants, shops and a marina with transient docking for visitors arriving by water.

Lake Pepin itself, formed by the delta of the Chippewa River, is the real draw — sailing, fishing, kayaking in summer, ice boating and cross-country skiing in winter. Pepin is about 70 miles south of Minneapolis-St. Paul and makes a pleasant weekend escape from the Twin Cities.
