St. Thomas is the busiest of the US Virgin Islands — a lush, hilly island of 32 square miles that was Danish for 250 years before the United States bought the territory in 1917 for $25 million in gold. The Danish legacy is everywhere: red-roofed buildings climbing the hills of Charlotte Amalie, Danish street names (Dronningens Gade, Kongens Gade), and the second-oldest synagogue in the western hemisphere, built in 1833 with a sand floor to muffle the footsteps of its Sephardic founders.

Charlotte Amalie, the capital, is one of the busiest cruise ports in the Caribbean, and on days when four or five ships are in port the old town can feel overrun. But the crowds thin after 4pm when the ships leave, and the duty-free shops along Main Street give way to the quieter back alleys — Riise Alley, Royal Dane Mall, the old warehouses now full of jewellers and rum shops. Fort Christian, the 17th-century Danish fortress on the waterfront, is the oldest building on the island and worth a look even if fortress museums aren't usually your thing.

The real draw is the beaches. Magens Bay, on the north shore, is the one everyone photographs — a long curve of white sand and calm turquoise water, protected by a heart-shaped bay and regularly voted one of the most beautiful beaches in the world. There's a small entry fee and it gets busy when cruise ships dock, but it earns the hype. Coki Beach next to Coral World is the best for snorkelling straight off the sand; Sapphire Beach on the east end is quieter and has better views of neighbouring St. John.

Rent a car if you can — driving is on the left, which catches Americans out, but the island is small and the roads wind through rainforest, past pastel houses and open-air bars with views over the water. Take the Skyride up to Paradise Point for the sunset view over Charlotte Amalie harbour, and the 20-minute ferry to St. John for a day in the Virgin Islands National Park, which covers two-thirds of that island and has the best beaches in the territory.
