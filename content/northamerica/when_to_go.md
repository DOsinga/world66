---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

North America stretches from the Arctic to the tropics, so there's no single best time to visit — it depends entirely on where you're going. When it's snowing in [Alaska](/northamerica/unitedstates/alaska), it can be 35 degrees on a beach in [Costa Rica](/northamerica/costarica). The continent covers so many climate zones that you can always find somewhere in season. That said, the US and Canadian summer (June through August) is peak travel season in the north, with the highest prices and biggest crowds to match.

The US northeast and upper Midwest have four dramatic seasons. Fall is arguably the best time — September through mid-October brings spectacular foliage across New England, upstate [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork), and Quebec. Summers are warm and humid, winters are brutal, and spring is mud season before it's anything pretty. [New York](/northamerica/unitedstates/newyorkstate/newyork) and [Chicago](/northamerica/unitedstates/illinois/chicago) are best from May through October. The American Southwest — [Arizona](/northamerica/unitedstates/arizona), Nevada, New Mexico — runs on an opposite calendar. Summer temperatures regularly exceed 40°C, and hiking the Grand Canyon or Death Valley becomes genuinely dangerous. Visit in spring (March-May) or fall (October-November) instead. The Pacific coast from [San Francisco](/northamerica/unitedstates/california/sanfranciscobayarea/sanfrancisco) to [Vancouver](/northamerica/canada/britishcolumbia/vancouver) is mild year-round but rainy from October through April. Summer in the Pacific Northwest is as good as anywhere on Earth.

[Canada](/northamerica/canada) has short, intense summers and long winters. [Toronto](/northamerica/canada/ontario/toronto) and [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal) are fantastic from June through September — outdoor festivals, warm evenings, the whole city comes alive. Winter in [Montreal](/northamerica/canada/quebec/montreal) means weeks of -20°C, which has its own appeal if you're prepared. The Canadian Rockies are a summer hiking paradise from July through September and world-class skiing from December through April. [Vancouver](/northamerica/canada/britishcolumbia/vancouver) is the mildest major Canadian city but gets a lot of winter rain.

[Mexico](/northamerica/mexico) and Central America follow a different rhythm: dry season and wet season. The dry season runs roughly November through April, which is when most travelers visit. Skies are clear, roads are passable, and temperatures are comfortable. This is high season for the Pacific coast beaches, the Maya ruins, and the colonial highlands. Prices and crowds peak from December through March, especially around Christmas and Semana Santa (Easter week).

The wet season — May through October — brings afternoon downpours, lusher landscapes, and far fewer tourists. It's not a washout; mornings are usually sunny, and the rain comes in predictable bursts. Locals call it the "green season" and it's a perfectly good time to travel if you don't mind getting rained on. Prices drop significantly and you'll have popular sites to yourself. The exception is the Caribbean lowlands and Pacific coast, where the rain can be relentless from September through November.

Hurricane season runs June through November across the Caribbean, the Gulf Coast, and the east coast of Central America, peaking in August and September. This is a real consideration for the Caribbean islands, [Cancun](/northamerica/mexico/cancun), the [Yucatan](/northamerica/mexico/yucatan), [Belize](/northamerica/belize), and the Florida/Gulf coast. You probably won't get hit by a hurricane, but you might get trapped by a tropical storm or deal with days of heavy rain. Travel insurance is worth it during these months. The ABC islands (Aruba, Bonaire, Curacao) and Trinidad sit below the hurricane belt and are safe bets year-round.

[Greenland](/northamerica/greenland) has a narrow window. June through September is the only realistic time for most visitors — that's when you get the midnight sun, when fjords are navigable, and when hiking is possible. Even then, weather is unpredictable and cold. July and August are the "warmest" months, with temperatures around 5-10°C in the south. If you want to see the northern lights, September is your best bet. Winter tourism exists in a small way, but it's dark, extreme, and limited to a few towns.

For timing specific activities: the major national parks of the US and Canada — Yellowstone, Yosemite, Zion, Banff — are best in September, when the summer crowds thin and fall colors start. Many parks close roads and facilities from October through May due to snow. If you're diving in [Belize](/northamerica/belize) or [Honduras](/northamerica/honduras)'s Bay Islands, visibility is best from April through June. Whale watching on the Pacific coast peaks in winter (December-March) in Mexico's Baja and summer (June-September) in British Columbia and Alaska. Surfing in [Costa Rica](/northamerica/costarica) and [Nicaragua](/northamerica/nicaragua) is best from April through October when the Pacific swells are consistent.
