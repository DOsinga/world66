Buenos Aires likes to call itself the Paris of South America. It is more accurate to say that it is the most European of South American cities — and the least European of European ones. The architecture is Haussmann-adjacent, the politics baroque, the national self-image a combination of European pretension and Latin American melancholy, and the result is one of the more fascinating cities on the continent.

The centre, **Microcentro**, fans out from Plaza de Mayo with the Casa Rosada and the Cabildo, up the world's widest avenue (Avenida 9 de Julio) past the iconic Obelisco, and on to Recoleta. **Recoleta** is the most European quarter — wide boulevards, old money, and the famous cemetery where Evita is buried. **Palermo** further out is the hip neighbourhood, split into Palermo Soho (cafes and boutiques), Palermo Hollywood (restaurants, bars, and film studios), and a long park that runs along the river.

South of the centre, things get more raw. **San Telmo**, the old colonial quarter, is the place for Sunday antiques markets and tango dancers on the streets. **La Boca** further south, with the corrugated-iron houses painted in improbable colours, is the old Italian immigrant district and home to Boca Juniors, the most fanatical football club in a very fanatical country.

This is a city that runs late. Dinner rarely starts before 9 pm, clubs get going at 2 am, and the streets are lively until sunrise at weekends. The food is heavy on beef (Argentine steak is the obvious thing, best eaten at a *parrilla*) and on Italian-inflected pasta. **Tango** is everywhere — in the *milonga* clubs for the real thing, on the street in San Telmo, and in polished-up evening shows for tourists.

Politics here is a national pastime. The economy has boomed and crashed for decades; at any given moment, the peso-dollar exchange rate is a dinner-party topic. Bring cash in US dollars, change at the "blue dollar" rate, and expect prices to make no sense.
