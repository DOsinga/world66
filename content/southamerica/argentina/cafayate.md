Cafayate is a small, sun-baked town in the Calchaquí Valley of northwestern Argentina, surrounded by vineyards that produce some of the country's best white wines. At 1,660 metres elevation and with more than 300 days of sunshine a year, this is one of the highest wine regions in the world, and the Torrontés grape — floral, dry, and distinctly Argentine — reaches its peak here.

The town itself is quiet and walkable, centred on a leafy plaza with a handful of bodegas within easy strolling distance. Several wineries offer tastings and tours, and the wine is remarkably affordable. The main square has good empanada shops and ice cream parlours that serve wine-flavoured helado — the Torrontés flavour is worth trying.

The drive to Cafayate from [Salta](/southamerica/argentina/salta) through the Quebrada de las Flechas and Quebrada de Cafayate is one of the most spectacular road trips in Argentina. The Ruta 68 winds through a canyon of red sandstone formations — the Garganta del Diablo, the Anfiteatro, the Castillos — carved by wind and water into improbable shapes. Most visitors make the drive a loop, going south through the Quebrada and returning via Cachi and the Cuesta del Obispo.
