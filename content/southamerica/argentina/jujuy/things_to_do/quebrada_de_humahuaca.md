  The Cerro de los Siete Colores above Purmamarca gets its name from the seven distinct mineral colours visible in the rock — red from iron oxide, yellow from limonite, green from copper, white from limestone. The best light falls in the late afternoon, when the sun drops low and the stripes deepen.

Running north from just outside the city for over 150 kilometres, the Quebrada de Humahuaca is the canyon that defines the province. Its walls shift through extraordinary colours — ochre, crimson, lavender, green — while the Rio Grande threads along the valley floor past adobe villages that have barely changed in centuries. The UNESCO designation (2003) recognised both the landscape and the living Andean culture: the weekly markets in Humahuaca and Tilcara still trade in the same ways they have for generations. The ruins of Pucará de Tilcara, a pre-Inca hilltop fortress above Tilcara village, are the most substantial archaeological site in the quebrada and easy to reach on foot from the village centre.
