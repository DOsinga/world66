Junín de los Andes is a small town in the Neuquén province of Argentine Patagonia, sitting on the banks of the Chimehuin River at the foot of the Andes. It is one of Argentina's premier fly-fishing destinations — the Chimehuin and the nearby Malleo and Aluminé rivers are full of brown and rainbow trout introduced in the early twentieth century, and anglers come from around the world for the season (November to April).

The town itself is quiet and unpretentious, without the resort-town polish of nearby [San Martín de los Andes](/southamerica/argentina/sanmartindelosandes) 40 kilometres to the south. That is part of its appeal: Junín feels like a working Patagonian town that happens to be surrounded by spectacular scenery. The Lanín volcano (3,776 metres), a near-perfect snow-capped cone, dominates the western horizon and is the centrepiece of Lanín National Park.

Beyond fishing, the area is good for hiking and horse riding in the Andean foothills. The road west to the Chilean border passes through monkey puzzle (*araucaria*) forests — ancient, alien-looking trees that are one of Patagonia's most distinctive landscapes. Lake Huechulafquen, about 25 kilometres west, is surrounded by forest and overlooked by Lanín, and is worth the drive even if you are not fishing.
