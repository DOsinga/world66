Oruro is a high-altitude mining city on the Bolivian altiplano, sitting at over 3,700 metres. It is not, honestly, a place most travellers would visit outside of one extraordinary week each year — but that week makes it unmissable.

The Carnaval de Oruro, held in February or early March, is one of the most spectacular folk festivals in South America and a UNESCO Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity. The centrepiece is the Entrada, a parade lasting up to eleven hours in which thousands of dancers perform the Diablada (Dance of the Devils) and other traditional dances through the streets in elaborate, fantastical costumes, all in honour of the Virgen del Socavón — the Virgin of the Mines. If you plan to attend, book accommodation months in advance; the city fills completely.

Outside carnival season, Oruro is a scrappy, windswept mining town with a certain raw appeal. The Santuario del Socavón, built into a hillside above a former mine shaft, is worth a visit, and the mining museum underneath it gives a sobering sense of the tin and silver extraction that built — and impoverished — this city.
