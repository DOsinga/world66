Potosi was once the largest and wealthiest city in the Americas. Founded in 1545 following the discovery of silver in the Cerro Rico — the "rich mountain" that looms above the city — it grew with astonishing speed: by the early 17th century it had a population rivaling London or Paris, and the phrase "worth a Potosi" entered the Spanish language as a synonym for fabulous wealth.

The silver ran thin in the early 19th century and Potosi contracted sharply, which paradoxically preserved much of its colonial architecture. A tin boom gave the city a second life, but it was never again what it had been. UNESCO recognized the city as a World Heritage Site. The Casa Real de la Moneda, the old royal mint, is the great attraction — an extraordinary colonial building that tells the story of silver, slavery, and empire in considerable depth. Dozens of baroque churches and grand mansions fill the streets around it.

Be aware that Potosi sits at over 4,000 metres, making it the highest city on earth. Altitude sickness is common; give yourself a day or two to acclimatize before doing much walking. Trips into the Cerro Rico mines, still worked today under difficult conditions, are popular with travelers and sobering — you will come out understanding the human cost of all that silver.
