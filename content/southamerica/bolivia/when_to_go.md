---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

Bolivia's geography is so varied — from altiplano at 4,000 metres to Amazon rainforest near sea level — that no single time of year is perfect for everything. That said, the dry season from May to October is the best all-round window for most visitors.

**May to October (dry season)** brings clear skies, crisp air, and passable roads in the highlands. The Andes are at their most photogenic, trekking is straightforward, and you won't get stuck behind a flooded river or a landslide. Nights in June and July are genuinely cold on the altiplano — below zero in places — so pack warm layers. The same period works well for Santa Cruz and the lowland cities, which avoid the worst of the summer heat and humidity.

**November to March (wet season)** means rain in the highlands and serious flooding in the lowlands. Roads close, flights get delayed, and some rural areas become inaccessible. But the wet season has its own appeal: the vegetation turns intensely green, rivers fill for rafting and kayaking, and — most famously — the Salar de Uyuni floods to a shallow film of water that creates a perfect mirror reflection of the sky. If photographing the salt flats is your main reason for coming, January to March is when to go.

**Carnaval in February** is the one time of year many people specifically plan around. [Oruro](/southamerica/bolivia/oruro)'s Carnaval is one of the great spectacles of South America — a UNESCO-listed event with tens of thousands of costumed dancers parading through the streets for two days straight. Book accommodation months ahead if you want to be there.

**Easter week** brings much of the country to a standstill. Many businesses close and transport fills up fast — not the ideal time to be catching buses between cities.

April and the October-November shoulder months are underrated: the rains have either just ended or haven't yet begun, prices are lower, crowds are thinner, and the light is beautiful.
