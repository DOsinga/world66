---
title: "Books"
type: section
---

South America has produced some of the most extraordinary literature of the past century. Start with Gabriel Garcia Marquez's *One Hundred Years of Solitude* — it's the book that defined magical realism and captures the surreal, layered quality of life in [Colombia](/southamerica/colombia) better than any travel guide could. Isabel Allende's *The House of the Spirits* does something similar for [Chile](/southamerica/chile). Jorge Luis Borges' short stories are set in [Buenos Aires](/southamerica/argentina/buenosaires) and beyond, bending reality in ways that feel oddly appropriate once you've spent time on the continent. Mario Vargas Llosa's novels paint vivid pictures of [Peru](/southamerica/peru) — try *The Feast of the Goat* or *Aunt Julia and the Scriptwriter*.

For travel writing, Bruce Chatwin's *In Patagonia* is the classic — a strange, elliptical journey through southern [Argentina](/southamerica/argentina) and [Chile](/southamerica/chile) that defined a genre. It's more impressionistic than practical, but it captures the end-of-the-world feeling of Patagonia perfectly. Paul Theroux's *The Old Patagonian Express* traces a train journey from Boston to the tip of South America and is at its best in the South American stretches. More recently, Mark Adams' *Turn Right at Machu Picchu* is a funny, well-researched account of retracing Hiram Bingham's steps in [Peru](/southamerica/peru).

Eduardo Galeano's *Open Veins of Latin America* is essential background reading. It's a passionate, angry account of how five centuries of exploitation shaped the continent. It's polemical — Galeano himself later said he'd outgrown some of it — but it provides context you won't get elsewhere. For a deeper look at the colonial period, Charles Mann's *1491* and *1493* reshape your understanding of the pre-Columbian Americas and what happened after contact.

If you're heading into the wild, pick up David Grann's *The Lost City of Z*, about Percy Fawcett's doomed Amazon expeditions — it reads like a thriller and makes the jungle feel both alluring and terrifying. For the natural world, Adrian Forsyth and Ken Miyata's *Tropical Nature* is a superb introduction to rainforest ecology. Henry Walter Bates' 19th-century *The Naturalist on the River Amazons* remains one of the great accounts of exploring [Brazil](/southamerica/brazil)'s interior.

Finally, for food — a growing reason to visit — pick up a copy of Virgilio Martinez's *Central*, which explores [Peru](/southamerica/peru)'s extraordinary culinary biodiversity through the lens of one of the world's best restaurants. For Argentine food culture, Francis Mallmann's *Seven Fires* captures the open-flame cooking tradition of the pampas and Patagonia in a way that will have you booking a flight to [Buenos Aires](/southamerica/argentina/buenosaires).
