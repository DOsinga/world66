Brasilia is Brazil's purpose-built capital, a city that exists because someone decided it should. Inaugurated in 1960, it was designed by urbanist Lucio Costa and architect Oscar Niemeyer as a modernist utopia — a city shaped like an aeroplane when seen from above, with monumental government buildings, sweeping concrete curves, and vast open spaces where most capitals would have narrow streets.

The result is unlike anywhere else in Brazil. Brasilia is impressive rather than charming — the scale of the architecture, the symmetry of the Esplanada dos Ministerios, and Niemeyer's cathedral are genuinely striking. But it is a city built for cars, not pedestrians, and the rigid zoning means nightlife, restaurants, and daily life happen in numbered blocks rather than organic neighbourhoods. If you are coming from [Rio de Janeiro](/southamerica/brazil/riodejaneiro) or Sao Paulo, the contrast is stark.

That said, Brasilia rewards anyone interested in twentieth-century architecture, and the eating scene is surprisingly diverse — settlers from across Brazil and the world brought their cuisines along.
