---
title: "Eating Out in Brasilia"
type: section
---

As the capital of Brazil, Brasilia has plenty of fine dining options for those with cash to eat out. While those to whom money is no object can eat exceptionally well, visitors on more modest budgets will have no trouble finding good, filling food that won’t break the bank. An influx of settlers from across the globe means that dining here is truly a multi-cultural experience, with options from fine French cuisine to traditional Italian and chic sushi restaurants. There are many top-flight hotels in the Asa Norte area of the city, most of them boasting very good, though expensive, restaurants that are open to non-guests. Cheaper options can be found along Rua 405/406.

**Churrascarias**

This being Brazil, you shouldn’t miss the chance to eat at a traditional churrascaria (grilled meat restaurant). If those hunger pangs are really kicking in, look for churrascarias that serve the food rodizio style, where, for a set price, cut after sizzling cut of meat is brought over to your table until you finally buckle. These restaurants range from cheap to prices as high as the quality of meat served. A good option is the Brasilia branch of famous churrascaria chain Porcao, at Setor de Clubes Sul, trecho.

Vegetarians might find meat restaurants an unlikely option, but churrascarias also feature extensive salad bars and buffets that offer everything from sushi to risottos, alongside delicacies such as palm hearts and stuffed olives.

**Brazilian Pizza**

Brazilians love their pizza, and you’ll find pizza joints all over Brasilia. Again, this is often served rodizio-style, and you can find yourself eating all the slices you can manage, along with salad and sides, for less than the price of a takeaway pizza. Be prepared for some unusual takes on the standard pizza-making procedures. One pan-Brazilian quirk is to buy-pass the tomato sauce on the base in favor of squirting ketchup on top of the cheese (this practice is not favored by upscale pizza restaurants) and another is the concept of the ‘dessert pizza’. Should you wish, it is possible to round off your pizza-fest with decadent slices covered with toppings such as chocolate, strawberry, banana and cinnamon.

**Street Eats**

It’s possible to eat cheaply, and even relatively well, while on the go in Brasilia. Look out for street corner juice bars that sell juices and vitamin as (fruit juice mixed with milk) along with sandwiches and snacks. A cheap and very tasty nibble is pao de queijo, little dough balls made from yucca flour and cheese. Along with tapioca, which is served as a kind of pancake served with cheese, coconut or other fillings, this is a good option for anyone avoiding wheat.
