Armação dos Búzios sits on a rocky peninsula about 170 km east of Rio de Janeiro, and it has been Brazil's most fashionable beach resort since Brigitte Bardot put it on the map in 1964. A bronze statue of her still stands on the Orla Bardot boardwalk, a reminder of the moment this quiet fishing village turned into something altogether more stylish.

The peninsula has more than 20 beaches, each with its own character. João Fernandes is the social hub — calm, clear water, a line of colourful fishing boats, and vendors weaving through the crowd. Ferradura is sheltered and popular with families. Geribá faces the open Atlantic and draws surfers. The quieter beaches on the northern side, like Tartaruga, are where you go to see sea turtles and snorkel without crowds.

The town centre, Vila de Búzios, has the cobblestone Rua das Pedras running through it — a strip of open-air restaurants, boutiques, and bars that gets lively after dark. The food leans heavily on seafood: grilled fish, prawns, and moquecas made with produce landed that morning. Caipirinhas flow freely.

Despite the gloss, Búzios is genuinely beautiful. The light in the late afternoon, the fishing boats coming in against a green-forested hillside, the warmth of the water — it earns its reputation. The crowds can be intense in January and February; April and May are quieter, cooler, and better for actually enjoying the place.
