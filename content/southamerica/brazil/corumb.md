Corumbá sits on the western bank of the Paraguay River at the Bolivian border and calls itself the capital of the Pantanal — not an empty boast. The town is the main gateway into the southern Pantanal, the vast flooded wetland that is one of the world's great wildlife habitats. Jaguars, giant otters, capybaras, and hundreds of bird species live in the surrounding floodplains.

Known as the "white city" for the pale calcareous rock that underlies its soils, Corumbá has a colonial centre with 19th-century buildings and casarões (townhouses) left over from its days as a prosperous river port. The Paraguay River waterfront remains the social heart of the town.

Fishing and cattle ranching are the traditional industries; tourism now runs alongside them. Most visitors use Corumbá as a base for boat trips, fishing expeditions, and fazenda (ranch) stays deep in the Pantanal.
