Diamantina is a UNESCO World Heritage colonial town in the mountains of northern Minas Gerais, built on diamond wealth in the 18th century and remarkably well preserved since. The town clings to steep hillsides at around 1,200 m altitude, its cobbled streets lined with colourful colonial houses, baroque churches, and the ornate mansions of long-gone diamond merchants.

It is quieter and less touristed than its more famous neighbours Ouro Preto and Tiradentes, which gives it a more genuine feel. The Museu do Diamante tells the story of the diamond rush and the slave labour that powered it. The Casa de Chica da Silva — home of an enslaved woman who became one of the wealthiest people in colonial Brazil — is one of the most remarkable stories in the town's history.

The surrounding landscape is extraordinary: the Serra do Espinhaço, Brazil's oldest mountain range, with cerrado vegetation, waterfalls, and the start of the Estrada Real, the old royal road that connected the mining towns to the coast. The Caminho dos Escravos (Slave Road), a stone-paved trail built by enslaved people, can be hiked in a few hours and is both beautiful and sobering.

Diamantina is also the birthplace of Juscelino Kubitschek, the president who built Brasília. The town celebrates Vesperata, an open-air concert series where musicians play from the balconies of colonial houses in the main square — if you can time your visit for one, do.
