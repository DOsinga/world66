---
title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

Brazil celebrates at a scale and intensity that can genuinely overwhelm. The calendar is packed, but a few festivals stand apart.

**Carnival** is the centrepiece — four to five days of street parties, samba parades, and costumed revellers that descend on every city in the country in February or March (exact dates shift with the Catholic calendar, forty days before Easter). Rio's Sambadrome parade is the televised face of Carnival: weeks of rehearsal, tonnes of feathers and sequins, and samba schools competing before packed stands through the night. But Salvador's Carnival is arguably more democratic — enormous sound trucks (trios elétricos) drive slowly through the city while hundreds of thousands of people dance in the streets around them. Recife and Olinda's frevo-and-maracatu Carnival has its own fierce identity. Wherever you go, book accommodation months ahead and expect prices to double.

**Lavagem do Bonfim** takes place in Salvador on the second Thursday of January. A 10-kilometre procession walks from the Church of Conceição da Praia to the Church of Bonfim, led by Baianas in white dresses who wash the church steps with perfumed water. The procession is a blend of Catholic tradition and Candomblé ritual, and the street party that follows goes on all day.

**Festas Juninas** (June Festivals) are among the most underrated celebrations in Brazil. Held throughout June in honour of Saints Anthony, John, and Peter, they're biggest in the northeast — the cities of Caruaru (Pernambuco) and Campina Grande (Paraíba) both claim the title of the world's largest. Expect forró music, square dancing, stalls selling corn-based sweets and food, and elaborate mock-wedding pageants. The atmosphere is warm and family-oriented.

**Boi Bumba** takes place in Parintins on the Amazon River every June, drawing tens of thousands of people to a remote city to watch two competing folklore groups — the red Garantido and the blue Caprichoso — dramatise a folktale about the death and resurrection of an ox, using elaborate floats, costumes, and theatrical staging. It's one of Brazil's most visually spectacular events and remains largely undiscovered by international travellers.

**Réveillon in Rio** — New Year's Eve on Copacabana beach — draws millions of people dressed in white to watch fireworks launched from offshore barges. It's as much a religious event as a party: offerings to Iemanjá, the Afro-Brazilian goddess of the sea, are floated out on the water at midnight.
