Jaú National Park (Parque Nacional do Jaú) is one of the largest protected areas of tropical rainforest in the world, covering more than 2.2 million hectares in the state of Amazonas, about 220 kilometres west of Manaus. It was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2000 as part of the Central Amazon Conservation Complex, recognizing its exceptional biodiversity and near-pristine state.

The park protects a vast expanse of blackwater Amazon forest drained by the Rio Jaú and its tributaries, all flowing into the Rio Negro. The dark, tea-coloured water is rich in tannins and low in nutrients, which keeps it largely free of mosquitoes — a genuine relief for visitors. Caimans are abundant throughout the waterways, with roughly one per kilometre of habitat.

Wildlife here includes jaguars, giant river otters, pink river dolphins (botos), manatees, and hundreds of bird species. The forest itself ranges from dense closed-canopy jungle to more open formations along the floodplains, all of it deeply remote.

Visiting the park requires advance planning. Access is by motorboat from Novo Airão, a small town about 140 kilometres from Manaus via road across the Rio Negro. Overnight stays inside the park mean camping or floating lodges — there are no permanent lodgings within the boundaries.
