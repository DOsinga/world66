São Paulo is the largest city in the southern hemisphere, the economic engine of Brazil, and — to be honest — not a pretty city. Endless concrete, traffic that holds the world's worst-congestion records, a sprawl that takes hours to cross. What São Paulo lacks in beauty it makes up for in energy and diversity. This is the biggest Japanese city outside Japan, the biggest Italian city outside Italy, and home to over a million Lebanese descendants; the food, the music, and the nightlife draw from all of them.

The central **Centro** and **Praça da Sé** hold the old colonial and early-20th-century city. South of here, **Avenida Paulista** runs along the ridge as the city's great thoroughfare, with the São Paulo Museum of Art (MASP) as centrepiece. **Jardins** just west of Paulista is upmarket dining; **Vila Madalena** further on is boho bars and graffiti streets (the Beco do Batman is one of the better-known street-art alleys in the world). **Vila Olímpia** and **Itaim** are the newer money, with the towers of the financial district at the southwest edge.

São Paulo's food is genuinely exceptional. The city has the best **Japanese food** in Latin America (the Liberdade district for classic izakaya, D.O.M. and Maní for the fine-dining side). The **Mercado Municipal** downtown is the daily food market — try the oversized mortadella sandwich at Hocca Bar. And São Paulo is still the working capital of Brazilian **cachaça** and **caipirinha**; almost every restaurant does a respectable version.

There is no beach (the coast is 60 km away down the Serra do Mar) but the city has a serious art scene: **MASP**, the **Pinacoteca**, the **Museu de Arte Contemporânea** on the university campus, and the two-yearly **São Paulo Bienal** in Ibirapuera Park (founded 1951, second only to Venice).

Safety is a real concern — not at tourist-catastrophe levels, but Centro can feel rough after dark and robberies in traffic jams happen. Stick to the main neighbourhoods at night and use Uber instead of street taxis.
