Calama is a desert city in northern Chile's Atacama region, at 2,250 metres above sea level in the dry Loa River valley. It's primarily a mining city — the Chuquicamata copper mine nearby is the largest open-pit copper mine in the world — but most travelers know it as the transit hub for [San Pedro de Atacama](/southamerica/chile/sanpedrodeatacama), the oasis village and gateway to the Atacama Desert, about 100 kilometres east.

There's not much reason to linger in Calama itself — it's a functional mining town without much charm — but you will likely pass through on the way to or from San Pedro. The town has an airport with connections to Santiago and Antofagasta, a reasonable selection of hotels, and good bus connections to San Pedro (around 90 minutes on the paved road). The Parque El Loa near the centre is the main open space, and there are a few decent restaurants along the main commercial strip.

If you're arriving from Bolivia via the Uyuni salt flat crossing, Calama is typically the first Chilean city you'll reach, and after several days in remote highland landscapes it feels unexpectedly well-supplied.
