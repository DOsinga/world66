Chillán is a mid-sized city in south-central Chile, the capital of Ñuble Region, known for two things: it was the birthplace of Bernardo O'Higgins, Chile's independence hero, and it is the gateway to the Termas de Chillán, one of Chile's best hot springs and ski resorts.

The city itself was largely rebuilt after a devastating earthquake in 1939 that killed some 24,000 people. It is functional rather than beautiful, but the Feria de Chillán — the large outdoor market — is one of the best in central Chile, selling produce, leather goods, woven textiles, and longaniza chillanejas, the local sausage that is famous across the country.

The Escuela México, a school built with Mexican aid after the earthquake, contains murals by David Alfaro Siqueiros and Xavier Guerrero — an unexpected piece of Mexican muralism in the Chilean countryside. The Termas de Chillán, about 80 kilometres east in the Andes, has excellent skiing in winter (June–September) and volcanic hot pools year-round. The Volcán Chillán is an active volcano that last erupted significantly in the 1970s; hiking around it is spectacular.
