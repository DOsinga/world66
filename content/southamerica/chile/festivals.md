title: "Festivals"
type: section
---

**Fiestas Patrias (September 18–19)**

Chile's independence celebrations are the biggest events in the national calendar. Around the 18th, virtually the entire country stops for several days of eating, drinking, and traditional entertainment. Fondas — temporary outdoor venues — spring up in parks and fields across the country, serving empanadas, chicha (fermented grape or apple drink), and grilled meat. The cueca, Chile's national dance, is performed everywhere. It is one of the best times to see Chilean culture in full swing.

**Festival Internacional de la Canción de Viña del Mar (February)**

South America's biggest music festival, held annually in [Viña del Mar](/southamerica/chile/vinadelmar), draws major international and Latin American acts to an outdoor stage on the coast. The event has run since 1960 and is televised across the continent. Tickets sell out quickly; the city fills up and accommodation prices spike.

**Tapati Rapa Nui (February)**

Easter Island's two-week cultural festival celebrates Rapa Nui heritage through traditional competitions — including canoe races, body painting, and athletic events — along with music and dance. It's one of the most extraordinary cultural events in South America. Book flights and accommodation months in advance.

**Fiesta de la Tirana (July 12–18)**

Held in the small northern town of La Tirana in the Atacama, this religious festival draws over 100,000 pilgrims to honour the Virgen del Carmen. The highlight is the diablada: hundreds of dancers in elaborate devil masks and costumes perform to drums and brass in a spectacle that blends Catholic devotion with pre-colonial Andean tradition.

**Vendimia (March–April)**

Harvest festivals take place across Chile's wine valleys each autumn, with the most famous at Curicó in the Maule region. Events typically include grape-stomping, folk music, markets, and considerable quantities of the new vintage. A good excuse to explore the wine country outside of the summer rush.

**We Tripantu (late June)**

The Mapuche new year, marking the return of the sun after the winter solstice. Celebrated in the Araucanía region and in Santiago (on the hill of Santa Lucía and elsewhere), it involves traditional music, food, and ceremony. A quieter, more local event than the others listed here, but an interesting window into Mapuche culture.

**Ñam Gastronomy Festival (March, Santiago)**

An annual food festival in Santiago bringing together chefs, producers, and street food vendors to showcase Chilean and Latin American cuisine. A good opportunity to eat well and widely in the capital.
