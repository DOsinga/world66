---
title: "Food and Drink"
type: section
---

Chilean food is honest, filling, and heavily shaped by the sea and the land. It is not the most adventurous cuisine in South America, but at its best — fresh seafood, good wine, a slow weekend barbecue — it is very good indeed.

**Seafood**

Chile has one of the world's longest coastlines and its seafood is excellent. The standard is particularly high in port cities like [Valparaíso](/southamerica/chile/valparaiso) and in the south. Look for *locos* (abalone), *machas* (razor clams, usually served with parmesan and white wine), *centolla* (king crab, a speciality of Punta Arenas), and *piure* (a weird, red sea creature that Chileans love and most foreigners find challenging). *Caldillo de congrio* — conger eel stew — is a national classic; Pablo Neruda wrote an ode to it.

**Empanadas**

Chile's empanadas are different from those elsewhere in South America. The most traditional filling is *pino* — minced beef, onion, olives, raisins, and a hard-boiled egg — in a large, baked pastry. They are eaten everywhere, at every social occasion, and are arguably the closest thing to a national dish.

**Casuela and stews**

*Cazuela de ave* (chicken stew with corn, potatoes, and pumpkin) is Chile's go-to comfort food. *Chupe de mariscos* is a rich seafood chowder. *Curanto*, traditional to Chiloé, is a feast cooked in a pit in the ground — seafood, potatoes, meat, and sausage piled together over hot stones.

**Asado**

Weekend barbecue (asado) is a serious ritual in Chile. Beef, lamb, and chorizo over charcoal, eaten slowly with wine and salad. In Patagonia, *cordero al palo* — a whole lamb roasted on a cross-shaped spit over an open fire — is the version to seek out.

**Street food and snacks**

*Completo* is the Chilean hot dog, loaded with avocado, sauerkraut, tomato, and mayonnaise. It is impossible to eat tidily. *Choripán* (chorizo in bread) is the barbecue staple. *Mote con huesillo* — a sweet drink of dried peaches and husked wheat — is the traditional summer street drink.

**Desserts**

*Manjar* (dulce de leche) appears in everything. *Tres leches* cake, *torta mil hojas* (thousand-layer cake with manjar between the layers), and *sopaipillas pasadas* (fried pumpkin pastries in sweet syrup) are the classics.

**Wine**

Chilean wine has improved dramatically since the 1990s. The main red grape is Cabernet Sauvignon, but Carmenère — which all but disappeared from France after a 19th-century blight and survived in Chile — is the variety most associated with the country. The Casablanca and San Antonio valleys (near [Valparaíso](/southamerica/chile/valparaiso)) produce excellent whites, particularly Sauvignon Blanc and Chardonnay. Wine is inexpensive by international standards. A decent bottle in a supermarket costs less than USD 5.

**Pisco**

Pisco — a grape brandy made in the Elqui Valley — is Chile's national spirit, though Peru also claims it. Pisco sour (pisco, lemon, sugar, and sometimes egg white) is the standard aperitif. Chileans also drink pisco with Coca-Cola (called a *piscola*), which is ubiquitous at parties.

**Beer**

Cristal and Escudo are the dominant mass-market lagers. The craft beer scene has grown significantly in [Santiago](/southamerica/chile/santiago) and in tourist towns like San Pedro de Atacama and Pucón.
