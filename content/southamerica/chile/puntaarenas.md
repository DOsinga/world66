Punta Arenas sits on the edge of the **Strait of Magellan**, at the very bottom of continental Chile, and for most of its history it has been one of the most isolated cities in the world. Founded in 1848 as a Chilean military outpost to secure the strait, it grew rich in the second half of the nineteenth century on sheep, wool and shipping — every vessel from Europe to California came this way before the Panama Canal opened in 1914. That brief golden age left behind an extraordinary collection of belle-époque mansions built by British, Croatian and Spanish estancia owners, and most of the cemetery, bank buildings and central Plaza Muñoz Gamero feel more like a slightly weathered European provincial capital than a Patagonian port.

The city itself is windy, grey and unpretentious — a place of corrugated iron, bundled-up fishermen, and tough, friendly people. The Plaza and the **Museo Regional de Magallanes** (housed in the former Braun-Menéndez palace) are the obvious stops, and the **Cementerio Municipal**, with its manicured cypress avenues and the Croatian and English tombs, is worth more time than it sounds. From the harbour, boats run out to **Isla Magdalena**, a small flat island in the strait whose entire surface, from October to March, is covered by some 120,000 breeding Magellanic penguins.

For most travellers, though, Punta Arenas is a base. This is the gateway to Chilean Patagonia, **Tierra del Fuego**, and — through the sister-city of Puerto Natales — the glaciers and granite towers of **Torres del Paine**. It is also the jumping-off point for flights and cruises to **Antarctica**. Dress for the cold, the wind, and the long sub-polar summer evenings; bring good waterproofs, and prepare to eat extremely good lamb and king crab.
