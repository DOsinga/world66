Santiago de Chile sits in a wide valley at 520 metres, with the snow-capped Andes rising to more than 6,000 metres immediately to the east — on clear winter days you can see the mountains from almost anywhere in the city, and the sight of a modern metropolis of seven million people backed by that wall of rock and ice is genuinely dramatic. It is not the prettiest Latin American capital (Lima, Buenos Aires, and Bogotá all have more colonial charm), and the air quality can be grim in winter when the smog gets trapped in the valley. But Santiago has energy, excellent food, a strong cultural scene, and the kind of practical competence that makes it one of the easiest cities on the continent to navigate.

The centre of the city is the **Plaza de Armas**, the old colonial square with the cathedral, the post office, and the Museo Histórico Nacional. From there, the **Barrio Lastarria** and **Barrio Bellavista** are the two neighbourhoods most worth exploring: Lastarria is the artsy, café-filled quarter south-east of the centre, with the excellent **Museo de Bellas Artes** and the **GAM** cultural centre; Bellavista, across the Mapocho river at the foot of Cerro San Cristóbal, is the nightlife and restaurant district, and home to **La Chascona**, the Santiago house of Pablo Neruda, now a museum. Take the funicular up **Cerro San Cristóbal** for the panoramic view — on a clear day the entire Andes cordillera is visible from Aconcagua to the Maipo volcanoes.

Santiago's food scene has become one of the best in South America. The **Mercado Central**, the 19th-century iron-framed fish market by the river, is the classic lunch stop — eat a plate of *congrio frito* (fried conger eel) or *pastel de jaiba* (crab pie) at one of the stalls in the centre of the market, avoiding the tourist-priced restaurants around the edges. Chilean wine needs no introduction, and several vineyards in the **Maipo Valley** (Concha y Toro, Santa Rita, Cousiño-Macul) are less than an hour from the city and open for tours and tastings.

Day trips from Santiago are exceptional. **Valparaíso**, the colourful, crumbling port city an hour and a half west, is a UNESCO World Heritage site and a must. The ski resorts of **Valle Nevado**, **La Parva**, and **El Colorado** are barely an hour into the mountains — you can ski in the morning and be back in the city for dinner. And the vineyards of the **Casablanca Valley** (white wines, especially sauvignon blanc and chardonnay) are on the road to the coast.
