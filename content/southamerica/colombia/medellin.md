Medellin is the most striking Colombian city to visit. Once the most dangerous city in the world — Pablo Escobar's Medellin cartel ran it through the late 1980s — it has transformed itself more dramatically than almost any other place on the continent. The cable cars that climb from the metro stations up the valley walls to the once-forgotten barrios are a civic symbol, but the real change is in the street: Paisas, as locals call themselves, have thrown themselves at the project of reinvention.

The city sits in a narrow valley in the Andes at 1,500 metres, which gives it the mild year-round climate that earned it the nickname **La Ciudad de la Eterna Primavera** — the City of Eternal Spring. Average temperatures hover around 22°C, and it rarely gets hot or cold. You will not need either sunscreen or a coat most days.

Downtown is dense and chaotic, anchored by **Plaza Botero** — thirty or so bronze sculptures of deliberately rotund figures donated by the painter Fernando Botero, Medellin's most famous son. The **Museo de Antioquia** on the same square holds the bulk of his painted work. North of there, in the reinvented barrio of **Comuna 13**, the graffitied outdoor escalators that carry residents up the hillside are one of the best urban walks in Latin America.

El Poblado is where most travellers stay and drink — a leafy district full of cafes, good restaurants, and a nightlife scene that runs late. For a slower pace, take the Metrocable up to **Parque Arvi**, a cloud-forest reserve that starts at the top of the cable car line.
