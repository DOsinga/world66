Chone is an agricultural city in the interior of Manabí, Ecuador's broad Pacific province, sitting in a valley where the Río Chone gathers tributaries from the coastal cordillera before widening toward the mangrove-edged estuary near Bahía de Caráquez. The city runs on cacao — this region has been producing it for centuries, and the arriba variety from the Manabí interior is among the most prized fine-flavor cacao in the world. Balsa wood, kapok, and cattle farming fill out the local economy, and the city has the matter-of-fact, working character of a place that produces things rather than sells experiences.

The mangroves along the lower Río Chone and its tributaries are extensive enough to support boat excursions, though these are locally organized rather than tourist-packaged. In the city itself, the town square and surrounding streets have the cheerful, slightly chaotic feel of a coastal Ecuadorian market town. The food is the main reason to pay attention: Manabí cuisine is widely considered the richest and most distinctive in Ecuador, and in Chone you eat it as locals do — ceviche de camarón with chifles, corviche (fried balls of green plantain stuffed with peanut-spiced fish), seco de pollo, and the slow-cooked pork dishes that appear on every corner at weekends.

Chone positions itself as a gateway rather than a destination. Routes north and south along the coast connect to Esmeraldas and Manta respectively, while the road east climbs into cloud forest in the direction of the western Andes foothills. The city's most distinctive cultural marker is the rodeo montuvio — a tradition of the montuvio people, the mestizo agricultural communities of coastal Ecuador, involving bull-riding and equestrian competitions that draw serious crowds during local festivals, particularly in the second half of the year.
