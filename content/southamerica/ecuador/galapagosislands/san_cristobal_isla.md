San Cristóbal is the oldest island in the Galápagos archipelago and the one with the most history. Darwin made landfall here in 1835 — his first stop in the islands — and found what he later described as the most extraordinary assemblage of creatures on earth. Two centuries later the island still delivers on that promise.

Puerto Baquerizo Moreno, the island's small capital, serves as the administrative centre of the Galápagos province. It is calmer and less developed than Puerto Ayora on Santa Cruz, which makes it a good base for travellers who prefer a quieter pace. Sea lions sprawl across the benches in the main plaza as if they own the place — because, functionally, they do.

The island has excellent snorkelling and diving, particularly around Kicker Rock (*León Dormido*), a dramatic pair of volcanic columns rising 150 metres from the sea. The marine life here — Galápagos sharks, hammerheads, rays, sea turtles, and penguins — is as good as anything in the archipelago. On land, the Interpretation Center above town gives the clearest account of Galápagos natural history of any site in the islands, and the Galapaguera tortoise reserve lets you see giant tortoises without trekking into remote highlands.

San Cristóbal receives fewer visitors than Santa Cruz, which means less crowding at most visitor sites. The tradeoff is fewer departures for inter-island boats and fewer tour operators. For independent travellers willing to be flexible, this island is often the most rewarding entry point to the Galápagos.
