---
title: "When to Go"
type: section
---

Ecuador straddles the equator, which means temperatures don't swing dramatically between seasons the way they do at higher latitudes. What matters more is rainfall — and the patterns differ markedly between the highlands, the coast, and the Amazon.

**The Andes (Sierra).** June to September is the dry season and the best time for hiking, volcano climbing, and clear mountain views. Days are sunny and crisp; nights are cold. The wet season runs roughly October to May, bringing afternoon rain and frequent cloud. Travel is perfectly doable in the wet season — the landscape turns vivid green and the highland markets continue regardless — but mountain views can be obscured for days at a time.

**The Pacific Coast.** The warmest, most swimmable conditions run from December to April. Yes, this is technically the wet season, but on the coast rain usually means short afternoon downpours rather than all-day grey. May to November is drier but notably cooler, with a persistent mist called *garúa* common on the southern coast.

**The Amazon.** Rain falls year-round. The slightly drier months are August to November, which makes jungle trails more walkable, but the Amazon is fundamentally wet — embrace it. River levels are highest from March to July, which can be an advantage for boat travel and reaching remote oxbow lakes.

**The Galápagos.** The islands have two seasons. December to May is warmer (water temperatures around 24°C), with calmer seas and more rain. June to November is cooler and drier, with stronger currents — better for diving and snorkelling with cold-water species like hammerheads and penguins. Wildlife viewing is excellent year-round; different species peak in different months.

**Peak tourist season** is mid-June through August and the Christmas–New Year period. Prices rise and popular lodges fill quickly. If you're going to the Galápagos, book well ahead regardless of season.
