---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Falklands food is hearty, British-influenced, and much better than you might expect for somewhere this remote. Local produce — mutton, lamb, fish, and garden vegetables — makes up the backbone of most meals.

Lamb and mutton are the staples, slow-cooked to be tender and served with root vegetables. The seafood is excellent: mullet and trout are common, and Patagonian toothfish (sold as sea bass) appears on better menus. Squid is a local speciality; the Malvina House Hotel in Stanley is known for its squid dishes.

One Falklands tradition worth experiencing is "smoko" — the mid-morning and mid-afternoon break, served at farms and lodges with home-baked cakes, biscuits, and scones. It is an institution, and refusing seconds is considered impolite.

In Stanley, the dining options are limited but decent. The Malvina House Hotel restaurant is the most formal. The Waterfront Kitchen Café and West Store Café offer reliable daytime meals. The Globe Tavern does pub food in a convivial setting. For fish and chips, Michele's Café is a local favourite.

Local beer from Falkland Beerworks is well worth trying — the Peat Cutter and Rock Hopper ales are the flagship brews, named after things that actually belong here. South American wines are widely available. Spirits and imported goods are reasonably priced because the islands have low duty rates.

Outer island lodges typically offer full board, and the quality depends on the cook. Farm stays often produce some of the best meals on the islands — fresh, simple, and served in quantity.
