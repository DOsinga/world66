---
title: "Health"
type: section
---

The Falkland Islands are a safe destination with no serious infectious disease risks, but the medical infrastructure is limited and travel insurance is not optional — it is a condition of entry.

**Hospitals and medical care**

The King Edward VII Memorial Hospital (KEMH) in Stanley is the only hospital. It is well-staffed with UK-qualified doctors and has a full surgical team, but it is a small facility. Any serious or complex condition will require medical evacuation to Punta Arenas in Chile or Montevideo in Uruguay. Your insurance must explicitly cover air evacuation.

**Vaccinations**

No specific vaccinations are required. There are no tropical diseases and no malaria risk. Make sure routine vaccinations are up to date before you travel.

**The weather is the main hazard**

Hypothermia and exposure are real risks in the Falklands, particularly for people venturing out into the camp or onto the outer islands. Temperatures can drop quickly and the wind-chill is punishing. Dress in proper layers, carry waterproofs, and tell someone your plans before heading into remote areas.

**Landmines**

Argentine forces laid approximately 30,000 landmines around Stanley and other strategic areas during the 1982 occupation. Demining operations completed in November 2020 declared the islands officially mine-free. However, if you come across anything suspicious in an isolated area — particularly near old defensive positions — treat it cautiously and report it. Do not enter any area that still displays warning signs.

**Other practical health notes**

UV levels can be high even on overcast days due to the latitude. Sunscreen is advisable on outdoor days. Tap water is safe to drink throughout the islands.
