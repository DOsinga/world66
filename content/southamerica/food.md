title: "Food"
type: section
---

South American food is not one cuisine but many, shaped by geography, history, and the particular mix of peoples in each country. What ties it together is a reliance on a few exceptional ingredients — potatoes (which originated in the Andes), corn, beans, cassava, and fresh chiles — and a tradition of cooking that has absorbed African, European, and Asian influences without losing its local character.

[Peru](/southamerica/peru) has the most celebrated food on the continent and competes for recognition as one of the world's great culinary destinations. Ceviche — raw fish cured in lime juice with ají chili and red onion — is the national dish and an art form: the best versions are eaten within hours of the catch, at a cevichería near the ocean. Lomo saltado, a stir-fry of beef, tomatoes, and peppers served with rice and fries, shows the Chinese influence (brought by 19th-century Cantonese immigrants) that runs through Peruvian cooking. Lima has a restaurant scene that draws international attention; places like Central and Maido consistently rank among the world's best.

[Argentina](/southamerica/argentina) means beef. An asado — the Argentine barbecue — is not just a cooking method but a social ritual lasting hours, involving different cuts cooked slowly over wood embers. The country produces world-class wine, particularly Malbec from the Mendoza region and Torrontés from the north. In Buenos Aires, the milanesa (breaded cutlet) and the choripán (chorizo sandwich) are everyday eating. Dulce de leche appears in everything: pastries, ice cream, cookies. Provoleta, a thick round of grilled provolone cheese, is the standard start to any asado.

[Brazil](/southamerica/brazil) is vast and its food varies enormously by region. The national dish, feijoada, is a slow-cooked black bean stew with pork and smoked meats, traditionally eaten on Saturdays with rice, farofa (toasted cassava flour), and orange slices. In Bahia, the food is West African in character: acarajé (black-eyed pea fritters fried in palm oil), moqueca (fish stew with coconut milk and dendê oil), and vatapá (a thick seafood paste). Churrasco — Brazilian barbecue — is served rodízio style in churrascarias: waiters circle the tables with skewers of different meats until you flip your token to red.

[Colombia](/southamerica/colombia) runs on arepas — cornmeal patties eaten at every meal, filled or topped with cheese, meat, or eggs depending on the region. The bandeja paisa, the hearty plate of beans, rice, chicharrón, fried egg, plantain, and arepa, is the filling standard of the Andean interior. Fresh fruit juices made from maracuyá (passion fruit), guanábana, and lulo are exceptional and cheap. Colombian coffee, grown in the highlands, is among the world's best, though the finest single-origin beans tend to get exported — seek out specialty cafes in Bogotá or Medellín to drink them at home.

[Chile](/southamerica/chile) eats simply and well. Empanadas de pino — pastry filled with beef, onion, egg, and olives — are the default snack everywhere. Fresh seafood, particularly in the coastal cities and the south, is excellent: caldillo de congrio (conger eel soup) is the classic; locos (abalone) are expensive and worth it. Chilean wine — Carménère, Sauvignon Blanc from the Casablanca Valley — is world-class and absurdly affordable when bought in-country.

[Bolivia](/southamerica/bolivia) and the Andean countries eat more modestly. Salteñas, Bolivia's juicy baked empanadas, are eaten as mid-morning snacks. Api, a hot drink made from purple corn with cloves and cinnamon, is the Andean winter staple. Guinea pig (cuy) is a traditional Andean protein, roasted whole and served at festivals — it's worth trying once. Quinoa, now fashionable globally, has been grown in the Andes for thousands of years and appears in soups and salads throughout the region.

Street food is reliable and cheap across the continent. A busy stall at a market is almost always a safe bet — high turnover means fresh ingredients. Markets are often the best places to eat: Mercado Central in Santiago, Mercado de San Telmo in Buenos Aires, Mercado Surquillo in Lima all reward an hour of exploration.
