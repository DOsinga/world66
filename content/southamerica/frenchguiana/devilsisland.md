Just off the coast of [Kourou](/southamerica/frenchguiana/kourou) are three islands: Ile Royale, Ile Saint-Joseph, and Ile du Diable (Devil's Island). Together they are known as the Iles du Salut (Salvation Islands).

France operated a penal colony here from 1852 to 1953, incarcerating over 80,000 prisoners in total. The most infamous was Devil's Island itself, reserved for political prisoners. Alfred Dreyfus, the French army officer falsely convicted of treason in the 1890s, was held here. Henri Charrière, whose memoir Papillon describes repeated escape attempts, was another resident — though historians dispute some of his account.

Today the islands are overgrown with tropical vegetation and populated by howler monkeys, agoutis, and seabirds. The ruins of the prison buildings remain largely intact and make for a haunting visit. Ile Royale is the largest and most visited, with a small auberge, restaurant, and camping facilities. Ile Saint-Joseph has the solitary confinement cells and is particularly atmospheric. Ile du Diable itself, where Dreyfus was held, cannot be visited.

Boats depart from Kourou's Vieux Bourg port (end of Avenue Charles de Gaulle) in the mornings and return in the late afternoon. The crossing takes about 30 minutes. Book ahead during the July–November dry season and French school holidays.
