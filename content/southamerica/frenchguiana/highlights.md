---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

French Guiana is not a country that hands its best experiences to casual visitors. Most of what makes it remarkable requires planning, time, and a degree of physical effort. That said, the rewards are genuinely unusual.

**The Iles du Salut** are the most accessible highlight. Three small islands off the coast of [Kourou](/southamerica/frenchguiana/kourou) — Ile Royale, Ile Saint-Joseph, and Ile du Diable — formed one of France's most notorious penal colonies from 1852 to 1953. Over 80,000 prisoners were sent here, including Alfred Dreyfus and Henri Charrière (Papillon). Today the islands are overgrown with jungle, patrolled by howler monkeys and agoutis, and offer some of the most atmospheric ruins in South America. You can visit on a day trip from Kourou, or stay overnight on Ile Royale.

**The Guiana Space Centre** at Kourou is one of the world's busiest rocket launch facilities and a genuine piece of the present, not just history. Free tours of the centre are available on non-launch days; watching an actual launch (Ariane, Soyuz, or Vega) is a rare and extraordinary spectacle. Check the Arianespace schedule before booking your trip.

**Saul** is a small village in the forested heart of the country, reachable only by light aircraft. It sits inside the Guiana Amazonian Park and is the territory's main trekking hub, with trails of varying difficulty through pristine primary forest. Guided walks, night hikes, and longer multi-day routes through the jungle are all possible from here. Wildlife is abundant: tapirs, anteaters, toucans, and hundreds of butterfly species.

**The Maroni River** forms the border with Suriname and is the cultural heartland of the Maroon (Bushinengue) peoples. Saint-Laurent-du-Maroni is the main gateway; from here you can take pirogues upriver to Maroon villages, pass through dramatic river rapids, and enter territory that has very little in common with the coastal French Guiana. Hiring a local guide is strongly recommended.

**The Cacao Sunday Market** is a quiet revelation. The Hmong community that settled here in the 1970s sells fresh vegetables, tropical fruits, embroidered textiles, and extraordinary bowls of pho-style noodle soup. It's about an hour from Cayenne and worth the trip.

**Awala-Yalimapo** on the northern coast hosts one of the world's most important leatherback sea turtle nesting beaches. Between April and July, turtles come ashore at night to lay eggs. Guided night tours are organized with strict protocols to protect the nesting females.
