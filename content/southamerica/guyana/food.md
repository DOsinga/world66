---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Guyanese food is a genuine fusion cuisine shaped by the country's layered history: African, Indian, Chinese, Portuguese, Amerindian, and British influences all show up in the same meal. It is hearty, flavourful, and underrated.

**Pepperpot** is the national dish — a slow-cooked meat stew (usually beef or pork) with cassareep, a thick sauce made from cassava root that gives the dish its dark colour and distinctive bitter-sweet depth. It is traditionally eaten on Christmas morning but available at many restaurants year-round.

**Cook-up rice** is the everyday comfort food: rice and peas (usually black-eye peas or split peas) cooked together with coconut milk, meat, and herbs. Every family has its version. **Curry** — usually chicken, goat, or duck — reflects the strong Indo-Guyanese influence and is served with roti or rice. The roti here is excellent, especially the dhal puri (flatbread stuffed with split peas).

**Metemgee** is a thick, stew-like dish of root vegetables — cassava, plantain, yam, eddoes — cooked in coconut milk, often with fish or dumplings. It is old Guyanese home cooking at its best.

For snacks, look for **pholourie** (fried split-pea balls with mango sauce), **bara** (fried flatbreads), and fresh **coconut water** sold from road-side carts. Pepper sauce — made from wiri wiri peppers, Guyana's small but fierce local chilli — appears on every table.

Seafood is good on the coast: fresh prawns, butterfish, and hassar (a local armoured catfish) are all worth trying.

Georgetown has a reasonable restaurant scene ranging from Chinese restaurants and roti shops to a growing number of newer places catering to the influx of oil industry workers and tourists. Outside the capital, eating options are limited — most lodges and guesthouses prepare meals for their guests.
