---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Guyana's population of around 800,000 is one of the most ethnically diverse in the Americas, a legacy of centuries of migration and forced labour. The two largest groups are Indo-Guyanese, descended from indentured workers brought from India after emancipation, and Afro-Guyanese, descendants of enslaved Africans. Together they make up roughly 80 percent of the population. Smaller communities include mixed-heritage Guyanese, Amerindian peoples (about 10 percent), and smaller Chinese and Portuguese communities dating to the same era of indentureship.

This diversity shows in everyday life — in the food, the religious festivals, and the neighbourhoods of Georgetown. The country is predominantly Christian, with a substantial Hindu minority and a smaller Muslim community, and all three traditions are publicly observed. Diwali, Eid, and Christmas are all national holidays.

English is the official language and is spoken universally, though Guyanese Creole — a fast, lilting English-based patois — is what you'll hear in markets, on minibuses, and among friends. Amerindian communities in the interior maintain their own languages alongside English.

One of the defining facts of Guyanese society is emigration. Decades of political instability and limited economic opportunity sent large numbers abroad, primarily to the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom. The Guyanese diaspora is enormous relative to the home population, and remittances remain a significant part of the economy. The recent oil boom has begun to change the calculus for some.
