**Cuzco** (Qosqo in Quechua, "the navel of the world") was the capital of the Inca empire, and it remains one of the great historical cities of the Americas. The Spanish who arrived in 1533 tore down much of the Inca city and built their baroque churches, convents, and mansions on top of the foundations — so that today half the walls of the centre are Inca stonework, the other half colonial.

The heart of the city is the **Plaza de Armas**, with the cathedral on one side and the Iglesia de la Compañía de Jesús on another. From there the main axis is the Avenida del Sol, running southeast toward the **Qorikancha** — the Inca Temple of the Sun, where a Spanish monastery sits bizarrely on top of the original Inca walls. The San Blas quarter northeast of the plaza, reached by steep narrow streets, is the artists' district, with the best craft shops and a handful of good small restaurants.

Around the city are the ceremonial Inca sites — **Sacsayhuamán**, **Q'enqo**, **Puca Pucara**, and **Tambomachay** — reachable on a morning's walk or taxi circuit. Further afield is the **Sacred Valley of the Incas**, with Pisac, Ollantaytambo, and Chinchero, and of course **[Machu Picchu](/southamerica/peru/machupicchu)** — the main reason most travellers come to Cuzco in the first place.

Cuzco sits at 3,400 metres. Altitude sickness (*soroche*) is a real consideration: take it easy on day one, drink coca tea if the headaches start, and don't plan a big hike for the first day or two. The high season is May to September (dry, cold nights, the Inti Raymi festival on 24 June); the wet season runs November to March, with the Inca Trail closed for maintenance in February.
