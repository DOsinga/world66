---
title: "Eating Out"
type: section
---

Eating is the main reason to spend time in Lima. Over the last twenty years the city has become one of the great food capitals of the world: two or three of its restaurants are usually on the World's 50 Best list in any given year, and the cooking a few rungs down is still outstanding. The signature dish is **ceviche** — raw fish "cooked" in lime juice with red onion, *rocoto* chilli and sweet potato, traditionally eaten at lunchtime. Every neighbourhood has its favoured **cevichería**; the ones in the working-class district of **Surquillo** are cheaper and often better than their fancier Miraflores equivalents.

Beyond ceviche, look out for **tiradito** (sashimi-cut raw fish with a Peruvian twist), **causa limeña** (a cold layered potato terrine), **ají de gallina**, **lomo saltado** (the Peruvian-Chinese wok-fried beef that is the national comfort food), **anticuchos** (grilled beef hearts, best from a street cart), and the Peruvian-Japanese *nikkei* cuisine that grew out of the city's large Japanese-descended population. The world-famous fine-dining places — **Central**, **Maido**, **Astrid y Gastón**, **Kjolle**, **Mayta** — are concentrated in Miraflores and Barranco and need to be booked weeks or months ahead. For something more approachable, the old-school *cevicherías* **La Mar** and **Pescados Capitales**, the bistro **Isolina** in Barranco, and the *chifa* (Peruvian-Chinese) restaurants of the centre are reliably excellent.
