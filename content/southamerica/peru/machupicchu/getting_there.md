---
title: "Getting There"
type: section
---

You cannot drive to Machu Picchu. The site sits above the village of **Aguas Calientes** (officially Machupicchu Pueblo) at the bottom of the Urubamba gorge, and the only ways in are by train or on foot.

**By train**, two operators — **PeruRail** and **Inca Rail** — run services from Cusco (3–4 hours), Ollantaytambo in the Sacred Valley (1.5–2 hours), and sometimes Urubamba. The train lines hug the Urubamba canyon and the ride is a beautiful experience in itself. Tickets range from cheap "Expedition" class to the panoramic "Vistadome" and the luxurious Hiram Bingham dining train; book as far in advance as possible, especially in high season (May to September). On arrival at Aguas Calientes, a short **Consettur shuttle bus** climbs the 30-minute switchback road up to the site entrance. You can also walk up, but it is a strenuous 90-minute climb on an unshaded stone staircase.

**By foot**, the famous **Inca Trail** takes four days from km 82, and the various alternative treks — Salkantay, Lares, Inca Jungle, Choquequirao — take anywhere from four to nine days and all end at Aguas Calientes. All trekkers still need a separate entry ticket for Machu Picchu itself.

Buy your Machu Picchu **entry ticket** online well in advance through the official government site. There are strict daily visitor limits, several different circuits through the site, and timed entry slots. Bring your passport — they check it at the gate.
