The Santuario Nacional Tabaconas Namballe is a large protected area in Peru's far north, near the Ecuadorian border. Its remoteness has kept it remarkably intact — dense cloud forest covers the lower slopes, giving way to páramo grassland at the highest elevations, with lakes scattered across the mountaintops.

The sanctuary is home to the endangered Andean mountain tapir, one of the rarest large mammals in South America, as well as the spectacled bear and a wide diversity of birds and orchids. The páramo ecosystem — a high-altitude grassland found only in the northern Andes — functions almost like a sponge, drawing moisture from the clouds and feeding the rivers below.

Access is from the village of Tabaconas, reachable by colectivo from [Jaén](/southamerica/peru/northern_pacific_t/jan), [San Ignacio](/southamerica/peru/san_ignacio/san_ignacio), or [Huancabamba](/southamerica/peru/huancabamba). The trek to the highland lakes takes about six hours uphill through the forest. Horses can be arranged in Tabaconas. The park is officially open only for scientific tourism, but visits can still be arranged locally — check current access rules before making the trip.

The sanctuary faces threats from mining concessions on its borders and encroaching settlement. It remains one of the least-visited protected areas in Peru.
