---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

**[Paramaribo](/southamerica/suriname/paramaribo)** is the obvious starting point. The historic inner city — a UNESCO World Heritage site — is built almost entirely of wood and has survived better than most colonial capitals in the region. Fort Zeelandia, the Presidential Palace, Saint Peter and Paul Cathedral (one of the largest wooden buildings in the Western Hemisphere), and a neighbourhood where a synagogue and a mosque stand side by side: it is a genuinely unusual city.

**The Central Suriname Nature Reserve** covers 1.6 million hectares of primary tropical forest in the west-central interior. The reserve was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2000 and encompasses the older Raleighvallen, Eilerts de Haan, and Tafelberg reserves. It protects jaguars, giant river otters, tapirs, giant armadillos, eight species of primates, and around 400 bird species including the harpy eagle and Guiana cock-of-the-rock. Getting there requires a flight or a long drive and boat.

**[Brownsberg Nature Park](/southamerica/suriname/brownsbergnaturepark)** sits on a plateau 560 metres above the Brokopondo reservoir, about 130 km south of Paramaribo and reachable by road. It is the easiest jungle destination from the capital, offering hiking trails, waterfalls, and good wildlife spotting including howler monkeys and dozens of bird species. STINASU manages the park and runs a basic but comfortable lodge on site.

**The Maroon villages of the upper Suriname River** are among the most compelling cultural destinations in South America. Maroons are descendants of people who escaped enslavement during the colonial period and built independent communities in the forest interior. The Saramaka and Ndyuka communities have their own languages, music, and intricate woodcarving traditions. River trips from Atjoni take you through spectacular jungle scenery with stops at villages along the way.

**The Commewijne District**, reached by boat or over the bridge east of Paramaribo, offers old plantation estates set in open countryside. The restored Mariënburg sugar estate gives context to the colonial and post-colonial history that shaped modern Suriname. The river crossing by ferry or water taxi is pleasant in itself.

**Galibi Nature Reserve**, near the mouth of the Marowijne River on the Atlantic coast, is one of the most important nesting sites for leatherback turtles in the world. The hatching season runs from March to August, with peak activity in May and June. It is also home to Kaliña (Carib) Amerindian communities.
