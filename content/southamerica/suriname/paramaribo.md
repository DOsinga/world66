Paramaribo is one of the most unusual capitals in the Americas — a Dutch colonial city transplanted into the tropics, where wooden plantation houses line broad avenues and the sound of church bells, mosque calls, and Hindu temple music can all be heard from the same street corner. The historic inner city, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is a testament to Suriname's improbable mix of cultures: Dutch, Creole, Javanese, Hindustani, Chinese, and indigenous communities have coexisted here for centuries, and the architecture, food, and faces of the city reflect every one of them.

The heart of the old city clusters around Onafhankelijkheidsplein (Independence Square) and the waterfront along the Suriname River. Fort Zeelandia, the 17th-century Dutch fortress that once guarded the river approach, now houses the national museum and anchors the most atmospheric corner of the historic district. Nearby, the Cathedral of Saints Peter and Paul — one of the largest wooden churches in the Western Hemisphere — looms over a neighbourhood where a synagogue and a mosque stand on the same street, an arrangement that is more or less unique in the world.

Many of the colonial townhouses are in various states of decay, which gives Paramaribo a slightly melancholy beauty — faded white facades, shuttered verandas, overgrown gardens — but enough have been restored to make a walking tour genuinely rewarding. The Palmentuin, a palm-shaded garden beside the Presidential Palace, is the best place to sit down, watch the capuchin monkeys bicker overhead, and take stock of the city at a more leisurely pace.
