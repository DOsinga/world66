---
title: "People"
type: section
---

Suriname has a population of around 620,000 — one of the smallest in South America — concentrated almost entirely in Paramaribo and the narrow coastal strip. The interior is inhabited by Maroon communities and small numbers of Amerindian peoples, with vast stretches of rainforest in between.

The ethnic mix is extraordinary. Hindustani (Indo-Surinamese) descendants of indentured labourers brought from northern India in the late 19th century make up the largest single group, at roughly 27% of the population. Creoles — people of mixed African and European descent — account for around 18%. Javanese, descendants of labourers from colonial Java, make up about 14%. Maroons — descendants of Africans who escaped enslavement in the 17th and 18th centuries and built independent communities in the rainforest interior — constitute around 21%, a share that has been growing. Amerindian peoples, Chinese, and white Surinamese make up the rest.

Each community maintains its own cultural traditions, festivals, languages, and foods, while sharing Dutch as a common official language and Sranang Tongo as an everyday lingua franca. Paramaribo is one of the few places in the world where a Hindu temple, a mosque, a synagogue, and a Catholic cathedral stand within a few minutes' walk of each other — and where this proximity is unremarkable.

The Maroon communities of the interior are culturally among the most distinctive in the Americas. The Saramaka, Ndyuka, Matawai, and other Maroon groups developed sophisticated societies in the forest during centuries of conflict with Dutch colonial forces, eventually winning formal treaties of peace and autonomy. Their woodcarving, textile work, and oral traditions are recognized as a major contribution to the African diaspora.

Suriname's religious life is similarly diverse: Hinduism, Islam, Christianity in various forms (including the Moravian church, which has deep roots in Suriname), and indigenous spiritual practices all coexist. Public holidays span all major religions.
