Suriname is South America's smallest sovereign country and one of its least visited — which is part of the appeal. More than 90% of the land is covered by intact tropical rainforest, most of it untouched. The coast holds a different world: a flat, fertile strip where Paramaribo's Dutch colonial wooden buildings stand alongside Hindu temples, mosques, synagogues, and Chinese shops, all within a few blocks of each other.

The country was colonised by the Dutch, worked by enslaved Africans and later by indentured labourers from India, Java, and China. Their descendants all stayed, and Suriname is now one of the most ethnically diverse nations on earth. Hindustani, Javanese, Creole, Maroon, Amerindian, Chinese, and European communities each maintain their own traditions while sharing a common national identity built around the Dutch language and an easy-going pluralism.

Most travellers come for the nature. The Central Suriname Nature Reserve — a UNESCO World Heritage site covering 1.6 million hectares — is one of the largest protected tropical forests in the world. Brownsberg Nature Park, reachable by road in a couple of hours from [Paramaribo](/southamerica/suriname/paramaribo), offers an accessible introduction. The Maroon villages along the upper Suriname River are a world apart: communities of people descended from escaped enslaved Africans who built their own culture deep in the forest.

Suriname rewards travellers who are willing to slow down, ask questions, and get off the main roads. It is not set up for mass tourism, and that is its greatest asset.
