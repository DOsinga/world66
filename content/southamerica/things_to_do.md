---
title: "Things to Do"
type: section
---

South America has some of the most awe-inspiring archaeological sites on the planet. [Machu Picchu](/southamerica/peru/machupicchu) is the headline act, but the Sacred Valley around Cusco is packed with Inca ruins that get a fraction of the visitors. [Peru](/southamerica/peru)'s Nazca Lines are best seen from a small plane — the desert etchings are genuinely mysterious. In [Colombia](/southamerica/colombia), the multi-day trek to Ciudad Perdida (the Lost City) through the Sierra Nevada is harder to reach than Machu Picchu and arguably more rewarding. Bolivia's Tiwanaku and Easter Island's moai (technically [Chile](/southamerica/chile)) round out a continent where ancient civilizations left extraordinary marks.

Wildlife here is on another level. The Galapagos Islands off [Ecuador](/southamerica/ecuador) remain one of the best places on Earth to see animals that have no fear of humans — blue-footed boobies, marine iguanas, sea lions at arm's length. [Brazil](/southamerica/brazil)'s Pantanal is the place to spot jaguars, giant otters, and an insane density of birdlife. The Amazon basin offers jungle lodges across several countries, with [Ecuador](/southamerica/ecuador) and [Peru](/southamerica/peru) providing the most accessible entry points. In Patagonia, you can watch whales at Peninsula Valdes and penguins at Punta Tombo.

Trekking ranges from gentle day walks to serious multi-day expeditions. The Torres del Paine W Trek in [Chile](/southamerica/chile) is deservedly famous — five days of glaciers, granite towers, and turquoise lakes. Argentina's Fitz Roy circuit near El Chalten is equally spectacular with fewer crowds. In [Peru](/southamerica/peru), the classic Inca Trail requires booking months ahead, but alternatives like the Salkantay and Lares treks are just as beautiful. [Bolivia](/southamerica/bolivia)'s Cordillera Real and [Ecuador](/southamerica/ecuador)'s Avenue of the Volcanoes offer high-altitude routes for experienced trekkers.

The cities are reason enough to come. [Buenos Aires](/southamerica/argentina/buenosaires) is one of the world's great cities — tango, steak, architecture, nightlife that doesn't start until midnight. [Rio de Janeiro](/southamerica/brazil/riodejaneiro) combines beaches, mountains, and samba in a setting that never gets old. [Bogota](/southamerica/colombia/bogota) has reinvented itself with a thriving food scene and street art culture. [Lima](/southamerica/peru/lima) is now a serious culinary destination, with restaurants that rival anything in Europe. Valparaiso in [Chile](/southamerica/chile) charms with its hillside funiculars and colorful chaos.

Adventure sports are everywhere and often absurdly cheap. Sandboard down massive dunes at Huacachina in [Peru](/southamerica/peru). Raft white water on the Futalefu in [Chile](/southamerica/chile) or the Cotahuasi Canyon in Peru. Paraglide over [Lima](/southamerica/peru/lima)'s coastal cliffs. Mountain bike down Bolivia's "Death Road" from La Paz — it's touristy but genuinely thrilling. Surf big waves at Punta de Lobos in Chile or learn on the mellow breaks of Montanita in [Ecuador](/southamerica/ecuador).

The food alone justifies a trip. [Peru](/southamerica/peru) has arguably the best cuisine in the Americas — ceviche, lomo saltado, causa, and a culinary creativity that draws from indigenous, Spanish, African, Chinese, and Japanese traditions. [Argentina](/southamerica/argentina) does beef better than anywhere: a proper asado is a slow-cooked ritual, not just a meal. [Chile](/southamerica/chile)'s wine regions produce world-class reds at prices that would be unthinkable in Napa. Colombian street food — arepas, empanadas, fresh fruit juices — is cheap, delicious, and everywhere. In [Brazil](/southamerica/brazil), feijoada and churrasco are essential eating.
