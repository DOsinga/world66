## Carnaval (January–February)

Uruguay hosts the longest Carnaval in the world — a celebration that runs for roughly 40 days from mid-January through early March. The main events take place in [Montevideo](/southamerica/uruguay/montevideo), and unlike the Carnavals of Brazil, this one has an intimate, neighbourhood feel rather than a stadium-spectacle atmosphere.

The centrepiece is **Las Llamadas**, a two-night candombe drum procession through the historically Afro-Uruguayan neighbourhoods of Barrio Sur and Palermo. Hundreds of drummers, dancers in elaborate costumes, and flag-bearers fill the streets in an electrifying competition. The **Teatro de Verano** in Parque Rodó hosts nightly performances of *murga* (satirical musical theatre) and *parodistas* (comedy troupes) throughout the season.

If you're planning to come for Carnaval, book accommodation at least two to three months in advance. Hotels and hostels fill up fast, particularly for the Llamadas weekends.

## Semana Criolla (Easter Week)

Held during Holy Week at the Parque Rural in Montevideo, the Semana Criolla is Uruguay's premier gaucho festival. Expect rodeos, horse-breaking exhibitions, folk music, traditional food stalls, and displays of Uruguayan rural life. Entry is free or low-cost; it draws large local crowds and has a genuinely festive atmosphere.

## Fiesta de la Patria Gaucha (March, Tacuarembó)

The gaucho culture capital of Uruguay, Tacuarembó in the interior hosts this week-long festival celebrating all things gaucho: horsemanship, folk music, craft markets, and traditional dress. One of the most authentic cultural events in the country, and well worth the detour from the coast.

## Festival Casapueblo (New Year's Eve, Punta del Este)

The iconic Casapueblo building near [Punta del Este](/southamerica/uruguay/puntadeleste) hosts a sunset ceremony on New Year's Eve combining art, music, and a sound-and-light show. The area around Punta del Este is at its most festive during the December–February summer season.
