---
title: "Food"
type: section
---

Uruguayan food is shaped almost entirely by European immigration — Spanish, Italian, and to a lesser extent French and German. Indigenous culinary traditions are minimal. The result is a hearty, meat-heavy cuisine that prioritises quality ingredients over complex preparation.

**Asado** is the social institution at the heart of Uruguayan life. This is not just grilled beef but a whole ritual — wood fire, a long iron grill (*parrilla*), and time. A proper asado includes short ribs (*tira de asado*), sausages (*chorizos*), blood sausage (*morcilla*), sweetbreads (*mollejas*), and whatever else the cook decides to throw on. The cooking takes hours. Joining a family asado is the best possible introduction to Uruguayan culture. Restaurants specialising in grilled meats are called *parrillas* and you'll find them everywhere.

**Chivito** is Uruguay's national sandwich and completely misnamed — it contains no goat. The classic version has a thin beef steak, ham, bacon, melted cheese, lettuce, tomato, olives, a fried egg, and mayonnaise. It's enormous and extremely filling. A point of local pride.

**Mate** is everywhere. The dried leaves of the yerba mate plant are packed into a small gourd, hot water is poured over them through a metal straw (*bombilla*), and the drink is sipped and refilled repeatedly. It's bitter, caffeinated, and entirely an acquired taste. Uruguayans carry a thermos of hot water with them throughout the day and share the gourd as a social act. Accept if offered.

**Milanesa** — a breaded and fried escalope of beef or chicken — is the everyday comfort food of restaurants and homes alike.

**Medialunas** are the Uruguayan version of the croissant, slightly sweeter and smaller. A morning coffee and medialuna at a bar is the standard breakfast.

Wine from the Canelones department, just north of Montevideo, is worth exploring. Tannat is the signature grape — a dense, structured red that pairs well with asado.

Vegetarians will find things manageable in Montevideo and the main towns, but the countryside is firmly carnivore territory.
