---
title: "Highlights"
type: section
---

Venezuela has landscapes and natural wonders that rival anything on the continent — which makes the country's current difficulties all the more striking. For travelers willing to navigate the practical challenges, the rewards are real.

**Angel Falls (Salto Ángel)** is the world's highest uninterrupted waterfall at 979 metres, dropping off the face of Auyán-tepui in Canaima National Park. The standard approach is a small-plane flight from Ciudad Bolívar to Canaima, followed by a motorized canoe journey up the Río Churún and a short hike. The surrounding landscape — flat-topped tepuis (table mountains), savanna, and jungle — is unlike anywhere else on earth. The tepuis are among the oldest geological formations on the planet, and their isolated summits have evolved their own plant and animal species found nowhere else.

**Los Roques Archipelago** is a national park of over 300 islands and cays about 160km north of Caracas. The waters are turquoise, the reefs healthy, and the white-sand beaches largely undeveloped. It's a superb destination for snorkeling, diving, kitesurfing, and simply doing nothing. Small posadas on the main island, Gran Roque, provide comfortable if basic accommodation. Day-trip flights from Caracas take about 40 minutes.

**The Gran Sabana** is a vast highland plateau in Bolívar state, stretching toward the borders of Brazil and Guyana. The landscape is extraordinary: golden savanna dotted with tepuis, rivers that cascade over pink quartzite rocks, and indigenous Pemón communities. Mount Roraima — the tepui that inspired Arthur Conan Doyle's *The Lost World* — can be reached on a 5–6 day trek from the village of Paraitepuy. The summit is a bizarre landscape of black rock, pink quartz, and carnivorous plants in permanent mist.

**Mérida and the Andes** offer a different Venezuela: cool mountain air, colonial streets, world-class adventure sports, and the Mukumbarí cable car (one of the world's highest, reaching 4,765m on Pico Espejo). The city has a large student population and a lively atmosphere. The surrounding Andes provide excellent trekking, mountain biking, paragliding, and access to Los Llanos.

**Los Llanos** — the vast tropical plains covering roughly a third of the country — are one of South America's best wildlife-watching destinations. During the dry season (December–April) water retreats into pools and channels, concentrating extraordinary numbers of animals: caimans, capybaras, anacondas, giant anteaters, jabiru storks, scarlet ibis, and hundreds of other bird species. Working ranch-style lodges (hatos) offer guided wildlife excursions. Venezuela has recorded over 1,400 bird species, more than the whole of North America.

**[Choroni](/southamerica/venezuela/choroni) and the Henri Pittier coast** — A spectacular mountain road through the Henri Pittier National Park drops to the colonial village of Choroni and the fishing hamlet of Puerto Colombia. The beach (Playa Grande) is one of the best easily accessible from Caracas. The village has posadas, a lively drum culture (tambores), and a genuinely beautiful setting.

**[Coro](/southamerica/venezuela/coro)** is a well-preserved colonial city on the northwestern coast with a striking landscape of desert dunes on its doorstep (the Medanos de Coro). It's a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of Venezuela's oldest settlements. The Paraguaná Peninsula beyond it has beaches and the ghost of the old refinery boom.

**[Margarita Island](/southamerica/venezuela/margaritaisland)** has long been Venezuela's most-visited tourist destination — a duty-free island with decent beaches, resorts, and a historic capital at La Asunción. It remains more visitor-ready than most of the country, though the duty-free shopping that was once a draw is far less compelling today.
