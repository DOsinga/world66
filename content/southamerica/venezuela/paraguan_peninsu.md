The Paraguaná Peninsula juts out from the northwest corner of Venezuela, connected to Falcón State only by a narrow land bridge near Coro. It's dry, windy, and flat — arid scrubland dotted with goats and cardón cacti, with a steady breeze blowing in from the Caribbean almost year round. The wind draws kitesurfers, and the warm-water beaches on the western and northern coasts are uncrowded by Venezuelan resort standards.

Punto Fijo, on the western coast, is the industrial heart of the peninsula — home to the Amuay and Cardón refineries, together one of the largest oil refining complexes in the world. The surrounding coast has beaches at Los Taques and El Pico.

The peninsula's main natural landmark is Cerro Santa Ana, a prominent hill rising above the flat plain and declared a national natural monument. The hike to the summit begins in the village of Moruy — local guides can be hired there — and passes through zones of increasing humidity as you climb, from scrub at the base to cloud forest near the top. The views over the peninsula and toward Aruba are the reward. The small towns of the interior — Moruy, Pueblo Nuevo, Jadacaquiva — have attractive churches and a cottage industry in hand-crafted wooden furniture.
