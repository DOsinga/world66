Dengue fever is a viral disease transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito, most active during the day and typically found near human habitations. The disease is common in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly during and after the rainy season.

**Where you're at risk**

Risk is greatest in Southeast Asia, the Indian subcontinent, Southern China, Central and South America, the Caribbean, Mexico, and much of Africa. Some risk exists in Taiwan, the Pacific Islands, and northern Australia. The disease is rare above 1,300 metres altitude.

**Symptoms**

Sudden high fever, severe headache, muscle and joint pain, nausea, vomiting, and a rash appearing 3–5 days after onset. Symptoms typically last one to four weeks. A more severe form — dengue hemorrhagic fever — is rare in travellers but can be serious.

**Prevention**

No vaccine is available. Reduce risk by:

- Wearing long sleeves and trousers
- Using insect repellent containing DEET (30–35%)
- Staying in screened or air-conditioned rooms when possible
- Using bed nets

Aedes mosquitoes breed in small water containers — tyres, buckets, flower pots — not just swamps, which is why the disease is prevalent in cities.

**Treatment**

No specific treatment exists. Rest, fluids, and paracetamol (acetaminophen) for fever. Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen. Seek medical attention if symptoms are severe or if you develop signs of bleeding.
