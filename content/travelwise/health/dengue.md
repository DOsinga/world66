Dengue Fever is a mosquito-transmitted viral disease occurring chiefly in tropical and subtropical areas of the world. Occurence is usually seasonal: during and shortly after the rainy season. Although the risk of acquiring dengue fever during travel to tropical areas is relatively low, outbreaks of Dengue fever have occurred with increasing frequency in recent years in most countries of the Tropics. 

Aedes aegypti mosquitos, that transmit Dengue fever are most active during the day and are usually found in or near human habitations. Dengue is predominant in urban centers, but may also be found in rural areas; it is rare above 4000 feet/1300 metres of height. No vaccine for dengue fever is available, therefore travelers should avoid mosquito bites by wearing long pants and long-sleeved shirts and using bednets and insect repellents on skin and clothing. See also protection measures against mosquito bites on our health/malaria page. 

Symptoms of Dengue fever include high fever, severe headaches, malaise, muscle and joint pain, vomiting and a rash appearing about three days after the first symptoms. These symptoms are nonspecific and may easily be mistaken as signs of other infections ( eg, flu, measles or malaria). Dengue is often characterized by a sudden onset of symptoms and usually lasts from one to four weeks until complete recovery. More severe forms of the disease ( Dengue hemorrhagic fever = DHF) are very rare among travelers. 

There is no causal treatment of Dengue, symptoms can be alleviated with bed rest and medications to reduce fever, such as acetaminophen (= paracetamol). Aspirin should be avoided, especially in children.

The risk is greatest in the Indian subcontinent, Southeast Asia, Southern China, Central and South America (except Chile, Paraguay, and Argentina), the Caribbean (except Cuba and the Cayman Islands), Mexico, and Africa. There is a somewhat lower risk for travelers to Taiwan and the Pacific Islands. The Middle East and Northern Australia have a still lower risk of Dengue transmission. New Zealand is free of dengue fever. 

**BACKGROUND INFORMATION** 

**Description** Dengue fever is a viral disease transmitted by urban Aedes mosquitoes, usually Aedes aegypti, the same insect that also transmits yellow fever. The four different Dengue viruses are immunologically related, but do not provide cross-immunity. This implies, that you can fall ill with Dengue fever several times (as long as you keep catching a "new" virus strain). Dengue fever is characterized by sudden onset, high fever, severe, often frontal headache, joint and muscle pain, often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and a rash. The rash appears 3 - 5 days after onset of fever and may spread from chest and belly to arms, legs, and face. The disease is usually benign and self-limited, although the time to complete recovery may be prolonged up to four weeks. Many cases of nonspecific or even subclinical infection occur, but dengue may also present as a severe, sometimes fatal hemorrhagic disease called Dengue hemorrhagic fever (DHF). The risk ov developing DHF is highest in children < 15 yrs. of age who experience their second or third dengue infection. Thus, most travelers are at low risk for DHF. 

**Occurrence** Dengue fever is a rapidly expanding disease in most tropical areas of the world. There are now over 2.5 billion people living in areas at risk of infection and an estimated 100 million cases of dengue fever occuring each year, 500,000 (= 1%) of which are DHF, with 25,000 fatalities (= 5% of all DHF cases). Epidemics caused by Dengue viruses have become more frequent and larger in the past 20 years. Now, Dengue fever has become endemic in most tropical (and subtropical) countries of Africa, Asia, Central and South America (including Mexico and the Caribbean) and the South Pacific (see above map). After an absence of about 30 years, cases of Dengue fever have occured "even" in the US (southern Texas). In Southeast Asia, major epidemies of the severe Dengue form (DHF) have also become more frequent, occuring currently every 3 to 4 years in most countries. 

B?TRANSMISSIONAedes aegypti, the mosquito that spreads Dengue viruses, prefers to feed on humans during the daytime and is usually found near human dwellings, often indoors. The mosquitos are not dependent on swamps, rivers or lakes to breed, their breeding sites also include artificial water containers such as discarded tyres, barrels, buckets, flower pots or thrown-away tin cans. This may explain, why the disease is predominant in urban centers (but may also be found in rural areas). The risk of transmission is highest during and around the rainy season. 

**Prevention** As in all insect-borne diseases, the cornerstone of prophylaxis is to avoid insect bites. You can reduce the risk of acquiring dengue by remaining in well-screened or air-conditioned rooms when possible, by wearing clothing that covers the arms and legs, and by applying insect repellent to both skin and clothing. The most effective repellents are those containing diethyl-metatoluamide (DEET) at a concentration of 30-35%. Higher concentrations of DEET should not be used on the skin, particularly in children. See also protection measures against mosquito bites on our malaria page. 

**Disease symptoms** Dengue fever is characterized by sudden onset, high fever, severe headaches, joint and muscle pain, nausea, vomiting and rash. The rash appears 3-4 days after the onset of fever. Infection is diagnosed by a special laboratory blood test. The illness may last up to 10 days, but complete recovery can take 2 to 4 weeks. Dengue is commonly confused with other infectious illnesses such as influenza, measles, malaria, typhoid, and scarlet fever. The symptoms of dengue can be treated with bed rest, fluids, and medications to reduce fever, such as acetaminophen. Aspirin and ibuprofene should be avoided. More severe forms of the disease (Dengue hemorrhagic fever or Dengue shock syndrome) are very rare among travelers. Symptoms initially are indistinguishable from dengue fever, but the illness progresses to faintness, shock, and generalized bleeding. Dengue does not produce long-term complications. See a physician if you become sick with Dengue symptoms during travel or within a month of returning from travel in a tropical area. Be prepared to give your complete travel itinerary, so that the physician can evaluate the possibility that your symptoms were caused by a Dengue infection. 

Vaccination / Treatment Neither a vaccine nor specific treatment for Dengue fever is available.
