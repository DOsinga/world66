Naxalism is an armed Maoist insurgency that has affected parts of [India](/asia/india) since the late 1960s. Named after Naxalbari, a village in West Bengal where the movement began, it remains one of the most significant internal security challenges in the country. For travelers, understanding where Naxalite activity occurs is important for planning safe routes through central and eastern India.

The areas most affected — sometimes called the "Red Corridor" — stretch through rural and forested districts in states including Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Odisha, Bihar, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh, and Telangana. Naxalite groups operate primarily in remote, underdeveloped areas far from major cities and tourist routes. Attacks typically target security forces and government infrastructure, but travelers in affected areas face risks from roadblocks, strikes (bandhs), and general instability. Kidnappings of outsiders, while rare, have occurred.

If you are traveling through central or eastern India, check current advisories before venturing into rural districts away from main highways and railways. Major cities like Hyderabad, Kolkata, Bhubaneswar, and Ranchi are safe. The risk is in the interior — forested hill districts with limited infrastructure where police presence is thin. Local people and state tourism offices can advise on current conditions. If a district is described as "Naxal-affected," take that seriously and travel during daylight hours on main roads.
