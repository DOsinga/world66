# Travel Guide in Sosua

*Centralamericathecaribbean > Thecaribbean > Dominica > Puertoplata > Sosua > Travel Guide*

Drenched in sunlight and pristine beaches, it is easy to understand why any traveler would want to read a **Sousa Travel Guide** before heading to this tourist rich area in the Dominican Republic. New to tourism as a whole, the city was widely unknown until tourists began visiting in the 1980s.

Sousa itself is a small town located near the Puerto Plata International Airport. The area is accessible to vacationers via Highway 5 and is broken down into three sectors, each offering something different for tourists. El Batey is the most commonly visited sector and is famous for sport fishing and water sports, while Sousa Abajo and Los Charamicos offer other vacation possibilities including vivid nightlife or serene rural areas.

**El Batey**

Water sports' lovers will feel at home in El Batey as the Sousa Bay is a haven for scuba and snorkeling. The waters are calm and clear and sheltered making them a perfect sanctuary for many species of fish. Beautiful reef structures are common in the bay as well. In the winter months, whale watching is a common tourist activity, as well.

**Sousa Abajo**

The area means literally "Lower Sousa" and is located along a river running through a valley and into Sousa Bay. The area is largely rural and is comprised mostly of the homes of workers who commute to El Batey, Cabarete Bay and Playa Dorada.

**Los Charamicos**

If nightlife** **is your cup of tea, then the Los Charamicos is the area you're most likely to enjoy. Located between El Batey and Sousa Abajo, the area is best known for the night clubs, dancing, bars and urban style.

**Beaches**

As with most destinations in the Caribbean, white sands and warm waters are common in Sousa, as well. Beaches here include the newly and naturally formed beach just north of the Bay as well as crescent shaped Sousa Beach, Paradise Beach, Playa Imbert, and Playa Chiquita.

**Historical Cheese Factory**

The population of Sousa is largely made up of Jewish Refugees who were offered citizenship in the 1940s--before, during and just after--World War II. The Dominican government provided the refugees with land and resources. From this the people created a cheese factory. The factory is still standing today, and a museum offers a glance at the transition these early settlers experienced. Descendants from the originals refugees are still in the area today.
